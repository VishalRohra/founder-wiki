## Ideas, Validation, and Getting Started

- [[Startup Ideas]] (idea generation, finding ideas, schlep filter, tarpit ideas, SISP, wells vs craters, founder/market fit, larval market)
  How to find, evaluate, and validate startup ideas. Covers Graham's organic discovery method, the well model (narrow-but-deep vs broad-but-shallow), dangerous idea filters (schlep blindness, unsexy filter), sitcom ideas, and recipes for on-demand generation. [7 sources]

- [[Becoming a Founder]] (should I start a startup, students and startups, college and startups, career paths, too young to start, working at a startup, ZIRP recovery)
  Whether and when to start a startup, including advice for students, career path tradeoffs (founder vs executive vs employee), what holds people back, and how hackathon projects and academic backgrounds translate into companies. [12 sources]

- [[Cofounders]] (co-founders, finding a cofounder, cofounder matching, solo founder, cofounder conflict, equity split, cofounder questions)
  The cofounder relationship as the foundation of a startup. Where to find cofounders (school, work, YC matching), how to evaluate them (10-question framework), why solo founders face severe disadvantages, and how to structure the commitment. [7 sources]

- [[YC Essential Startup Advice]] (YC advice, essential startup advice, pocket guide, launch now, write code talk to users, 90/10 solution)
  YC's canonical compilation of its most important advice: launch early, write code, talk to users, focus on the 90/10 solution, and raise money quickly then get back to work. A single-article distillation of YC's operating principles. [1 source]

## Building and Iterating (Pre-PMF)

- [[Early-Stage Tactics]] (do things that don't scale, first users, user acquisition, 0 to 1, Collison installation, manual user recruitment, launch strategy)
  The unscalable work required to get a startup off the ground. Covers manual user recruitment (Collison installation), the contained fire strategy, the consulting model, pulling a Meraki (hardware), releasing early, and why big launches and partnerships fail. [5 sources]

- [[Product Development]] (building product, feature prioritization, MVP, iteration, shipping, two-week cycles, dev cycle, product meetings)

## Frameworks

- [[Do Things That Don't Scale]] (Collison installation, contained fire, consulting model, Meraki, manual user recruitment, startup ideas as vectors)
  Graham's framework for the unscalable labor required to start a startup. The essay that named the Collison installation and introduced startup ideas as vectors (what you build + what you do to get going). [1 source]

- [[Default Alive or Default Dead]] (fatal pinch, Plan A Plan B, runway calculation, overhiring, growth-spending disconnect)
  Graham's diagnostic question: at current expense and revenue trajectory, will you reach profitability before running out of money? Covers the fatal pinch (when it becomes too late to save yourself) and the overhiring trap. [1 source]

- [[The Equity Equation]] (1/(1-n), equity formula, stock trade test, employee equity calculation, investment evaluation)
  Graham's general formula for evaluating any trade of company stock. Give up n% only if the trade makes the remaining (100-n)% worth more than the whole was before. Applies to investor deals, employee grants, and partnerships. [1 source]

- [[Pitch Deck Design]] (deck design, slide design, demo day slides, legible simple obvious, Kevin Hale)
  Hale's three-principle framework for pitch deck design: legible, simple, obvious. Adapted from his pre-Demo Day presentation to every YC batch, with specific rules for fonts, layout, and information density. [1 source]

- [[Feature Prioritization Frameworks]] (Built For Me, Switch To Us, Three Numbers Matter, feature prioritization, what to build next, product prioritization)
  Shear's three mental models for deciding what to build, drawn from Justin.tv and Twitch. Startups progress through them as challenges shift: founder intuition, competitive user interviews, then quantitative metric decomposition. [1 source]

- [[Mission-to-Metrics]] (mission to metrics, mission strategy metrics, creating alignment, purpose and alignment, company alignment)
  Rowghani's framework for how CEOs create purpose and alignment: Mission (purpose), Strategy (direction), Metrics (pace). The essential context a growing company needs to perform, with examples from Pixar and Amazon. [1 source]

- [[Three Traits of Great Leaders]] (measuring leadership, leadership measurement, trust-based leadership, leadership traits)
  Rowghani's framework for measuring leadership, derived from observing Catmull, Jobs, Lasseter, and Iger at Pixar and Disney. Identifies the three traits that distinguish great leaders from competent managers. [1 source]
