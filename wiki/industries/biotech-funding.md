# Biotech Startup Funding

[[Jared Friedman]] argues that biotech startup funding in 2019 resembles tech startup funding circa 2005, and that the same forces [[Paul Graham]] predicted would transform tech fundraising are now transforming biotech. Graham's predictions for tech (more startups, cheaper to start, new kinds of investors, more technical founders, founders keeping control) all came true, and Friedman sees them playing out again in biotech.

## How Tech Fundraising Changed (2005-2019)

In 2005, VCs preferred to fund companies that already seemed like a sure bet and favored MBAs with executive experience over unproven technical founders. They asked for onerous terms and often replaced founders with favored executives. The only institutional seed model was the "business incubator" where VC firms would incubate well-connected founders in their offices.

Then the cost to start a tech company plummeted: open source software, modern web frameworks, SaaS developer tools, cloud hosting, and better distribution channels. Technical founders who could not raise off a PowerPoint were now able to launch products and get users with minimal funding, then use traction to raise.

These companies only needed small amounts of money to get started, but no institutional investors made small investments. This insight led to YC's creation and the hundreds of seed funds that followed. Because companies raised VC later (with leverage from traction), the balance of power shifted. Founders retained control. Investors lost the power to fire founders. And "something surprising" happened: "despite their inexperience, the founders were often the right people to run the company."

## The Current Biotech Landscape

Early-stage biotech funding is dominated by the "venture creation model." The VC firm creates the company, assembles a team from their pool of entrepreneurs-in-residence, incubates the startup in the VC's offices, invests a large amount ($10M+) upfront, and takes a controlling ownership stake.

This model made sense when starting a biotech company was prohibitively expensive. But new infrastructure has dramatically reduced costs:

- Low-cost CROs: [WuXi Biologics](http://wuxibiologics.com), [Evotec](http://evotec.com), [ChemPartner](http://chempartner.com)
- [Science Exchange](https://www.scienceexchange.com/): instant, cost-effective access to CROs and scientific supplies
- Fully equipped [lab space](https://mbcbiolabs.com/) [by the bench](http://www.berkeleybiolabs.com/)
- Lab supply companies: [Quartzy](https://www.quartzy.com/), [Happilabs](https://www.happilabs.org/)
- Affordable lab robots: [OpenTrons](https://opentrons.com/) for automating batch experiments
- Computational drug discovery: [Atomwise](https://www.atomwise.com/) for in silico experiments
- Lower-cost patent filing: [Cognition IP](https://www.cognitionip.com/)
- Streamlined FDA submission: [Enzyme](https://www.enzyme.com/)

As a result, founders can make real progress proving a concept for as little as $100K.

## YC Biotech Examples

**Shasqi**: In 2015, Jose Mejia Oneto left orthopaedic surgery residency to pursue localized chemotherapy delivery. When he applied to YC, he had developed the technique in academia but had not tried applying it to therapeutics in animals. Using just YC funding, in less than three months he showed in a breast cancer mouse model that his localized delivery outperformed conventional chemo.

**Athelas**: Tanay and Deepika started a company making at-home blood test devices for oncology patients using computer vision. They started in college with $40K in investment, built a working prototype, and during YC ran a 350-patient initial study with very good results. The device is now FDA cleared, serving thousands of patients.

Friedman notes: "The point here is not that these companies will ultimately succeed -- we don't know that yet. My point is that with just a seed investment and a few months, they managed to go as far along the curve as companies that had to raise millions of dollars before."

## Predictions for the Future

Because costs have dropped, it is now possible to start a biotech company the way people start a tech company: raising incrementally rather than a giant amount upfront, keeping control, and working on your own idea rather than one VCs come up with.

This has drawn a new kind of biotech founder. Many are grad students or postdocs. "Previously their career options were to stay in academia or to join a big pharma company. Starting their own company is now a viable third option."

Traditional biotech investors still seek controlling legal terms that "went out of vogue in tech in the early 2000's." But a new crop of biotech and tech/biotech crossover funds have created a vibrant seed investor ecosystem. YC bio companies typically raise $1-5M seed rounds after each batch.

Running clinical trials remains very expensive, and biotech companies will ultimately need to raise large amounts. But this parallels tech: the biggest YC software companies have each raised over $1B. "The important part is that these companies were able to get started with less than $100K and to de-risk their idea enough to raise more money later."
