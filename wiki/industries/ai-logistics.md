# AI in Logistics

Logistics is emerging as one of the largest vertical markets for AI agents. The industry is characterized by high transaction volumes, manual processes, phone-heavy workflows, and thin margins -- making it ripe for AI automation [1].

## Why Logistics

The logistics industry runs on phone calls, emails, and spreadsheets. Freight brokers -- intermediaries who match shippers with carriers -- spend most of their day on calls negotiating rates, tracking shipments, and handling exceptions. Each interaction follows predictable patterns but requires enough judgment that simple automation has historically failed [1].

AI voice agents are particularly well-suited to this domain because:
- **High call volume**: A single broker may make 50-100 calls per day
- **Predictable conversation patterns**: Rate negotiations, shipment status updates, and exception handling follow templates
- **Clear success metrics**: Did the load get booked? At what rate? On time?
- **Willingness to pay**: Even small efficiency gains at scale justify significant software spend [1]

## Case Study: Happy Robot

Happy Robot, a YC company, has sold seven-figure contracts to the top three largest logistics brokers in the world for AI voice agents [1]. The company uses the [[Forward Deployed Engineers]] model: founders talk directly to CIOs of major logistics companies, rapidly customize the product, and close deals through impressive demos that competitors cannot match.

The progression from six-figure to seven-figure deals happened within months, demonstrating how quickly AI companies can move upmarket when the product genuinely solves the problem [1].

## The Broader Pattern

Logistics represents a template for how AI will penetrate traditional industries: start with the most repetitive, phone-heavy workflows; demonstrate 10x improvement in speed or cost; use the FDE model to close initial enterprise deals; then expand into adjacent workflows within the same customer [1].
