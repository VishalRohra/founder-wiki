# Simple Products

Many of the largest companies in existence started as products that did exactly one thing. [[Dalton Caldwell]] and [[Michael Seibel]] argue that founders systematically overestimate the number of features needed to launch and underestimate the danger of building a multi-feature product that does not work reliably [1]. The pattern across OpenSea, Gusto, Amazon, and others is consistent: start with one use case, make it work, then expand.

## Boiling the Ocean

Caldwell and Seibel call the instinct to build a complete, elaborate product before acquiring a single user "boiling the ocean" -- attempting something basically impossible or intractable [1]. The mistake begins with a lie: "Every founder starts their company with a lie in their head that they know what their customer wants and they know how to solve the problem. And the more you build before putting your product in front of a user, the more you just let that lie go wild" [1].

The antidote is contact with reality. Getting something -- anything -- in front of a user reveals how many hypotheses were correct and how much was "just the lie that got me started doing my company. A useful lie, but maybe not the best lie if you want to solve your user's problem" [1].

## Googleitis

Seibel coined the term "Googleitis" for the pattern of looking at companies as they exist today and not understanding what they looked like at the beginning [1]. Founders aspire to build the next Amazon and plan to build everything Amazon has today. Caldwell counters: "When Amazon started, it was a really simple website that just sold books. It didn't do anything else. I think it didn't even do that that well" [1].

## Case Studies

### OpenSea: A Simple NFT Marketplace

OpenSea was in YC in 2018. The founders pivoted from a different idea. They spent one to two years grinding with "a really simple website that just let you buy and sell NFTs and like barely worked" [1]. They shipped constantly -- daily or weekly -- and deliberately did not build their own wallet (users had to use a third-party wallet). By focusing on the core use case and building network effects, "we all woke up one day and this is one of the hottest companies in the world, and everyone wants to invest, and they're worth billions of dollars" [1].

Seibel emphasizes the obscurity period: "Don't forget the two or three years they were grinding away in obscurity just trying to make the dang thing work, doing the simplest thing that had basically no features" [1].

### Gusto (ZenPayroll): Online Payroll

Gusto today offers payroll, benefits, org charts, and 401k management. When it started in YC Winter 2012 as ZenPayroll, it did exactly one thing: payroll [1]. The status quo was calling ADP, having someone come to your office to sign paperwork, then calling every month to confirm you wanted to run payroll. Gusto said: "We can just do that online. And that's all it's going to do. And you're going to love it" [1].

Caldwell uses Gusto to illustrate the asymmetry of simplicity vs. complexity: "A product that doesn't work with lots of features is infinitely worse than a product with one feature that works." A hypothetical Gusto that offered healthcare, benefits, 401k, and payroll but occasionally lost employees' money would be universally hated. "That sounds like Verizon or something. That's a great way to earn a hated company -- have a lot of features that don't reliably work" [1].

## The Copy-and-Paste Fallacy

A related mistake is assuming that product-market fit transfers across geographies [1]. A YC founder wrote: "We copied another successful business model from an overseas market and wrongly assumed that product-market fit was a given. If the product works in country X, it's going to work in country Y. We scaled up hiring quickly and burnt a lot of money before we realized that the users we were selling to did not want our product" [1].

Caldwell identifies the deeper problem: founders substitute external validation for ground truth. "What we want to believe is that by getting validation from investors or the press or my friends or employees or winning a startup competition, that meant it was good." He compares it to a driver's license: "Lots of people can get into trouble with their driving habits even though you have a driver's license" [1]. Having permission to start (funding, press, a plausible thesis) does not mean the product works.

## Ground Truth Over Authority

Caldwell and Seibel make a meta-point about advice itself. The purpose of their guidance is to direct founders toward customers, not to serve as the authority. "People email us after these videos... Our whole point is, 'Go talk to customers, go figure it out.' And they email us and they're like, 'Can you give me advice?' They kind of aren't getting the point of these videos" [1].

Seibel traces this to how people learn. In school, physics is taught by a teacher -- you never have to derive the laws from first principles. "What we're basically saying is that all of the ways that you learned in the past don't work here. You actually have to go observe the problem, talk to the people who have the problem, try to figure out what's going on, and iterate" [1].

## Discipline Over Intelligence

The conversation turns to why some founders execute and others do not. Seibel argues that discipline -- not intelligence -- is the primary differentiator among founders [1].

Caldwell illustrates with the low-runway effect: "When someone's back is against the wall, the quality of their decisions get much better." He compares it to writing a paper in college -- great work happens in the final 24 hours that could have happened at any point during the semester [1].

Seibel frames the metric: "If you looked at their calendar, if you shadowed them, the folks that are really good at this are just better at doing focused work. There's kind of nothing else" [1].

The encouraging implication: unlike raw intelligence, discipline is improvable. "You can't really change your raw intelligence, but you can get better at this. Like, this is a little bit like working out. If doing focused work for someone who likes to procrastinate is a little bit painful, the more they do it, the less painful it gets" [1]. Caldwell concludes: "Smart people certainly have an advantage, but man, hard workers are dangerous" [1].

## Crowdfunding as a Cautionary Tale

Caldwell and Seibel discuss hardware crowdfunding as an instance of the same mistake -- building elaborate plans before validating fundamentals [1]. The failure mode:

1. No idea how to build the product
2. No idea what it should cost
3. No idea whether it solves the user's problem
4. But a lot of money up front and enormous expectations

The result: "There's no surer way to make strangers on the internet hate you." Campaigns that fail to ship on time or at all create thousands of angry backers who track founders down and demand refunds. "We've seen this ruin people... It never leaves you. You get that permanent Wikipedia page that's not what you want" [1].

Seibel's revised best practice for hardware: "Do it after you've shipped and manufactured a couple of versions already" -- use crowdfunding at V2 or V3, not V1 [1].

The broader principle extends to any model where money is collected before value is delivered: "If you take money from people at scale on the internet, they rightfully will hold you accountable" [1].
