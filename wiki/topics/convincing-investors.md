# Convincing Investors

Convincing investors is not a performance problem. It is a truth problem. [[Paul Graham]] argues that inexperienced founders make the same mistake as people who hurt themselves lifting heavy things: they try to lift with their back instead of their legs. Founders try to convince with their pitch rather than letting the startup do the work [1]. The correct approach is to first understand why the startup is worth investing in, then explain that clearly.

[[Dalton Caldwell]] extends this principle beyond investors to all stakeholders: co-founders, employees, customers, and partners. "If you really have that fire, that you're onto something that's worth doing and you're convinced, then it's much easier to convince other people to join you" [3]. The mechanism is the same -- genuine conviction is more persuasive than polished performance. See [[Recruiting Early Team Members]] for the full treatment.

## What Investors Are Looking For

Investors seek startups that will be very successful. Because startup outcomes follow a power law with a steep curve, most investors treat "big success" as binary. The conventional wisdom is that roughly 15 startups per year become the big successes, and investors are interested in a founder only if there is some chance, however small, of being one of those 15 [1].

Three things make a startup look like a sufficiently good bet [1]:

1. **Formidable founders**
2. **A promising market**
3. **Some evidence of success so far**

## Formidable Founders

Most investors decide within the first few minutes whether a founder seems like a winner or a loser, and once that opinion is set, it is hard to change [1]. If investors think you are a winner, they focus on reasons to invest. If not, they focus on reasons not to [1].

Graham defines "formidable" as someone who seems like they will get what they want, regardless of obstacles. The way to seem formidable as an inexperienced founder is to stick to the truth [1]. Graham writes:

> "Convince yourself that your startup is worth investing in, and then when you explain this to investors they'll believe you." [1]

This requires being a domain expert. Investors can tell quickly whether founders truly understand their market by how they answer questions [1].

## The Three-Step Recipe

Graham distills the approach for founders who are not naturally formidable [1]:

1. Make something worth investing in.
2. Understand why it is worth investing in.
3. Explain that clearly to investors.

Use the same matter-of-fact language used to convince yourself. Conciseness is a test many investors explicitly use [1]. Alfred Lin emphasizes this is doubly important because the explanation must work at one remove -- it must convince not just the partner you speak with, but that partner when re-telling it to colleagues [1].

## Handling Rejection

Getting rejected is common -- even for the best companies. Graham advises against trying to bluff about other investors' interest [1]. The better approach is to explain why investors rejected you and why they are mistaken. Experienced investors know that the best ideas are also the scariest [1].

Dropbox was part of a YC Boston batch. All the Boston investors passed. A couple weeks later, Dropbox raised a Series A from Sequoia [1].

## Authenticity and Motivation

The defining quality of founders who build billion-dollar companies is that they are genuinely interested in what they are building. The company is their project. Every billionaire founder could have stopped sooner. They kept working because there was nothing else they would rather do [2].
