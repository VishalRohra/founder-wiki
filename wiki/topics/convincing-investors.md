# Convincing Investors

Convincing investors is not a performance problem. It is a truth problem. [[Paul Graham]] argues that inexperienced founders make the same mistake as people who hurt themselves lifting heavy things: they try to lift with their back instead of their legs. Founders try to convince with their pitch rather than letting the startup do the work [1]. The correct approach is to first understand why the startup is worth investing in, then explain that clearly.

## What Investors Are Looking For

Investors seek startups that will be very successful. Because startup outcomes follow a power law with a steep curve, most investors treat "big success" as binary. The conventional wisdom is that roughly 15 startups per year become the big successes, and investors are interested in a founder only if there is some chance, however small, of being one of those 15 [1].

Three things make a startup look like a sufficiently good bet [1]:

1. **Formidable founders**
2. **A promising market**
3. **Some evidence of success so far**

## Formidable Founders

Most investors decide within the first few minutes whether a founder seems like a winner or a loser, and once that opinion is set, it is hard to change [1]. If investors think you are a winner, they focus on reasons to invest. If not, they focus on reasons not to. The same startup characteristic — say, a rich market with a slow sales cycle — gets framed positively or negatively depending on this initial impression [1].

Graham defines "formidable" as someone who seems like they will get what they want, regardless of obstacles. It is close to "confident" but with the added dimension of being justifiably confident [1].

The way to seem formidable as an inexperienced founder is to stick to the truth. Most people can seem confident saying "one plus one is two" because they know it is true. The same principle scales to more impressive claims, as long as the founder has first convinced themselves [1]. Graham writes:

> "Convince yourself that your startup is worth investing in, and then when you explain this to investors they'll believe you." [1]

This requires being a domain expert. Investors can tell quickly whether founders truly understand their market by how they answer questions. A non-expert who is merely enthusiastic will register as an instance of the Dunning-Kruger effect [1].

### What YC Looks For

The YC interview is not a pitch — it is a question-and-answer session. Interviews are 10 minutes long (7 minutes would suffice in practice, but 10 is socially convenient) [2]. The partners need random access to the founders' knowledge, not a sequential presentation [2].

Graham states that if he had to decide whether to fund startups based on a single question, it would be: "How do you know people want this?" [2]. The most convincing answer is "because we and our friends want it," especially when followed by evidence of a crude prototype spreading by word of mouth [2].

What YC looks for above all is founders who understand some group of users and can make what they want. The motto is "Make something people want" [2]. The best thing a founder can do in a YC interview is teach the partners about their users [2].

The worst advice Graham has heard about YC interviews is to take control and deliver a prepared message. When founders do this, it wastes the 10 minutes on prefabricated pitch material instead of genuine answers [2].

### Honesty Over Polish

When asked what could go wrong, the worst answer is "nothing." That convinces partners the founder is either a fool or a liar. Far better to go into detail about risks — that is what experts do [2]. The same principle applies to competitors. Competitors rarely kill startups (poor execution does), but founders should know who their competitors are and discuss strengths and weaknesses candidly [2].

The partners do not expect a perfect idea at seed stage. They expect promising hypotheses. But they do expect founders to be thoughtful and honest [2].

## The Market

Founders think of startups as ideas; investors think of them as markets [1]. The total addressable market (TAM) is the number of potential customers multiplied by average annual spend. The market has to be big, but it does not have to be big yet. Graham notes that it is often better to start in a small market that will turn into a big one, or from which the founder can move into a big one [1].

The standard of proof varies by company age. A three-month-old company at Demo Day only needs to be a promising experiment. A two-year-old company raising a Series A needs to show the experiment worked [1]. At three months, a growth graph is evidence the founders are effective. At two years, it must be evidence of a promising market and a company tuned to exploit it [1].

### The "Why Now?" Question

Every company that gets really big is "lucky" in the sense that growth comes from riding an external wave. To make a convincing case, founders must identify a specific trend they will benefit from. The question "why now?" is critical: if it is such a great idea, why hasn't someone else done it? The ideal answer is that it only recently became a good idea because something changed, and no one else has noticed yet [1].

Microsoft illustrates this. Selling Basic interpreters was not going to produce huge growth, but it positioned Microsoft to expand up the stack as microcomputers grew powerful enough to support more software. Microcomputers turned out to be a bigger wave than even the most optimistic observers predicted in 1975 [1].

## Handling Rejection

Getting rejected is common — even for the best companies. Some VCs would have rejected Microsoft. Some rejected Google [1]. The most common question from investors is "who else is investing?" which puts rejected founders in an awkward position [1].

Graham advises against trying to bluff about other investors' interest. It is the most common lie told to investors, and most founders cannot pull it off [1]. The better approach is to tackle the problem head-on: explain why investors rejected you and why they are mistaken. Experienced investors know that the best ideas are also the scariest, and they all know about the VCs who rejected Google [1].

This strategy works best with the best investors, who are both hard to bluff and already believe most other investors are conventional-minded. Raising money is not like college admissions — a startup rejected by mediocre VCs may still be funded by top ones [1]. Graham recounts that Dropbox was part of a YC Boston batch. All the Boston investors passed. A couple weeks later, Dropbox raised a Series A from Sequoia [1].

## The Three-Step Recipe

Graham distills the approach for founders who are not naturally formidable [1]:

1. Make something worth investing in.
2. Understand why it is worth investing in.
3. Explain that clearly to investors.

Use the same matter-of-fact language used to convince yourself. Conciseness is a test many investors explicitly use, reasoning correctly that founders who cannot explain plans concisely do not really understand them. Alfred Lin emphasizes this is doubly important because the explanation must work at one remove — it must convince not just the partner you speak with, but that partner when re-telling it to colleagues [1].

## Authenticity and Motivation

Founders' motives are usually mixed: money, status, genuine interest in the problem, and unwillingness to work for someone else. The last two are more powerful motivators than the first two [2]. Founders doing it just for money will take the first large acquisition offer. Founders doing it just for status will discover less painful ways to seem cool [2].

The defining quality of founders who build billion-dollar companies is that they are genuinely interested in what they are building. The company is their project. Every billionaire founder could have stopped sooner — gotten acquired or hired someone else to run the company. They kept working because there was nothing else they would rather do [2].

Graham writes:

> "The most reliable way to become a billionaire is to start a company that grows fast, and the way to grow fast is to make what users want." [2]

YC rejects founders whose approach is to exploit people. Bad people make bad founders: their exploitation begins with co-founders (destroying the company's foundation), then moves to users (the hardest people to fool), and the best outcome is a small acquisition before the deception collapses [2].
