# Startup Design

Design is an underappreciated competitive advantage for startups. Karri Saarinen, founder of Linear, argues that brand and product design should be treated as a core competency from day one, not an afterthought [1]. Dylan Field, CEO of Figma, sees AI as fundamentally transforming the design process itself [2]. Ryo Lu, Cursor's head of design, provides practical critique of how startups present themselves on the web [3].

## Brand Design: Lessons from Linear

Saarinen built Linear into one of the most admired B2B products partly through obsessive attention to design. His key principles [1]:

**Design is decision-making.** Every visual choice -- color, typography, spacing, animation -- communicates values. Linear's dark-mode-first, minimal interface signals "serious tool for serious builders" without saying it explicitly [1].

**Consistency compounds.** Small design inconsistencies erode trust over time. Saarinen advocates for design systems even at very early stages -- not a full component library, but a small set of rules about color, type, and spacing that every screen follows [1].

**Brand is not logo.** Brand is the sum of every interaction someone has with your product and company. The name, the domain, the onboarding flow, the error messages, the changelog -- all of these are brand design [1].

**Speed and quality are not opposed.** Linear ships fast precisely because good design systems reduce decision fatigue. When the rules are clear, execution is faster [1].

## How AI Will Transform Design

Dylan Field describes Figma's approach to AI in design tools [2]. The transformation goes beyond auto-generating layouts: AI enables rapid prototyping of entire flows, automatic translation of wireframes into polished designs, and -- most importantly -- closing the gap between design and code. When a designer can go from concept to working prototype in minutes instead of days, the entire product development cycle accelerates [2].

Field's key insight: AI will not replace designers but will shift their role from pixel-pushing to decision-making. The scarce resource becomes taste -- knowing what to build and whether it is good -- not the ability to execute the pixels [2].

## Startup Website Review

Ryo Lu (Cursor's head of design) reviewed startup websites with specific, actionable feedback [3]. Common problems:

- **Hero sections that say nothing**: The most common mistake is a hero that uses buzzwords without communicating what the product does for whom
- **Too many calls to action**: Multiple competing buttons confuse the user about what to do next
- **Ignoring mobile**: Most first impressions happen on phones
- **Generic stock imagery**: Photos of diverse teams in conference rooms signal "we have nothing real to show"
- **Poor typography hierarchy**: When everything is bold or large, nothing stands out

The best startup websites share traits: they show the product immediately, communicate the value proposition in one sentence, and have exactly one call to action above the fold [3].
