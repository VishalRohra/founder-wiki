# Competing with Big Companies

YC group partners [[Michael Seibel]], [[Harj Taggar]], and [[Brad Flora]] observe that one of the most common failure modes among founders doing well is psyching themselves out about competitors [1]. Founders replace excitement about their own company with fear about a competitor -- usually triggered by a competitor's fundraising announcement, press coverage, or a new hire from a marquee company [1].

## The Lies Founders Tell Themselves

### "My competitor raised more money, so they'll win"

Seibel frames the depressing but useful truth: "Most likely everyone's gonna die. You and your competitors, none of you will make something that people want" [1]. The second thing founders fail to account for is that many markets support multiple winners. "How many banks are there? One bank doesn't look at the other banks and say, well, I guess that's it, we got to fold up shop" [1].

### "My competitor must be doing great"

[[Brad Flora]] notes that TechCrunch announcements hide internal chaos. "Years later you talked to someone that worked at that company and they say, 'Oh, man, what a mess. The CEO was on a bender and was all sad because we lost all of our big customers'" [1]. Founders are exposed to their own company's flaws every day but see only competitors' carefully crafted exterior -- their marketing [1].

Seibel describes the shift in perspective from founder to YC partner: "As a partner, you work with these companies and you start realizing that all of them are kind of really badly flawed somehow. Literally in any YC batch, I could take every single company and pick out some fatal flaw. And you know that some of them are going to turn out to be worth billions of dollars" [1].

### "We're in a land grab situation"

The "land grab" framing is "definitely a bit of a meme" at YC [1]. [[Harj Taggar]] plays devil's advocate by voicing the founder fear: "We're in a land grab situation. If I don't get those customers unprofitably as fast as possible, my competitors will beat me" [1]. Taggar's diagnostic questions cut through it: "Are your customers talking about them? Are you losing deals? Have you looked at the product? How do you stack up?" The answers are usually reassuring -- customers are not talking about the competitor [1]. Seibel calls it "a confusion of interfaces" -- founders think a competitor in the New York Times means their customers are having a worse experience [1].

### "My competitor must know something I don't, so I should copy them"

Seibel tells the story from his own experience at Justin.tv competing with Ustream and Livestream. All three companies were copying each other's features. Justin.tv built a feature exclusively for copyright owners to take content off the site -- a feature with "no clear user value at all." Two weeks later, a competitor released the same feature. "We were like, 'Oh crap, this is clearly the blind leading the blind.' They don't even know why we have this" [1].

The company only started working when Emmett Shear and Kevin Lin "started actually talking to the users and figuring out what they wanted instead of the years where all the competitors just looked at each other, copying each other's dumb features" [1].

## When Competition Actually Matters

### No competitors at all

Hearing "we don't have any competitors" in a YC interview is a red flag [1]. "That shows a lack of understanding of the customer and what the customer is doing right now to solve the problem" [1]. Founders should always know what alternatives exist.

### Your competitor objectively has a better product

Seibel acknowledges this happened to him personally. The day after Socialcam was acquired, a close friend messaged saying he could finally delete the app because he had been using something else the entire time -- "I guess we didn't have the best" [1].

### Structural platform advantage

The most dangerous competitive situation is when a competitor owns the platform. [[Brad Flora]]'s company, Perfect Audience, used the Facebook Exchange API along with eight or nine competitors. When Facebook launched Custom Audiences and started selling the same service directly, "Facebook was now a competitor, and they own the platform. They wrote all the code. They control pricing. So that's a time when you should worry about competition. And so we sold the company" [1].

Seibel draws from another example: his former co-founders Justin Kan and Emmett Shear created a calendar company that was crushed six months in when Google Calendar launched. "What happens when someone has an actual product structural advantage?" [1].

Seibel also notes the Microsoft Teams vs. Slack dynamic: Teams is probably not as good as Slack, but Microsoft's massive sales distribution advantage lets a "good enough" product win in the enterprise [1].

### Structural advantage with a bad product loses

Taggar observes that a structural advantage paired with a poor product usually fails. Microsoft's music player, mobile operating system, and many Facebook product launches demonstrate this. "A structural advantage with a crap product doesn't win. A structural advantage with a product that's good enough can win" [1]. But "just as often, they don't nail it" -- and Taggar notes that the partners struggle to find examples of FANG companies launching products that actually killed fast-growing successful startups [1].

Flora points to Snap as evidence: "Snap lives, right?" Seibel adds TikTok and notes that for founders, the takeaway is simple: "Build a better product, and if you do that, then all of these other advantages are just kind of moot points. It's really rare for the company that has the structural advantage to also put out a great product" [1].

## The Instacart Example

Taggar raises Instacart as a case study. Instacart faced the "what about Amazon?" question from day one. Taggar asks: "How the hell does anyone compete with one of the greatest companies of all time on the thing that they're experts at?" The answer: "just picking the right avocado was too big of a challenge for this trillion-dollar company" [1]. The lesson is that even in spaces dominated by massive incumbents, specific execution challenges create opportunities.

## The Cruise Example

When Kyle Vogt started Cruise, Google was already a decade into self-driving cars. An investor told Seibel that "Kyle's screwed from day one." Seibel's response drew on his direct experience as Kyle's co-founder at Justin.tv: "Kyle has built the most complicated products inside of our company. Whenever there was a ridiculously stupid technical challenge that no one else could do, Kyle volunteered for it. I would never bet against Kyle. And the crazier the more I wouldn't bet against him" [1].

## The Real Strategy: Build a Better Product

Flora summarizes the partners' collective advice: "Instead of worrying about all these stupid things like land grabs and whatever all the other crap, man, just build a better product for your users. And you might get surprised at how well that strategy goes. Stay informed. Know what your competitors are building and how you stack up against them. But don't let it deflate you" [1].

Seibel's approach in office hours is to redirect founder anxiety to the fundamentals. "What's your growth rate? Customers churning? What's the pipeline on sales look like? And if they can answer all those questions well, you're like, 'Let's get back to it. Keep going. Why are we stopping?'" [1].


## The Innovator's Dilemma in AI Search (Aravind Srinivas)

[[Aravind Srinivas]] provides a detailed case study of competing with a dominant incumbent through structural advantage. Google cannot easily build Perplexity's product because if users stop clicking links, ad revenue (~$200 billion/year) declines. "Wall Street just automatically panics if search revenue goes down" [2]. Srinivas's edge: "Obsession about the user and good product taste" [2].

## Outcompeting with Superior Retention (Tony Xu)

[[Tony Xu]] competed against better-funded rivals (Uber Eats, GrubHub) with a structural advantage: superior retention and frequency. "One of the hardest things to really fool is the retention and the engagement of a product" [3]. Every marketing dollar went further for DoorDash because retained users compound [3].

## Concentration as Competitive Strategy (Sam Altman)

[[Sam Altman]] describes how OpenAI, far less resourced than DeepMind, used focus as a weapon: "They're going to try a lot of things, and we've just got to pick one and really concentrate" [4]. The startup advantage is not resources but conviction and speed [4].

## Three Structural Advantages for Startups (Sam Altman)

[[Sam Altman]] identifies three specific areas where startups repeatedly beat big companies [5]:

### Ideas That Sound Bad But Are Good

At a big company, a product manager with an idea that sounds bad but is actually good must get "everybody from your boss, sometimes all the way up to the CEO to say yes. So one no can kill you." At a startup, any of thousands of investors at Demo Day can say yes. "For those ideas that sound bad but are good, because of this phenomenon, this one no versus one yes, startups can win" [5].

### Fast-Changing Markets

A startup's great advantage is agility and speed. "The more a market is changing the higher the number of decisions you get to make and the higher number of tweaks to your product and strategy you get to make." Big companies will make those decisions "on average, worse and certainly much slower" [5].

### Big Platform Shifts

Startups come in clusters, usually after a major platform shift. Large companies operate on annual cadences and "are not good at making a big enough strategic pivot. The battleship just turns too slowly." A startup can commit fully to a new direction overnight [5]. See [[Why Now]] for Garry Tan's complementary treatment of platform shifts.

# Competing with Big Companies

## Innovation Complacency: The Google Cash Problem (Garry Tan)

[[Garry Tan]] presents a structural argument for why big companies fail to innovate, using Google as the primary example [6]. In 2012, Peter Thiel confronted Google CEO Eric Schmidt with the observation that Google had $30-50 billion in cash and "no idea how to invest that money in technology effectively" [6]. By the time of Tan's video, Google held $135 billion in cash [6].

Tan identifies the root cause: "There are limits that are not cash. There are limits of recruiting, limits of real estate, limits of regulatory limits" [6]. Big companies cannot simply convert money into innovation. Their size creates organizational drag -- bureaucracy, politics, drama -- that prevents them from executing on new ideas [6].

The former CEO of Waze, Noam Bardin, experienced this firsthand after Google acquired Waze for $1 billion. He described "an ever-increasing percent of our time went to non-user value creation tasks. And that changes the DNA of the company quickly from customer-focused to corporate guidelines-focused" [6].

Tan's conclusion: "A small startup can beat even the largest companies on the planet. You care a lot more. You can hire better. You can deliver a better product" [6]. The big company CEO spends most of their time keeping plates spinning. A founder only has to spin one plate really well [6].

### Amazon as the Exception

Tan identifies Amazon as the rare large company that consistently reinvests rather than hoarding cash. Amazon barely turned a profit for almost twenty years despite growing revenue every year. The result: 50% of all U.S. e-commerce and 32% of cloud infrastructure [6].

The key difference is Jeff Bezos's approach to resource allocation. Bezos refused the job of "driving up the stock price." Instead, he worked backward from free cash flow to find controllable inputs. For established businesses, this means 1-2% improvements. But Amazon also took "big risks on brand new products like AWS, Kindle, and Alexa" [6].

Tan contrasts this with Google's approach: "$5 to $20 billion per year in profit that they actually couldn't figure out what to do with. It's not a picture that is building the future in an aggressive way" [6].

### Steve Jobs on Interpersonal Computing

Tan includes a 1990 Steve Jobs clip where Jobs predicted that electronic organizations would replace management hierarchies because they can be formed in "literally fifteen minutes worth of setup" and can "live for as long as they're needed and then vanish" [6]. Tan frames this as the computer revolution creating "a new kind of management, a new type of hiring, a new type of company, and a new way to come together to create" [6].

## Individual-Level Competitive Moat (Sam Altman)

[[Sam Altman]]'s 2019 *How to Be Successful* extends the corporate competitive-moat argument to individual careers. "Most people understand that companies are more valuable if they are difficult to compete with. This is important, and obviously true. But this holds true for you as an individual as well. If what you do can be done by someone else, it eventually will be, and for less money" [7]. The moat-builders he names: personal relationships, a strong personal brand, or expertise at the intersection of multiple fields. "Most people do whatever most people they hang out with do. This mimetic behavior is usually a mistake -- if you're doing the same thing everyone else is doing, you will not be hard to compete with" [7]. Applied to founders, this means the competitive advantage stance is lived at the individual level before it is instantiated at the company level — the kind of person the founder is already determines the kind of company they can build.

## Rate of Change as Structural Advantage (Sam Altman)

[[Sam Altman]]'s 2020 *How to Invest in Startups* names the structural mechanism underlying the three-structural-advantages framework above: "Startups are likely to happen in many more industries -- startups can win wherever costs can be low and cycle time can be fast. Startups do particularly well in industries with rapid technological change, because their fundamental advantages over large competitors are speed and focus. A higher rate of change gives startups more opportunities to be right and the large competitor more opportunities to stumble" [8]. The implicit corollary: picking startup markets partly means picking markets where the rate of change is high enough that incumbents cannot amortize stability. Slow-changing markets favor incumbents regardless of quality; fast-changing markets systematically favor the startup mode. See [[Why Now]] for the timing side of this.
