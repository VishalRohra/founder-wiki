# Competing with Big Companies

YC group partners [[Michael Seibel]], [[Harj Taggar]], and Brad Flora observe that one of the most common failure modes among founders doing well is psyching themselves out about competitors [1]. Founders replace excitement about their own company with fear about a competitor -- usually triggered by a competitor's fundraising announcement, press coverage, or a new hire from a marquee company [1].

## The Lies Founders Tell Themselves

### "My competitor raised more money, so they'll win"

Seibel frames the depressing but useful truth: "Most likely everyone's gonna die. You and your competitors, none of you will make something that people want" [1]. The second thing founders fail to account for is that many markets support multiple winners. "How many banks are there? One bank doesn't look at the other banks and say, well, I guess that's it, we got to fold up shop" [1].

### "My competitor must be doing great"

Flora notes that TechCrunch announcements hide internal chaos. "Years later you talked to someone that worked at that company and they say, 'Oh, man, what a mess. The CEO was on a bender and was all sad because we lost all of our big customers'" [1]. Founders are exposed to their own company's flaws every day but see only competitors' carefully crafted exterior -- their marketing [1].

Seibel describes the shift in perspective from founder to YC partner: "As a partner, you work with these companies and you start realizing that all of them are kind of really badly flawed somehow. Literally in any YC batch, I could take every single company and pick out some fatal flaw. And you know that some of them are going to turn out to be worth billions of dollars" [1].

### "We're in a land grab situation"

The "land grab" framing is "definitely a bit of a meme" at YC [1]. Taggar's diagnostic questions cut through it: "Are your customers talking about them? Are you losing deals? Have you looked at the product? How do you stack up?" The answers are usually reassuring -- customers are not talking about the competitor [1]. Flora calls it "a confusion of interfaces" -- founders think a competitor in the New York Times means their customers are having a worse experience [1].

### "My competitor must know something I don't, so I should copy them"

Seibel tells the story of Justin.tv competing with Ustream and Livestream. All three companies were copying each other's features. Justin.tv built a feature exclusively for copyright owners to take content off the site -- a feature with "no clear user value at all." Two weeks later, a competitor released the same feature. "We were like, 'Oh crap, this is clearly the blind leading the blind.' They don't even know why we have this" [1].

The company only started working when Emmett Shear and Kevin Lin "started actually talking to the users and figuring out what they wanted instead of the years where all the competitors just looked at each other, copying each other's dumb features" [1].

## When Competition Actually Matters

### No competitors at all

Hearing "we don't have any competitors" in a YC interview is a red flag [1]. "That shows a lack of understanding of the customer and what the customer is doing right now to solve the problem" [1]. Founders should always know what alternatives exist.

### Your competitor has a better product

Seibel acknowledges this happened to him. The day after Socialcam was acquired, a close friend messaged saying he could finally delete the app because he had been using something else the entire time -- "I guess we didn't have the best" [1].

### Structural platform advantage

The most dangerous competitive situation is when a competitor owns the platform. Flora's company, Perfect Audience, used the Facebook Exchange API along with eight or nine competitors. When Facebook launched Custom Audiences and started selling the same service directly, "Facebook was now a competitor, and they own the platform. They wrote all the code. They control pricing. So that's a time when you should worry about competition. And so we sold the company" [1].

Seibel notes the Microsoft Teams vs. Slack dynamic: Teams is probably not as good as Slack, but Microsoft's massive sales distribution advantage lets a "good enough" product win in the enterprise [1].

### Structural advantage with a bad product loses

The partners observe that a structural advantage paired with a poor product usually fails. Microsoft's music player, mobile operating system, and many Facebook product launches demonstrate this. "A structural advantage with a crap product doesn't win. A structural advantage with a product that's good enough can win" [1]. But "just as often, they don't nail it" [1].

Snap survived Instagram Stories. TikTok survived Facebook's repeated attempts. The partners note it is hard to find examples of FANG companies launching products that killed fast-growing successful startups [1].

## The Instacart Example

Instacart faced the "what about Amazon?" question from day one. "How the hell does anyone compete with one of the greatest companies of all time on the thing that they're experts at?" [1]. The answer: "just picking the right avocado was too big of a challenge for this trillion-dollar company" [1]. The lesson is that even in spaces dominated by massive incumbents, specific execution challenges create opportunities.

## The Cruise Example

When Kyle Vogt started Cruise, Google was already a decade into self-driving cars. An investor told Seibel that "Kyle's screwed from day one." Seibel's response: "Kyle has built the most complicated products inside of our company. Whenever there was a ridiculously stupid technical challenge that no one else could do, Kyle volunteered for it. I would never bet against Kyle. And the crazier the more I wouldn't bet against him" [1].

## The Real Strategy: Build a Better Product

Flora summarizes the partners' collective advice: "Instead of worrying about all these stupid things like land grabs and whatever all the other crap, man, just build a better product for your users. And you might get surprised at how well that strategy goes" [1].

Taggar adds the practical note: "Stay informed. Know what your competitors are building and how you stack up against them. But don't let it deflate you" [1].

Seibel's approach in office hours: redirect founder anxiety to the fundamentals. "What's your growth rate? Customers churning? What's the pipeline on sales look like? And if they can answer all those questions well, you're like, 'Let's get back to it. Keep going. Why are we stopping?'" [1].
