# Competing with Big Companies

Fear of large competitors is one of the most common psychological traps YC group partners observe in founders. [[Michael Seibel]], [[Harj Taggar]], and [[Brad Flora]] describe the pattern: a company doing great will psych itself out because a competitor raised five million dollars or got press coverage, replacing excitement with fear [1]. The advice they repeat: most likely, everyone is going to die. Competitors are not the threat founders think they are.

## The Lies Founders Tell Themselves

### "My competitor raised money, so they're winning"

Raising money is not the same as building a product people want. Founders read TechCrunch and see announcements, then assume the competitor's interior matches their polished exterior [1]. But years later, people who worked at those competitors describe chaos: "everyone was leaving, the CEO was on a bender, we lost all our big customers" [1]. Every startup is badly broken somehow -- founders only see how screwed their own company is, while seeing everyone else's marketing [1].

### "We're in a land grab situation"

A common claim, but when partners ask the follow-up questions -- "Are your customers talking about them? Are you losing deals? Have you looked at their product?" -- the answers reveal that customers are usually not talking about the competitors [1]. It is a "confusion of interfaces" to think that a competitor in the New York Times means customers are having a worse experience with your product [1].

### "My competitors must know something I don't"

Seibel tells the story of Justin.tv building a feature exclusively for copyright owners -- something visible on the site but with no clear user value. Two weeks later, a competitor released the same feature. "We were like, oh crap, this is clearly the blind leading the blind. They don't even know why we have this" [1]. The company only started working when the founders talked to actual users instead of copying competitors [1].

## When to Worry About Competition

### Structural Advantages

Legitimate competitive concerns exist when a platform owner enters your market. Brad Flora's company Perfect Audience used the Facebook Exchange API. When Facebook launched Custom Audiences and sold it directly, they owned the platform, the code, and pricing. "That's a time when you should worry about competition. And so we sold the company" [1].

Microsoft Teams vs. Slack is another example: Microsoft may not have the better product, but "good enough" combined with massive enterprise distribution can win [1].

### The Pattern: Structural Advantage Alone Is Not Enough

A structural advantage with a bad product loses. Microsoft's music player and mobile operating system both failed despite the company's distribution. A structural advantage with a product that is good enough can win, especially when a startup has already specced the product for the incumbent to copy [1].

But even this pattern is rare. The partners struggle to find examples of FAANG companies launching products that killed fast-growing startups [1]:
- Snapchat survived Facebook's copies
- TikTok survived Facebook's copies
- Instacart survived Amazon's grocery efforts -- "picking the right avocado was too big of a challenge for this trillion-dollar company" [1]
- Cruise survived despite Google having a decade head-start on self-driving cars [1]

### Having No Competitors Is a Red Flag

When a founder says "we don't have any competitors" in a YC interview, the partners "buckle up" [1]. It shows a lack of understanding of the customer and what they are doing now to solve the problem. It is very unlikely that no customer has ever tried another solution [1].

## AI and the OpenAI Question

[[Dalton Caldwell]] and [[Michael Seibel]] address the specific fear that OpenAI and large AI companies will kill all startups [2]. Their answer: unless you believe AGI will be created in the short term, OpenAI is not going to kill all startups. History suggests the opposite -- major technological changes (farming, electricity, internet, mobile, cloud computing) have consistently created more businesses, and startups are relatively advantaged over incumbents during rapid transitions [2].

The key distinction is between "cargo culting AI" -- superficially adding AI features to raise money -- and genuinely using AI to build better features that dramatically increase retention, product quality, or willingness to pay [2]. The second is real value creation, analogous to building high-quality mobile apps when the App Store launched [2].

Seibel notes that until large AI companies solve AGI, that will be their first, second, and third goal. Using existing AI tools to make people's lives better is simply not on their priority list, creating a massive opportunity for startups [2].

## The Core Advice

"Build a better product for your users. It's the club you can bludgeon everyone else with" [1]. Instead of worrying about land grabs, structural advantages, or competitor fundraising: know what your competitors are building, but do not let it deflate you. Spend time on things you have control over [1].
