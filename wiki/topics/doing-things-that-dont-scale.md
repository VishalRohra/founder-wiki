# Doing Things That Don't Scale

One of the most common types of advice given at Y Combinator is to do things that don't scale [1]. [[Paul Graham]] wrote the definitive essay on this in July 2013, arguing that startups take off because the founders make them take off. The belief that startups either take off or don't -- that you build something, make it available, and either people beat a path to your door or the market doesn't exist -- is wrong [1].

"Actually startups take off because the founders make them take off. There may be a handful that just grew by themselves, but usually it takes some sort of push to get them going. A good metaphor would be the cranks that car engines had before they got electric starters. Once the engine was going, it would keep going, but there was a separate and laborious process to get it going." [1]

## Recruit Users Manually

The most common unscalable thing founders must do at the start is recruit users manually. Nearly all startups have to do this. You cannot wait for users to come to you [1].

Stripe is the canonical example. Despite solving an urgent problem -- one where you might think users would find them on their own -- the Collison brothers were famous within YC for aggressive early user acquisition [1]. They invented what YC calls the "Collison installation": when anyone agreed to try Stripe, instead of saying "Great, we'll send you a link," the Collisons would say "Right then, give me your laptop" and set them up on the spot [1].

Two reasons founders resist manual recruitment [1]:
1. **Shyness and laziness.** They would rather sit at home writing code than go out and talk to strangers and probably be rejected by most of them. But for a startup to succeed, at least one founder (usually the CEO) will have to spend a lot of time on sales and marketing.
2. **The numbers seem too small.** Founders think this cannot be how the big, famous startups got started. They underestimate compound growth. If you have 100 users and grow 10% a week, after a year you have 14,000 users; after two years, 2 million [1].

Airbnb is another classic example. In the early days, Brian Chesky and Joe Gebbia went door to door in New York, recruiting new users and helping existing ones improve their listings. Graham recalls them always showing up to Tuesday YC dinners with rolly bags because they had just flown back from somewhere [1].

## The Fragility of Early Startups

Almost all startups are fragile initially [1]. Airbnb now seems like an unstoppable juggernaut, but early on it was so fragile that about 30 days of going out and engaging in person with users made the difference between success and failure [1].

Inexperienced founders, investors, reporters, and know-it-alls on forums unconsciously judge larval startups by the standards of established ones. "They're like someone looking at a newborn baby and concluding 'there's no way this tiny creature could ever accomplish anything'" [1].

The right question about an early-stage startup: not "is this company taking over the world?" but "how big could this company get if the founders did the right things?" [1]. Even Bill Gates underestimated Microsoft -- he returned to Harvard for the fall semester after starting the company [1]. Microsoft did not seem impressive when it was just a couple of guys in Albuquerque writing Basic interpreters for a market of a few thousand hobbyists [1].

## Delight Your Users

Founders should take extraordinary measures not just to acquire users but to make them happy [1]. Wufoo sent each new user a hand-written thank you note, for as long as they could (which turned out to be surprisingly long) [1].

Three reasons founders do not focus enough on delighting individual users [1]:

1. **Engineering training.** Engineers are trained to build robust and elegant things, not to be slavishly attentive to individual users. But at an early-stage startup, the founder is Kirk, not Scotty.
2. **Worry about scalability.** In their current state, they have nothing to lose. "See if you can make it happen" -- having too many users to personally delight would be a great problem to have.
3. **Low standards from experience.** Their standards for customer service have been set by big companies. Tim Cook does not send you a hand-written note after you buy a laptop. He cannot. But a small startup can, and that is an advantage of being small.

Graham states: "I have never once seen a startup lured down a blind alley by trying too hard to make their initial users happy" [1].

## Insanely Great Experience, Not Product

Steve Jobs's phrase "insanely great" applied to early startups means something different than it did at an established Apple [1]. For a larval startup, it is not the product that should be insanely great, but the experience of being your user. The product is just one component. You can and should give users an insanely great experience with an early, incomplete, buggy product, if you make up the difference with attentiveness [1].

This over-engagement is not just a permissible technique for getting growth rolling. For most successful startups it is a necessary part of the feedback loop that makes the product good [1]. The feedback from engaging directly with your earliest users will be the best you ever get. When you are big enough to resort to focus groups, you will wish you could go over to users' homes and offices like you did when there were only a handful of them [1].

## The Contained Fire Strategy

Sometimes the right unscalable trick is to focus on a deliberately narrow market. "It's like keeping a fire contained at first to get it really hot before adding more logs" [1].

Facebook did this. At first it was just for Harvard students -- a potential market of a few thousand people. But because they felt it was really for them, a critical mass signed up. When Graham interviewed Mark Zuckerberg at Startup School, Zuckerberg said that while it was a lot of work creating course lists for each school, doing that made students feel the site was their natural home [1].

Among companies, the best early adopters are usually other startups. They are more open to new things by nature and because, having just been started, they haven't made all their choices yet. When they succeed they grow fast, and you with them [1].

## Pulling a Meraki: Hardware Startups

For hardware startups, there is a variant called "pulling a Meraki" [1]. The Meraki founders (Robert Morris's grad students) got started by assembling their routers themselves. Hardware startups face a catch-22: the minimum order for a factory production run is usually several hundred thousand dollars, but without a product you cannot generate the growth to raise that money [1].

Pebble assembled the first several hundred watches themselves. If they had not, they probably would not have sold $10 million worth on Kickstarter [1]. Eric Migicovsky said one of the things he learned was "how valuable it was to source good screws" [1].

## The Consulting Technique

For B2B startups, Graham sometimes advises taking over-engagement to an extreme: pick a single user and act as if you were consultants building something just for that one user [1]. The initial user serves as the form for your mold. Keep tweaking till you fit their needs perfectly, and you will usually find you have made something other users want too [1].

At Viaweb, when merchants would not use the software themselves, Graham and Morris offered to make stores for them. "We felt pretty lame at the time. Instead of organizing big strategic e-commerce partnerships, we were trying to sell luggage and pens and men's shirts" [1]. But it was exactly the right thing because it taught them how merchants would use the software. The feedback loop was sometimes near-instantaneous: in the middle of building a merchant's site, Graham would find he needed a feature they did not have, spend a couple hours implementing it, and resume building the site [1].

## Being Your Own Software

A more extreme variant: when you only have a small number of users, you can sometimes do by hand things you plan to automate later [1]. This lets you launch faster, and when you finally automate yourself out of the loop, you know exactly what to build because you have muscle memory from doing it yourself.

Stripe delivered "instant" merchant accounts to its first users by having the founders manually sign them up for traditional merchant accounts behind the scenes [1].

## Big Launches Usually Don't Work

Graham explicitly warns against the "Big Launch" -- launching simultaneously in 8 publications with embargoes on a Tuesday [1]. Think of successful startups: how many of their launches do you remember? All you need from a launch is some initial core of users. How well you are doing a few months later will depend more on how happy you made those users than how many there were [1].

Partnerships also usually do not work as a way to get growth started. "Six months later they're all saying the same thing: that was way more work than we expected, and we ended up getting practically nothing out of it" [1].

## Startup Ideas as Vectors

Graham proposes thinking of startup ideas not as scalars but as pairs: what you are going to build, plus the unscalable thing you are going to do initially to get the company going [1]. In most cases the second component is: recruit users manually and give them an overwhelmingly good experience [1].

In the best case, both components of the vector contribute to the company's DNA. The unscalable things you do to get started are not merely a necessary evil but change the company permanently for the better [1]. If you have to be aggressive about user acquisition when you are small, you will probably still be aggressive when you are big. If you have to work hard to delight users when you only have a handful, you will keep doing it when you have a lot [1].

## Connection to Other Principles

The essay on [[Doing Things That Don't Scale]] connects directly to Graham's essay on [[Default Alive or Default Dead]]. In that essay, he argues that instead of hiring to boost growth, startups should seek growth "by doing things that don't scale, or by redesigning the product in the way only founders can" [3]. Airbnb waited 4 months after raising money at the end of YC before hiring their first employee. The founders were terribly overworked, but they were overworked evolving Airbnb into the astonishingly successful organism it became [3].

Graham's earlier essay on the hardest lessons also reinforces this: "Release early" and "keep pumping out features" -- get a version 1 out fast, then improve it based on users' reactions [2]. Wufoo released their form-builder before the underlying database, getting 83,000 people to sit in the driver's seat. Linux users complained about Flash, so Wufoo rewrote their software. If they had waited to release everything at once, they would not have discovered the problem until it was deeply wired in [2].


## DoorDash: Deliveries as Strategy (Tony Xu)

[[Tony Xu]] and all four DoorDash co-founders did every delivery themselves for the first two years. This was not just a phase -- twelve years after founding, every person at DoorDash, including Xu, still does deliveries every year [4].

The practice produced three categories of insight no dashboard could provide:

1. **Customer insight**: The earliest customers were families with young children. The founders learned what they wanted, which restaurants they preferred, and that they kept coming back without marketing spend or discounts [4].
2. **Geographic strategy**: By personally experiencing deliveries in different areas, the founders discovered that need was strongest in suburbs, not city centers. This became DoorDash's contrarian strategy against better-funded competitors targeting urban density [4].
3. **Driver recruitment credibility**: Having done every delivery gave the founders firsthand knowledge of the job, making them credible when recruiting the first drivers on Craigslist [4].

## Bump: One-Person Customer Support (David Lieb)

[[David Lieb]] was the sole customer support person at Bump from founding through the Google acquisition, replying to every email and blocking 1-1.5 hours daily for it. "It taught me that you listen to people, and generally, they will tell you what you should do" [5]. When seeking product direction, Lieb called the top 100 Bump users globally -- an unscalable act that produced the pivotal insight that users were sharing photos, not contacts [5].

## PlanGrid: Lending iPads and Walking Users Through the App Store (Tracy Young)

[[Tracy Young]] describes one of the most extreme examples of unscalable tactics. PlanGrid's target users -- construction workers -- did not own iPads in 2012. PlanGrid's solution: loan out iPads for free, then sit with each user to walk them through the App Store, type in passwords, set up accounts, and load their construction projects [6]. Young called every user weekly: "Hey it's Tracy, just checking in. How's the new update going?" [6].

## Lob: The Inkjet Printer Assembly Line (Harry Zhang)

[[Harry Zhang]] ran Lob's initial fulfillment on his home inkjet printer. Orders came through the API into a database, and Zhang printed letters from his apartment. At peak early volume, he and his co-founder watched TV while running an assembly line of letters they stuffed by hand [7].

## Gobble: CEO as Delivery Driver (Ooshma Garg)

[[Ooshma Garg]] prototyped Gobble with email, PayPal, and her personal car. She created a calendar of chefs and their daily menus, emailed friends asking if they wanted to order, collected payments via PayPal, and personally drove deliveries [8]. When Gobble pivoted to dinner kits, the team tested the concept by having TaskRabbit workers deliver prototype kits to friends in Palo Alto [8].
