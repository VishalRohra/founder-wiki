# Doing Things That Don't Scale

One of the most common types of advice given at Y Combinator is to do things that don't scale [1]. [[Paul Graham]] wrote the definitive essay on this in July 2013, arguing that startups take off because the founders make them take off. The belief that startups either take off or don't -- that you build something, make it available, and either people beat a path to your door or the market doesn't exist -- is wrong [1].

"Actually startups take off because the founders make them take off. There may be a handful that just grew by themselves, but usually it takes some sort of push to get them going. A good metaphor would be the cranks that car engines had before they got electric starters. Once the engine was going, it would keep going, but there was a separate and laborious process to get it going." [1]

## Recruit Users Manually

The most common unscalable thing founders must do at the start is recruit users manually. Nearly all startups have to do this. You cannot wait for users to come to you [1].

Stripe is the canonical example. Despite solving an urgent problem, the Collison brothers were famous within YC for aggressive early user acquisition [1]. They invented what YC calls the "Collison installation": when anyone agreed to try Stripe, instead of saying "Great, we'll send you a link," the Collisons would say "Right then, give me your laptop" and set them up on the spot [1].

Two reasons founders resist manual recruitment [1]:
1. **Shyness and laziness.** They would rather sit at home writing code than go out and talk to strangers and probably be rejected by most of them.
2. **The numbers seem too small.** If you have 100 users and grow 10% a week, after a year you have 14,000 users; after two years, 2 million [1].

Airbnb is another classic example. In the early days, Brian Chesky and Joe Gebbia went door to door in New York, recruiting new users and helping existing ones improve their listings [1].

## How to Get Your First Users

Getting the first users is one of the most asked questions by new founders [4]. The practical advice converges on:

- **Start with people you know.** Friends, family, former colleagues. Not because they are representative users, but because they will give honest feedback and forgive imperfections.
- **Go where your users are.** If building for developers, post on Hacker News. If building for restaurants, walk into restaurants. If building for students, go to campuses.
- **The Collison Installation.** Do not give people a link and hope they sign up. Set them up yourself, right then, on their device.
- **Be the product.** Before the product is good enough, manually do what the product will eventually automate. Stripe delivered "instant" merchant accounts by manually signing users up behind the scenes [1].

## The Forward Deployed Engineer as DTDS

In the AI era, the [[Forward Deployed Engineers]] model is the natural evolution of doing things that don't scale. The founder goes to the customer's office, sits next to the end user, understands their workflow, and customizes the AI agent for them -- often by modifying prompts during or immediately after the meeting. This is the Collison Installation applied to enterprise AI [3].

## Delight Your Users

Founders should take extraordinary measures not just to acquire users but to make them happy [1]. Wufoo sent each new user a hand-written thank you note [1].

Graham states: "I have never once seen a startup lured down a blind alley by trying too hard to make their initial users happy" [1].

## The Contained Fire Strategy

Focus on a deliberately narrow market first. "It's like keeping a fire contained at first to get it really hot before adding more logs" [1]. Facebook started at Harvard. Microsoft started with Altair owners. The narrowness is not the goal -- depth is [1].

## Big Launches Don't Work

Graham explicitly warns against relying on a "Big Launch" [1]. There may even be an inverse correlation between launch magnitude and success. Partnerships also usually fail as growth strategies: "Six months later they're all saying that was way more work than we expected, and we ended up getting practically nothing out of it" [1].

## Startup Ideas as Vectors

Graham proposes thinking of startup ideas not as scalars but as pairs: what you are going to build, plus the unscalable thing you are going to do initially to get the company going [1]. In most cases the second component is: recruit users manually and give them an overwhelmingly good experience [1].
