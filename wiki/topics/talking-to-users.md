# Talking to Users

"Talk to your users" is one of the most repeated pieces of advice in the YC canon, cited by [[Paul Graham]], [[Michael Seibel]], [[Gustaf Alströmer]], and virtually every YC group partner across dozens of sources. Alströmer, former Head of Growth at Airbnb and YC group partner since 2017, provides the most systematic treatment of how to do it well [1].

## Why the Best Founders Talk to Users Throughout the Company's Lifetime

Most people have the formation of new startups completely wrong. They think product ideas come from lazy Sundays or late-night coding sessions [1]. Alströmer contrasts the dramatized depiction of Facebook's founding in The Social Network with a real photograph of Brian Chesky spending a weekend with Amal, Airbnb's very first guest. The difference: a two-way conversation with a real customer [1].

In 2010, Chesky launched an experiment: he gave up his apartment and lived in 50 different Airbnbs over several months. By doing this, he got a chance to talk to every one of those 50 hosts each day [1]. Still today, Brian and Joe receive calls from hosts on their personal cell phone numbers -- the numbers they put on the website and shared with all hosts early on. They were not hiding behind anonymous email [1].

Alströmer frames this as almost radical. Most founders spend their time in front of computers trying to find scalable growth channels like Google Ads. Most founders still do not build personal connections with their users, hiding behind info@ emails or do-not-reply emails [1].

## Finding Users to Interview

Alströmer identifies several sources for finding potential users to interview [1]:

- **Personal network**: Easiest to reach but may give less honest answers to avoid offending you
- **Coworkers and former coworkers**: A great source, especially if they know the topic well
- **LinkedIn**: Search for people with relevant titles or keywords
- **Reddit forums, Slack/Discord communities**: Common sources cited by YC founders
- **In-person events**: Sometimes the most effective for certain industries

## How to Run a User Interview

The interview should be conducted over video call, phone, or in person. A five-minute video interview yields more insight than 5,000 survey responses [1].

### Core Principles

1. **Build rapport with the interviewee.** They are going to give answers to questions nobody has asked them before. They need to feel comfortable [1].

2. **Do not introduce your idea until the end of the call, or not at all.** Doing this too early biases their answers. The interviewer's role is to listen, not talk [1].

3. **Ask open-ended follow-up questions.** "Tell me about that." "What do you mean by that?" "Can you tell me more about that?" "Why is that important to you?" These techniques get people to open up about their problems [1].

4. **Take notes.** If not recording, take notes during the interview. The notes are the raw material for product decisions [1].

### Six Key Questions

Alströmer provides a specific question framework [1]:

1. "Tell me how you do X today." (X = the specific problem, task, or goal)
2. "What is the hardest thing about doing X?"
3. "Why is it hard?"
4. "How often do you have to do X?"
5. "Why is it important for your company to do X?" (Dive deep on motivation)
6. "What do you do to solve this problem for yourself today?"

On the last question, Alströmer emphasizes: if you can watch them actually do it -- screen sharing during a video call, showing their laptop -- that is the ideal state. Understanding their current behavior is critical because changing behavior is hard [1].

### Questions to Avoid

1. "Will you use our product?" (They will say yes; it means nothing) [1]
2. "Which features would make product X better?" (Their job is to tell you about the problem; your job is to think about features) [1]
3. Yes/no questions (You want concrete, real examples) [1]
4. "How would a better product X look like to you?" (They are not product developers) [1]
5. Asking two questions at the same time (confuses the interviewee and the answers) [1]

### The Biggest Danger: Focusing on Features Instead of Problems

Users generally have good problems but bad solutions [1]. Alströmer gives two specific examples:

**Gmail**: Early users asked Paul Buchheit to display both the inbox and the email they were reading on the same screen. The real reason was that Gmail was too slow, and users did not want to wait to load each email. The solution was not a split-screen view -- it was making Gmail faster [1].

**Airbnb**: Many guests wanted hosts' phone numbers so they could call them. The real reason was that guests did not fully trust the platform. Airbnb had not displayed enough trust signals, so guests needed to feel comfortable by getting to know the host before booking [1].

Users also have no incentive to say no to additional features. If asked "would you like feature A, B, or C?", they will say yes to everything. The founder must prioritize [1].

## From Interviews to MVP

After five to ten interviews [1]:

1. Organize learnings using sticky notes or similar software. Bucket them into different problem categories.
2. Identify which problem matters the most.
3. Write down conclusions and create a hypothesis for what the solution might be.
4. Do not over-intellectualize this process. Build an MVP as fast as possible.
5. Test the MVP with the same users.

### Determining if the Problem Is Valuable

Three tests [1]:

1. **Are people paying money for other solutions today?** If competitors are getting paid well, that is a good sign.
2. **Do people already have solutions they are happy with?** Excel and Google Spreadsheets are competitors to hundreds of startups. Moving someone off a spreadsheet requires a dramatically better experience.
3. **How easy is it to sell to this audience?** Selling to plumbers or contractors is notoriously difficult compared to selling to startups.

## Getting First Customers: Tactical Advice

Alströmer provides a systematic framework for converting user interviews into first customers [2].

### Founders Must Do Sales Themselves

Founders should learn to do sales because talking to customers and sales are different sides of the same coin [2]. You cannot outsource engineering, and sales has to be part of the founders' DNA. You should not hire a sales team until you know how to do sales yourself -- only then will you know what good looks like [2].

Examples of founders who took on the sales job and became great at it: Tony Xu (DoorDash), Mathilde Collin (Front), Tracy Young (PlanGrid), and Steve Jobs [2].

### The Sales Email

A great sales email should be [2]:

- Short: max six to eight sentences
- Clear language with no jargon or buzzwords
- Address the problem the potential customer is having
- Plain text only (no HTML formatting)
- State that you are the founder
- Include social proof (YC, impressive past companies)
- Include a link to a simple website with screenshots
- Include a call to action (request for a call or meeting)

### Working Backwards from Goals

Most founders do not send enough outreach because they do not work backwards from their goals [2]. Alströmer provides a conversion funnel example:

- 500 outreach emails sent
- 50% open rate = 250 opens
- 5% response rate = ~20 responses
- 50% convert to demo = 10 demos
- 20% close rate = 2 customers

The critical mistake: founders send 100 emails, get zero customers, and conclude "sales doesn't work." They did not send enough emails to reach statistically meaningful conversion rates [2].

### Easiest Customers First

First customers should be the easiest to close [2]:

1. **Sell to people you know.** Selling to strangers is harder.
2. **Sell to startups.** Bigger companies have more bureaucracy, procurement departments, and longer decision-making chains. Startups have short decision-making lines and the founder can often reach the decision-maker directly.
3. **Most people are not early adopters.** The reason you must send hundreds of emails is not that people hate your product -- most just archive the email. You are looking for the small percentage who love trying new things.

### Charge From the Start

If you do not charge your customers, they are not a customer, and you do not have a company [2]. Customers paying money is the strongest signal that you are providing real value.

Instead of free trials in B2B, offer a money-back guarantee or the ability to opt out of an annual contract after one month [2]. Increase your price until customers are complaining but still paying [2].

## Keeping Users Involved

After initial interviews and MVP testing, keep interviewees involved throughout the process [1]:

- Create a Slack instance or WhatsApp group with early customers
- Make them feel they have exclusive access to a future world-changing product
- Show them the product as it progresses
- Ship features in response to their feedback -- this builds trust
- Connect them with other people doing the same thing (they rarely get this opportunity)

## How YC Founders Actually Got Their First Customers

A survey of 50+ founders from a recent YC batch reveals the most common methods [3]:

- **Cold email and outreach**: The most frequently cited method. One founder described going door to door, standing in the sun waiting for a food truck owner to take a break
- **Personal network**: "As with a lot of founders, your first few customers come from your network"
- **Community launches**: Reddit, LinkedIn, Twitter/X, GitHub. One founder went from 50 Twitter followers to 10,000 and 20,000 GitHub stars in days
- **Waitlists and launches**: Several founders used LinkedIn posts and waitlists to generate inbound interest
- **Creative approaches**: One founder responded to recruiting emails from biotech companies by saying "No, but would you like to buy software that does the same thing?"

The pattern is consistent with the broader YC canon: [[Doing Things That Don't Scale]] is how every successful company starts, regardless of what their growth channels look like at scale [2].
