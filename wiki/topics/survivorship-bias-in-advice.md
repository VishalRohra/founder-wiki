# Survivorship Bias in Startup Advice

Prominent technology leaders frequently give advice that contradicts the path they actually followed to become successful. [[Dalton Caldwell]] and [[Michael Seibel]] call this the "do as I say, not as I did" problem and argue that founders who take famous people's advice at face value -- without researching their actual histories -- are making a dangerous mistake [1].

## The Pattern

A successful person gives advice that sounds reasonable but does not reflect what they did to become successful. The audience, often in their twenties, only knows these people as successes. They have no reason to question the advice. Caldwell and Seibel, who are old enough to have watched these people's careers from the beginning, see the disconnect clearly: "We saw what they did. We know their story. And we're just like, 'That's weird. You didn't do that'" [1].

## Three Examples

### Sam Altman

Altman recently suggested that the YC-style approach of starting small may not be optimal -- that founders should think bigger, raise more money, hire teams, and build labs [1]. Caldwell and Seibel know Altman's actual path: his first startup was Loopt, a mobile social product to see where your friends were in the city. He went to Stanford, did YC, raised a small amount of money, had a small team, and iterated. It was "a pretty garden variety path" with a "pretty normal outcome" [1].

Altman could not have started OpenAI as his first company. He needed the network, credibility, and experience that Loopt and YC provided. "If you actually wanted to emulate Sam, you would not immediately go try to start a lab and raise a hundred million dollars. Because that's not at all what he did" [1].

The people most influenced by Altman's "raise a hundred million" advice are precisely the people who will not be able to do it. "If you're one of the people out there that can't do a startup because they're like, 'If I can't raise a hundred million dollars, why even bother?' -- that's not real" [1].

### Elon Musk

Musk advocates for ambitious hardware projects -- atoms over bits, going to space, fixing the world. Seibel recounts a direct conversation with Musk about his actual path. Musk said he wanted to go to space, knew it required a lot of money, and came to Silicon Valley during the dot-com boom specifically to make money fast through conventional software startups (Zip2, then X.com/PayPal). "His ideas were pretty conventional. It wasn't hard tech. It wasn't deep tech" [1].

Musk leveraged his wealth and network from those exits to start SpaceX, and repeatedly bailed out Tesla with personal funds. "If he were not an extremely wealthy person, there's no way Tesla could have existed" [1].

Seibel concludes: "Most mere mortals out in the world need to do step one and can't skip to step two, even though step two sounds cooler. Mere mortals, including Elon -- literally Elon himself -- was a mere mortal" [1].

### Peter Thiel

Thiel is known for critiquing college. But he attended Stanford for both undergraduate and law school, worked at a famous law firm and a famous bank. "He went to college. And not only did he go to college -- he went to college" [1].

Caldwell acknowledges Thiel's critique is valid in parts. But the A/B test does not exist. "My sense is there was some aspect of those experiences that had a profound impact on his later success" [1].

## Why Leaders Give This Advice

Seibel assumes positive intent. These are successful people trying to help. Three mechanisms explain the disconnect [1]:

1. **Blindness to backstory.** They are so used to their own history that they "almost become blind to it, just like we can." What they focus on is what they would do now, given what they know now.

2. **Advice they wish they could tell their younger selves.** Seibel suspects much of this advice is what these people wish they had done differently. Altman might have raised more money sooner. Thiel might have skipped the law firm. But those were formative experiences that may have been necessary.

3. **Context collapse.** Videos and blog posts cannot be personalized. YC office hours are personalized -- "we read their application, we interviewed them, we accepted them." Public advice is aimed at the void. The nuance that would make it useful for a specific person is lost.

## The Billion-Dollar Origin Stories

A complementary YC discussion reveals that the biggest companies all had humble, indistinguishable beginnings [2]. Multiple group partners share stories of interviewing companies that became massive.

**Solugen** (industrial chemicals, now a major company) showed up to their YC interview with a beaker of hydrogen peroxide -- most of the hydrogen peroxide they had ever produced. During the batch, they sold bottles from their garage. [[Jared Friedman]] was convinced not by the technology but because "these guys were doers. They weren't waiting for anyone's permission" [2].

**Amplitude** (now a public analytics company) applied with a voice-to-text Android app. Paul Buchheit "hammered" the founder Spencer, arguing Google would crush them. Spencer was "just so intense in his comebacks and just determined." They pivoted a year and a half after YC but that intensity was the signal [2].

**Segment** ($3.2 billion acquisition by Twilio) applied with a tool for professors to poll students during lectures. "No one wanted this. It had zero traction." The partners pushed them to work on something technically hard, but the founders were "obstinate" about fixing education. Eventually they found the Segment idea, but it took time [2].

**Brex** started as a VR startup. **Reddit** was shared among the first YC batch. The pattern is consistent: the origin story looks nothing like the eventual company [2].

Brad Flora adds that the best founders at interview are clear, succinct, and honest about what they do not know. The Jeeves application had two sentences for progress: "We've completed a deal with our first bank partner and are ready to start onboarding initial customers." Flora was impressed by the confidence to say so little [2].

## How YC Tries to Avoid the Trap

Three practices [1]:

1. **Tell their own full stories.** At the beginning of each batch, partners share their complete backstories -- everything they screwed up, every mistake. "It helps people get to know us, to contextualize us as humans. And it helps to know how many mistakes you can make and still do something interesting."

2. **Give advice they would give their own children.** "If I tell this to my kids, probably it's good for you too." Seibel attended Yale and was not impressed, but recognizes it is where he met his co-founders: "Without them, I wouldn't be here right now. So if that's the only thing Yale did for me, it changed my life."

3. **Personalize advice in office hours.** In private, YC partners sometimes give advice that contradicts the public YC canon because the founder's specific situation warrants it. A multi-time founder known to be a strong fundraiser might be advised to raise more money. "That doesn't apply to most people" [1].
