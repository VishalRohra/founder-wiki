# Pitching Investors vs. Customers

[[Michael Seibel]] argues that the most common pitching mistake is using the same pitch for investors and customers [1]. The two audiences have fundamentally different knowledge, motivations, and needs.

## Why the Same Pitch Fails

Customers typically know a lot about the problem being solved and are personally interested in the solution. Investors may not have the problem at all and are interested only insofar as it can build a big company [1].

## The Customer Pitch

When talking to a customer [1]:
- **Use jargon.** Industry language builds credibility with people who know the space.
- **Let them talk more than you.** Get them to speak about their problem.
- **Focus on how the solution helps them personally, right now.**
- **Get into nitty-gritty details.** Screenshots, live demos, specific functionality, onboarding process, and how pricing applies to them specifically.

Where to use it: sales calls, website front page, help pages, FAQs, user interviews [1].

## The Investor Pitch

When talking to an investor [1]:
- **Eliminate jargon.** It hurts rather than helps. Investors do not share the customer's vocabulary.
- **Avoid marketing language.** "We're building a platform" or "the most unique thing ever" is fluff that investors must filter out.
- **Use concrete, simple descriptions.** Instead of "Google organizes the world's information" (which tells an investor nothing), say: "Google's a new type of search engine. You go to our website, type what you want to know, click enter, and we report an ordered list of websites ranked by how other websites link to them. Those results are way more relevant" [1].

What investors want to know [1]:
1. What do you do? (In the simplest language possible)
2. How far along are you?
3. How big is the total addressable market?
4. How do you plan to charge users?
5. What do you know about this market that others don't?
6. Who's on your team -- specifically, can you build the product?

Seibel's test: "If your front page of your website and your pitch deck are saying the same thing and you don't have a product where you assume the customer is also a user -- a product like Yelp or Uber -- you might be doing it wrong" [1].
