# Wealth vs. Money

[[Garry Tan]] argues that founders should pursue wealth, not money. Drawing on [[Paul Graham]] and philosopher Alan Watts, Tan distinguishes between money (an integer in a database) and wealth (the capacity to create value and generate more value over time) [1].

## Money Is Not Wealth

Paul Graham: "If you're in the middle of Antarctica where there's nothing to buy, it wouldn't matter how much money you had. Wealth is what you want, not money" [1]. Alan Watts made the same point in the 1970s: money "has the same relationship to real wealth -- actual goods and services -- that words have to meaning. As words are not the physical world, money is not wealth" [1].

Tan warns that focusing on money traps people in a consumptive cycle: "You'll end up focused on that next promotion or the trappings of upward mobility" [1]. He describes his own Microsoft years as an example: "My employer was buying my time, and working there was tiring. I got this money, and I'd consume -- buy a new shirt at Banana Republic or a nice martini. I was stuck in a consumptive mode" [1].

## The Path to Wealth: Skills

Wealth creation begins with acquiring skills, particularly skills that are rare and valuable. Tan uses Dennis Rodman as an illustration: Rodman started as a janitor at an airport, then had a growth spurt and pursued basketball. But what made him a Hall of Famer was developing a skill nobody else had -- studying the trajectory and spin of every shooter's ball to position himself for rebounds. "He did something nobody else did or even knew that they should do, and that won his team five NBA championships" [1].

In technology, the most valuable skills are those closest to actual creation: engineering, design, and product. "Being an engineer, designer, or product person is the easiest way to be a craftsperson in modern society. These are the next generation construction workers of our era" [1].

## Above the API vs. Below the API

Tan cites Venkatesh Rao's framework: you either live above the API or below it [1]. An Uber driver works below the API -- following directions from an app, using no specialized skills. The people who created Uber work above the API -- building and maintaining the systems that give people what they want. "If you can do things nobody else can do, you'll have skills that are literally priceless. And to be frank, this is one of the more brutal facts of capitalism" [1].

## The Wealth Machine

Tan introduces the concept of a "wealth machine" -- a business that takes money in, turns the crank, and spits out more money than it started with [1]. The key property: the output can be reinvested to create more wealth.

He contrasts four types:
- **Wealth machine** (money + wealth): a business with virtuous cycles. "You can take the money that comes out and feed it back in" [1].
- **Get-rich-quick scheme** (money, no wealth): a one-time transaction with no repeatable mechanism. "Lambos can be bought with money, but they can't actually be converted into even more money" [1].
- **LARPing startup** (no money, no wealth): hiring people, spending money, and wasting it all. "A special form of this is when startups decide to sell twenty-dollar bills for ten dollars or even give them away for free" [1].
- **Truly great startup** (both): generates both more money and more of the capacity to generate money. Three virtuous cycles work together: talent (better teams attract better people), customers (happy users tell others), and capital (growth enables more fundraising). "That's what [[Product-Market Fit]] is, and that's the key to this whole startup thing" [1].

## Leverage: Products That Work While You Sleep

Tan frames his personal career arc through the lens of leverage. At age 14, he got his first job cold-calling the Yellow Pages. He was happy getting $7, then $10, then $20 per hour to write code. "But what I wish I knew earlier was that I have a really finite number of hours in the day and in my life. You can get to sixty or seventy hours per week of work, but beyond that you run out. But if you make a product that can make money for you while you sleep, that's leverage" [1].

He uses the Apple founding story to illustrate how leverage emerges naturally: Jobs and Wozniak made computers for themselves, then their friends, then the circle got bigger. "We didn't think about starting a company. We were just doing it for ourselves" [1]. Naval Ravikant frames it as escaping finite games: "Wealth is about positive sum games" and "getting out of the sort of finite games like selling your time" [1].

## Ownership Over Salary (Sam Altman)

[[Sam Altman]]'s 2019 *How to Be Successful* reinforces Tan's wealth-not-money frame with the specific operational claim: "You get truly rich by owning things that increase rapidly in value. This can be a piece of a business, real estate, natural resource, intellectual property, or other similar things. But somehow or other, you need to own equity in something, instead of just selling your time. Time only scales linearly" [2]. The framing is explicitly anti-salary: "The biggest economic misunderstanding of my childhood was that people got rich from high salaries. Though there are some exceptions -- entertainers for example -- almost no one in the history of the Forbes list has gotten there with a salary" [2]. The implied follow-through: "The best way to make things that increase rapidly in value is by making things people want at scale."

Altman's first principle in the same essay gives the compounding argument that makes ownership matter: "A medium-sized business that grows 50% in value every year becomes huge in a very short amount of time ... It's worth a lot of effort to find [compounding businesses] and create them" [2]. Paired with Tan's wealth-machine framing, the synthesis is: salary is linear time-for-money, ownership in a compounding business is exponential, and the entire wealth-vs-money distinction is a rediscovery of the same asymmetry from different angles. See [[Leaving Big Tech]] for Altman's warning about the specific way salary-lifestyle matching destroys risk-taking capacity.
