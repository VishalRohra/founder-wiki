# Startup Culture

Tim Brady, YC group partner and co-founder of Yahoo (1994), defines company culture as behavior -- "that implicit set of behaviors inside of your company" that, when done right, "inform the employees inside of your company how to behave when it hasn't been explicitly laid out for them" [1]. The right culture supports a good business "in a lot of intangible ways that are hard even to describe" [1].

## Why Culture Matters at the Earliest Stage

Brady frames culture around three company phases: the idea stage (talking to customers, iterating), the search for [[Product-Market Fit]], and scaling. The people in place before scaling begins are the company's "cultural DNA" -- they will be heavily involved in hiring and training the next wave of employees [1].

Brady refers to this as the "first 20 employees," though the specific number matters less than the principle: the employees present when scaling begins propagate whatever culture exists [1]. If the early employees embody the right values, there is a much higher likelihood of building a strong, coherent culture. If mistakes are made -- wrong types of people hired early -- those mistakes compound. "It'll be much harder later on to correct course and try to build a coherent company" [1].

The good news: at the idea stage, establishing culture requires little time. "For the most part, it's just some conversations with your co-founder" [1].

## Six Things to Do Now

### 1. Be Proud of the Problem You Are Solving

If the founder does not have the problem personally, they need to identify with the people who do and be genuinely proud of solving it for them [1]. Building a company is long and difficult. Without pride in the work, it is hard to maintain the energy and enthusiasm needed to sustain the company through difficult periods [1].

"Everyone around you will see how you feel about the company. And to a large degree that will set the tone for your culture" [1].

Brady describes a YC alum whose team started by helping retailers liquidate excess inventory but eventually ended up in the makeup-for-teenage-girls business. They did not identify with the problem or the customers. Employees noticed the founders' lack of enthusiasm. The company shut down [1].

A common failure: choosing an idea with ego -- "because it sounds good to tell their friends at a party" -- rather than one the founder genuinely cares about [1].

### 2. Create a Long-Term Vision That Others Will Follow

A compelling vision attracts people who identify with the problem and want to help solve it. The vision should give purpose to the work, not describe the work itself [1].

Three examples [1]:
- **Tesla**: "To accelerate the world's transition to sustainable energy." No mention of electric vehicles. Inspires a broad range of people beyond just engineers who understand the technical challenges.
- **Microsoft (original)**: "A computer on every desk in every home." In the early 1980s, this was "crazy talk" -- computers were only for businesses and hobbyists. But this vision attracted the hobbyists with the skills to build what Microsoft needed.
- **Google**: "Organize the world's information and make it universally accessible and useful." No mention of a search engine.

### 3. Define Values and Behaviors

Have a conversation with co-founders about the types of values and behaviors to cultivate inside the company. At this stage, the purpose is practical: use the list as a filter for hiring. The list should be short (fewer than five items) and informal [1].

Brady provides two corporate examples of what a polished version might look like [1]:
- **Spotify**: "Innovative. Collaborative. Sincere. Passionate. And playful."
- **Atlassian**: "Open company, no bullshit. Build with heart and balance. Don't fuck with the customer. Play as a team. Be the change you seek."

These clearly originated from founder conversations. "I don't wanna work in an environment that's highly political, no bullshit" -- that translates directly into a hiring filter [1].

### 4. Make Values Externally Focused

Build a culture focused on the customer rather than on internal behavior. Internal values are not wrong, but externally focused values serve the company better over time [1].

Brady contrasts two famous examples [1]:

**"Move fast and break things" (Facebook):** An internally focused value. If a project manager or engineer is deciding what to build next, this offers no guidance beyond speed. "Shouldn't be a surprise when you look at this that some of the privacy violations that they've been charged with have occurred at Facebook. I don't think for a second anyone at Facebook set out to violate anyone's privacy, but their culture certainly didn't help them" [1].

**"Don't be evil" (Google, original):** Outwardly focused. "It lets the employees and the world know, 'Hey, we're a force for good.'" Google's 20% time policy produced independent engineer projects, and "it's pretty impressive that you haven't heard of any of those go astray, given the data they're sitting on" [1].

### 5. Have a Conversation About Diversity

Brady means diversity broadly -- not just ethnic and gender diversity, but diversity of opinions. "Can you create a culture where people with diametrically opposed opinions, strongly held, can coexist?" [1]. Research suggests companies that foster this type of environment tend to be more creative and better at problem-solving [1].

The challenge: the standard early-stage advice is to hire from personal networks -- former colleagues, friends from school. These are known quantities with known skills and values, which is good. But "they're also probably a lot like you. And you can find pretty quickly that you've built a pretty homogeneous environment" [1].

Brady warns: "If you think you're gonna wake up at 100 employees and then start a diversity program, you're fooling yourself. It's way too hard. It's too late by then" [1].

### 6. Put a Hiring Plan in Place

From the very first employee, follow a structured hiring process that incorporates the values conversations, the diversity goals, and the behavioral filters [1]. After the first few hires, reconvene with the co-founder a month or two later to assess whether the process worked -- "Did it filter the right way? Do you have the right type of people in your company at this point?" [1].

Plan on evolving the process. "You want it tested by the time you get to the point where you have to scale fast" [1].

## The Model-It-Yourself Principle

The values list cannot just be a document. Founders must model the behaviors themselves. "For better or for worse, the early employees will look to you for the cultural cues. You can't say, 'Do as I say, not as I do.' You have to walk the walk" [1].
