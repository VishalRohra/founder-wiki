# Startup Terminology

[[Dalton Caldwell]] provides definitions for the most common terms in the startup world, emphasizing that many are less precise than founders assume [1].

## MVP (Minimum Viable Product)

The keyword is "viable." A product that does not work and is useless to everyone is not viable. An MVP must be useful enough to serve some purpose for the customer. "It doesn't just mean a simple product that you built that doesn't do anything. It's got to be useful to someone for it to be viable" [1].

## Product-Market Fit (PMF)

The state where a startup has built something people use and like, and the biggest issue is no longer defining what people want but growing to serve as many people as possible [1]. Pre-PMF, startups spend all their time testing assumptions and talking to customers. Post-PMF, the job is to maintain that state and scale. "The tactics and priorities you should have are very different depending on which of these two modes you're in" [1]. See [[Product-Market Fit]] for the full treatment.

## Burn Rate

How much money the bank account decreases each month. If you start a month with $1 million and end with $900,000, the burn rate is $100,000 [1]. "Not paying attention to this is harmful to your startup's health" [1].

## Venture Capital

VCs invest small amounts to buy equity in startups with the hope that enough become very large to return multiples of the fund. The majority of a VC portfolio does not succeed, but investments in the really big winners pay for everything [1]. Caldwell notes the original venture capital was in the whaling industry: investors funded multiple ships hoping one successful whale capture would pay for all the failed expeditions [1].

## Angel Investor

An individual investing their own personal money (not from a fund), typically at the earliest stage. They write smaller checks ($20K-$50K) and do not do it as a full-time job. There are no qualifications; it is usually someone who has money and invests as a hobby or side activity [1].

## Seed Round

The first meaningful amount of money a startup raises. There is no technical definition -- it can range from $300K on a SAFE to much larger amounts. It is the first round of any consequence, and "whatever amount of money it is that you raised, you would refer to as a seed round" [1].

## Series A, B, C

Subsequent rounds after the seed. Key differences from seed rounds: a lead investor is typically involved, they may take a board seat, and they traditionally get significant ownership (around 20% in a Series A). Each subsequent round increments the letter. Companies at late stages may reach Series E, G, or even H [1].

## SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity)

A financial instrument created by Y Combinator (specifically Carolyn Levy) as an alternative to convertible notes. A SAFE has fewer terms, no interest rate, no maturity date, and no repayment requirement. "The advantage of a SAFE is there's just less terms to worry about" [1]. See [[Seed Fundraising]] for detailed SAFE mechanics.

## Convertible Note

A debt-like instrument where an investor gives money that converts to equity at a future priced round. It typically includes interest and may require repayment. Caldwell advises founders to read the fine print carefully [1].

## TAM (Total Addressable Market)

A thought experiment: if 100% of potential customers purchased the product, how much revenue would the company generate? No one reaches 100% of TAM [1].

Critically, TAM calculations can be wrong. Tesla's TAM for electric cars was tiny at founding -- you had to believe the EV market would grow to approximate the entire car market. Uber's TAM for taxis was small, but Uber dramatically expanded the market by creating a better experience that attracted riders who never used taxis [1]. "Great products tend to grow the TAM" [1].

## Profitability

Revenue exceeding expenses. The important nuance: how do profit margins change at scale? Many startups are unprofitable at small scale but have attractive margins at large scale. Google made no revenue for its first few years but had extremely high margins on advertising once monetization launched [1].

## Bootstrapping

Starting a company without raising venture capital, using personal funds or business revenue instead. A good option for businesses that will reach $5-10M in annual revenue and not grow beyond that. "One of the reasons bootstrapping is a great option is it's entirely within your control. You don't need the approval of an investor" [1].

## ARR and MRR

**ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue):** Revenue from customers on contracts that renew annually. Ten customers on $100K annual contracts = $1M ARR [1].

**MRR (Monthly Recurring Revenue):** Revenue from monthly subscriptions. A product like ChatGPT that bills monthly would report MRR rather than ARR [1].

The "recurring" is important: the revenue must genuinely recur through subscriptions or renewing contracts [1].

## Valuation

The price at which the last investor invested. For a company that raised $2M on a $20M post-money SAFE, the valuation is $20M. Unlike public stock prices, startup valuations do not imply there are always buyers and sellers at that price. "Startups with high valuations don't always succeed" [1].

## IPO (Initial Public Offering)

When a privately held company sells shares on public exchanges (NASDAQ, NYSE). An IPO allows employees, founders, and investors to realize value from their ownership. It signals that a company is "financially mature, growing well, and has built something of enduring value" [1].

## Equity and Stock Options

Equity is direct ownership. Stock options are rights to purchase equity at a future date at a preset price. Employees at startups commonly receive stock options rather than equity directly. Caldwell advises: "Always read the fine print and understand exactly what you are buying and selling" [1].
