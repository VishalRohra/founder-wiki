# Startup Terminology

[[Dalton Caldwell]] defines the most common terms in the startup world [1]. This article serves as a reference glossary, with links to deeper treatment in other wiki articles where available.

## MVP (Minimum Viable Product)

The keyword is "viable." A product that does not work and is useless to everyone is not viable. An MVP must be useful enough to serve some purpose for the customer. "It doesn't just mean a simple product that you built that doesn't do anything. It's got to be useful to someone for it to be viable" [1]. See [[Startup Ideas]] for guidance on building and testing MVPs.

## Venture Capital (VC)

Venture capitalists invest small amounts of money to buy equity in startup companies, hoping that enough of those companies become very large to return multiples of the original investment [1]. "The majority of a Venture Capital portfolio does not succeed, but the investments in the really big, successful companies pay for everything" [1].

Caldwell shares a historical note: "The original Venture Capital was in the whaling industry. The way it worked is when people would go out to hunt whales, a lot of times the ventures would fail. And so the people that needed money to start a ship needed to raise money from investors" who would invest in multiple ships hoping one successful whale capture would pay for all failed expeditions [1]. See [[Bootstrapping vs. Venture Capital]] for when VC is and is not appropriate.

## Angel Investor

An angel investor invests their own personal money, not from a VC fund. Angels tend to be the earliest-stage investors, writing smaller checks ($20,000-$50,000), and investing as a hobby or side project rather than a full-time job. "There's no qualification to be an angel investor. It's usually just someone that has money" [1].

## Profitability

At its simplest, making more money each month than you spend [1]. Caldwell emphasizes examining how profit margins change at scale. A startup may not be making money at a small scale, but if margins increase as it grows, the business model can still be attractive [1].

Google made no revenue for its first few years, but the profit margins on online advertising are very high. "Once Google turned on their monetization engine, they started to make an enormous amount of money" [1].

## Burn Rate

How much the company's bank account decreases per month [1]. If a company has $1 million and ends the month with $900,000, the burn rate is $100,000. "As a startup founder, it is critical to watch your burn rate... Not paying attention to this is harmful to your startup's health" [1]. See [[Financial Survival]] and [[Runway Management]] for detailed treatment.

## Seed Round

The first amount of money a new startup raises of any consequence [1]. There is no technical definition of size. A $300,000 raise on a SAFE can be called a seed round. A $100 million raise at a billion-dollar valuation is sometimes also called a seed round. In seed rounds, "anything goes. It could be that it's entirely composed of $50k checks and there is no lead investor" [1]. See [[Seed Fundraising]].

## Series A, B, C (and Beyond)

Unlike a seed round, a Series A typically involves a lead investor who gets a significant ownership percentage (traditionally around 20%) and often a board seat [1]. Subsequent rounds increment alphabetically: Series B, C, D, and so on. "I think I've definitely seen" Series G and Series H [1]. Valuations vary widely across rounds; the round letter alone does not indicate the valuation [1]. See [[Series A Fundraising]] and [[Series A Term Sheets]].

## Product-Market Fit (PMF)

When a startup has built something people use and like a lot, and the biggest issue is no longer defining what people want but growing and serving as many people as possible [1]. Before PMF, "startups are just spending all their time trying to test their assumptions, trying to build, trying to design, trying to talk to customers." After PMF, "your job is to stay in that state, to keep product market fit, and to grow and scale to the best of your ability" [1]. See [[Product-Market Fit]] for the comprehensive treatment.

## Bootstrapping

Starting a company without raising venture capital, using personal funds or business revenue [1]. "One of the reasons bootstrapping is a great option is it's entirely within your control." It is a "much, much better option for businesses that you expect to get to $5 to $10 million a year in revenue" [1]. See [[Bootstrapping vs. Venture Capital]].

## Convertible Note

A debt-like financial instrument where an investor gives a startup money with terms that may include interest payments or repayment obligations [1]. Caldwell warns founders to "read the fine print very carefully" [1].

## SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity)

Created by Y Combinator (specifically Carolyn Levy), a SAFE is an alternative to a convertible note [1]. It has fewer terms and fewer investor rights than a convertible note. "If you're raising as a startup founder and you are not doing a priced round and selling preferred equity, a SAFE is a great option to quickly close money in a seed round" [1].

## Equity

Ownership in a startup company, expressed as a percentage that adds up to 100% [1]. Employees may receive stock options rather than direct equity -- "a right to, in the future, execute on the stock options to receive equity at that point." Caldwell advises: "Always read the fine print and understand exactly what you are buying and selling" [1]. See [[Equity Split]].

## TAM (Total Addressable Market)

A thought experiment: if 100% of potential customers purchased the product, how much revenue would the company generate [1]. TAM calculations can underestimate the market. When Tesla started, the TAM for electric cars was tiny; the thesis required believing electric cars would eventually capture the broader car market. Uber dramatically increased the TAM for car services by creating a better experience that brought in people who did not previously use taxis [1]. "Great products tend to grow the TAM" [1].

## Valuation

What valuation the last investor invested at [1]. Unlike public stock prices, startup valuations do not mean there are active buyers and sellers. "Startups with high valuations don't always succeed. And so just think of it as a helpful term but not necessarily always accurate" [1].

## IPO (Initial Public Offering)

When a privately held company sells shares on a public exchange (NASDAQ, NYSE) [1]. IPOs are important to startups because they provide a path for employees, founders, and investors to realize financial returns. An IPO is "a great sign that the company is doing well... financially mature, that it is growing well and has built something of enduring value" [1].

## ARR and MRR (Annual/Monthly Recurring Revenue)

ARR measures yearly revenue from contracts or subscriptions that renew [1]. "If you have ten $100,000 contracts that renew yearly, that would be a million dollars of ARR." MRR is the monthly equivalent, more commonly used when billing is monthly (e.g., a ChatGPT subscription). "Make sure if you're reporting, if you're telling an investor you have ARR, make sure it actually recurs" [1].
