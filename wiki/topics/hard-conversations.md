# Hard Conversations

[[Dalton Caldwell]] and [[Michael Seibel]] argue that having honest, uncomfortable conversations is one of the most important skills for startup founders, and that avoiding them is one of the most common failure modes [1]. The avoidance operates at every level: between founders and advisors, between co-founders, and between founders and employees.

## What Makes a Conversation Hard

Caldwell defines it by inversion: "If it's a conversation you're looking forward to having, it is not a hard conversation." A hard conversation involves high stakes, vulnerability, saying something the other person does not want to hear, or disagreeing with someone who has power over you. The easy version is "Hey, you're great. Way to go." The hard version is everything else that actually matters [1].

Seibel describes learning this as a YC partner. In his early batches, he measured his effectiveness by how much fun founders had talking to him. "Only after a couple batches did I realize that doesn't really do anything." When he started sharing his actual opinions about products and work ethic, "the job changed" [1].

## The Question Behind the Question

60 to 80 percent of the time at YC office hours, a founder arrives with one question and the conversation ends up being about something entirely different. Caldwell and Seibel call this "the question behind the question" [1].

**Example: The pre-made decision.** A founder comes to office hours having already made a decision. They want the hour to validate that decision. The easy response: "Seems like you've thought about this really well. I see no errors in your logic." The hard response: "Hey, it seems like you've already made a decision. Cool, why are we talking about this?" Often the real issue is that a co-founder disagrees, and the founder wants to bring the partner's authority into the argument [1].

**Example: The YC application anxiety.** People approach Seibel with "really important questions about applying." The actual questions are content-free. The real question is: "I feel insecure, and I want validation that I should apply" [1].

**Example: The deep-dive request.** A founder asks for a two-hour meeting. The surface reason varies. The real reason is usually one of: they think the partners do not understand their business, they want face time for relationship-building, they are disorganized and need two hours to produce twenty minutes of productive conversation, or they want to substitute talking to an advisor for talking to their users [1].

Seibel's response to the last pattern: "Folks, so often I'm just like, 'You're having a conversation with me that you should have with your customers. Talk to them. I don't use your product. Talk to the people who do'" [1].

## Hard Conversations with Co-Founders

The failure pattern: two people become co-founders without ever having had a genuine disagreement. The relationship is entirely superficial -- "We're best friends. We worked together. It's all wonderful." Then the first real disagreement arises, and the relationship explodes [1].

Caldwell identifies two failure modes [1]:

1. **No pressure release.** Co-founders only have superficial conversations for years. Pressure builds. Eventually someone snaps.
2. **Constant open fighting.** The opposite extreme, where disagreements are never resolved and the relationship is perpetually combative.

The skill is learning to disagree productively -- to "pull your punches and have a disagreement that's a real disagreement and actually release the pressure of the disagreement in a way that doesn't cross the line so that the relationship is irreparably harmed" [1].

This is why YC prefers co-founders who have a pre-existing relationship beyond the startup. When there is a friendship worth maintaining even if the startup fails, both people are more careful about what they say. When the only connection is the startup, and the startup is struggling, there is nothing to protect [1].

Seibel adds a critical nuance about conflict styles. Some people lean into conflict and want to talk it through immediately. Others avoid conflict, need time to process, or do not work through disagreements via conversation. Failing to read these signals creates more problems. "You can't blame the other person for not acting correctly. You have to take responsibility because you can both blame each other into an explosion all day long" [1].

## Hard Conversations with Employees

The same avoidance pattern operates between founders and employees. If founders only have easy, superficial conversations with their team, problems accumulate until an explosion [1].

Seibel offers two specific points for startup employees:

1. **Allow the possibility that you picked the wrong person.** The employee might not be the right fit. They might do ten times better work somewhere else. "Allowing that idea makes their life better just as much as it would make your life better" [1].

2. **Good startup employees want honesty.** They joined a startup because they want to impact the outcome. Sharing bad news -- even "we only have two months of runway" -- can be motivating rather than demoralizing. "For great startup employees, they're like, 'All right, how the fuck are we gonna fix this?'" The worst outcome is employees who are clueless about the company's real situation: "Well, I thought everything was going fine. You never told me anything was wrong" [1].

## Hard Conversations with Yourself

Caldwell and Seibel acknowledge that they struggle with this too. "We're not saying we always get this right 100 percent of the time." The only thing they claim to do well is continuously ask themselves: "Are we lying to ourselves? Is there a question behind the question?" [1].

Seibel closes with a metaphor from The West Wing. A character who has overcome addiction sees a colleague struggling but hiding it. Instead of offering superficial advice, he "jumps down to the bottom of the well" with the person. His justification: "I've climbed out of this well before, and I know the way out." Seibel applies this to advising founders: "I've made all these mistakes before, and the only thing that I could say is I know the way out" [1].
