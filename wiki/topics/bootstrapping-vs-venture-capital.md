# Bootstrapping vs. Venture Capital

[[Dalton Caldwell]] and [[Michael Seibel]] argue that the bootstrap-versus-VC debate is a "confusingly controversial topic" that is, in reality, not much of a debate at all [1]. The vast majority of businesses should not raise venture capital. Venture capital is not interested in investing in the vast majority of businesses. Neither of these facts is controversial among people who understand how venture capital works [1].

## The Scale of the Distinction

The percentage of businesses started each year that receive venture funding is in the single digits, possibly less than 1% [1]. The mismatch between reality and perception arises because VC-backed companies dominate media coverage, YouTube content, and Twitter discourse. Caldwell observes: "If you're just consuming content, watching media, watching YouTube, Twitter, you don't realize that" VC-backed companies are a "weird outlier, freak occurrence" [1].

Seibel identifies a specific source of confusion: shows like Shark Tank portray fundraising in a way that has "nothing to do with reality" in the venture capital industry. "If you're starting a business that can appear on Shark Tank, for the most part you probably shouldn't raise venture capital" [1].

## Venture Capital as a Product

Venture capital exists for a specific purpose: investing in something where the investment could be worth 100x to 1,000x more [1]. Seibel frames the consequence plainly: "Trying to put that jet fuel into something that isn't going to grow to be big, everyone is going to be sad and lose" [1]. No one wins -- not the investor, not the founder, not the users [1].

Caldwell extends the point: "Starting a VC-backed company is really hard. Not the best way to get rich. Not the best path." He compares it to professional athletics: "It's like getting into the NBA is really hard. Like, not like the best path to have a good career is to be like, 'Oh, I'll just make the NBA'" [1].

Most people who are rich did not raise venture capital. Real estate investing, the stock market, law, medicine, and finance all produce more wealthy people than venture-backed startups [1].

## Bootstrapping Is a Win

Caldwell and Seibel are explicit that building a profitable non-VC-backed software business is winning. Seibel shares a personal example: a friend who built a product generating $30,000 to $50,000 per month that required only 7-10 hours per month of maintenance. "The entire time I was working on my startup, he was living life. I would watch his life on Facebook and be like, 'This is amazing. He traveled the world, he got married, he had kids'" [1]. Seibel admits: "97 percent of the time I was watching that Facebook, I was like, 'Am I the idiot?'" [1].

Caldwell also defines bootstrapping in a separate talk: starting a company using personal funds or the business's revenue, without raising venture capital [2]. He identifies it as a "great option" for businesses expected to reach $5 to $10 million per year in revenue and likely not grow beyond that. "It's entirely within your control. You don't need the approval of an investor to go bootstrap a company" [2].

## When VC Makes Sense

Seibel identifies the specific conditions under which venture capital is the right tool: "There are some businesses where you actually need money upfront." If a software business needs millions of dollars or tens of millions of dollars to reach break-even, "there aren't other mechanisms in our economy to give that to you" [1].

Caldwell uses Google as an example: "I don't know how you could have bootstrapped that." The founders of Google could not have bought the servers and built the infrastructure without outside capital [1].

Seibel frames VC as enabling incremental entrepreneurship that would not exist otherwise -- "not as cannibalizing anything" [1]. The transaction is straightforward: "I give you money now. You give me a lot more money later, or at least you try really hard to" [1]. If the business cannot plausibly return a lot of money, the transaction does not make sense for either side [1].

## The Empirical Record

Seibel states a fact: "Empirically, none of the trillion-dollar software companies are bootstrapped with no VC dollars ever" [1]. This does not mean founders should aspire to build trillion-dollar companies. It means that if someone claims bootstrapping can produce outcomes at that scale, the historical record does not support the claim [1].

## The Decision Is Revisitable

Caldwell notes that the bootstrap-vs-VC decision is not permanent. "If you don't want to start a VC-backed company now, but your bootstrap product starts doing well and you want to do it in the future, great. You can. There's no one who is going to say, 'No, you said you were bootstrapped on Twitter. I will not give you money'" [1].

## The Fake Controversy

Caldwell argues the entire debate is "fake news" -- a manufactured controversy that serves content creators who monetize engagement. "I don't think anyone thinks it's more moral or good or right to start a VC-backed company or a non-VC-backed company. I don't think this is an actual debate" [1].

Seibel identifies the pattern that fuels outrage: people point to a silly company that raised a lot of money and ask, "If they funded that, why won't they fund my social network for dogs?" Caldwell's response: "Don't fall for the rage bait. Don't let yourself get enraged by obvious engagement bait" [1]. The person who funded that silly company probably made a mistake. That does not invalidate the logic of venture capital [1].

Seibel also notes that wanting to raise venture capital should come from an internal drive, not from external pressure: "That should come from inside of you, not from YouTube." Caldwell agrees: "If you need to be talked into it, that's a bad sign" [1].
