# Series A Fundraising

The Series A is the first major priced equity round for most startups. It involves two distinct challenges: building and delivering a compelling pitch, and navigating the legal and organizational complexity of closing. This article covers both, drawing on Janelle Tam's guide to Series A pitches and [[Aaron Harris]] and Jason Kwon's diligence checklist.

## The Series A Pitch

Janelle Tam, YC's Series A Program Manager, compiled feedback from hundreds of hours working 1:1 with founders on their Series A pitches and decks. Every piece of advice in her guide was given to at least one YC founder internally [2].

### Purpose and Principles

The purpose is to show investors why they should invest. The pitch provides a clear and concise overview of the business already built, then paints a picture of where it could go and why raising money will help get there [2].

Six principles underlie this:

1. **It is an investor pitch, not a customer pitch.** Investors may not have encountered the problem being solved. Good pitches explain the problem and its impact, show how the product solves it, and demonstrate why it is a good business [2].
2. **Clarity is paramount.** Investor attention is a limited resource. Make the pitch as simple and straightforward as possible. The best pitchers use plain English, avoiding jargon. Where jargon is necessary, they define each term clearly on first use [2].
3. **Give an overview, not a download.** The goal is to tantalize investors with enough information to keep them interested, leaving room for questions. A pitch should be 15-20 minutes, setting up a 40-45 minute discussion. If investors are not asking questions, the slides are probably too complicated [2].
4. **Start with what you have built.** Compared to the seed round, it is harder to raise a Series A on promise alone. Investors want to see results [2].
5. **Paint a picture of where it is going.** Once you establish credibility through what you have already built, show how it could grow into a massive company [2].
6. **Show that money is the obstacle.** The deck should make clear that capital -- not incompetence, poor product quality, or lack of [[Product-Market Fit]] -- is what stands between the company and the next level [2].

### The Starting Exercise

Tam describes a structured process for building the pitch [2]:

1. Write the narrative as 10-15 bullet points -- the "fundraising vertebrae"
2. Under each bullet, list supporting data points
3. Build the deck around this list: each bullet is one slide. If more than one point per slide, the slide is too complicated. The deck should be 10-15 slides plus appendix
4. Write out every question investors could ask, especially about metrics, and prepare clear, credible, data-backed answers

### Deck Structure

**Title (1 slide):** Company name or logo and a one-sentence description. The one-liner must be concrete ("We build and operate robot greenhouses" not "Reinventing agriculture"), understandable to a layperson, and specific to what the company does [2].

**Traction Teaser (1 slide):** Placed early to grab attention and convince the investor the presentation is worth listening to [2].

**Problem (1 slide):** The problem must match the solution. Present from the customer's perspective. Show, don't tell -- "Medical billing is broken" is vague; "Here's how medical billing works today: patients need to do X steps, which takes Y months and results in Z% claims approved" is concrete [2].

**Solution (1 slide):** Side-by-side comparison with status quo. Quantify impact with numbers. Focus on what exists now, not future plans. Show how the customer uses it, not feature lists [2].

**Traction In-Depth (a few slides):** Numbers should tell a story, starting with the most important metric -- usually revenue. For enterprise: revenue growth, sales cycle, ACV, salesperson efficiency, churn. For consumer: revenue plus growth rate, organic vs. paid acquisition, CAC/LTV, engagement and retention. For hardtech/biotech: technology efficacy data, patents filed, LOIs, specific milestones [2].

Show at least 4-6 months of trend data. YC's [benchmarks](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/1k-benchmarks) assume Series A fundraising 18-24 months after seed [2].

Three traps in presenting numbers: information overload (identify "hero facts"), too few numbers (at Series A you need data), unclear numbers (cumulative numbers almost always hide weak monthly data) [2].

**Market (1 slide):** Bottoms-up calculation: number of prospective customers x value of each customer. Use real numbers from the current business, not top-down report figures [2].

**Competition (1 slide):** Show why the company is 10x better than alternatives. Convince the investor the moat is defensible [2].

**Vision (1 slide):** Show how the company becomes a $10B company. "Here's where you can dream and talk about all of the hypotheticals you've judiciously kept from the first part of your deck" [2].

**Team (1 slide):** If the team is a comparative advantage, place as the second or third slide. Otherwise, show who the founders are, why they are the right people, and quality of team recruited. Signaling risk: including a Series A fund among existing investors prompts the question of whether they are leading the round [2].

**Use of Funds (1 slide):** The climax. Cover how much is being raised, what it will fund, and where the company will be in 18-24 months [2].

**Appendix:** Ammunition for the Q&A. Include slides answering anticipated investor questions, financial projections, and detailed use of funds [2].

### Design and Practice

Optimize for clarity, not beauty. "Keeping it as simple and bare-bones as possible" is usually best [2].

Schedule 1-hour practice sessions: 20 minutes pitch, 20 minutes conversation, 20 minutes feedback. A quick test for individual slides: flash it to someone who has never seen it and ask what point it makes [2].

For video pitches: turn on video with good lighting and audio, one person should pitch, record and review yourself, keep a bullet-point script visible but avoid reading it [2].

### Iterating During Fundraise

Set aside 1-2 hours daily to review each meeting. After every pitch, ask: What excited investors most? Where did they zone out? What objections arose? [2]

## The Closing Process

### The Closing Timeline Problem

Founders generally underestimate how long closing a Series A takes. [[Aaron Harris]] and Jason Kwon (General Counsel of YC Continuity) observe that closing an A "can take more than a month, much of which is often spent tracking down documents for lawyers" [1]. Having all diligence materials organized in a single data room before signing a term sheet can cut as much as a week off the closing process [1].

### The Series A Diligence Checklist

Kwon assembled this checklist based on involvement in hundreds of financings with "nearly every law firm and VC fund in the valley" [1]. Everything listed below should be assembled in a data room before signing a term sheet.

**Corporate Records and Charter Documents:** All minutes of directors' and stockholders' meetings, written consents, Certificate of Incorporation, Certificates of Designation, Rights, Bylaws, subsidiary documentation, and organizational charts [1].

**Business Plan and Financials:** Current business plan, financial projections, and most recent financial statements [1].

**Intellectual Property:** List of all trademarks, patents, copyrights, and domain names with filing/registration documentation. Documentation of IP assignments to the company [1].

**Securities and Cap Table:** List of stockholders with issuance dates and prices. List of option holders with grant dates and exercise prices. Agreements relating to outstanding options, warrants, conversion rights, preemptive rights. Documents evidencing registration rights or shareholder agreements. Vesting schedules including acceleration provisions. Evidence of qualification under Rule 701 and state blue sky laws [1].

**Material Agreements:** Standard terms of service, agreements exceeding $25,000, property leases, debt documents, IP licenses, insurance policies, judgments, joint venture agreements, consulting contracts, and list of officers and directors [1].

**Disputes and Litigation:** Correspondence relating to pending or threatened actions, IP infringement allegations, labor disputes [1].

**Employee Information:** Complete employee and consultant list with title, salary, bonus, commission plan, classification, and state of residence. Standard offer letter template. Employment agreements with severance or acceleration provisions. Equity incentive plans. Employee handbook. Loans to employees. For foreign employees, country-by-country benefits listing [1].

### The Equity Grant Timing Trap

Harris and Kwon flag a critical timing issue [1]: once a term sheet is signed, most practitioners believe the company can no longer use its existing 409A valuation for option grants because a "material event" has occurred that implies a higher valuation.

The consequences cascade: team members hired earlier who have not yet received grants will get options at a higher strike price, reducing their upside, creating a morale issue at the worst possible time [1].

The solution is preventive: "make sure there isn't a significant backlog of equity grants while you are in the middle of your fundraise process" [1]. Clear all pending or promised equity grants before beginning the Series A process.
