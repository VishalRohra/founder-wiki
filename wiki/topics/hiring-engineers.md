# Hiring Engineers

Hiring engineers is one of the most difficult and time-consuming tasks for early-stage startup founders. [[Harj Taggar]], cofounder of Triplebyte (YC S15), describes it as "incredibly hard" because founders are already stretched thin on time, and the process involves significant dead ends and frustration [1]. The challenge is compounded by the fact that traditional recruiting methods do not work well for very early companies, and most of the effective strategies require significant founder involvement [1].

The same core principles -- personal networks first, speed in the funnel, genuine pitch over polished pitch -- apply when recruiting salespeople and other key hires [4].

## Start With Your Personal Network

For a startup's first engineering hire, personal networks are the most important source by far [1]. Hiring someone the founder has already worked with is the best option because the working relationship is already known. At the earliest stage, the quality of that relationship "could be the difference between persevering to success and shutting down the company" [1].

Taggar recommends a specific process for working personal networks [1]:

1. Make a list of the best engineers you know, whether you think they are available or not. Go through Facebook and LinkedIn to jog your memory.
2. Invite them to lunch or dinner to talk about the startup.
3. Make the ask: "Would you consider joining us?"
4. Whatever they answer, follow up: "If you did join us, which engineers would you most want to hire?"
5. Ask for introductions to those people.
6. Repeat with each introduction.

Taggar notes that founders are often surprised by how reluctant they are to fully explore their networks. Asking friends to leave their jobs is scary, and rejection from friends feels more awkward than rejection from strangers. But optimizing for the startup's success requires pushing past this discomfort [1].

For the first three engineering hires, Taggar recommends focusing exclusively on personal network hiring. After three hires, founders should supplement with other channels to improve the diversity and composition of the team [1].

## Other Hiring Channels, Ranked

Taggar ranks the remaining channels in order of effectiveness for early-stage companies [1]:

**Hiring Marketplaces** (e.g., Triplebyte, Hired): Strictly inferior to personal networks for a first hire, but the second-best option. They provide quick access to vetted, motivated-to-move engineers. The cost is typically around $25,000 per experienced engineer hire. Success depends heavily on the effectiveness of the company's pitch and closing process. Some marketplaces do rigorous technical vetting, which can double the onsite-to-offer conversion rate (40% vs. the industry standard 20%) [1].

**Inbound Generation**: Most early-stage startups get little organic inbound from good engineers. Job sites like Stack Overflow Jobs, the monthly Hacker News "Who is hiring?" thread, and AngelList can help. Developer tool companies have a natural advantage because their product is already interesting to engineers. Taggar recommends blogging about controversial or unusual technical choices to attract attention, and investing time in making job postings personal and distinctive rather than generic [1].

**Cold Outreach**: Messaging engineers on LinkedIn, Hacker News, or GitHub. The challenge is standing out from the overwhelming number of messages good engineers receive. Personalization is essential. Email is more effective than platform messages. Expect it to take up to six months before this channel results in a hire [1].

**Recruiters**: A technical recruiter will likely use cold outreach, and is unlikely to achieve higher response rates than messages from a founder. If used, work with contract-basis recruiters with agreed hourly rates and cancellation terms. Train them thoroughly on the company pitch before starting [1].

**Meetups**: High variance. Business conferences are "almost certainly a complete waste of time." Smaller, informal, deeply technical meetups can work, but only if the founder is either an engineer who can gain respect through technical conversation or a highly charismatic personality. Good for building a long-term network, not for making a hire right now [1].

**Agencies**: Traditional recruiting agencies have "bad adverse selection bias" on their candidates. Most good engineers will not work with them. Taggar cannot think of a startup he knows that made its first engineering hire through a recruiting agency [1].

## The Hiring Funnel: Speed and Experience

Generating an offer for an engineer is expensive. It takes roughly five onsite interviews to produce a single offer (the industry standard onsite-to-offer rate is around 20%), consuming approximately 30 hours of engineering productivity [2]. Given this cost, maximizing offer acceptance rates is critical.

### Speed as Competitive Advantage

Being fast and responsive is one of the biggest advantages a startup has over larger companies when competing for engineers [2]. Taggar identifies two reasons companies fail at this:

1. **Entropy**: Maintaining an organized candidate tracking system is hard. It naturally trends toward disorder. Using software (initially a spreadsheet or Airtable, graduating to a real ATS like Lever as the company grows) and assigning clear responsibility for daily candidate review can counteract this.
2. **Psychological bias**: Companies rationalize that candidates who dropped out due to slow response "weren't interested anyway." This is bad reasoning. Managing a job search is overwhelming, especially for the best candidates with the most options. Every candidate who drops out before receiving a reply should be treated as a failure [2].

### The First Call

The first call with a candidate typically lasts 25 minutes. Taggar recommends asking the candidate what they are looking for early in the call, not at the end, so the pitch can be tailored throughout [2]. For companies under 20 people, a founder should always do this call. The non-technical/sales founder should handle it to protect the technical founder's maker time, even if they cannot speak to technical challenges in detail [2].

### The Onsite Interview

Taggar surveyed engineers about bad onsite experiences. The top three complaints were interviewers being (1) unprepared and not knowing anything about the candidate, (2) not familiar with the technical questions they asked, and (3) determined to prove their own intelligence to the interviewee [2].

He recommends writing an interviewing guide emphasizing on-time, prepared, and friendly behavior, and selecting only the friendliest engineers as interviewers. A centralized question bank that interviewers work through together before using on candidates is essential [2].

For lunch, Taggar describes Triplebyte's own practice: Slack the team in the morning, gather at the lunch table with the candidate, have the office manager introduce them, and do a lighthearted icebreaker. "We've had candidates tell us this experience was the deciding factor in joining" [2].

## Making and Closing the Offer

### Two-Step Offer Presentation

Taggar recommends a specific sequence [2]:

**Step One (same day as onsite if possible):** The hiring manager (founder at early stage) calls to say the day went well and they want to make an offer. Mention specific reasons for excitement. Multiple people can join the call.

**Step Two (next day):** Call to walk through offer details. Gauge the candidate's familiarity with equity concepts before diving in. Do not push hard for a decision on this call.

### Competing With Big Companies

As a startup, it is hard to compete with Facebook and Google compensation packages. Taggar identifies four arguments that have been most successful [2]:

1. **Learning**: Real decision-making responsibility comes immediately at a startup.
2. **Career Progression**: Rising through the ranks only happens at fast-growing startups. "The Chief Product Officer of Facebook ($500bn market cap) is 36 years old."
3. **Opportunity Cost**: Working at a public technology company is always available. The startup is a unique opportunity right now.
4. **Mentorship**: If experienced engineers on the team are willing to mentor, make this explicit.

## Recruiting Salespeople

The same fundamentals apply to recruiting sales reps: personal networks first, founder involvement in early pitches, and speed through the process [4]. For early-stage AI companies, the hiring challenge is compounded by needing salespeople comfortable with technical products and rapid iteration -- the "script kiddie vs. hacker" distinction Ryan Junee makes in [[Enterprise Sales]] applies directly to sales hiring [4].

## What Job Seekers Should Know

Ryan Choi, YC's head of Work at a Startup, offers advice from the candidate's perspective [3]. For resumes, each position should cover: what you worked on, how you got work done, and what impact you had (growth numbers, cost savings, business results) [3].

For cold outreach, highlight two to three things you would be excited to work on at the company. Being a genuine user of the product is a significant advantage. When evaluating startups, ask about runway and burn rate [3].
