# Becoming a Founder

The question of whether, when, and how to become a startup founder is addressed by multiple YC partners. [[Michael Seibel]] provides the most comprehensive frameworks, covering career path analysis, college preparation, and the order of operations for actually starting. [[Dalton Caldwell]] adds the psychological dimension -- how to think about information consumption and self-conviction. Justin Kan offers the case for (and against) joining a startup as a stepping stone.

## Should I Start a Startup?

[[Michael Seibel]] frames the decision simply: "there is a certain type of person who only works at their peak capacity when there is no predictable path to follow, the odds of success are low, and they have to take personal responsibility for failure (the opposite of most jobs at a large company)" [3].

He acknowledges the counterarguments honestly [3]:
1. The vast majority of startups are not successful
2. For talented technical people, it is relatively easy to get a high-salary job
3. Large companies offer opportunities to work on difficult problems that only occur at scale

But for people who have the founder personality type, a predictable corporate path will never draw out their best work. The startup path is not for everyone, and Seibel is explicit that it requires self-knowledge [3].

## Decisions That Block Future Founders

Seibel frequently talks to people in their late 20s and 30s who want to start startups but cannot. A decision they made when they were younger is either blocking or creating a serious barrier. The most common blockers [1]:

- They did not learn how to write code
- They never met anyone in college who knew how to code
- They significantly increased their expenses early in life, did not save, and now cannot afford to begin a company
- They created roots post-college in a city that is not startup-friendly

## Advice for College Students

Seibel frames this as what those blocked founders would tell their younger selves [1]:

**Learn to code.** "You don't have to be the best developer, and you don't have to write code your entire life if you don't want to -- but knowing how to code gives you the proverbial golden ticket." The number one barrier to starting a tech startup is not knowing how to code [1].

**Meet people with real skills.** College puts you next to designers, programmers, salespeople, and scientists. These people could become cofounders or early employees [1].

**Be ready to hit the ground running.** Seibel disagrees with using your 20s to find yourself. "Your 20s are your most valuable working years: You often have very few obligations (no family), a very low burn rate (you're used to living in a dorm), and you are still young enough to meet new people and learn new skills" [1]. The prime startup years are ages 22 to 32. "Every year you are not doing a startup you are losing 10% of this prime time" [1].

**Be careful with student debt.** Student debt forces people away from startups and into big companies [1].

## Consuming the Right Information

[[Dalton Caldwell]] addresses a subtle trap for aspiring founders: consuming too much startup media about fundraising creates a distorted view of what matters [4]. "Sometimes I meet founders where they don't even have a startup idea yet -- but because they consume all this information about fundraising 24/7, they're completely focused on fundraising without even knowing what their startup idea is" [4].

Caldwell's framework: "your thought process as a founder is heavily influenced by the five most common information sources you get." He recommends being deliberate about consuming media that inspires building things, not media focused on fundraising, valuations, or startup celebrity. "If it's inspiring you to build more things, if it's inspiring you to work harder, if it's inspiring you to be rowing in the right direction -- keep it. If it's making you kind of depressed and sad, stop consuming it" [4].

He also warns against overvaluing thought leadership: "The vast, vast, vast, vast, vast majority of successful founders that we funded at YC have effectively no presence whatsoever, especially not in the early stages" [4].

## Convincing Others to Join

[[Dalton Caldwell]] reframes the question "How do I convince someone to join my startup?" as a self-conviction problem [5]. "If you're not fully committed or if you have grave doubts that your idea is any good or it's even worth trying, how can you ever convince someone else? And people can tell if you don't believe in the thing you're doing" [5].

The test: "Do you ever know that feeling where an idea enters your head and you can't sleep at night and you can't think about anything else? That's a good sign" [5]. If you are immediately cynical about your idea, you are not convinced.

Caldwell makes a key observation about idea quality: "It would appear that it's equally hard to succeed in a startup that you don't care about and one that you do. And it's almost equally hard to succeed in a startup that's a very ambitious, audacious idea as it is to do something super incremental. If those are equally difficult, do you see why you should definitely do the way more audacious idea?" [5].

## Why You Should (and Should Not) Work at a Startup

Justin Kan, co-founder of Twitch and three-time YC founder, gives a characteristically unfiltered assessment [6]:

**Reasons not to join a startup:**
1. **Management generally sucks.** "There are two kinds of YC companies. There are the rocket ships with bad management, and then there are the other companies with bad management" [6].
2. **You probably will not get rich.** "If you think you're gonna join a startup and then be set for life, that is unlikely to happen" [6].
3. **If you want stability, go to a big company.** "We don't have five years of money, so if you want stability, I think you should go join Facebook" [6].

**Reasons to join a startup:**
1. **Access to jobs you are unqualified for.** Kan's example: Guillaume, a programmer recruited from the first Work at a Startup event, was running Justin.tv's entire Rails backend within a year -- a top 10 Rails site. "That was a job he was completely unqualified for and he would never have gotten the opportunity to do if he didn't join a startup" [6]. Guillaume went on to co-found Socialcam and then Triplebyte.
2. **Gateway to starting your own startup.** "Put yourself in positions where you're around people who want to do the things that you want to do" [6].
3. **Maximize speed of learning.** Kan uses Kyle Vogt (co-founder of Cruise) as example: recruited from MIT to Justin.tv, went from knowing nothing about scaling systems to architecting a live video system that was the fourth-largest bandwidth consumer in North America by the time Twitch sold to Amazon [6].

## The Three Paths in Tech

Seibel outlines three primary career paths in the tech industry: founder, executive, and employee [2].

### Founder
**Pros**: Work on something you are passionate about; high responsibility inspires extreme productivity; choose who you work with; learn at an extremely fast pace [2].
**Cons**: Incredibly stressful; probably will not maximize personal earnings; large-scale success often requires a decade-plus commitment; can significantly hurt personal relationships [2].

### Executive
**Pros**: Stable income; high prestige; higher likelihood of huge impact (given most startups fail) [2].
**Cons**: Internal politics matter as much as results; success can be blocked by others; takes a long time to get significant responsibility [2].

### Employee
**Pros**: More work and fewer meetings; flexibility; more time for friends and family [2].
**Cons**: No control over what you work on; no voice in major decisions; can be boring [2].

**Experience is overvalued.** "In almost all cases, no matter what knowledge you bring to the table, you will learn most of what you need to know about your problem, your customer, and the best solution after you start your company" [2].
