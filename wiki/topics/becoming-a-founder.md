# Becoming a Founder

[[Michael Seibel]] addresses the question of how to prepare for founding a startup from two angles: advice for college students on decisions that will help or hinder their ability to start a company later, and a framework for understanding the three career paths in tech [1] [2].

## Decisions That Block Future Founders

Seibel frequently talks to people in their late 20s and 30s who want to start startups but cannot. A decision they made when they were younger is either blocking or creating a serious barrier. The most common blockers [1]:

- They did not learn how to write code
- They never met anyone in college who knew how to code
- They significantly increased their expenses early in life, did not save, and now cannot afford to begin a company
- They created roots post-college in a city that is not startup-friendly

## Advice for College Students

Seibel frames this as what those blocked founders would tell their younger selves [1]:

**Learn to code.** This does not mean majoring in Computer Science. College offers a chance to learn the basics. Being a programmer gives the ability to work and start a company in any industry. "You don't have to be the best developer, and you don't have to write code your entire life if you don't want to -- but knowing how to code gives you the proverbial golden ticket." The number one barrier to starting a tech startup is not knowing how to code [1].

**Meet people with real skills.** College puts you next to designers, programmers, salespeople, and scientists. Make friends with people who have different skills. These people could become cofounders or early employees [1].

**Have fun.** For most successful founders, college grades and accolades had almost nothing to do with their future success. "Personally, the greatest career opportunity college gave me was the chance to make friendships with really smart people" [1].

**Take responsibility.** Join the leadership of a club where you must execute on a real-world task -- organizing a conference, creating a publication, volunteer work, playing on a sports team. Learn what you are good and bad at when working with others [1].

**Be ready to hit the ground running.** Seibel disagrees with the cultural norm of using your 20s to find yourself. "Your 20s are your most valuable working years: You often have very few obligations (no family), a very low burn rate (you're used to living in a dorm), and you are still young enough to meet new people and learn new skills." Use college to get partying out of your system and spend your 20s working hard [1].

**Be your own career counselor.** Career counseling at most universities is "really really bad." Use some college time to figure out what you want to do after graduation. Having a plan, even one you change, is better than no plan [1].

**Ignore parental pressure if needed.** Many people pursue careers because of parental pressure and poor career counseling. "The number of doctors and lawyers I know who really don't love their jobs or their lives is astounding" [1].

**Be careful with student debt.** Student debt forces people away from startups and into big companies. Once in a big company, expenses spin up (car, nice apartment) and the person prices themselves out of starting a company. Expensive private schools without good financial aid are worth avoiding. The best private schools are generous with financial aid [1].

Seibel states that the prime startup years are ages 22 to 32. "Every year you are not doing a startup you are losing 10% of this prime time" [1].

## The Three Paths in Tech

Seibel outlines three primary career paths in the tech industry: founder, executive, and employee. He leaves out investor because the best path to becoming an investor starts with being successful (or failing) at one of these three [2].

### Founder

**Pros**: Work on something you are passionate about; bring something new into the world; high responsibility inspires extreme productivity; choose who you work with; learn new skills at an extremely fast pace [2].

**Cons**: Incredibly stressful (even success hurts); probably will not maximize personal earnings; significant financial, skillset, and location hurdles to get started; large-scale success often requires a decade-plus commitment; commitment level can significantly hurt personal relationships [2].

**Strategy**: The initial goal is to accumulate four prerequisites [2]:
1. Potential teammates with the technical skills to build the MVP
2. A financial plan (savings, friends/family investment, bootstrapping, or reduced spending to create a 6-12 month runway)
3. A problem that you and your potential teammates are passionate about solving
4. An idea for how to solve the problem (the least important prerequisite, but still required)

A popular mistake is trying to hustle around a missing prerequisite instead of solving the underlying issue. If the team lacks technical skills, do not work with a dev shop -- make friends with people who have those skills and convince them to join [2].

When hard barriers prevent starting immediately, Seibel recommends moving to a tech hub (preferably the Bay Area) and working for a tech company until you can save money, meet the right teammates, or discover the right problem. "It usually takes at least 10 years to build a large and impactful company. It's fine to delay your start date in order to give yourself the best chance of success" [2].

**Experience is overvalued.** "In almost all cases, no matter what knowledge you bring to the table, you will learn most of what you need to know about your problem, your customer, and the best solution after you start your company" [2]. For a deeper look at why inexperience can be an advantage, see [[First-Time Founders]].

### Executive (Senior Manager at a Large Company)

**Pros**: Stable income and benefits; high prestige (only very successful founders have more); a higher likelihood of having a huge impact (given most startups fail); does not require building a team and acquiring money [2].

**Cons**: Producing results is not necessarily how you move up -- internal politics are as important or more; success can be blocked by others inside the organization; requires picking companies that will be growing and successful for a long time; takes a long time to get significant responsibility [2].

**Two strategies** [2]:
1. **Pick a fast-growing company early.** If the company grows rapidly for years, opportunities to become an executive emerge. This is similar to a VC's task of picking winners. Example: being one of the first 100 people at Facebook and staying ten years.
2. **Move diagonally up between established companies.** Start at Google, get hired at Dropbox as a team lead, move to Yahoo to become a director, move back to Google. This requires constantly watching for better opportunities inside and outside the current company.

### Employee (Individual Contributor / Middle Manager)

**Pros**: Stable income and benefits; more work and fewer meetings; more often directly affecting the customer; flexibility in where and how much you work with a high-demand skill set; more time for friends and family [2].

**Cons**: Productivity can be blocked by bad management; often no control over what you work on; no voice in major decisions; harder to become very wealthy; can be boring; easier to be fired if skills or productivity decline [2].

**Strategy**: Similar to the executive path. Either spot and join a quickly growing company, or find a way into a well-known successful company. It is much easier to move between brand-name companies when you start at a brand-name company. The path is easier to optimize as a software developer [2].

### The Cost of Exploration

Seibel warns against overextending the discovery phase. If it takes 5-10 years to get truly good at something and 10 years are spent discovering what to get good at, "it's going to take a long time for you to feel like a highly skilled productive person." Exploration is not wrong, but its costs must be understood and planned for [2].
