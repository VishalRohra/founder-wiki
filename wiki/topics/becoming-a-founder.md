# Becoming a Founder

The decision to start a startup is one of the most consequential career choices a person can make. [[Paul Graham]] has written extensively about who should start startups, when, and what holds people back. The YC perspective is that far more people are capable of founding startups than actually do it, but it is not for everyone.

## Who Should Start a Startup

Michael Seibel recommends asking yourself three questions:

1. Do I like being the underdog?
2. Do I seek hard challenges most people shy away from?
3. Do I thrive taking personal responsibility for success or failure?

"If you answer yes three times, then maybe starting a startup is for you," Seibel writes. "I cannot promise that doing a tech startup will make you rich but I can promise that it is one of the most challenging things you can choose to do."

Graham's test is more specific. In "Be Relentlessly Resourceful," he defines the ideal founder quality: someone who is [[relentlessly resourceful|Founder Mindset]] -- not merely determined, but flexibly determined, adapting on the fly like a running back. "If you want to know whether you're the right sort of person to start a startup, ask yourself whether you're relentlessly resourceful."

In "Billionaires Build," Graham identifies the deepest motivator of the most successful founders: genuine interest in what they're building. "What keeps them working is the same thing that keeps anyone else working when they could stop if they wanted to: that there's nothing else they'd rather do." The Airbnb founders' Obama-and-McCain-themed breakfast cereal story was "the single most important factor" in YC's decision to fund them -- not because of the cereal, but because it showed they were resourceful, determined, and could work together.

## The Counterintuitive Nature of Startups

Graham's "Before the Startup" lecture lays out six counterintuitive truths:

1. **Startups are counterintuitive.** Trust your instincts about people, but not about startup strategy. "If someone seems slippery, or bogus, or a jerk, don't ignore it."

2. **You don't need to be an expert on startups.** "The way to succeed in a startup is not to be an expert on startups, but to be an expert on your users and the problem you're solving for them." Mark Zuckerberg succeeded despite being "a complete noob at startups."

3. **Gaming the system stops working.** "There is no boss to trick, only users." Faking can fool investors for one or two rounds, but the company is ultimately doomed.

4. **Startups are all-consuming.** "If your startup succeeds, it will take over your life for a long time: for several years at the very least, maybe for a decade, maybe for the rest of your working life." Larry Page at 25 started running and hasn't stopped. YC-funded founders of big successes all say: "It never gets any easier."

5. **You can't predict if you're up to it.** "For the past 9 years it was my job to predict whether people would have what it took to start successful startups... the answer is: not much." The swaggering recruits are no more likely to be tough than the quiet ones.

6. **The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of them.** (See [[Startup Ideas]].)

## Why to Not Not Start a Startup

In his essay by that title, Graham systematically examines 16 common reasons people don't start startups and identifies which are legitimate and which are bogus.

### Reasons that are mostly bogus:

**Too young.** The most successful YC founder at the time, [[Sam Altman]], was 19. Two tests for whether someone is old enough: (1) Do they still have the "kid flake reflex" -- crying "I can't do it" instead of powering through? (2) When challenged, do they submit or rebel (both childish) or calmly ask "Really? Why do you think so?" (adult response).

**Too inexperienced.** "The best way to get experience if you're 21 is to start a startup." The Kiko founders started right out of college, failed, but became "extremely formidable" within a year. "There is no way they'd have grown so much if they'd spent that year working at Microsoft, or even Google."

**Not smart enough.** "If you're smart enough to worry that you might not be smart enough to start a startup, you probably are." Silicon Valley has a cult of smartness, but in reality most startups succeed through effort, not brilliance. "If you don't think you're smart enough to start a startup doing something technically difficult, just write enterprise software."

**Know nothing about business.** "You don't need to know anything about business to start a startup. The initial focus should be the product." Revenue is not what acquirers buy startups for; they buy strategic value, meaning something people want.

**No idea.** YC weights the team far more than the idea. "We're so sure the founders are more important than the initial idea that we're going to try something new: we're going to let people apply with no idea at all." The average YC startup changes 70% of its idea in the first three months.

**No room for more startups.** This is a fallacy. "No one claims there's any limit on the number of people who can work for salary at 1000-person companies. Why should there be any limit on the number who can work for equity at 5-person companies?"

**Fear of uncertainty.** "If you start a startup, it will probably fail." But no one will blame you, and managers at big companies all said they'd prefer to hire someone who'd tried a startup and failed over someone who spent the same time at a big company.

**Parents want you to be a doctor.** "Treat their opinions like feature requests. Even if your only goal is to please them, the way to do that is not simply to give them what they ask for."

### Reasons that are legitimate:

**Family to support.** Graham is explicit: "I wouldn't advise anyone with a family to start a startup." The alternative is a consulting business gradually converted into a product business, or joining an existing startup as an early employee (roughly "1/n^2 founder, where n is your employee number").

**Not ready for commitment.** A successful startup consumes at least three or four years. If you value freedom and flexibility above all else, that's a perfectly legitimate reason to wait.

**Need for structure.** "If you're one of these people, you probably shouldn't start a startup. In fact, you probably shouldn't even go to work for one."

**Not determined enough.** Determination is "probably the single best predictor of success." But Graham notes that "most hackers probably underestimate their determination. I've seen a lot become visibly more determined as they get used to running a startup."

## The Optimal Time to Start

Graham's recommended sweet spot for startup founders is the **mid-twenties**. Here's why:

**Advantages of youth (from "A Student's Guide to Startups"):**

1. **Stamina.** "I can't think of any successful startups whose founders worked 9 to 5."
2. **Poverty.** Living cheaply is critically important. "Nearly every startup that fails, fails by running out of money." Steve Wozniak designed the Apple II to use a TV as a monitor because he couldn't afford a real display. As Wozniak put it: "All the best things that I did at Apple came from (a) not having money and (b) not having done it before, ever."
3. **Rootlessness.** No house, no serious relationships, ability to move to a startup hub.
4. **Colleagues.** School concentrates potential cofounders. "If your nucleus spits out a neutron, there's a good chance it will hit another nucleus." By 30, people are tied down by jobs and families.
5. **Ignorance.** Paradoxically valuable. "If they'd known how hard it would be, they would have been too intimidated to start." Ignorance also leads to reinvention that can be better than what preceded it.

**Disadvantage of being too young:**

The biggest disadvantage is building things that look like class projects -- solving fake problems with implementation-heavy solutions, not understanding that "the market doesn't give a shit how hard you worked." Class projects are measured by distance from starting point. Real startups are measured by distance remaining to what users need.

The critical thing 24-year-olds have that 20-year-olds lack is understanding the relationship between money and work. "To the average undergrad the idea of getting rich translates into buying Ferraris. To someone who has learned from experience about the relationship between money and work, it translates to something way more important: it means you get to opt out of the brutal equation that governs the lives of 99.9% of people."

**Graham's evolved position on college founders:** YC initially encouraged starting in college but now tells students to wait until graduation. The rationale: starting in college provides a built-in escape hatch (you're still "a student"), which weakens commitment. The exception is founders who can't be talked out of it. "Our official policy now is only to fund undergrads we can't talk out of it."

## The Three Career Paths in Tech

[[Michael Seibel]] lays out three distinct paths: founder, executive, and employee. He attaches no value judgments: "In my ten years in The Bay Area I've seen friends lead successful and fulfilled lives following all three."

**Founder prerequisites (in order of importance):** (1) Potential teammates with technical skills to build an MVP, (2) a financial plan for 6-12 months, (3) a problem you are passionate about solving, (4) an idea for how to solve it. "A popular mistake is trying to hustle *around* a missing prerequisite instead of *solving the underlying issue*." If the team lacks technical skills, "don't work with a dev shop. Make friends with people who do have these skills. Convince them to join you." Experience is "over-valued (not completely unimportant -- but massively over-valued)."

**Executive path:** Two strategies exist. (1) Pick a rapidly growing company early and ride the growth. "If you were one of the first 100 people at Facebook and you stayed there for ten years, you would have many opportunities to become an executive." (2) Move diagonally up between brand-name companies until reaching an executive role.

**Employee path:** Stable income, more hands-on work, flexibility with high-demand skills. Strategy mirrors the executive path -- join a growing company or a well-known one. "It's much easier to go between brand name companies when you start with a brand name company."

Seibel warns against spending a decade in self-discovery: "if it takes 5-10 years to truly get good at something and you spend 10 years discovering what you want to get good at... it's going to take a long time for you to feel like a highly skilled productive person."

## Seibel's Letter to College Students

Seibel identifies specific decisions made in college that later create barriers to starting a startup: not learning to code, never meeting anyone who can code, increasing expenses early and becoming unable to afford starting a company, and creating roots in a non-startup-friendly city.

Key advice:
- **Learn to code.** "The number one barrier to starting a tech startup is not knowing how to code." Being a programmer is "the proverbial golden ticket to the *Willy Wonka* factory of the technology industry."
- **Meet people with real skills.** "It's very likely that these people could become your cofounders or early employees one day."
- **Have fun.** "For most successful founders, college grades and accolades had almost nothing to do with their future success."
- **Take responsibility.** Join leadership roles in clubs executing real-world tasks.
- **Hit the ground running.** "Your 20s are your most valuable working years" -- few obligations, low burn rate, still able to meet new people and learn new skills.
- **Be your own career counselor.** "Career counseling at most universities is really really bad."
- **Ignore your parents if needed.** "The number of doctors and lawyers I know who really don't love their jobs or their lives is astounding."
- **Be careful with student debt.** "Student debt is forcing people away from startups and into big companies."

Seibel's urgency: "if you are between the ages of 22 and 32 you are in your best startup years. Every year you are not doing a startup you are losing 10% of this prime time."

## From Hackathon to Company: Athelas

[[Tanay Tandon]] describes how [[Athelas]] (YC S16) evolved from a proof-of-concept at YC Hacks 2014 into a company shipping $250 medical devices. The first prototype: a rubber piece and spherical magnifier on a smartphone camera, with a toilet paper roll holding the blood sample, running computer vision for malaria cell counts. During YC (full-time), the team validated white blood cell counts across 350 patients against gold-standard Beckman Counters and submitted to the FDA for 510k approval.

## Finding Something Worth Building: Blake Scholl

[[Blake Scholl]] ([[Boom Supersonic]], YC W16) describes making a list of "startup ideas in descending order of how awesome it would be if they worked and completely leaving aside everything else." His advice: "Find something in the world that you desperately want to exist." The counterintuitive insight: "the bigger the idea actually, the easier it can be because it motivates you. It motivates the people around you. You can attract better people." But: "don't start a startup just to start a startup. I did that my first time and it was a horrible idea."

## The Half-Hearted Effort Problem

[[Paul Graham]] observes in [[Startup Mistakes]] that the most common failure is not spectacular flameouts but projects started on the side that never go anywhere. "Most founders of failed startups don't quit their day jobs, and most founders of successful ones do." The number who could have succeeded with full commitment is "probably an order of magnitude larger than the number who do make it."

## Working at a Startup: Practical Advice

[[Ryan Choi]] (YC) advises job seekers considering startups to ask about **runway** and **burn rate**. Many YC startups are well-funded, "some even with $10M+ raised." Founders should be transparent about these numbers.

For resumes, Choi recommends covering three things for each position: **what** you worked on, **how** you did it (technologies, team size, cross-functional work), and **impact** (growth numbers, cost savings, sales increase). On cold outreach: "suggest ways you'd be excited to contribute; include these suggestions on your resume, cover letter or in an intro email." At Twitter, Choi hired people who "stood out because they showed initiative: suggesting strong product ideas/improvements, outlining plans for go-to-market, or even writing sample code." When interviewing at Lyft, he "reversed engineered the API and built a small SDK" -- it took a couple nights with WireShark, but landed him the job.

## What to Do in College

Graham's ultimate advice for students: **"Just learn."**

Starting a startup in your early 20s is "like a brutally fast depth-first search. Most people should still be searching breadth-first at 20." The things you can do in your early 20s -- plunging deeply into projects on a whim, traveling cheaply -- become impossible once a startup consumes your life.

Specific guidance:

- **Don't study entrepreneurship.** "The way to become Larry Page was to become an expert on search. And the way to become an expert on search was to be driven by genuine curiosity." Taking classes on convertible notes or employee agreements is not just unnecessary but a warning sign of "playing house."
- **Learn powerful things.** "If you want to start a startup after college, what you should do in college is learn powerful things." Follow genuine intellectual curiosity.
- **Build things.** Look for peers who are "incurable builders" -- people who keep starting projects and finish at least some of them. "Above all else, above academic credentials and even the idea you apply with, we look for people who build things."
- **Find potential cofounders.** Work on projects with classmates. "Many students feel they should wait and get a little more experience before they start a company... Most students don't realize how rich they are in the scarcest ingredient in startups, co-founders."
- **Learn coding.** Even non-technical founders should understand how software works.
- **Develop the habit of thinking about users.** "The concept of users is missing from most college programming classes."

The YC guide "Startups for Students" (by Tamanna Khemani and Kat Mañalac) reinforces these themes and adds: roughly 90% of startups fail, but over 50% of YC companies older than five years are still alive. Only 7.9% of YC's top companies were founded by solo founders. Only 3 companies on YC's Top Companies list (Flexport, SnapDocs, UpKeep) were founded by a non-technical founder.

## Working at a Startup

For those who decide founding isn't right for them, working at an early-stage startup is the next best thing. Graham argues it's "a lot like being a founder" -- roughly "1/n^2 founder, where n is your employee number." The Startups for Students guide recommends:

- Use YC's [Work at a Startup](https://www.workatastartup.com/jobs) to apply to multiple companies with one resume.
- For internships, prefer post-Series-A companies (more structure, mentorship). For self-directed learners, seed-stage companies work.
- Ask founders: What will I work on? Will I see how the business is doing? Will I interact with customers?
- Ask about runway and burn rate -- this reveals both the startup's health and the founders' transparency.

Graham cautions against working at companies that are too comfortable. Google in 2006 was "a black hole" for startup founders: "zero startups come out of there. People seem to like working at Google too much to leave." A pleasant employer can represent a local maximum, requiring activation energy to escape.
