# Recruiting Early Team Members

How do you get a co-founder, a first employee, or an early partner to join your startup? [[Dalton Caldwell]] argues that the answer begins with self-conviction. Before you can convince anyone else, you must first convince yourself [1].

## Convince Yourself First

"If you're not fully committed or if you have grave doubts that your idea is any good or it's even worth trying, how can you ever convince someone else?" [1]. People can tell when a founder does not believe in what they are doing. Genuine conviction creates a magnetism that flows outward to co-founders, employees, customers, and partners alike [1].

The signal that you are convinced: "Do you ever know that feeling where an idea enters your head and you can't sleep at night and you can't think about anything else but it? And it starts to pervade your thoughts? That's a good sign" [1]. Conversely, if you are immediately cynical about the idea, not excited, or able to set it aside easily, that is a sign you are not convinced [1].

## Why Conviction Matters: Resilience Through Setbacks

Caldwell explains the mechanism through which self-conviction translates to startup survival. He has consistently observed two patterns at YC [1]:

**Teams that secretly don't believe** in what they are doing give up when they hit rough patches -- and every startup hits rough patches [1].

**Teams that are deeply convicted** overcome almost every kind of setback: running out of money, having to completely pivot and change the idea, losing team members. "If there's something deeply held in the mind of the startup founders about why they're doing what they're doing, they can keep going" [1].

This connects directly to [[Founder Mindset]] and [[Paul Graham]]'s concept of determination as the most important founder quality.

## Work on What Excites You

Caldwell makes a specific argument about idea selection that runs counter to conventional wisdom [1]. He encourages founders to work on the thing they are most excited about, even at the expense of:

- An idea that seems easier to raise money for
- An idea that seems more commercially viable
- An idea that impresses other people

The reasoning: "It would appear that it's equally hard to succeed in a startup that you don't care about and one that you do. And it's almost equally hard to succeed in a startup that's a very ambitious, audacious idea as it is to do something super incremental that's not that exciting" [1].

If the difficulty is roughly equal, always choose the more ambitious or exciting path. Caldwell uses a travel metaphor: "If you have to travel a thousand miles to go somewhere you want to visit versus travel a thousand miles to somewhere you're ambivalent about -- always go to the place you're much more excited about" [1]. Your excitement and enthusiasm increase the probability of arrival [1].

## Can Conviction Be Built Over Time?

Caldwell acknowledges that not everyone starts with a burning obsession. Conviction can grow through iteration [1].

The process: work on something as a side project. Pay attention to whether it feels like fun or like work. "If it feels like not work to work on your startup or your side project or what have you, and it's just something you want to do because it's entertaining or enjoyable to you, that's a good sign" [1].

External validation creates a positive feedback loop. When you put something into the world and people recognize and appreciate it, the positive response makes you more excited about spending time on it. "You can definitely get data points from the external world that cause you to take your side project or your idea more seriously" [1].

This connects to [[Dalton Caldwell]]'s broader advice on [[Creating Luck]] through building things and sharing them with people.
