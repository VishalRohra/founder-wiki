# Co-Founders

The co-founder relationship is one of the most consequential decisions in a startup's life and the number one reason startups fail at YC [5]. Of YC's [top companies](https://www.ycombinator.com/topcompanies), only 7.9% were founded by solo founders [3]. [[Paul Graham]] notes that exploitation of co-founders is often the first sign of a founder who will fail [4]. [[Harj Taggar]] frames the importance in three dimensions: co-founders increase the volume and quality of work, provide irreplaceable emotional support, and match the pattern of virtually every successful startup in history [5].

Co-founder failure is a consensus concern across the YC canon. [[Michael Seibel]], [[Harj Taggar]], [[Paul Graham]], [[Dalton Caldwell]], and multiple other YC partners independently cite co-founder breakups as the single most common cause of startup death, reinforced across 10+ sources.

## Why Co-Founders Matter

Taggar identifies three core reasons [5]:

1. **Productivity.** You can get more done and better work done with someone who has complementary skills and someone you enjoy discussing ideas with [5].
2. **Moral support.** Startups are intensely taxing. The best co-founder relationships balance each other out: when one founder is despondent, the other keeps them going. "Anyone who's working for you, you're the boss, and they're not as invested in the success of the startup as you are. And you can't really be as open or honest with them about the company as you can be with a peer" [5].
3. **Pattern matching to success.** Apple, Facebook, Google, and Microsoft all had co-founders. People associate these companies with a single person (Jobs, Zuckerberg, Gates), but at the earliest stages, all of them had co-founders they relied on [5].

## Finding the Right Co-Founder

### What to Look For

Taggar argues the single most important thing to know about a potential co-founder is how they handle stress [5]. Second: how well they help you handle stress. "Startups are a very incredibly stressful, intense experience. A great co-founder should be able to support you through those" [5].

The best co-founders are people you have worked with under stressful conditions or close friends whose character you know well [5]. Skills matter but should not be overly fixated on -- "more important than anything is just working with someone that you like working with and you trust" [5]. Care more about trajectory than current skill set [5].

Co-founders must share the same high-level goals. If one wants a high-pressure, fast-growing, VC-backed company and the other wants a lifestyle business, friction is inevitable [5].

### How Co-Founders Meet

Data from YC's top companies shows school and work are the dominant channels [3]. Google, Yahoo, and Microsoft were all founded by people who met in school [3]. College is described as "an ideal place to find a co-founder" [3].

Taggar recommends looking for people to do projects with before committing to a company [5]. Projects are lower commitment than "let's start a company," so more people say yes. Working on projects reveals what kinds of personalities and skills complement yours. "Projects that you work on in your spare time often tend to be some of the greatest sources for actually finding great startup ideas" -- Facebook started as a project [5].

### The Co-Founder Search Algorithm

For founders ready to start now, Taggar provides a concrete algorithm [5]:
1. Make a list of the people you know who would be great co-founders
2. Ask each one to coffee and propose starting a company together
3. If they say no, ask: "If you were starting a company, who would be your top 3-4 co-founders?"
4. Ask for introductions to those people
5. Repeat the process, growing the tree of potential co-founders

A common blocker: founders assume everyone they know is unavailable. "I know lots of people who would be a great potential co-founder, but they just got a job at Google and they will never leave." Taggar's advice: never assume who is available. Always make the ask [5].

## Why You Need a Technical Co-Founder

Caldwell and Seibel are direct: "Based on the thousands of companies YC has funded over the years, companies lacking a technical co-founder underperform" [8]. The argument that no-code tools, dev shops, or part-time consultants can substitute "is an inspiring story that gets clicks on LinkedIn" but does not match reality [8].

The analogy Seibel uses: "I'm going to build a rocket to the moon, but no one here knows anything about physics" [8].

Concrete reasons:

1. **Speed of iteration.** Companies like DoorDash and Airbnb "lived and died by their speed of shipping software." Using white-label software or offshore dev shops, "there's a zero percent chance those companies would have worked" [8].
2. **Engineering talent compounds.** When business people start by assessing engineers, they naturally hire worse engineers. A weak engineering foundation compounds badly over time [8].
3. **Better competitive position.** "They could raise more money. They could have nicer offices. It didn't matter. Having better engineering is the gift that gives you more shots every day" [8].

### How to Find a Technical Co-Founder

Caldwell and Seibel offer specific advice [8]:

1. **Stop disqualifying people.** Many non-technical founders want a CTO with ten years of experience managing teams of 50-100. "You're twenty-three years old with no years of experience. Why do you need that?" [8]
2. **Take a step sideways.** If you don't know the right people, go work at a startup [8].
3. **Pitch adventure, not tasks.** "Adventure isn't 'here's my idea for a social network for dogs, will you build my website?' It's 'let's build a company together'" [8].

## Do Technical Founders Need a Business Co-Founder?

The short answer: not necessarily [9]. The important question is not whether a co-founder is "technical" or "business" but whether someone on the team has the appetite to handle non-coding tasks [9].

"It's much easier for a technical person to do things that just require general intelligence than it is for a business person to learn how to code" [9]. Evidence from the most valuable companies: Google (two technical founders), Nvidia (all electrical engineers), Microsoft (two technical founders), Stripe (two technical founders) [9].

When a business co-founder genuinely helps: when selling into an industry where domain expertise and customer language matter [9]. But this is a benefit, not a requirement [9].

## Co-Founder Conflict and Relationship Debt

Seibel identifies co-founder conflict as the third biggest killer of post-seed startups [7]. He uses the concept of "relationship debt" -- analogous to technical debt: "The more and more relationship debt you get, just like technical debt, the harder and harder it is to execute. And then at some point your relationship with the co-founder falls over" [7].

### Why Co-Founder Relationships Break Down

Taggar identifies three primary causes [5]:

1. **Loss of mutual respect.** The most common cause. Co-founders often split responsibilities, and problems creep in when one feels the other is not performing. "If that respect breaks down and one co-founder feels they could do the other co-founder's job better than them, it's very hard to recover" [5].
2. **Multiple people wanting to be CEO.** The CEO title carries symbolic weight as the person trusted to make final calls. When multiple people want that role, it signals fundamental distrust [5].
3. **Mismatched work ethic expectations.** If one co-founder wants to work constantly while the other wants work-life balance, the relationship becomes strained under pressure [5].

Caldwell and Seibel expand on common mistakes in a dedicated episode [6]: equity fights, the "unequal effort" perception, failure to have hard conversations, and bringing on co-founders out of convenience rather than genuine fit.

### Preventing Breakups

**Level 3 conversations.** Set aside dedicated time, outside of any active crisis, to talk about how each co-founder feels. Pay down relationship debt regularly [7].

**Explicit roles and responsibilities.** Each person knows their domain and can be trusted to execute within it [7].

**Founder Syncs.** Recurring weekly 1-on-1 meetings between co-founders. Amy Buechler created this concept and wrote a [detailed guide](https://www.notion.so/The-Founder-Sync-54f855a12de749fa9e74e928ca7fa5a5) on running them [2].

**Don't avoid disagreements.** Taggar's primary advice: anytime co-founders disagree, have the conversation immediately. Procrastinating on hard conversations creates "death by a thousand cuts" [5].

## Protecting Technical Co-Founders

Caldwell and Seibel identify a pattern they see repeatedly in YC applications: "It's crystal clear what's going on" -- a technical person is being exploited [10].

**Equity**: Close to equal equity is recommended. The anti-pattern: a non-technical business founder has 90% and the technical person has 10% "with no good reason other than sharper elbows" [10]. Seibel's reasoning: "The vast majority of the journey is ahead of you, not behind you" [10].

**Decision-Making**: If all decisions are made in meetings the technical person is not invited to, and they are "basically used as a robot to write code" -- that is exploitation [10].

**The College Student Pattern**: The MBA who recruits a CS student to "just build the website" -- often not even offering equity -- is one of the most common exploitation patterns [10].

## Equity and CEO Decisions

Taggar recommends defaulting to a 50/50 equity split [5]. At the earliest stage, both founders will make equal contributions, and "all the work is years and years ahead of you." See [[Equity Split]] for the full treatment.

On the CEO title: practically, it does not matter at the two-person stage. The CEO title matters externally, particularly for investors who want to see a clear leader and tiebreaker [5]. A red flag: when both co-founders intensely want to be CEO. "If you think it's really important to you to be the CEO, and you both feel that way, you're not good co-founders for each other" [5].

## 10 Questions to Discuss with a Potential Co-Founder

YC compiled the 10 most important questions, derived from analyzing why founding teams break up [2]:

1. Why do you want to do this startup? What are your personal goals?
2. Roles, titles, and division of responsibilities. Who will be CEO?
3. How will you split equity?
4. Where will the company be based?
5. What idea will you work on? Are you willing to pivot?
6. What needs to happen for each person to go full-time?
7. Personal financial situation.
8. Working schedule.
9. Team building philosophy.
10. Conflict resolution. What happens if you decide not to work together anymore?

A [Google Doc template](https://yc.link/10-questions-cf) is available. The recommended process: each founder copies the document, writes answers independently, then shares and discusses [2].

### Checklist Before Committing

**Before deciding to work together** [2]:
- Meet in person and do a trial project
- Do reference calls on each other (transparently)
- Have your significant other meet your potential co-founder

**After deciding to work together** [2]:
- Create a system to prioritize tasks
- Set up recurring weekly Founder Syncs

## YC's Co-Founder Matching Platform

Y Combinator runs a [co-founder matching platform](https://www.ycombinator.com/cofounder-matching) where founders describe themselves and what they are looking for [1]. The platform has produced companies accepted into YC, including Sequin (YC S21, raised $5.7M) and Kiwi Biosciences (YC S21, raised $1.5M) [1].

Taggar advises that the formula that works well: find people who you have things in common with, who are the kinds of people you would have eventually met anyway. Teams where co-founders studied similar fields, are roughly the same age, and have overlapping interests work best [5].

## The Gusto Example: Long-Lasting Co-Founder Relationships

Josh Reeves, co-founder and CEO of Gusto, describes how all three Gusto co-founders have remained actively involved for 13+ years [16]. The ingredients: a shared love of technology, a desire to tackle meaningful problems, deriving joy from helping others, and "productive discontent" -- being simultaneously proud of progress and eager for the next step [16].

## Solo Founders

While YC does fund solo founders, the data is not in their favor. Only 7.9% of YC top companies were solo-founded [3]. Taggar's advice: in 90% of cases, spend all efforts trying to find a great co-founder before starting [5].

The narrow exception: if you have a very specific idea with high conviction based on domain experience AND you are an engineer who can build the first version yourself, you can start solo while searching [5]. Drew Houston of Dropbox is the canonical example [5].

## Why You Shouldn't Copy Your Heroes

Caldwell and Seibel caution against taking current advice from successful founders at face value [15]. Sam Altman's first startup was Loopt -- not a moonshot lab. Elon Musk started with Zip2 and PayPal. Peter Thiel went to Stanford and law school. "Most mere mortals need to do step one" [15].
