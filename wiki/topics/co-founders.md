# Co-Founders

The co-founder relationship is one of the most consequential decisions in a startup's life. Of YC's [top companies](https://www.ycombinator.com/topcompanies), only 7.9% were founded by solo founders [3]. [[Paul Graham]] notes that exploitation of co-founders is often the first sign of a founder who will fail — the co-founder relationship is the foundation of the company [4].

## Why Co-Founders Matter

A co-founder provides three things: task distribution, emotional support, and complementary expertise [3]. One co-founder might be a talented programmer while the other excels at sales. Beyond functional roles, co-founders help bear the emotional weight of building a startup. Building a company from scratch is mentally and emotionally taxing, and it is usually too much for one person to manage [3].

## How Co-Founders Meet

Data from YC's top companies shows the most common ways founders connected [3]:

- School and work are the dominant channels
- Working on projects or extracurricular activities together is one of the best starting points
- College is described as "an ideal place to find a co-founder" [3]

Among women founders specifically, the distribution of how co-founders met shows similar patterns, though the data set (78 entries for women founders vs. 68 for top companies) reveals some differences in network composition [3].

## YC's Co-Founder Matching Platform

Y Combinator runs a [co-founder matching platform](https://www.ycombinator.com/cofounder-matching) where founders describe themselves and what they are looking for, and the system shows profiles that most closely match [1]. The platform has produced companies that were accepted into YC and raised significant funding.

### Sequin (YC S21)

Vrinda Gupta had left her job after launching credit cards like the Chase Sapphire Reserve at Visa. She spent a year as a solo founder building a product to help women build credit, raising a pre-seed and building an MVP. She needed a technical co-founder who was mission-driven and had fintech expertise [1].

She matched with Mark Thomas, who had ten years of engineering experience at PayPal, cared about gender equity, and had spent six years as CTO of family-oriented startups. Gupta recalls: "We met the day after we matched, and the day after, and then the day after that" [1]. They went through YC's Summer 2021 batch and raised $5.7M [1].

### Kiwi Biosciences (YC S21)

Anjie Liu started Kiwi Biosciences to solve her own problems with irritable bowel syndrome. She had a founding scientist for R&D but wanted a co-founder to help build a consumer brand and commercialize the product [1].

She found David Hachuel on the co-founder matching platform. Hachuel had previously sold a startup in the same space. They took a structured approach: both founders answered all 50 questions from [First Round's co-founder questionnaire](https://proof-assets.s3.amazonaws.com/firstround/50%20Questions%20for%20Co-Founders.pdf) and found deep alignment on "all the important things" — conflict resolution, vision for culture, and more [1]. They worked remotely for four months, met in person after getting into YC, and raised $1.5M [1].

## 10 Questions to Discuss with a Potential Co-Founder

YC compiled a list of the 10 most important questions to discuss before committing to work together, derived from talking to teams who met on co-founder matching and from analyzing the most common reasons founding teams break up [2]. The best time to go through these is after getting to know someone, finding an idea both are interested in, and before committing.

YC emphasizes that no questionnaire substitutes for spending a lot of time together in person. It usually takes several months to know if you are compatible [2].

A [Google Doc template](https://yc.link/10-questions-cf) is available. The recommended process: each founder copies the document, writes answers independently, then shares and discusses [2].

### The 10 Key Questions

1. **Why do you want to do this startup? What are your personal goals, financial and non-financial?** [2]
2. **Roles, titles, and division of responsibilities. Who will be CEO?** Roles change over time, but plan the first 6-12 months and decide on CEO [2].
3. **How will you split equity?** Resolve early. YC provides guidance in the [co-founder matching manual](https://yc.link/10-questions-cf-equity) [2].
4. **Where will the company be based? In-person or remote?** [2]
5. **What idea will you work on? Are you willing to pivot?** [2]
6. **What needs to happen for each person to go full-time?** Examples: raise $X from investors, validate with a paying customer, enjoy working together for two months [2].
7. **Personal financial situation.** Willingness to work without salary, how long savings last, minimum salary needed, whether either will invest money [2].
8. **Working schedule.** Days, hours, sustainability, non-work priorities [2].
9. **Team building philosophy.** In-person vs. remote, cultural values, approach to hiring and management [2].
10. **Conflict resolution. What happens if you decide not to work together anymore?** [2]

### Getting to Know Each Other

Beyond the 10 key questions, YC recommends additional questions including: how friends describe your strengths and weaknesses, what you are proud of accomplishing, free-time interests (successful co-founders consistently have overlapping outside-of-work interests), companies and founders you admire, and long-term life goals [2].

On stress responses, psychologist and founder coach Amy Buechler wrote about how attachment styles (anxious vs. avoidant) affect co-founder relationships [2].

### Checklist Before Committing

**Before deciding to work together** [2]:
- Meet in person and do a trial project
- Do reference calls on each other (transparently — agree on timing and swap suggested references)
- Have your significant other meet your potential co-founder

**After deciding to work together** [2]:
- Create a system to prioritize tasks and decide what to work on
- Set up a recurring weekly 1-on-1 ("Founder Syncs," a concept created by Amy Buechler) to discuss how the working relationship is going. Buechler wrote a [detailed guide](https://www.notion.so/The-Founder-Sync-54f855a12de749fa9e74e928ca7fa5a5) on running them [2]

## Solo Founders

While YC does fund solo founders, the data is not in their favor. Only 7.9% of YC top companies were solo-founded [3]. Roughly 10% of companies in each YC batch have a non-technical founder, but among YC's top companies, only three were founded by non-technical founders: Flexport, Snapdocs, and UpKeep [3].
