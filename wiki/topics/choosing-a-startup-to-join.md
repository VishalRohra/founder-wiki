# Choosing a Startup to Join

Choosing which startup to join is an investment decision. Both [[Harj Taggar]] and [[Sam Altman]] frame it this way: an early employee takes significantly less cash compensation than they could earn at a large company, compensated with equity [1] [2]. The same principles that guide startup investing apply to choosing where to work.

## First: Be Sure You Want to Work at a Startup

Taggar begins with a reality check. Working at a startup is not for everyone, and it is fine if it is not your thing [1].

Paul Buchheit provides a simple test: "If you're happy working where you are and you don't have a strong desire to do anything else, you're probably going to get paid less and have to work more if you leave for a startup" [1].

## Take Roles You Are Unqualified For

Altman argues this is "the number one secret to having a really great career" because it produces the fastest rate of personal growth [2]. Careers have a compound-interest dynamic: "the work you do now, the learning you do now, the improvement you make early in your career gets to pay off for all the rest" [2].

Startups are where this opportunity exists: "You can get jobs you are widely unqualified for" [2]. When someone takes a role they are unqualified for, "it either goes way worse or way better than expectations. And a lot of the times it goes way better" [2].

## Evaluation Criteria

### Users Who Love the Product

The single most important piece of diligence according to both Taggar and Altman: a core group of early users who are fanatical about the product [1] [2]. Altman's version: "Is this product so good that people are telling other people they've got to use it?" [2]. A small number of users who love the product matters much more than a large number who kind of like it. Airbnb's early users were few but fanatical [1].

Taggar advises asking to talk to some users directly. If the depth of their passion is high, even in a small group, that is a strong signal [1].

### Growth Trajectory Over Absolute Numbers

The most important factor is not the absolute numbers but the growth trajectory. "A startup that has a million dollars in monthly revenue but it's had a million dollars in monthly revenue for the last twelve months in a row -- that's less exciting than a startup that has ten thousand in monthly revenue but the previous month it only had five" [1].

Altman extends this: trust exponential growth even when it is hard to intuit. "I've been investing in startups for eight or ten years now. I still have not managed to get good at intuiting exponential growth in my head. So I have to do it in a spreadsheet every time" [2].

### Growth Rate of the Market, Not Its Size

Both speakers warn against evaluating the size of the market. This is the most common mistake investors make, and it is how they miss the really big ones [1] [2].

Altman uses the Uber deck as an example: their initial TAM was stated as approximately $2 billion. "But it turns out that the market of people that want to move around cities easily grows quickly when you have a better product" [2].

### The Founders and Team

Only join a startup where you are genuinely impressed by the founders and their early hires [1]. Taggar specifically warns against evaluating founders by traditional credentials. Instead, evaluate founders relative to their starting point. A 19-year-old from Milwaukee who managed to raise a seed round and build a company "might be more impressive than an engineer who worked at Google for fifteen years and is in sort of the same place" [1].

Altman describes exceptional founders as "so good that they end up bending the world to their will. And they can do this without a lot of business experience" [2].

### Real Trends vs. Fake Trends

Altman's test: "Real trends are ones where a new technology platform comes along and the early adopters use it obsessively and tell their friends how much they love it. A fake trend is one where people may buy the product, but don't use it" [2]. The iPhone was a real trend: early owners "used it for hours every day." VR (as of 2018) was a fake trend [2].

### Ideas That Sound Bad But Are Good

Most ideas that sound bad are bad. But a class of ideas that sound bad but are good is "where YC has made most of its money" [2]. A startup only needs one "yes" out of many possible investors; a big company needs every person in the chain to say yes [2].

## Employee Equity

Altman states bluntly: "You probably aren't getting enough" [2]. Most startups are not generous enough with employee equity. "The amount of value that you create as an early employee versus a founder is not the one-hundred-x difference that you usually see reflected on a cap table" [2].

## Choosing Who Gives You Advice

"Most people will bring whatever biases they have about what a career should look like or what a safe choice is" [2]. Find a small group of people who are "open-minded and intellectually rigorous thinkers" [2].
