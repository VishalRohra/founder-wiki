# Runway Management

Running low on runway is one of the most dangerous situations a startup can face, not only because of the financial reality but because of its psychological effects on founders. Dalton Caldwell addresses this directly: "The hardest part of dealing with a low runway situation is managing your own psychology. You have to simultaneously manage your own anxiety to not be overly negative about your prospects, but also not be irrationally positive" [1].

This article covers how to assess runway, the psychology of low-runway situations, counterproductive thinking patterns, practical cost reduction, and the decision to shut down.

## Prerequisites: Know Where You Stand

Before anything else, founders must understand exactly how much cash and runway they have. Caldwell directs founders to two essential readings by [[Paul Graham]]: [The Fatal Pinch](http://paulgraham.com/pinch.html) and [Default Alive or Default Dead](http://paulgraham.com/aord.html) [1].

The core framework: if you are Default Dead (your current trajectory leads to running out of money before reaching profitability), it is your responsibility as a founder to immediately take actions to become Default Alive [1]. The mechanisms are straightforward: "Either you need to grow revenue more quickly, cut costs, or both" [1].

## Counterproductive Ideas

Caldwell identifies seven common thought patterns that cause founders to rationalize inaction when they are Default Dead [1]:

1. **"The Fatal Pinch does not apply to me"** - Believing your company is a special exception
2. **"Investors will continue to fund my company if I run out of money"** - Assuming future fundraising is guaranteed
3. **"If I fail at fundraising I can just sell the company"** - Treating acquisition as a reliable fallback
4. **"My conversations with potential acquirers or investors are very far along and likely to happen"** - Confusing activity with commitment
5. **"Acquirers won't buy us if I cut costs"** - Believing a smaller team reduces acquisition attractiveness
6. **"My employee morale will plummet if I cut costs"** - Prioritizing comfort over survival
7. **"New investors won't fund us if I cut costs"** - Believing cost-cutting signals weakness to investors

Caldwell warns: "Don't let these ideas be the justification(s) for why you choose to remain Default Dead" [1].

## Understanding Negotiating Leverage

Caldwell presents a graph showing how leverage deteriorates over time for a company with declining runway [1]:

Three lessons from this graph [1]:

1. **Delaying action is a bad strategy.** Make changes to become Default Alive now, not later.
2. **Game theory works against you.** From a game theory perspective, an investor or acquirer benefits by stalling and dragging you along until you have no leverage. "An opportunistic acquirer or investor is unlikely to say 'no' outright, and will keep their options open as you become increasingly desperate."
3. **Without corrective action, things get worse.** "If things look bleak now and you take no corrective action, it is overwhelmingly likely the situation will get worse, not better."

## Reducing Burn

The least painful option is to dramatically increase revenue immediately [1]. When that is not possible, cost cuts must follow a clear priority.

### What to Cut First

1. **PR, marketing expenses, perks, and parties** - The easiest to cut. "Don't blow your money on this stuff" [1]
2. **Payroll** - The most likely source of high burn. As noted in [[Paul Graham]]'s Fatal Pinch, "over-hiring is usually the root cause of high burn" [1]
3. **Real estate/lease costs** - Binding agreements and very difficult to exit. "Real estate obligations are a common cause of death for later stage companies" [1]

### Handling Layoffs

If staff reductions are necessary [1]:
- Treat former employees well
- Be transparent with remaining employees
- "You should always treat your staff as you would want to be treated"

## The Point of No Return

When a company has less than three months of cash, founders must face the possibility of shutdown head-on [1]. Caldwell identifies less than two months as "the point of no return" in many cases [1].

At this stage, the required actions are [1]:
- Immediately lay off employees and provide severance
- Pay down obligations
- Use remaining cash for shutdown costs

The consequences of not acting: "If you don't do this and instead end up with zero cash and outstanding payroll, tax or other obligations, things will get Very Bad" [1].

### Shutdown Principles

Caldwell lists specific considerations for an orderly shutdown [1]:

- **Shut down when you hit the point of no return.** Do not drag things out.
- **Do not become insolvent.** Pay debts, especially tax and payroll obligations.
- **Consult lawyers.** In messy scenarios, founders can face personal liability.
- **Avoid no-upside situations.** "Don't drag things out and end up in a no upside situation: no upside for you, for your employees, for your investors, or for your customers."
- **Behave with integrity.** "Even if things go poorly, behave in a way you would be proud of."

### On Reputation and Defeat

Caldwell acknowledges the psychological difficulty: "Often the toughest thing for a founder that has made it this far is to 'admit defeat'" [1]. His advice on reputation: "it's just as important to handle situations well when things go poorly as it is when they are going great" [1].

## Two Things to Remember

Caldwell closes with two principles that override everything else [1]:

1. **Don't lie to yourself**
2. **Act quickly and decisively**
