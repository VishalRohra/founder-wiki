# Sales for Founders

Founders must learn to sell before hiring a sales team. [[Gustaf Alstromer]], YC Group Partner and former Head of Growth at Airbnb, argues that sales is a skill founders can and must acquire because it is "effectively different sides of the same coin" from talking to customers [1]. [[Nicolas Dessaigne]], former CEO of Algolia, reinforces this: "If you, as the founder, cannot sell your product, nobody else can" [2].

## Why Founders Must Do Sales Themselves

Alstromer provides four reasons founders should not delegate sales early [1]:

1. **You need to know your customer.** The same reasons founders cannot understand what to build -- not understanding the problem -- are the same reasons they cannot sell. Sales teaches customer knowledge [1].
2. **Sales gives full control of your destiny.** Just as engineering cannot be outsourced, sales must be part of the founders' DNA [1].
3. **You cannot tell if the product is bad unless you've tried to sell it.** Hiring a salesperson before you know how to sell means you cannot evaluate their performance or distinguish bad selling from a bad product [1].
4. **If you know the problem, you are an expert.** "You are uniquely qualified to understand them. So you'll see -- it actually will be way easier than you think" [1].

Alstromer cites Tony Xu of [[DoorDash: Creating a Market from Scratch|DoorDash]], Matilda of Front, Tracy Young of [[PlanGrid: Building Software for an Industry That Had Never Used It|PlanGrid]], and Steve Jobs as founders who learned sales and became great at it [1].

Dessaigne adds that at Algolia, the founders did not have a sales deck until $10 million ARR. Even their account executives sold through live demonstrations rather than slides [2]. PostHog's CTO serves as the company's sales leader -- "they see sales as a nurturing problem, which is an awesome approach for a dev tool" [2].

## The Sales Funnel

Alstromer translates sales jargon into "founder speak" [1]:

| Founder Speak | Sales Speak |
|---|---|
| Make a list of customers to reach out to | Prospecting / lead generation |
| Send them an email or LinkedIn message | Outreach |
| Schedule and run a demo or meeting | Discovery / demo |
| Talk pricing | Negotiation |
| Close them as a customer | Close |
| Onboard them to use the product | Onboarding |

The last step -- onboarding -- is critical and often forgotten. "If you forget the last step, then you will have lots of churn because people don't actually know how to use your product" [1].

Track all of this in a simple spreadsheet or CRM with columns for industry, company, title, name, email, and LinkedIn. Tools Alstromer recommends: Apollo.io, Close.com, Pipedrive, and Hunter.io [1].

## Prioritizing Prospects

Alstromer's core principle: "Your first customers should be your easiest" [1]. Four specific tactics:

1. **Focus on prospects most likely to close.** As responses come in, prioritize by likelihood of closing. Avoid those who are dragging the process. It is acceptable to say: "It's been great getting to know you, and I've learned a lot, but we should talk again in six months" [1].
2. **Sell to people you know.** Selling to your network is easier than selling to strangers [1]. Brex recruited its first ten customers directly from the YC batch [1].
3. **Sell to startups first.** Startups have short decision-making lines, no procurement department, and the decision-maker is often reachable directly. "Bigger companies have more bureaucracy, more processes. They even have a specific department that is in charge of negotiating with you" [1].
4. **Accept that most people are not early adopters.** Most recipients will archive your email without reading it. "They just don't care. They're not going to try a new product." The solution is volume: send more messages to find the early adopters, rather than trying to convince non-adopters [1].

## Writing a Great Sales Email

Alstromer provides a template for outbound emails [1]:

1. **Short:** Maximum six to eight sentences. "People don't have time to read long emails" [1].
2. **Clear language.** No jargon, no buzzwords. Say exactly what the product does [1].
3. **Address the prospect's problem** [1].
4. **Plain text only.** No HTML formatting. Write it like you would to a friend [1].
5. **Say you are the founder.** Describe why you and your team are impressive. Include social proof: YC batch membership, impressive previous companies, relevant expertise. "Show, not tell. Don't say you're an expert" [1].
6. **Include a link to your website.** The site should have screenshots, not drawings or stock art. Short videos or GIFs that get to the point immediately can work well [1].
7. **Include a call to action.** Ask for a call, a meeting, or a self-serve signup [1].

The Brex example email worked despite being "probably a little bit too long" [1]. It opened with scarcity ("ten spots for beta users"), addressed the target directly ("corporate credit card focused on technology companies"), stated the value proposition ("we don't require a personal guarantee"), and removed the price objection ("it's free") [1].

## Working Backwards from Goals

The most common founder mistake in sales is not doing enough outreach because they do not work backwards from their goal [1].

Alstromer walks through the math [1]:

**Example 1 (sufficient volume):**
- 500 outreach emails sent
- 50% open rate = 250 opens
- 5% response rate = ~20 responses
- 50% convert to demo = 10 demos
- 20% close rate = 2 customers

**Example 2 (insufficient volume):**
- 100 outreach emails sent
- Same conversion rates = 0 customers

The conclusion founders draw after Example 2: "Sales is not working for me, and I should just do marketing or SEO or something else." This is wrong. "You don't have the data to make that call. You did not do enough outreach" [1].

Key takeaways [1]:
- You do not know your sales conversion rate until you have tracked enough data
- Because most people are not early adopters, there is massive drop-off in outbound sales
- "You cannot close five customers from 10 leads. It's not possible"
- Most founders do not work backwards from their sales funnel, do not do this exercise, and therefore do not succeed in sales

## Charging Is Non-Negotiable

Alstromer argues forcefully against free trials and unpaid pilots in B2B [1]:

"If you don't charge your customers, they are not a customer, and you don't have a company. Customers paying you money is a great sign that you're providing them real value" [1].

If a prospect does not want to pay, that is a signal to move on, not to offer the product for free [1].

For B2B, Alstromer recommends a money-back guarantee over a free trial: "We charge you. If you're not happy, you can get the money back in 30 days." Or offer the ability to opt out of an annual contract and pay monthly instead [1].

The pricing principle: "Increasing your price until your customers are complaining but still paying is the right way to go" [1]. See [[Pricing and Monetization]] and [[B2B Pricing]].

## The Unscalable-to-Scalable Bridge

Even if the long-term growth strategy is word of mouth, Google SEO, or referrals, that is not how companies start [1]. Alstromer uses Airbnb as the example: the sources of growth at scale (word of mouth, Google search, referrals, Facebook advertising) were not how Airbnb got its first 2,000 customers. "They did things that didn't scale, and they looked different than the ones they do at scale" [1].

If the scalable channel is SEM or SEO, find where those people identify themselves online (it may not be Google). If the scalable channel is product-led or viral, use personal networks first. If the channel is sales, the early version looks like everything described above -- just done by the founders instead of a team of 100 [1].

Lenny Rachitsky's newsletter documents how YC and non-YC B2B companies got their go-to-market started. Companies like Amplitude, Stripe, and Front all began with outbound founder emails [1].

## Dev Tool Sales Are Different

Dessaigne argues that selling a dev tool differs from selling other products [2]:

- **No sales deck.** "Developers don't want to waste their time reading through a sales deck." Show demonstrations instead [2].
- **Lean into tech.** At Algolia, "when the buyer is not technical and wants a turnkey solution, we most of the time lose the deal. But when the buyer is technical or if tech has a lot of influence in the decision, we win way more often" [2].
- **Bottom-up selling.** Many enterprise employees may already be using the product. Sales should check for existing usage and help management understand what the product is already doing, rather than pitching top-down [2].
- **Rename the role.** Algolia's first salespeople called themselves "product specialists" on LinkedIn. "They had to own that stage. They had to understand the product intently" [2].

On when to hire a sales team, Dessaigne suggests waiting until approximately $1 million ARR [2].

## Sales as a Learnable Skill (Sam Altman)

[[Sam Altman]]'s 2019 *How to Be Successful* treats sales as a general career skill, not just a founder task: "All great careers, to some degree, become sales jobs. You have to evangelize your plans to customers, prospective employees, the press, investors, etc. This requires an inspiring vision, strong communication skills, some degree of charisma, and evidence of execution ability" [3]. The framing consolidates founder-sales (to users, as Alstromer describes) with recruiting, pitching, and press work under a single capability.

Two claims worth extracting:

- **Written communication compounds.** "Getting good at communication -- particularly written communication -- is an investment worth making. My best advice for communicating clearly is to first make sure your thinking is clear and then use plain, concise language" [3]. The ordering matters — unclear writing is almost always unclear thinking.
- **Belief is the sales multiplier.** "The best way to be good at sales is to genuinely believe in what you're selling. Selling what you truly believe in feels great, and trying to sell snake oil feels awful" [3]. This matches [[Dalton Caldwell]]'s [[Recruiting Early Team Members|self-conviction argument]] — the persuasion primitive is the seller's own belief, not the pitch's polish. And the deliberate-practice claim: "Getting good at sales is like improving at any other skill -- anyone can get better at it with deliberate practice. But for some reason, perhaps because it feels distasteful, many people treat it as something unlearnable."
- **Show up in person.** "My other big sales tip is to show up in person whenever it's important. When I was first starting out, I was always willing to get on a plane. It was frequently unnecessary, but three times it led to career-making turning points for me that otherwise would have gone the other way" [3]. The expected value is positive because the rare high-variance wins outweigh the routine losses.
