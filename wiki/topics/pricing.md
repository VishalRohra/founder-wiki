# Pricing

Pricing is one of the most consequential and underexplored decisions for early-stage startups. [[Kevin Hale]] provides the foundational framework in Startup Pricing 101 [1], while [[Dalton Caldwell]] and [[Michael Seibel]] hammer on what they consider the single most common pricing mistake: undercharging [2].

## Most Startups Are Undercharging

The consensus across multiple YC partners is that early-stage startups almost universally price too low. [[Dalton Caldwell]] states this directly: most startups that come through YC are undercharging for their product [2]. The psychological reasons are clear: founders are afraid that higher prices will scare away users, and they discount their own product's value because they can see all its flaws.

Caldwell and Seibel argue that raising prices is one of the highest-leverage things a startup can do [2]. The effects compound: higher prices mean better unit economics, which means more runway, which means more time to find [[Product-Market Fit]]. Higher prices also attract more serious customers who are more likely to actually use and benefit from the product.

The test: if no one is pushing back on your price, you are probably too cheap. Some price resistance is healthy -- it means you are capturing real value [2].

## Pricing Frameworks

[[Kevin Hale]] lays out a systematic approach to startup pricing [1]:

**Value-based pricing** is the recommended approach for startups. Rather than pricing based on cost (cost-plus) or competitors (competitive pricing), price based on the value the customer receives. If your product saves a customer $100,000 per year, charging $10,000 is reasonable even if your costs are minimal [1].

**The price thermometer**: Hale describes a spectrum from too cheap (no one takes you seriously, you cannot sustain the business) to too expensive (no one buys). The sweet spot is where customers feel they are getting a good deal but you are capturing enough value to build a real business [1].

## Pricing and Growth

Pricing interacts with growth in ways founders often miss. Too-low pricing can actually slow growth because:
1. Revenue is too low to fund customer acquisition
2. Free or cheap customers tend to be less engaged and harder to retain
3. The company cannot afford to invest in the product improvements needed to grow
