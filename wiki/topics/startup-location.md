# Startup Location

[[Dalton Caldwell]] and [[Michael Seibel]] stage a deliberate disagreement about San Francisco specifically -- Caldwell prefers the suburbs, Seibel lives on Market Street -- but converge completely on the underlying advice: startups should be in the Bay Area [1]. The disagreement is over city versus suburb within the same ecosystem, not whether to be in the ecosystem at all.

## The Fake Disagreement

Caldwell prefers living in a place that is "warm and bright and sunny" with "trees everywhere," away from dense-city distractions. He has small children and values a yard [1]. Seibel grew up in suburban New Jersey and perceived getting to the city as aspirational. He likes restaurants, bars, and the chip-on-the-shoulder feeling of being surrounded by things he could not yet afford [1].

The resolution: "Would you recommend someone start a startup outside of the Bay Area?" Caldwell: "I genuinely would not" [1]. The Bay Area is large enough to accommodate both lifestyles. Google, Facebook, and Apple headquarters are not in San Francisco -- they are on the Peninsula and in Cupertino. "Clearly in the history of this stuff, all these great companies were built outside of San Francisco" [1]. The claim that every company must be in downtown San Francisco is "factually wrong" [1]. But the claim that startups should be in the Bay Area region is strongly supported.

## Why the Bay Area: Network Effects

The core argument is network effects. Caldwell draws the analogy to the products they fund: network effects make Airbnb, Uber, and Facebook valuable. The Bay Area has network effects for startups [1].

The critical property of network effects: "If you're not benefiting from them, you don't know what you are missing" [1]. Seibel elaborates: "It's all the people that you won't meet, all the chance encounters you won't have, all the employees you won't have, all these surface area for luck" [1].

Both observed that people who left the Bay Area during COVID all came back. Seibel: "All of them said the same thing, which was basically like, 'I missed the community.' But I never really understood that I was consuming the community. I thought I was working all the time, but I actually was consuming a community of people who are really into startups. And when I moved to Seattle or Austin or New York, that community wasn't as strong, and I missed it" [1].

Caldwell adds: "And they also don't say that as publicly" -- the return is quieter than the departure [1].

## The Industry Analogy

Seibel frames it in terms of other industries: the best finance people end up in New York or London. The best entertainment people end up in LA. "When you want to be top 0.001, you want to be around other people who are trying to be top 0.001" [1]. Neither Caldwell (from Texas) nor Seibel (from New Jersey) grew up in the Bay Area. They both migrated there for the same reason [1].

The analogy extends to acting: "Think about how many people, their story of their big break is that something happened in Los Angeles or New York" [1]. The work they did before that break was putting themselves in position for the luck to happen.

## The Noise That Confuses Founders

Caldwell identifies several sources of misleading discourse [1]:
- **Politicians** trying to attract tech jobs to their cities (Miami, etc.) -- they really want a Google office, not startup culture
- **Social media flamewars** using photos of San Francisco's problems to generate engagement
- **Founders not in the Bay Area** who read Twitter and assume Bay Area = living in the Tenderloin

The smartest founders "look past all these stupid debates" and recognize the network effect [1].

## The Odds Argument

Caldwell brings it back to probability: "There's certainly anecdotes of people that were discovered outside the Bay Area, and everything slipped into place for them. But if you actually just look at the numbers of how you increase the odds of success, moving to the big leagues was a key part of it" [1].

He uses a blackjack analogy: you can win without knowing basic strategy, but "it is so easy to get that one hour of instruction, and that increases your odds so much. Getting into the Bay Area is so relatively easy compared to all the other things you have to choose correctly. Why not pick up the easy wins? It's an easy percentage multiplier" [1].

Seibel adds the sports parallel: "The average NBA player was the best basketball player anywhere. Sometimes startup founders miss this point. They're kind of like, 'I want to be in the game,' versus 'I want to win the game'" [1]. You can play basketball for a high school team in any city, but that is not sufficient to make the NBA.

## Anecdotes vs. Numbers

Seibel anticipates the counterarguments: Shopify in Ottawa, MongoDB in New York. "The anecdotes are true: you can do a startup outside the Bay Area, of course. And maybe there's a really good reason you want to do that -- health reasons or family. Great, of course. But if all you want to do is play the odds and optimize for success, I can't really understand why you wouldn't want to be here" [1].

Caldwell emphasizes: "That's not anecdotes, that's numbers" [1].
