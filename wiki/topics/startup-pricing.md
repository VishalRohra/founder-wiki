# Startup Pricing

[[Kevin Hale]] argues that pricing is the most neglected lever for growth, yet it produces the highest return on effort [1]. A survey of over 500 SaaS companies found that a 1% improvement in pricing optimization yields a 12.7% return, compared to 3.32% for acquisition efforts and 6.7% for retention [1]. Despite this, most startups avoid touching their pricing out of fear that getting it wrong will drive away all their customers [1].

## The Pricing Thermometer

Hale introduces a framework called the [[Pricing Thermometer]] that makes the forces behind pricing concrete [1]. Three variables sit on a vertical axis: cost at the bottom, price in the middle, and value at the top.

- **The gap between cost and price** is the startup's margin -- its incentive to sell. The larger this gap, the more the company is motivated to push the product to customers, fund better salespeople, and invest in distribution [1].
- **The gap between price and value** is the customer's incentive to buy. The larger this gap, the easier it is for customers to say yes [1].

There are two approaches to setting price. **Cost-plus pricing** starts with the cost and adds a margin. **Value-based pricing** starts with the value the customer perceives and works backward. Hale states that "almost consistently across all businesses, everyone will tell you, you should strive for value-based pricing" because it allows charging more and manipulating the incentive to buy [1].

## The Four Pricing Mistakes

Because founders do not understand the relationship between cost, price, and value, they place their price in an arbitrary location, leading to four common mistakes [1]:

1. **Pricing too low.** "You consistently undercharge" is the number one pricing advice YC gives to startups [1]. This is the most common mistake.
2. **Underestimating costs.** The result is margins too thin to cover customer acquisition costs [1].
3. **Not understanding value.** Either the founder does not understand how the customer values the problem being solved, or the founder cannot convince the customer of the value the product offers [1].
4. **Focusing on the wrong customers.** Founders assume that building a better product and charging half the competition's price will win. It almost never does, because early-stage startups are selling to early adopters, not mainstream customers [1].

## Early Adopters Are Not Price-Sensitive

Hale draws on the product lifecycle curve to explain why the "cheaper wins" logic fails for startups. Startups in the product development and introduction stages are targeting the first 2-5% of potential buyers -- early adopters [1]. These customers behave fundamentally differently from mainstream buyers:

- They care about **benefits above all else** -- getting an edge over competition, doing something much better [1].
- They are **willing to take risks** on unproven products [1].
- They are **not price-sensitive**. If anything, a low price creates reputation risk: "It's like, why is it too good to be true? What is the catch?" [1]

The implication: do not undercharge when selling to early adopters. A higher price signals quality and seriousness. Go after early adopters first, and do not take it personally when mainstream customers are not ready for the product -- "They were never going to be" [1].

## Price Optimization in Practice

Hale demystifies price optimization by reducing it to a simple table [1]:

| Price Point | Conversion Rate | Sales Volume | Revenue |
|-------------|----------------|--------------|---------|
| $X | Y% | Z units | $Total |

Try different price points, measure conversion rate and sales volume for each, and calculate total revenue. The winner is the row with the highest revenue [1]. At lower price points where revenue is still positive, those represent opportunities for discount pricing or tiered pricing [1].

## The Danger Zone: Pricing and Customer Acquisition

Hale introduces a framework linking price to acquisition strategy. The vertical axis represents price; the horizontal axis represents sales process complexity [1]:

**Self-Serve (under $2,000/year):**
- Marketing must be almost entirely inbound [1]
- Support must be self-serve or minimal [1]
- No sales team -- cannot afford one at this price point [1]
- Conversions should happen same-day in a self-serve model [1]

**Transactional ($2,000 to $10,000/year):**
- Marketing can generate qualified leads [1]
- Support can offer SLAs and onboarding training [1]
- Can have inside sales reps, SDRs, and product demo staff (but not dedicated field sales) [1]
- Sales cycle should not exceed one to three months [1]

**Enterprise (over $25,000/year):**
- Marketing can invest in branding and trust-building [1]
- Support is high-touch: phone support, dedicated customer success managers [1]
- Sales structure includes sales managers, territory divisions, and sales engineers [1]
- Sales cycle of six to twelve months [1]

**The Garbage Zone** sits between self-serve and transactional: if it takes months to close a deal but the revenue per customer is low, acquisition costs will be unsustainable. "All of your work should be towards increasing the perceived value of your product or service" to escape this zone [1].

### The Billion-Dollar Math

Hale uses a concrete exercise: to build a billion-dollar company, the rule of thumb is $100 million per year in revenue. At each price point, calculate how many customers are needed to reach $100M [1]:

- **$100/year** -- need 1 million users (consumer territory) [1]
- **$1,000/year** -- enterprise territory [1]
- The SMB range in between is where companies "really, really struggle" because these customers "treat their money like consumers but look like they might be an enterprise" [1]

## The 10/5/20 Rule

Hale closes with a practical heuristic for founders who do not know where to start with pricing [1]:

1. **10X value**: Set a price that is one-tenth of the perceived value. If the product costs $10, the customer should easily perceive it as worth $100. If the 10X value is not immediately obvious, the incentive to buy is too low [1].
2. **5% increases**: Once a starting price exists, practice raising it by 5% at a time [1].
3. **20% loss threshold**: Keep raising prices until 20% of deals are lost. That is the approximate sweet spot between pricing too low and too high [1].

## Pricing as a Growth Driver

Hale summarizes: "If you've never touched the pricing of your product, then you're losing out on lots of potential growth" [1]. The key questions founders should be able to answer: Do you understand your costs? Do you understand why your price is where it is? Do you understand the value your customer perceives? When entering a sales meeting, a founder should be able to say: "I know exactly what this is going to be worth to you. So when I tell you what the price is going to be, you're going to be like, 'Damn, that's totally worth it'" [1].
