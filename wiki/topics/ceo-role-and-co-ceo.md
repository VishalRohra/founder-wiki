# CEO Role and the Co-CEO Mistake

[[Garry Tan]] draws on his personal experience co-founding Posterous to argue that the co-CEO structure is a mistake for nearly all startups, and that founders must honestly assess whether they need to be CEO [1].

## The Co-CEO Failure

In 2010, Tan and his co-founder were pitching at Andreessen Horowitz. Ben Horowitz asked: "So who's the CEO?" Tan and his co-founder looked at each other and said "Both." Tan recalls: "The meeting might as well have ended at that moment" [1].

Horowitz later wrote about this in 2013: "Shared command always seems really attractive to the people at the top of the organization... It looks much less attractive to those who do all the work in the organization. To them, it looks like frustration, chaos, and delay" [1].

The operational problem is speed. With co-CEOs, every hard decision requires both people to agree, which slows decision-making to the point where "your startup will die" [1].

Tan identifies the archetype that tempts founders: Larry Page and Sergey Brin of Google. "What was good enough for Google, surely could be good enough for us, right? Whoa, whoa, no. Look, most people are not Google at their Series A" [1].

## How to Know If You Should Be CEO

Tan's self-diagnostic question: "If it was broken, I needed to be able to fix it. If things are broken, are you okay with someone else being in charge of fixing it? If you're going to be incredibly frustrated that you don't have the authority and can't fix it, then maybe you do need to be CEO" [1].

Tan frames this as a matter of self-knowledge, not ego: "It doesn't have to be an ego thing. It's just something that you need in your life. It's okay to need that. It's okay to need agency" [1].

## Tan's Personal Mistake

After the Andreessen Horowitz meeting, Tan discussed the CEO question with his co-founder. His co-founder became CEO after their Series A. Tan admits: "If I'm being honest with you, I regret that. I learned you may not be interested in authority, but authority is interested in you" [1].

The root of the mistake: Tan wanted to keep coding. "I had to decide whether I wanted to be CEO or if I could keep designing and writing code and shipping product. Writing code and shipping product, those felt really good to me in the moment. I chose sticking with the things that made me feel good" [1]. It took him twenty years to realize he needed to be CEO [1].

## What the CEO Must Do

Tan identifies the specific responsibilities that only a CEO can handle, which become critical after [[Product-Market Fit]] [1]:

- Convince people to follow you
- Hire and manage
- Fundraise and manage investors
- Drive the vision of the company
- Be "the magic that brings all of those things together to build a great product or service"

Tan is direct: "Being CEO is a very specific skill set. And unless you are signing up to do those things and do them to the best of your ability, don't" [1].

## The Three Types of Founders

Tan observes that founders sort into three categories regarding the CEO question [1]:

1. Those who never want to be CEO -- and that is fine.
2. Those who love another function but realize they need to become CEO to steer the ship.
3. Those who "knew the day you quit your job that you absolutely have to get that authority."

The advice for all three: "Don't delude yourself about whether or not you should be CEO. If you know, you know. Then it's on you to acquire the skills and manifest it" [1].
