# Creating Luck

[[Dalton Caldwell]] argues that luck in startups is not random. It is manufactured through speed. "The way to create luck is to move much faster than everyone else and to give yourselves as many opportunities for that lucky thing to happen to you" [1].

## The Speed Principle

If everyone is running at five miles an hour, imagine running at fifteen. You can run circles around everyone. A startup moving fast gets more opportunities to talk to more customers, change ideas more often, and write code more quickly. "Everything gets easier if you move much faster" [1].

The founders that get the luckiest are the founders that move the fastest, giving themselves "as much surface area as possible to get super lucky" [1].

## Brex: From VR Headsets to Credit Cards

Brex is the canonical example. When YC initially funded the Brex founders, they were working on a VR headset idea. If they were not fast movers, "they would have worked on that idea, realized it wasn't gonna work, and given up" [1].

Instead, they realized the VR idea was not working within about six weeks. They did not simply pick one new idea. They iteratively went through half a dozen or more different ideas, quickly talking to experts about each one and crossing them off as non-viable. "By doing this really fast process, really quickly during the batch, they zeroed in on what became Brex very quickly" [1]. See [[Startup Ideas]] for more on the idea iteration process.

## Retool and Magic: The Same Pattern

Retool followed the same pattern -- changing ideas quickly to find a much better one [1]. Magic (originally funded to do something completely different) moved so fast that they built full prototypes of ideas and even launched them to customers, eventually finding an idea that took off [1].

In all three cases, the companies "dramatically increased the surface area of luck" through speed [1].

## Creating Luck Pre-Company

For people who have not yet started a company, Caldwell identifies two strategies [1]:

**Know interesting people.** Meet and learn from as many interesting people as possible [1].

**Do uncomfortable things.** "A lot of the times, founders that may not get as lucky just stay at home and they wait for the world to come to them and they wait to be recognized for their secret genius. Whereas the folks that are able to create luck just go out there and interact with people and tell other people about their ideas" [1].

### Mark Zuckerberg as a Case Study

Caldwell cites Zuckerberg's pre-Facebook history as an example of luck-creation through prolific making. Before Facebook, Zuckerberg "was constantly creating prototypes and giving them to users. He was creating music plug-ins for Winamp. He was making hacks for his friends. He was always just kind of making stuff and giving it to people" [1].

This habit of building and sharing created the surface area from which Facebook emerged. "You can dramatically increase the chance that you will get lucky if you just make things. You learn how to make things really fast. You learn how to make things by yourself or with other people that are interesting. And you give them to other people. You don't just hide them to yourself. And if you just do that a lot, things start happening for you" [1].

## When to Play to Strengths vs. Go Outside Comfort Zone

Caldwell draws a nuanced line [1]:

- If you have the technical skills to build the product you want to build, it is acceptable to "get in way over your head" in terms of domain knowledge. Sometimes outsiders see opportunities that insiders have given up on [1].
- If you have "absolutely no relevant skills" and are trying to break into something that requires technical skills, "you're gonna have a pretty hard time" [1].
- Knowing nothing about biology and deciding to cure cancer is "a pretty tall order" [1].

## Creating Luck at Scale (Series A+)

The speed-as-luck principle applies beyond the earliest stage. At Series A and later, it manifests as a culture of fast-moving experimentation. "Hire people that move so fast that they get lucky too" [1]. If the founder is fast-moving and continues to hire people who iterate through ideas quickly, the entire culture becomes "continuously lucky" [1].
