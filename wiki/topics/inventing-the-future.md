# Inventing the Future

Alan Kay, one of the pioneers of personal computing and a Turing Award winner, delivered a two-part lecture at Stanford (for Sam Altman's "How to Start a Startup" series) on how transformational technology gets invented. Part 2 covers the role of context in intelligence, the psychology of human perception and blindness, and why the most important ideas from Xerox PARC still have not been adopted [1].

## Context Is Worth 80 IQ Points

Kay opens with a comparison: Leonardo da Vinci, arguably the most brilliant person of his era, could not invent a single motor. Henry Ford, with nowhere near Leonardo's IQ, built millions of cars and sold them for $800 each. "The difference between the two was that Ford was born into the right century and Leonardo was born into the wrong century" [1].

The accumulated knowledge of the 19th century -- ultimately traceable to the context shift that Newton initiated in the 17th century -- made more difference than any level of individual smartness [1].

Kay formalizes this as **Principle Zero**: "To make progress, context or point of view is worth 80 IQ points. If you've got the context of science and math, you are gazillions of times smarter, effectively, than the smartest people of antiquity" [1].

His summary: "Knowledge is silver, context is gold, and IQ is just lead" [1].

The trap for smart people: being the smartest in a classroom of 30 or 100 feels meaningful, but "if you happened to be in a room that had the hundred smartest people in the world and you were the smartest person in that room, you're still not as smart as the other ninety-nine working together" [1].

## The Pink Plane and Blue Worlds

Kay uses a visual metaphor to explain paradigm shifts [1]. Most people live on a "pink plane" -- their default worldview. Thoughts are like ants crawling over it. Everything they see is pink, but they do not know it is pink because they have never experienced anything else. "This is like a fish not knowing it's in water" [1].

Occasionally, someone has an "outlaw thought" -- a blue thought -- that reveals an orthogonal world. Newton's shift from medieval to scientific thinking. ARPA's shift from mainframes to personal computing. Each is a move from one colored plane to another. "Once you're in that blue world, you realize there's probably a lot of them" [1].

**Einstein at five**: Einstein's father gave him a compass while he was recovering from measles. Most children would accept the compass as it is. Einstein remembered it as the most important moment of his childhood: "There must be something deeply hidden behind things." He did not take the world at face value [1].

## Human Perception Is Broken

Kay uses hands-on demonstrations to show that human perception routinely manufactures false information [1]:

### The Blind Spot

Every human eye has a region with no light-sensitive cells (where blood vessels pass through). The brain fills this gap by making up what it thinks should be there -- an unconscious confabulation. "We have a blind spot in our eyes that our brain makes up stuff to fill in. It's just making it up" [1].

**Principle 22**: We dream while awake. The brain fills in its blind spot the same way it generates dreams. Most people think they only dream at night, "but in fact, human brains are set up so that we actually dream" continuously [1].

**Principle 23**: "We can't learn to see until we realize and admit that we're blind. And the biggest problem with most human beings is they don't understand that they are blind because they think they can see" [1].

### Size Constancy

Holding two identical poker chips at different distances, the retinal image of the farther chip is half the size. But the brain's belief system overrides the retinal data and presents both chips as the same size. This "size constancy" illusion means the mind's eye -- beliefs about the world -- overrides the sensory eye [1].

Artists learn to work around this by measuring with outstretched arms. Most people never discover the discrepancy [1].

### The Shepard Tables

Two tabletops that appear to be completely different shapes are in fact identical. Even knowing this, the visual system refuses to see it. "The natural state of humanity is to be crazy, because crazy is not having good models in our mind for what's going on out there" [1]. First demonstrated by Roger Shepard at Stanford [1].

## What Made It Out of Xerox PARC

Kay provides a detailed scorecard of which PARC inventions successfully transferred to the broader world [1]:

| Invention | % Adopted |
|---|---|
| Laser printer | ~90% |
| PostScript | 100% (Adobe founders were PARC alumni) |
| Ethernet | 100% |
| GUI | ~70% (missing: unlimited desktop/project model) |
| Desktop publishing | ~70% |
| Internet protocols | 100% |
| Programming (Smalltalk) | ~10% |
| CPU architecture | 0% (Intel resisted) |
| Dynabook concept | ~10% |

The pattern: "What came out most successfully were the things for which people out there didn't think they already knew how to do it." Everybody thought they already knew how to program, so Smalltalk's radical approach met maximum resistance [1].

Kay shows a restored 1978 Smalltalk system (recovered from Xerox's dump, where disc packs had been thrown out) with capabilities still absent from modern operating systems: persistent project-based workspaces, live editing of running animations, gesture recognition, and the ability to connect any two objects via messaging. "Wouldn't you like to be able to do that today? This is completely natural. It's what you get very easily if you just have unlimited objects" [1].

### What Is Still Missing

Not a single mainstream operating system includes the collaborative content-sharing capability that Engelbart demonstrated in 1968 [1]. The ability to share any content with everyone else, allowing them to interact with it in real time, requires operating-system-level support that macOS, Linux, and Windows lack.

Kay attributes this to operating systems that predate 1965 in their fundamental design: "All three operating systems we have today that are the main ones are old, old ideas" [1].

## Darwinian Processes Do Not Optimize

Kay corrects a common misconception: evolution does not optimize. It fits organisms into niches. "If that environment isn't the right kind of environment, the process of evolution is not going to give you something that's very interesting" [1]. As computing becomes less interesting (lower expectations, less ambitious goals), the selection pressure produces less interesting software.

This is why innovations like Sketchpad's direct manipulation -- which Sam Altman looked at and asked "Why aren't they doing it today?" -- disappear. "Nobody even thinks about that. Nobody even thinks of doing WYSIWYG on web media" [1].

## Human Universals vs. Inventions

Anthropologists studying thousands of traditional cultures identified traits present in every culture (human universals -- virtually all genetic) and traits absent from many (inventions that had to be discovered) [1].

**Genetic universals** (things humans want): hunting/gathering, social bonding, language, stories, news, theater, revenge. Every one is "a legal drug." Entrepreneurs can build technological amplifiers for any of these cravings, and people will buy them [1].

**Inventions** (things humans need but are not genetically prepared for): agriculture, the idea of progress, writing/reading, scientific representation, slow thinking, similarities over differences, legal systems. These are hard to learn because they are not genetically prepared [1].

Kay's challenge to entrepreneurs: "Try to do it without appealing to the parts of their nature that they're helpless in front of." The technologies of today are "the informational equivalent of cave people with nuclear weapons" because educational systems have not kept pace with technological capability [1].

## Principles Summary

- **0**: Context/point of view is worth 80 IQ points
- **1**: Debugging is harder than programming -- don't use all your cleverness writing the program
- **2**: The goodness of results correlates most with the goodness of the funders (good funders give gold medals ahead of time, knowing 70% will turn to lead)
- **21**: Reality is a low-pass filter -- you need enormous ideas so that filtering retains something useful
- **22**: We dream while awake (the brain fills in blind spots with confabulations)
- **23**: We cannot learn to see until we realize we are blind
- **24**: The mind's eye is different from the sensory eye; we act according to beliefs, not data
