# Press and PR

Press is a small but real part of the founder's job. The best advice, according to [[Michael Seibel]], came from Mike Arrington and M.G. Siegler at an SV Angel CEO conference: "Treat PR like biz dev" [1].

## The Core Principle: PR as Business Development

Seibel spent over $100,000 on PR agencies and PR people as an early-stage startup before learning this lesson [1]. The shift: develop relationships with reporters as people, get introduced through warm connections instead of cold emails, and create a fair value exchange. "Reporters want to talk to CEOs and co-founders -- not PR people" [1].

## Step 1: Generate News

Most founders pitch "profile pieces" about their company. These stories aren't valuable to reporters because they're not timely. "What makes a valuable story is news" [1].

Four categories of startup news [1]:

1. **Product Launches and Features.** Things coming out today, not yesterday or tomorrow. User-facing features. Seibel's examples from Socialcam: releasing video filters, soundtracks, or user profiles [1].

2. **Product/Sales Milestones.** Hitting [a million users](https://zapier.com/blog/one-million-users/), $100k MRR, or [a million guests per night](https://techcrunch.com/2015/05/27/airbnb-hopes-to-have-almost-a-million-stays-a-night-by-summer/) [1]. Seibel recommends sandbagging: "wait until you're 25% past the milestone to announce -- so that you're that much closer to announcing the next milestone" [1].

3. **Significant BD Deals or Customers.** Landing a team at Airbnb or Instacart, or [partnering with GM](https://www.fastcompany.com/3055059/gm-partners-with-lyft-to-build-a-network-of-self-driving-cars) to build a network of self-driving cars. Announce if you have permission [1].

4. **Fundraising.** "For better or worse, fundraising is conflated with traction/success so it is a topic people want to read about" [1].

## Step 2: Build Direct Press Contacts

"Don't spray and pray. It's stupid" [1].

The best method: warm introductions. Have someone who has been covered by the reporter introduce you. Proper etiquette is to ask for an intro to only one reporter per friend [1]. For YC founders, the YC network is valuable since "the reporter you want to meet has definitely written about someone you know" [1].

The intro email should be readable and reply-able in 30 seconds [1]:
- Describe your news in one sentence
- For an [exclusive](https://www.quora.com/What-is-a-press-exclusive-and-how-does-it-work): say so
- For an [embargo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/News_embargo): include date and time
- Request a 20-minute call

On the call, have 3-5 reasons why your news and company matter. At the end, offer notes and collateral (screenshots, graphs, logos, pictures) -- this provides "an opportunity to be courteous *and* provide reporters with the language you prefer" [1].

## Step 3: Offer Exclusives

"Almost all startups should be offering their story as an exclusive to one tech reporter at a time" [1]. The target audience -- investors, potential employees, motivated customers, early adopters -- exists in high concentration in a few tech outlets. "Getting blanket coverage across a bunch of tech blogs is often not worth the struggle and can piss people off when embargoes are broken" [1].

## Step 4: Maintain Consistent Cadence

Traditional PR philosophy says to maximize mentions per story by combining all news into one big story infrequently. Seibel disagrees for startups: "you're better off getting one story every month or every other month versus one huge story every year" [1].

Spread news across multiple stories. Maintain a queue of 3-5 pieces of news to announce over the coming 6 months. "It takes the pressure off you if one of those pieces of news doesn't get picked up, and it helps you keep a regular cadence of positive news" [1].

After 6-12 months of consistent cadence and real news, you should be "relatively friendly with two to five reporters" -- they have written about your company enough that they reply to most emails and you have probably met in person [1].

## Expectations and Time Allocation

Expect a 25-50% success rate if you are providing genuine news and doing everything right [1].

"Make sure you aren't dedicating more than 5% of your time to press and don't expect PR to create observable miracles (tons of users, customers, investment offers, etc)" [1]. Most benefits only become obvious weeks or months after a story posts. PR is "like funding basic research in science, you can't predict the advances but they do happen when you invest consistently over time" [1].

## Later-Stage PR

[[Sam Altman]] advises companies to "ignore marketing and PR for a long time" in the beginning [2]. "Press will not make your startup" [2]. But once the product is working, "switch from not caring about it to caring about it a little bit" [2].

Key later-stage principles [2]:
- **Never outsource key messaging** to a PR firm or head of marketing. The founder defines the message.
- **Build journalist relationships directly.** "No journalist wants to talk to a PR firm." Pick 3-4 journalists who like and understand you.
- At some point (Seibel cites Dropbox and Airbnb), press organically pays attention [1]. This is when PR people become valuable as a buffer: "They create a buffer that allows you to be more strategic about what you announce, when, and to whom" [1].
