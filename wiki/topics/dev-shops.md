# Dev Shops and Outsourced Development

[[Michael Seibel]] addresses a common question at YC: whether non-technical founders should outsource development of their initial product to a dev shop [1]. His answer is a strong caution against this path for startups seeking venture-scale outcomes.

## The Trap

The advice to use a dev shop "too easy to focus on the companies that have done this well and not hear the stories about the companies who have done this poorly" [1]. Seibel receives many emails from founders who tried this path and failed.

The trap unfolds in stages [1]:

1. **The MVP takes longer and costs more than expected.** The dev shop may be working on other projects, and the person building is not as motivated as a founder with equity.
2. **Progress feels real.** You go from nothing being built to things happening. "So you feel like you're making all this progress."
3. **The MVP launches and does not solve the problem.** This is expected -- MVPs rarely do on the first try.
4. **Iteration requires more money and more time with the dev shop.** Funds run low.
5. **The startup dies.** Unable to raise more money and unable to iterate further.

## Why Investors Care About the Team, Not the Product

The fundamental misunderstanding: non-technical founders think early investors want to see traction. In reality, "the vast majority of good early stage investors care more about whether you can build something than what you've built" [1]. At the point they invest, there is very little proof of success. What they want to see is the ability to iterate through multiple versions quickly [1].

Doing that with a dev shop takes longer and is far more expensive than doing it with a technical co-founder [1]. Additionally, "there are a lot of very good tech startup investors who do not want to invest in companies that are outsourcing development" [1].

A further problem: when a company does hire a developer, the outsourced code is often thrown away because the new developer disagrees with how it was built and wants to rebuild [1].

## The Alternative

Seibel's recommendation: invest the time in recruiting a technical co-founder. "Typically what ends up happening is you have to spend far less than you'd have to spend for that dev shop, you have a product that's released far faster, and you're much better set up to iterate on the solution" [1].

He acknowledges exceptions exist but emphasizes: "In my experience, that is by far the exception, not the rule" [1].
