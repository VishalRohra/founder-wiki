# Dev Shops

[[Michael Seibel]] warns that using a development shop to build a startup's initial product is one of the most common traps for non-technical founders [1]. While some companies have succeeded after using dev shops, Seibel states that this is "by far the exception, not the rule" [1].

## The Trap Pattern

The dev shop trap follows a predictable sequence [1]:

1. **A non-technical founder** believes they have a problem worth solving and understand their customer, but cannot build the solution.
2. **They are advised** to use a dev shop instead of recruiting a [[Technical Co-Founders|technical co-founder]]. The reasoning: build the first version, get traction, then hire a CTO.
3. **Development takes longer than expected.** The founder has little experience estimating software timelines, and the dev shop is working on other projects. The dev shop's team members are "not as motivated as they would be if they were a founder with a lot of equity in the product."
4. **Costs exceed projections.** The founder budgets $15,000-$30,000 and ends up spending far more. This is "extremely frustrating, especially if they've raised a little bit of angel money and they're pouring all of it into the building of the initial product."
5. **Fundraising becomes harder.** Many good tech investors "do not want to invest in companies that are outsourcing development unless those companies are absolutely taking off and just growing ridiculously quickly, which is very rare."
6. **The code is thrown away.** If the founder eventually hires an engineer, the new hire often disagrees with the dev shop's architecture and rebuilds from scratch.

## Why It Feels Like Progress

The trap's power lies in the fact that it initially feels like progress. "You often go from nothing being built before you have the dev shop to things happening once you have the dev shop. So you feel like you're making all this progress" [1].

The reality becomes clear after the first version ships. The MVP almost never solves the problem on the first attempt. The founder needs to iterate, which means spending more money and more time with the dev shop. "Usually this is when your funds run low and you realize, 'Oh man, I can't make it'" [1].

## What Investors Actually Want

Seibel argues that founders who use dev shops misunderstand what early-stage investors look for. "The vast majority of good early stage investors care more about whether you can build something than what you've built" [1]. At the early stage, there is very little proof the company will succeed. The investor's real question is: "Can you iterate through multiple versions of your product until you can find a version that solves the problem that you want to solve?" [1].

Doing that with a dev shop takes longer and costs far more than doing it with a technical co-founder. True early-stage investors want iteration speed, not traction. Series A investors want traction [1].

## The Technical Co-Founder Alternative

Seibel's recommendation is clear: recruit a [[Technical Co-Founders|technical co-founder]] instead. "Typically what ends up happening is you have to spend far less than you'd have to spend for that dev shop, you have a product that's released far faster, and you're much better set up to iterate on the solution as you get feedback from your customers" [1].

The common objection is that finding a technical co-founder is too hard. Seibel's response: "So many companies fall into this trap that they should really reconsider whether or not spending that extra time recruiting a technical co-founder is going to be far more worth it than going with the dev shop" [1].

## Scope

Seibel limits this advice to venture-scale tech startups -- "startup that's going to generate a lot of value for a lot of people and, you know, be a multi-billion dollar company and that wants to raise money from the venture capital world." There are "lots of other situations where working with a dev shop is totally fine" [1].
