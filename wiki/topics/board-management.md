# Board Management

Board management is one of the most anxiety-inducing responsibilities for startup founders. As [[Anu Hariharan]] describes it, the anxiety is natural: "this is the first time the founder/CEO is subject to external supervision, and the board has powers that include the firing of the CEO and the senior management" [1]. This article covers the full lifecycle of board management: composition, meeting cadence, deck preparation, leveraging board members, and handling difficult board dynamics.

## Purpose of a Board

The main role of a board is to guide the company through major decisions: hiring and firing senior management, approving corporate actions (compensation, stock options, budget), and offering guidance on strategic decisions that impact the business long-term [1]. Every Delaware corporation must have a board once it starts operating, but in the early days it consists of founders and functions mainly as a technical body for approvals like option grants [1].

When people refer to "setting up a board," they usually mean adding outside directors after raising money, either investor directors or independent directors [1].

## Board Composition

### Size and Evolution

- Early-stage companies: 3-5 board seats [1]
- Public companies: 7-9 seats [1]
- Series A and Series B lead investors typically join the board; later-stage growth investors generally do not ask for seats [1]
- Composition changes significantly in the years before going public, starting with recruiting independent board members, then forming committees (audit, compensation) [1]

### Selecting Board Members

The primary attribute to select for in early-stage board members is trust, and "the only way you build trust is with time" [1]. Hariharan recommends getting to know VC partners on your target list at least 6 to 9 months before your fundraise. Mathilde Colin, CEO of Front, documented her approach to this in detail [1].

Before adding new board members, founders should assess their strengths, weaknesses, and whether they would want to work with them for 10+ years, given that the median time to IPO can produce board relationships of that duration [1].

Reference checking is critical. However, many founders observe that this has caused some VCs to become less transparent in feedback with their portfolio companies, since they fear bad references affecting future deals. This dynamic has led some founders to bring on independent directors sooner [1].

### The Rise of Trusted Independents

Several examples illustrate the trend toward deeply trusted independent board directors [1]:
- **Atrium**: Founder Justin Kan added [[Michael Seibel]] (a former co-founder at Justin.tv) to the board at Series B
- **Brex**: Founders Henrique and Pedro added Victor Lazarte (TFG founder/CEO), who had known them since age 16
- **Faire**: Founders added Brian Grassadonia (Square Cash leader), their former manager at Square

These relationships are "likened to family relationships, where the family privately shares the kind of direct feedback no one else will" [1]. The caveat: "this does not mean you add your friend to your board. You need someone you really trust, who is 100% independent, and has the ability to help both you and your team as you scale the company" [1].

## Running Board Meetings

### Meeting Cadence

At the Series A stage, meetings tend to be informal (like 1:1s), ranging from bi-weekly to bi-monthly [1]. By Series B, establish a regular quarterly cadence with all meetings scheduled a year in advance to ensure attendance. In-person meetings are strongly preferred [1].

### Agenda and Length

Board meetings typically last about 3 hours [1]. The critical mistake to avoid is treating board meetings as status update meetings. Hariharan advises:

- First 45 minutes: highlights, lowlights, and KPIs [1]
- Remaining time: deep dives on no more than 1-2 strategic topics [1]

She shares the actual agenda from a Gusto board meeting as a model [1]:

| Agenda Item | Duration |
|---|---|
| Highlights / Lowlights | 10 min |
| Performance / KPIs | 50 min |
| Strategic topic 1: 3 Year Strategic Plan | 45 min |
| Strategic topic 2: Engineering Org | 45 min |
| Closed Session | 30 min |

The result: no more than one-third of the meeting on the state of the business, with the majority on strategic issues [1].

Hariharan recommends identifying and aligning on key KPIs with board members early, as this "trains" board members to process operational data quickly and focus on the "why" behind hits and misses [1].

### Pre-Meeting Preparation

What makes Gusto's approach work happens outside the board meeting [1]:
1. Extensive prep on areas where input is sought (ideally topics already being discussed with the executive team, so materials serve double duty)
2. Pre-read deck sent at least one week in advance
3. Questions collected from each board member in a Google Doc three days before
4. Questions answered in the Google Doc by the morning of the meeting

Brex follows an even more structured 28-day preparation timeline [1]:

| Timing | Activity |
|---|---|
| T-28 days | Founders/Chief of Staff identify strategic topics; align with executive team |
| T-18 days | Outline topics, write content, share draft deck with executive team |
| T-11 days | Execs provide feedback; Finance finalizes deck |
| T-7 days | Final review; distribute deck to board |
| T-4 days | Board submits questions (especially on KPIs) |
| T-2 days | Brex consolidates and answers all board questions |
| T-0 | Set expectations upfront on where to spend time and seek feedback |

### Board Deck Structure

The board deck is "just a tool for discussion, not an end in and of itself" [1]. Its structure should align with the meeting agenda. Gusto's decks are 30-50 pages and follow this structure [1]:

- **Highlights / Lowlights**: 1 page
- **Performance / KPIs**: 15-20 pages
- **Strategic Topic 1**: 5-10 pages covering options considered, decision framework, product roadmap (including what they plan NOT to pursue), known vs. unknown risks, economic value assessment, discussion questions
- **Strategic Topic 2**: 5-10 pages covering current org, hiring scale, future org (18 months), talent assessment, retention/development/recruitment plan
- **Appendix**: 10 slides on organizational health (hiring metrics, close rates, employee pulse survey results)

### Involving Executives

Post-Series B, Hariharan recommends having specific executives join only for their agenda item (about 45 minutes) rather than attending the entire meeting [1]. The first approach (all executives attending) tends to turn the meeting into a presentation rather than a discussion and makes it harder for the CEO to discuss sensitive matters [1].

The targeted approach achieves three things: deeper discussion on strategic issues, executives hearing board feedback directly, and more time for the CEO and board to discuss sensitive matters privately [1].

General counsel and/or outside counsel should attend all board meetings to capture notes and handle issues requiring attorney-client privilege [1].

## Monthly Updates

In addition to quarterly meetings, a 2-page monthly update email at the beginning of every month is best practice [1]. The email typically includes: last month's performance summary, hiring update, burning issues, and specific asks for help.

Practical examples of effective asks [1]:
- **Brex**: Always makes two specific asks in monthly emails: introductions to business partners/customers, and help with key open roles
- **GitLab**: Asks for introductions to specific individuals at potential customer companies, and publicly applauds investors who have helped, "putting pressure on the entire board to do the same"

Monthly updates accomplish three things [1]:
1. Board members go above and beyond to help with specific asks
2. The board arrives better prepared for quarterly meetings
3. The CEO is forced to step back and objectively assess the business

Hariharan cites Jeff Bezos's advocacy for written narrative over slides: "written narrative is more compelling in sharing ideas than bullet points and slides" [1].

### Letter to the Board

Some CEOs use the board meeting as an occasion for deeper reflection [1]:
- **Peter Reinhardt** (Segment): Writes a 3-5 page memo covering highlights, lowlights, and time allocation analysis, including his own strengths and weaknesses as CEO
- **Faire**: Uses written memos to discuss strategic priorities, articulating decision frameworks and documenting uncertainties and blind spots. These memos help the board prepare and create a record of decision-making quality

## Navigating Board Dynamics

### Mixed Messages

Conflicting viewpoints are productive: "The job of the board is to push you on your strategy and to ask questions that help sharpen your thinking" [1]. CEOs should welcome disagreement and not feel pressure to reach consensus.

When conflict stalls progress, take the conversation offline with 1:1 or small group meetings. Use a decision-making framework (e.g., economic value, resources, cash needs, competitive threat) to guide the path forward [1].

A critical principle: "Your board members will never be as close to the business to make that decision. While board members provide important input all CEOs should seriously consider, the CEO has the authority to make the best decision on behalf of the company" [1].

### Asking for Help

Not every board member needs to be in the loop on every topic. "It is a poor use of time to update every board member 1:1 before every board meeting" [1]. Instead, follow up directly with specific board members on topics where they can be most helpful.

### Balancing Helpfulness vs. Control

"Be cautious of board members who are trying to make decisions for you. A board member's job is to help you think through the issues by offering tools or decision frameworks -- not to give you all the answers on how to run your business" [1].

Hariharan cites Micky Malka of Ribbit Capital as an exemplary board member: when the Brex CEO asked about typical credit loss rates, rather than giving a definitive answer, Malka shared examples from 10 startups in Ribbit's portfolio with their trajectories and pros/cons of each approach, empowering the founders to decide [1].

### Problem Board Members

If a board member is unhelpful or overbearing [1]:
1. First attempt a 1:1 meeting with constructive, specific feedback
2. Work with other board members to understand and address the issue
3. If behavior is destructive (meddling, micromanaging, leaking to press), consider either approaching someone senior at the board member's firm for a replacement, or adding more board members to create counterweight

The warning: if all board members echo the same feedback and the CEO is not listening, "the problem is likely you" [1]. Some CEOs bring executive coaches to board meetings to calibrate their perception of board dynamics.

### Removing Board Members

This is a last resort with two options [1]:
1. **Approach the firm for a replacement**: Burns bridges but works if the company is performing well (high leverage)
2. **Add more independent directors**: Creates counterweight without direct confrontation

Companies that are top performers in a VC's portfolio have more leverage than they realize in requesting board member changes [1].
