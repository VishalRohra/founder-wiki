# Scaling and Later-Stage Operations

[[Sam Altman]] outlines common issues founders face when their startups are 12-24 months in and doing well. The central shift: before [[Product-Market Fit]], the number one job is building a great product. As the company grows past approximately 25 employees, the main job shifts from building a great product to building a great company [1].

## Management

### The Transition Point

There is very little management in the beginning, and that works well. When there are fewer than 20 employees, most companies have everyone report to the founder. That is optimal for the early stage. But what works at 20 employees can be disastrous at 40. Structure must be in place before it is painfully necessary [1].

The starting structure is simple: every employee knows who their manager is, everyone has exactly one manager, every manager knows their direct reports, and people are clustered into sensible teams. "Clarity and simplicity are important -- I wouldn't try to innovate on management structure" [1].

A clear mission and clear goals reduce the burden of management. The better the job a founder does with these, the less management feels overbearing [1].

### Common Management Failures

**Avoiding senior hires**: In the early days, hiring senior people is usually a mistake. As the company scales, adding executives who have built companies before becomes valuable [1].

**Not delegating**: Many founders take on too much personally. This works temporarily but creates a breaking point as the company grows. Delegation is essential [1].

**Delegating poorly**: Some founders delegate grunt work but retain all decisions. This fails for scalability and for making employees effective. Instead, empower employees to own decision processes. "Let them know how you think about a problem, but let them own the decision process" [1]. Delegate the things you do not want to do. Retain involvement in the one thing you care most about [1].

**Personal disorganization**: Developing a system to track tasks and follow-ups is not critical when focused purely on product, but becomes crucial as the founder becomes a manager [1].

**Undercommunicating**: After a certain point, one-on-one conversations with everyone are impossible. The solution: learn to communicate in clear, succinct writing and share it with everyone. "It's one of the highest leverage things you can do, and it scales to thousands of people" [1]. All-hands meetings at least monthly remain important, as do one-on-ones with direct reports [1].

### The Alignment Test

Altman describes a classic diagnostic: "If I asked 10 employees what the top three goals are for the company, would they say the same thing?" Founders always say yes. When Altman actually asks the employees, no two say the same thing. Founders consistently overestimate how effectively they communicate their ideas. Reiterating the roadmap and goals must become habitual [1].

## HR

Most founders ignore HR early, but continuing to ignore it as the company scales is a major mistake. Good HR means three things: a clear management structure, a way for people to raise workplace concerns, and pathways for career evolution [1].

### Performance and Compensation

Performance feedback should happen frequently, even if light. People benefit from regularly hearing how they are doing [1].

Compensation must be structured with bands to keep things fair. "People talk, and they will eventually find out co-workers' compensation levels. If it's all over the place, it can be a complete disaster" [1].

### Equity

YC company data suggests that the most successful companies give out significant equity. Altman advises planning to give away 3-5% of the company each year for ten years from founding. This includes [refresher grants](https://blog.wealthfront.com/how-long-should-you-stay-at-your-job/) for employees approaching vesting cliffs. "You never want an employee in a place where they start thinking about leaving because they've vested three out of their four years. Always stay in front of people's vesting schedules" [1].

### Hiring Process and Diversity

At the 12-24 month mark with [[Product-Market Fit]] achieved, hire a full-time recruiter. Before that is premature. Additional practices [1]:
- **Build a diverse team**: Unity of vision, diversity of backgrounds. Myopic culture is less effective.
- **Announce offers internally**: Up to a few hundred employees, share potential job offers on an internal mailing list. Often someone will know something relevant about the candidate.
- **Structure onboarding**: Have a program so every new employee's first week is planned.
- **Build paths for early employees**: The original 10-15 employees may not fit new executive roles but are loved by peers and highly productive. Proactively discuss their career paths.

### Burnout Monitoring

Building a company is a marathon. Working 100-hour weeks is not sustainable. Monitor the team for burnout, ensure people take vacations, have new challenges, and do new things [1].

## Company Productivity

Building a company that can innovate repeatedly is the hardest thing in business. Most companies do one great thing then stop. Alignment is the key lever -- companies become unproductive when people are not on the same page or do not understand priorities [1].

The solution is a clear mission, roadmap, and goals. Everyone should know the roadmap for the next 3-6 months or a year. Transparent communication through weekly management meetings with direct reports and monthly all-hands meetings is critical [1].

### Longer-Term Productivity Practices

**Offsites**: Take the best people outside the normal workspace for a weekend of big-picture thinking [1].

**Legal document organization**: Assign someone to collect every agreement the company has signed. This saves headaches later [1].

**FP&A**: Building a great financial model of the business enables optimization that most founders miss. Most companies do not hire for this until they have a few hundred employees, but doing it sooner is worthwhile [1].

**Dedicated fundraiser**: Hiring a full-time fundraiser after the B round, whose job is to prepare for the C round, yields better results and less dilution than hiring an investment banker later [1].

**Unit economics**: Figure out how to make more money from each user than you spend, sooner rather than later. Most great companies historically had good unit economics soon after monetization, even if the company as a whole lost money. "It can be tempting to paper over a problem with the business by spending more money instead of fixing the product or service, but this is a major trap" [1].

**Runway management**: Even with great unit economics, never get down to just a couple months of cash in the bank [1].

## Founder Psychology

As the company succeeds, the highs get higher but the lows keep getting lower. People start rooting against the founder. Journalists and internet critics find ways to pick apart the work. Learning to ignore critics early is important [1].

Altman recommends making a genuine long-term commitment: "Have a strategy that assumes you will work on your company for the next 10 years -- it can be an extreme advantage that few founders leverage" [1].

Taking vacations is non-negotiable. Founders who go three or four years without a real vacation will burn out [1].

Focus must be maintained. It is what made the company successful in the first place. Common focus-killers include burnout and talking to potential acquirers [1].

## Marketing and PR

Ignore marketing and PR for a long time. "Press will not make your startup." But once the product is working, shift to caring about it a little [1].

Critical rules:
- Never outsource key messaging to a PR firm or head of marketing. The founder must figure out the company's message [1].
- Pick three or four journalists to develop close relationships with. "No journalist wants to talk to a PR firm; they're much happier to hear from the founder" [1].

## Dealmaking

Business development can start to matter at 12-24 months, assuming a great product exists. Key principles [1]:
- Develop personal connections with counterparts. No one wants to feel used transactionally.
- Set up competitive situations. This is basic negotiation -- you get good terms when you have alternatives and stay persistent.
- Ask for what you want. "Most of the time you won't get laughed out of the room, and you might actually get it" [1].
