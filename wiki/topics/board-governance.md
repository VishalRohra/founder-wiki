# Board Governance

Board management is one of the most anxiety-producing aspects of scaling a startup. [[Anu Hariharan]] notes that "this is the first time the founder/CEO is subject to external supervision, and the board has powers that include the firing of the CEO and the senior management." Despite this, boards, when composed and managed well, are a significant resource: "they will challenge you to make better decisions but are on your side."

## Purpose of a Board

The board's main role is to guide major decisions: hiring/firing senior management, approving corporate actions (compensation, stock options, budget), and offering strategic guidance. Every Delaware corporation must have a board once it starts operating. Initially the board is just the founders, functioning as a technical body for approvals. "Setting up a board" typically means adding outside directors after raising money.

## Board Composition

- **Series A and B investors** typically join the board. Later-stage growth investors usually do not ask for seats.
- **Early stage**: 3-5 seats. **Public company**: 7-9 seats.
- The #1 attribute for a board member is **trust**, built only through time. [[Anu Hariharan]] recommends getting to know VC partners 6-9 months before fundraising. Mathilde Colin of Front [documented her approach](https://medium.com/@collinmathilde/front-series-a-deck-f2e2775a419b).

**Independent directors**: Many founders are adding independents earlier. Examples from YC companies:
- Justin Kan (Atrium) added [[Michael Seibel]], a former co-founder, to his Series B board
- Brex founders (Henrique and Pedro) added Victor Lazarte, a founder from Brazil who had known them since age 16
- Faire founders added Brian Grassadonia (Square Cash lead, their former manager at Square)

These choices reflect CEOs' desire for "deeply trusted independent board directors." As Hariharan notes: "this does not mean you add your friend to your board. You need someone you really trust, who is 100% independent, and has the ability to help both you and your team as you scale."

For board structure and control terms, see [[Fundraising]] (Series A section) and [[Series A Term Sheet]].

## Managing Board Meetings

### Meeting Schedule

- **Series A stage**: Informal meetings (like 1:1s), bi-weekly to bi-monthly
- **Series B+**: Quarterly, all meetings scheduled a year in advance, in-person only

### Agenda and Length (~3 hours)

The most common mistake is treating board meetings as status updates. The best CEOs allocate only the first 45 minutes for highlights/lowlights and KPIs, then use the remaining time for 1-2 strategic deep dives.

**Example agenda from Gusto** (shared with permission):
- Highlights / Lowlights (10 mins)
- Performance / KPIs (50 mins)
- Strategic topic 1: 3-Year Strategic Plan (45 mins)
- Strategic topic 2: Engineering Org -- Current and Future (45 mins)
- Closed Session (30 mins: approvals, sensitive topics, feedback)

This structure means 1/3 of time on business state, 2/3 on strategic issues. Hariharan recommends identifying and aligning on key KPIs early to "train" board members to process them quickly.

### Board Deck

The deck is a tool for discussion, not an end in itself. Bryan Schrier from Sequoia has written on [preparing a board deck](https://www.sequoiacap.com/article/preparing-a-board-deck/).

**Example structure from Gusto** (30-50 pages):
- Highlights / Lowlights: 1 page
- Performance / KPIs: 15-20 pages
- Strategic topic 1 (5-10 pages): options considered, decision framework, product roadmap, areas not pursuing, known vs. unknown risks, economic value assessment
- Strategic topic 2 (5-10 pages): current org, hiring scale, future org (18 months), talent assessment, retention/development/recruitment plan
- Appendix: health of organization (10 slides), hiring metrics, close rates, employee pulse survey

### Pre-Meeting Preparation

**Gusto's approach**: Send a pre-read board deck at least a week in advance. Collect questions from each board member in a Google Doc three days before. Answer all questions in the doc by the morning of the meeting. The in-meeting discussion then focuses on the two strategic topics.

**Brex's timeline** (managed by co-founders, CFO, and Chief of Staff):
- T-28 days: Identify two strategic topics, align with executive team
- T-18 days: Outline topics, write/collect content, share draft with exec team
- T-11 days: Execs comment/edit, finance team finalizes including formatting
- T-7 days: Final review, distribute deck to board
- T-4 days: Board submits questions (particularly on KPIs)
- T-2 days: Brex consolidates and answers all questions
- T-0: Set expectations upfront for which sections need most time

### Executive Involvement

Two approaches to involving the executive team:
1. Ask all executives to attend the entire meeting (risk: meeting becomes a presentation, not a discussion)
2. Specific executives join for their agenda item (~45 minutes each) (preferred by Hariharan)

The second approach achieves: a meatier discussion, executives hearing board feedback directly, and more time for the CEO and board to discuss sensitive matters privately.

General counsel and/or outside counsel should attend all board meetings to capture notes and address attorney-client privilege matters.

### Monthly Updates

In addition to quarterly meetings, send a 2-page monthly update email. Typical contents: last month's performance summary, hiring update, burning issues, specific asks for help.

**Brex** always includes two specific asks: introductions to business partners/customers, and help with key open roles. **GitLab** asks for introductions to specific individuals at potential customer companies and publicly applauds investors who have helped, "putting pressure on the entire board to do the same."

Monthly updates accomplish three things: (1) board members go above and beyond to help with specific asks, (2) the board arrives better prepared for in-person meetings, (3) it forces the CEO to step back and reflect.

As [[Jeff Bezos]] has [argued about memos](https://www.inc.com/carmine-gallo/jeff-bezos-bans-powerpoint-in-meetings-his-replacement-is-brilliant.html), "written narrative is more compelling in sharing ideas than bullet points and slides."

### Board Letters / Memos

Some CEOs write longer reflections:
- **Peter Reinhardt (Segment)** writes a 3-5 page memo focusing on highlights, lowlights, where he would like to spend more time vs. where he is spending time, and his own strengths and weaknesses as CEO
- **Faire** uses written memos for strategic priorities during board meetings, including decision frameworks, reasoning, uncertainties, and blind spots

## Navigating Board Issues

### Mixed Messages

Conflicting viewpoints are a good thing. The board's job is to push on strategy and sharpen thinking. If conflict makes the conversation unproductive, take it offline to 1:1 or small group meetings.

A YC CEO resolved conflicting board opinions about product priorities by creating a decision-making framework (economic value, resources, cash needs, competitive threat). "Coming up with the framework also made it quite clear which path was the right one." A clear framework is especially helpful "when you decide to go against the board's advice."

Key principle: "While board members provide important input all CEOs should seriously consider, the CEO has the authority to make the best decision on behalf of the company."

### Asking Board Members for Help

Get to know where different board members can be most helpful and follow up directly outside board meetings. Do not waste time updating every board member 1:1 on every topic or before every meeting. "Allow your board members to challenge you and the team during the board meetings and encourage conflicting views."

### Balancing Helpfulness vs. Control

"Be cautious of board members who are trying to make decisions for you." Great board members ask questions to flesh out CEO thinking rather than stating opinions.

Hariharan cites Micky Malka of Ribbit Capital (Brex board member): when asked "What should our typical credit loss rate be?", Malka would share examples of 10 startups with their credit loss rate trajectories, pros and cons, but refrain from giving one definitive answer. "He empowers the founders to make the decision."

### Problem Board Members

If a board member is unhelpful: meet 1:1 with specific examples from past meetings. Focus on the issue, not the person.

If a board member works around the CEO (e.g., having direct meetings with senior management without the CEO's knowledge): one YC CEO resolved this by having 1:1 conversations with other board members, gathering feedback, and directly approaching the disruptive member. It took about 6 months to resolve. The CEO then worked with the rest of the board to set up a framework for when and how the board can intervene.

### Removing Board Members

Options: (1) approach someone senior at the board member's firm to seek a replacement (will burn bridges), or (2) add more board members (independent or via new investment rounds) to dilute the influence. If the company is performing well, the firm will work hard to find a replacement. If not, leverage is limited.

Critical self-check: "If all your board members are echoing the same feedback and you are not listening, then the problem is likely you." Some CEOs bring an executive coach to 1-2 board meetings to get feedback on dynamics.
