# AI and Jobs

The impact of AI on employment is one of the most debated topics among startup founders and investors. The consensus among YC partners and AI company leaders is nuanced: software engineering jobs as they exist today will not exist in five to ten years, the transition will be painful, but the long-term outcome is a world of abundance where humans focus on higher-order problems [1] [2] [3] [4].

## The Software Engineering Impact

Tom Blomfield's analysis: AI coding agents are to software engineers what the combine harvester was to farmers [1]. The productivity increase will be extreme -- a single person with AI tools is already ten times more productive than a current professional developer, and the capability curve is steep [1]. In the current YC batch, a third to half of companies write most of their code using AI agents [1].

The Jevons paradox argument -- that cheaper software will increase demand enough to preserve jobs -- is partially correct: demand for software will increase dramatically [1]. But Blomfield argues the automation will increase faster than demand: "I don't think it's even productivity per person because I think the person just goes to zero" [1].

Lieb's counterpoint: in the short to medium term, these tools give high-agency individuals superpowers. Getting to $1 million, $10 million, $100 million of revenue with a software idea is "happening faster than ever" [1]. The question is not whether jobs change but whether individuals adapt to leverage the tools.

## Beyond Software: Knowledge Work

The same pattern is spreading to law, medicine, finance, and accounting [1]. Two years ago at YC, the idea that AI could automate lawyering or accounting was "fringe." Now companies like Legora (AI for lawyers) are among the fastest-growing YC companies ever [1].

Blomfield observes that every industry is worried about AI's impact, and boards are asking every CEO "What is AI going to do to our business?" [1]. It is becoming a competitive disadvantage not to embrace AI tools: "Would you hire the lawyer that doesn't use a computer? Of course not. It's getting to that point with AI" [1].

## The MIT Study and What It Means

A widely cited MIT study on AI's impact on employment was broadly misunderstood [3]. The study's actual findings are more nuanced than the headlines suggested, and the implications for startups are specific: AI augmentation (making existing workers more productive) is happening faster than AI replacement (eliminating roles entirely). Startups that position as productivity tools for existing workers face less regulatory and adoption resistance than those positioned as replacements [3].

## The Optimistic Case

Lieb articulates the optimistic view: "If you just look at each step along this progression and you had to choose as an individual, do I want to live in this world or the world one click further into the future? I think in all cases your experience and what you can do with your life is going to be better in the next click forward" [1].

Amjad Masad rejects the dystopian framing: "The future of work is more human, more interactive, more multimodal. Once the making of things gets easier, the bottleneck goes back to how many ideas you can have" [4].

## The Painful Transition

Blomfield's biggest worry is the transition speed: "You could see hundreds of millions of people displaced and the idea that they're going to retrain in a different job I think is going to be extremely painful. And I can see the societal impact and turmoil being very, very grave for 10 or 20 years" [1].

Jobs involving physical work -- surgeons, plumbers, electricians -- are relatively protected [1]. Regulatory barriers will slow AI adoption in medicine and law even when AI is "provably better," analogous to self-driving car regulation [1].

## What to Do Now

The actionable advice from multiple speakers converges on:
1. **Stay current with AI tools** -- even if they are not perfect for your industry yet, they will cross the tipping point [1].
2. **Get good at identifying human problems** -- this skill becomes the most valuable in a world where building is easy [1].
3. **Build domain expertise** -- understanding a specific part of the world deeply is the moat that AI cannot easily replicate [1].
