# Startup Ideas

The single most important thing a startup does is choose what to build. How to find, evaluate, and generate startup ideas is one of the most reinforced topics in the YC canon, addressed by [[Paul Graham]], [[Michael Seibel]], [[Jared Friedman]], [[Dalton Caldwell]], [[Garry Tan]], and [[Harj Taggar]] across 20+ sources spanning 2005 to 2025.

The AI era has reopened the idea space. [[Jared Friedman]], [[Garry Tan]], [[Harj Taggar]], and Diana Hu argue that ideas which failed in the 2010s -- recruiting marketplaces, full-stack law firms, personalized tutors -- are now suddenly viable because LLMs can replace the human operations layer that destroyed gross margins [18]. The canonical startup advice of "sell before you build" has shifted: in the AI era, following your own curiosity and exploring what the latest technology makes possible is often a better path to finding ideas [18].

## The Organic Method: Notice, Don't Think Up

Graham argues across multiple essays spanning 2005 to 2014 that the way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas, but to look for problems -- preferably problems you have yourself [1]. The very best startup ideas tend to have three things in common: they are something the founders themselves want, that they themselves can build, and that few others realize are worth doing [1]. Microsoft, Apple, Yahoo, Google, and Facebook all began this way.

The verb Graham insists on is "notice," not "think up" [1]. At YC they call ideas that grow naturally out of the founders' own experiences "organic" startup ideas. The formula Graham distills:

> "Live in the future, then build what's missing." [1]

This phrase, attributed to Paul Buchheit, captures the method: place yourself at the leading edge of a rapidly changing field, and the ideas that will seem uncannily prescient to others will seem obvious to you [1]. Drew Houston realized he had forgotten his USB stick and thought "I really need to make my files live online." Bill Gates and Paul Allen heard about the Altair and thought "I bet we could write a Basic interpreter for it" [1].

Friedman reinforces this as the best approach in his 2020 Startup School lecture: "The best way to have startup ideas is to notice them organically. There are great startup ideas all around you, and when you have a prepared mind, you see them everywhere. The problem is that when people sit down and try to think of startup ideas, they tend to think of bad ones" [7]. He recommends becoming an expert on something valuable -- particularly by working at an existing startup, since many successful YC companies came from ideas founders had while working at someone else's startup [7].

Graham's advice to college students boils down to two words: "just learn" [4]. Become good at something that matters, work on problems that interest you, with people you like and respect [4].

Friedman argues that in the AI era, this organic method is even more powerful: "If you're living at the edge of the future and you're exploring the latest technology, there's so many great startup ideas, you're very likely to just bump into one" [18].

## Start with Problems, Not Ideas

Seibel frames idea generation through the lens of problems rather than ideas [8]. "There's a common misconception that your idea has to be great in order to start a company," he says, citing Justin.tv's original concept -- an online reality TV show -- which eventually became Twitch and sold to Amazon for nearly a billion dollars [8].

His recommended sequence [8] [12]:

1. **Start with a problem you have a personal connection to.** Having a personal connection helps for two reasons: you can judge whether a solution is in the ballpark, and when solutions fail, your connection to the problem sustains motivation through discouragement.
2. **Ask why you are uniquely qualified.** Not in a resume sense, but whether you have a perspective, angle, or personal experience that others lack.
3. **Build an MVP quickly.** Release it in days or weeks, not months.
4. **Hand-pick initial users.** The goal is not to see how many people want to use the product, but whether it solves the problem for any group of people.

Seibel advises keeping "problem books" rather than idea books: "Write down notes on the problems that you encounter on a daily basis or that your friends complain about" [8].

## The Idea Quality Score

Friedman provides a concrete formula for evaluating startup ideas, adapted from [[Dalton Caldwell]]'s framework [7]. Score each idea on four criteria and take the average:

1. **How big is this idea?** Look for existing large companies doing something similar, or a market that is small today but has a real chance of being huge. Coinbase is the example: Bitcoin was tiny in 2012, but the potential was enormous [7].
2. **Founder/market fit.** Are the founders domain experts? Flexport's founder Ryan worked in international trade for 10 years before starting the company -- "10 out of 10 founder/market fit" [7].
3. **How sure are you that you're solving a big problem?** Ideally from personal experience [7].
4. **Do you have a new, important insight?** Airbnb's founders had actually tried hosting strangers and discovered it was fun, when everyone else assumed it was dangerous [7].

Additional positive signals: you are making something you personally want; the idea only recently became possible due to a technological change; there are successful companies doing something similar elsewhere [7].

## The "Why Now" Framework

[[Garry Tan]] adds a dimension Graham does not emphasize: timing. The question great investors ask every startup is "Why now?" [14]. Tan identifies three categories of "why now":

### 1. New Technology

Technology progresses on curves. The key is orders-of-magnitude improvement, not incremental progress. General Magic tried to build a smartphone in 1994: $1,400, 1 MB of RAM, 480 bytes/second modem. Thirteen years later, the iPhone 1 had 16,000x the storage, 600x the network speed, and cost one-third the price [14].

"Look for the 10x. Look for the 100x. That's why tech is the most powerful thing" [14].

### 2. Behavior Change

Once a technology is broadly adopted, human behavior shifts, creating new opportunities. Smartphone adoption went from fringe to universal around 2012 -- exactly when Instacart launched. The founder explained: "This is the first time in history where smartphones are so prevalent that everyone has them. As a result, we can utilize people who have them to pick and deliver groceries" [14].

Webvan had failed 11 years earlier after burning $800M because the technology was not ready [14].

### 3. Regulation

Government mandates create massive waves of business activity. Parker Conrad spotted that Obamacare would mandate millions of small businesses to offer health insurance -- driving adoption of Zenefits, Gusto, and later Rippling [14]. Climate regulation (the Inflation Reduction Act) is creating similar opportunities in decarbonization [14].

## Don't Think Like an Investor

Caldwell and Seibel observe a damaging pattern: founders who approach idea selection the way a VC or management consultant would -- market sizing, competitive analysis, TAM calculations -- before writing a line of code or talking to a single customer [16].

Warning signs [16]:
- You want to present a slide deck before you have any customers
- You have 15 slides of market opportunity but no traction
- You describe your company the way you would describe a very large company
- Your idea came from reading VC Twitter, not from personal experience

What YC actually looks for: "Does your idea make any sense? Do you know anything about it? Do you have the tech skills to build the thing? And can you get any customers?" [16]

## Where Great Startup Ideas Come From: Timing, Contrarianism, and Scale

Caldwell and Seibel unpack three common themes from Airbnb, Coinbase, and Stripe [10]:

1. **Timing was critical.** In each case, pre-existing competitors existed but an opportunity to make something 10x better had opened up [10].
2. **Most people thought the ideas were horrible.** Either too hard to execute or simply bad ideas [10].
3. **The opportunities turned out to be bigger than the founders knew.** Each market expanded far beyond initial expectations [10].

## Simple Products That Became Big Companies

Caldwell and Seibel observe that many of the biggest YC companies started as remarkably simple products [11]. OpenSea began as a basic marketplace for CryptoKitties. Gusto started by doing payroll for a handful of small businesses. The lesson: founders should not wait for a complex, impressive idea. A simple product that solves a real problem can grow into something enormous [11].

## The Well Model: Depth Over Breadth

When evaluating an idea, Graham uses the metaphor of digging a hole [1]. Nearly all good startup ideas are wells -- narrow and deep. Made-up startup ideas are broad and shallow: "lots of people are mildly interested in a social network for pet owners" [1].

Microsoft was a well when they made Altair Basic. There were only a couple thousand Altair owners, but without this software they were programming in machine language [1]. Facebook had the same shape: exclusively for Harvard students, of which there are only a few thousand, but those few thousand users wanted it a lot [1].

The critical test: "Who wants this right now? Who wants this so much that they'll use it even when it's a crappy version one made by a two-person startup they've never heard of?" If you cannot answer that, the idea is probably bad [1].

A well must have a path out. Facebook started at Harvard but colleges are similar enough that it spread rapidly through all colleges, and from there to everyone else [1].

## AI Has Reopened the Idea Maze

The YC partners observe that AI has shifted the walls of the idea maze [18]. Entire categories of startups that failed in the 2010s are now viable because the underlying technology has changed:

**Recruiting marketplaces.** [[Harj Taggar]] ran TripleByte for five years starting in 2015. The company raised $50 million and reached $24 million ARR, but the human evaluation piece prevented it from scaling with software-like margins [18]. Today, companies like Mercor use LLMs to do the evaluation piece on day one [18].

**Full-stack law firms and services.** Justin Kan started Atrium, a full-stack law firm that failed because AI was not good enough to automate the knowledge work [18]. Now Legora, a YC company building AI tools for lawyers, is "one of the fastest growing companies we've ever funded" [18].

**Personalized education.** The dream of a personalized tutor in every pocket was never achievable before LLMs [18]. Speak, a language learning company, doubled down when GPT-3/3.5 arrived. Revision Dojo provides AI-powered exam prep with high DAU and power users [18].

**The full-stack thesis revived.** Balaji Srinivasan's "full stack startups" thesis from the 2010s failed because the human operations layer destroyed gross margins [18]. Now, with agents replacing the operations team, full-stack companies can have software-like margins [18].

## Tarpit Ideas

Caldwell and Seibel address tarpit ideas -- ideas that seem promising because many founders attempt them, but that consistently fail [17]. Common examples include social apps, dating apps, and general-purpose AI assistants. The key is not that these ideas are inherently bad but that they look deceptively achievable and attract founders without the specific advantages needed to win.

A new technology wave (like AI) does not eliminate tarpit ideas -- it creates new ones. "There will be tarpit ideas in AI just as there were tarpit ideas in mobile and social" [17].

## Sitcom Ideas and the Plausibility Trap

The most dangerous bad ideas are not the obviously bad ones but the plausible-sounding ones. Graham calls these "sitcom" startup ideas [1]. A social network for pet owners does not sound obviously mistaken. But the lukewarm reaction -- "Yeah, maybe I could see using something like that" -- sums to zero users [1].

Friedman codifies this as the SISP problem: "Solution In Search of a Problem" [7]. These are ideas like "Uber for plumbers" -- solutions looking for problems. "We see this so commonly at YC that we have a term for it" [7].

## The Still Life Effect: Why Smart People Have Bad Ideas

In a 2005 essay, Graham diagnosed why intelligent hackers end up with terrible startup ideas [5]. The core mechanism is the "still life effect": you come up with a random idea, plunge into it, and then at each point feel you have put so much time into it that this must be the idea [5].

Graham experienced this himself. In January 1995, he and Robert Morris started a company called Artix to put art galleries on the Web. Galleries were not interested. Art dealers are "the most technophobic people on earth" [5]. Eventually they pivoted to Viaweb, which succeeded [5].

## Dangerous Filters

Four unconscious filters block founders from seeing good ideas. Graham identified the first two [1]; Friedman expanded to four [7]:

**The [[Schlep Filter]]**: Most programmers unconsciously shrink from ideas involving tedious work. Stripe is the canonical example -- thousands of programmers saw the payments opportunity but avoided it [1] [7].

**The Unsexy Filter**: Prevents founders from working on boring problems. Gusto is the example -- thousands of programmers knew payroll software was terrible, but no one tried to fix it because the problem was boring [7].

**Ideas that seem too ambitious**: Founders instinctively shy away from ambitious ideas because they are intimidating. But these are often the ideas that turn into the biggest companies [7].

**Ideas with existing competitors**: Founders instinctively avoid crowded markets. But a crowded market actually signals demand. "When founders go into spaces with no existing competitors, they usually find out that the reason there are no competitors is because nobody wants the product" [7].

## Seven Recipes for Generating Ideas

Friedman provides seven concrete recipes, ranked from best to worst [7]:

1. **Start with what your team is especially good at.** About half of YC's most successful companies trace back to this recipe. Examples: SnapDocs (founder worked in mortgages for 10 years), Lattice (founders noticed bad performance review software), Mixpanel (founder worked at Slide which had built internal analytics tools) [7].
2. **Think of things you wish someone else would build for you.** DoorDash: the founders wanted Thai food delivered in the suburbs [7].
3. **What would you work on for 10 years, even if it didn't succeed?** Boom Supersonic came from this [7].
4. **Look for things that changed in the world recently.** PlanGrid put construction blueprints on tablets -- made possible by the iPad [7].
5. **Find successful companies and look for new variants.** Warning: ideas like "Uber for X" are often SISPs [7].
6. **Ask people you know for problems they want solved.** Works best when asking other startup founders [7].
7. **Look for broken industries.** LendUp disrupted payday lending [7].

Two additional AI-era recipes from Taggar [18]:
8. **Collapse multi-sided marketplaces.** What marketplaces that are three-sided or four-sided suddenly become two-sided with AI? Any marketplace with a human intermediary is a candidate [18].
9. **Revive failed full-stack ideas.** Look at tech-enabled services that failed in the 2010s because human operations destroyed margins [18].

## Evaluating Problem Quality

Seibel provides a diagnostic framework [8]:

- **Frequency**: How often does the user encounter the problem? Car shopping happens once every seven years -- the real customer is the car dealer, who has the problem every day [8].
- **Intensity**: How painful is the problem? Uber works because transportation is high-intensity and high-frequency [8].
- **Willingness to pay**: Starting with a price is almost always better than starting free [8].
- **How easy are customers to find?** If your customers are ridiculously hard to reach, you need a solution for that upfront [8].

## First Principles Thinking

Tan argues that billion-dollar startups are built by founders who break free from analogy-based thinking through three qualities [15]:

1. **Curiosity**: asking "why" repeatedly. The Toyota Production System's five-whys analysis exemplifies this [15].
2. **Empathy**: getting direct experience with the customer. Airbnb's founders literally slept under the same roof as their first users [15].
3. **Serenity**: accepting what you cannot change and fighting battles you can win. DoorDash retreated to the suburbs where competitors were not, found PMF and profitability there, then used those profits to win the cities [15].

## Following Your Passion: A Nuanced View

Caldwell and Seibel address the "follow your passion" advice directly [13]. The naive version -- "I'm passionate about X, so I'll build a startup about X" -- often leads to solutions in search of problems. The productive version is to develop passion through deep engagement with a real problem [13].

## Ideas That Were Mocked Before Succeeding

Many of the largest companies started with ideas that seemed unpopular or foolish [19]. Airbnb was rejected by every VC. Uber was dismissed as a luxury black car service. Instagram was a pivot from Burbn. Slack emerged from the wreckage of a failed video game called Glitch [19]. The pattern: the idea sounds bad to most people, which is precisely why the opportunity exists [19].

## New Technology Creates New Businesses

Caldwell and Seibel argue that major technology shifts (like AI) create the best startup opportunities because they enable things that were previously impossible [20]. The pattern repeats: the internet created Google, Amazon, and eBay; mobile created Uber, Instacart, and Snapchat; AI is creating the next wave. The founders best positioned to capture these opportunities are those who deeply understand the new technology [20].

## Competition Is Not What Kills You

A crowded market is a good sign -- it means demand exists and no solution is good enough [1]. "Way more startups hose themselves than get crushed by competitors" [3]. The three main self-destruction patterns: internal disputes, inertia, and ignoring users [3].

## The Marginal Niche Mistake

Most YC applicants suffer from choosing a small, obscure niche to avoid competition [2]. Graham compares this to a child closing his eyes when a fly ball comes his way. "If you make anything good, you're going to have competitors, so you may as well face that" [2].

## Having No Idea Is Not Fatal

Graham argues that not having an idea at the outset is not necessarily a problem, because most startups change their idea anyway. In the average YC startup, he estimates 70% of the idea is new at the end of the first three months [6]. Friedman reinforces: "You need a good enough initial idea, combined with great execution" -- Google was the 20th search engine, Facebook the 20th social network [7].

## The MLOps Lesson: Being Early Is Not Being Wrong

Friedman recounts a cautionary-then-vindication tale: in 2019, there were more YC applications for ML tooling companies than for companies applying ML to products [18]. The tooling companies had no customers because the ML did not work yet. Companies like Replicate stopped working on their product for months -- then exploded overnight when image diffusion models arrived. Deepgram, founded by two string theory PhDs, built speech-to-text technology that "didn't really work that well for a long time" -- then exploded when voice agents needed speech-to-text infrastructure [18].

The lesson: following genuine curiosity about technology, as [[Paul Graham]] advised, is how these founders ended up positioned correctly [18].
