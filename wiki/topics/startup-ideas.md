# Startup Ideas

The single most important thing a startup does is choose what to build. [[Paul Graham]] argues across multiple essays spanning 2005 to 2014 that the way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas, but to look for problems -- preferably problems you have yourself [1]. The very best startup ideas tend to have three things in common: they are something the founders themselves want, that they themselves can build, and that few others realize are worth doing [1]. Microsoft, Apple, Yahoo, Google, and Facebook all began this way.

## Your Idea Does Not Need to Be Great

[[Michael Seibel]] opens his advice on ideas by dismantling the assumption that a great idea is required to start a company [7]. He cofounded Justin.tv, which later became Twitch and sold to Amazon for almost a billion dollars. The original idea was to create an online reality TV show. "Clearly, it's hard to draw a line between that and creating a live video site where people watch video gamers and chat with their friends, but that's exactly what it became" [7].

The implication: the initial idea is a starting point, not a destination. Founders who wait for the perfect idea before beginning are filtering themselves out of the process.

## Start With a Problem, Not a Solution

Seibel's core advice is to start with a problem rather than an idea [7]. Starting with ideas is tricky because people immediately want to grade the idea. Starting with problems shifts the frame to evaluation criteria that are more useful:

- Do you have any personal relationship with this problem? Is it your problem, your friends' problem, your family's problem, a problem from work, from your community? [7]
- Having a personal connection to the problem helps in two ways: you can judge whether solutions are in the right ballpark, and when you are discouraged and solutions are not working, your connection to the problem sustains you [7].

Seibel recommends keeping a "problem book" rather than an idea book: "Write down notes on the problems that you encounter on a daily basis or that your friends complain about, their family complain about" [7].

## Brainstorm With Friends

The problem-discovery phase is also the perfect time to evaluate potential [[Co-Founders]] [7]. Brainstorming with friends reveals whether two people "trade ideas well" and can build on each other's thinking. "A lot of recruiting cofounders is about having really good brainstorming sessions where you talk about a problem, they elaborate on it, and you kind of realize that you can talk to each other well, you can build ideas together" [7].

## Unique Qualification

Once a problem is identified, Seibel advises asking: "Why are you uniquely qualified to work on this problem?" [7]. This does not mean credentials in the classical sense. It means: is there some angle on the problem that you understand that others do not? Some personal experience, some perspective, some way of attacking the problem that is not generally understood? [7]

For common problems, Seibel recommends studying prior attempts and existing solutions: "Look at some of the products that are trying to solve the problem or have failed to solve the problem, just so you can get a basic understanding of what other people thought their unique insight was and whether it was the same or different than yours" [7].

## Building and Testing the MVP

Seibel's advice on the MVP is direct: "Don't fall in love with it" [7]. The MVP is most likely not going to solve the problem, or will solve it very poorly. It is the first step in learning whether the problem is solvable at all.

"A lot of people fall in love with their product and are not in love with their problem or their customer. I advise the opposite. Be in love with your problem. Be in love with your cause. And treat your product in a way that it can change, it can develop, it can improve" [7].

The MVP should be released quickly -- in days or weeks. It will not be perfect and may not be something the founder is proud of. But it begins the learning process [7].

### Choosing Initial Users

Seibel argues that initial users should be "almost hand-picked" [7]. The goal with the MVP is not to see how many people want to use the product. It is to see whether, for any group of people, the product actually solves the problem [7]. "The best startups actually very heavily filter the people who are able to use the initial product and make sure that they're the right type of initial customer" [7].

See [[Getting First Users]] for the full treatment of finding and learning from early adopters.

## The Organic Method: Notice, Don't Think Up

The verb Graham insists on is "notice," not "think up" [1]. At YC they call ideas that grow naturally out of the founders' own experiences "organic" startup ideas. The most successful startups almost all begin this way. Apple, Yahoo, Google, and Facebook were not even meant to be companies at first -- they were side projects that grew out of things their founders built because there seemed a gap in the world [1] [4].

The formula Graham distills is:

> "Live in the future, then build what's missing." [1]

This phrase, attributed to Paul Buchheit, captures the method: place yourself at the leading edge of a rapidly changing field, and the ideas that will seem uncannily prescient to others will seem obvious to you [1].

Graham's advice to college students boils down to two words: "just learn" [4]. Become good at something that matters, work on problems that interest you, with people you like and respect. The component of entrepreneurship that really matters is domain expertise [4].

## The Well Model: Depth Over Breadth

When evaluating an idea, Graham uses the metaphor of digging a hole [1]. You can either dig a hole that is broad but shallow, or one that is narrow and deep -- like a well. Nearly all good startup ideas are wells.

The critical test: "Who wants this right now? Who wants this so much that they'll use it even when it's a crappy version one made by a two-person startup they've never heard of?" If you cannot answer that, the idea is probably bad [1].

## Dangerous Filters

Two unconscious filters block founders from seeing good ideas [1]:

**The [[Schlep Filter]]**: Most programmers wish they could start a startup by just writing brilliant code. They would prefer not to deal with tedious problems or get involved in messy ways with the real world. Stripe is the canonical example [1].

**The unsexy filter**: Similar to the schlep filter, except it keeps you from working on problems you despise rather than ones you fear [1].

## Idea Quality Score Formula

[[Jared Friedman]], crediting [[Dalton Caldwell]]'s "All About Pivoting" talk, presents a four-criteria scoring formula for evaluating startup ideas [8]:

1. **How big is this idea?** Look for existing large companies doing something similar (banks exist and are big, so a new kind of bank could be big). Or: the market is small today but has a real chance of being huge. Coinbase was started in 2012 when Bitcoin was tiny, but some believed it could replace all existing money [8].
2. **Founder/market fit.** Are the founders experts in the domain? Flexport's founder Ryan worked in international trade for 10 years before starting the company -- "10 out of 10 founder/market fit" [8].
3. **How sure are you that this is a big problem?** Ideally, personal experience with the problem [8].
4. **Do you have a new, important insight?** Airbnb's founders had actually tried hosting strangers and discovered it was fun, when everyone else assumed it was dangerous [8].

## Seven Recipes for Generating Ideas

Friedman lists seven recipes in order from best to worst [8]:

1. **Start with what your team is especially good at.** Generates automatic founder/market fit. Roughly half of YC's most successful companies trace back to this recipe. Examples: SnapDocs (founder worked in mortgage industry 10 years), Lattice (founders noticed bad performance review software at their company), Mixpanel (founder worked at Slide which had built internal analytics tools) [8].
2. **Things you wish someone else would build.** DoorDash: founders could not get Thai food delivered in the suburbs [8].
3. **What would you work on for 10 years even if it didn't succeed?** [[Boom Technology]] (supersonic airplane) came from this. Warning: can lead to passionate but unbusinesslike ideas [8].
4. **Things that recently became possible.** PlanGrid: construction blueprints on tablets, made possible by the iPad. Acquired for $1 billion [8].
5. **Successful companies with new variants.** Standard Cognition: Amazon Go technology for all retail stores. Warning: often produces "Uber for X" SISPs [8].
6. **Crowdsource by asking people for problems.** Works best with startup founders, who already know how to notice ideas [8].
7. **Industries that seem broken.** LendUp: replacement for payday lending. Warning: poor founder/market fit unless you already know the industry [8].

## Common Idea Mistakes

Friedman identifies four common mistakes [8]:

1. **Believing you need an amazing idea.** Google was the 20th search engine. Facebook was the 20th social network. They succeeded through execution [8].
2. **Jumping into the first idea.** If you will spend years on something, spend at least a couple of weeks deciding what to work on [8].
3. **Starting with a solution instead of a problem.** "Uber for plumbers" is a solution. What problem does it solve? This is the SISP pattern [8].
4. **Believing startup ideas are hard to find.** "They're easy to find, because there are many, many real problems left in the world" [8].

## Competition Is Not What Kills You

A crowded market is actually a good sign, because it means there is demand and none of the existing solutions are good enough [1]. Worrying that you are late is one of the signs of a good idea [1]. Friedman reinforces this: "when founders go into spaces with no existing competitors, they usually find out that the reason there are no competitors is because nobody wants the product." Dropbox launched against dozens of file-syncing solutions and beat them all with a better product [8].
