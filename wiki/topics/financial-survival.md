# Financial Survival

Nearly every startup that fails, fails by running out of money [5]. [[Paul Graham]] returns to this theme across multiple essays, developing increasingly specific frameworks for how founders should think about spending, fundraising, and the critical question of whether they will survive. YC Group Partners Brad Flora, Pete Koomen, Gustaf Alströmer, and Nicolas Dessaigne reinforce these principles with updated tactical advice for the current era [6].

## The Default Alive / Default Dead Framework

Graham's October 2015 essay introduced the most important diagnostic question for startups past the first 8-9 months [1]: assuming expenses remain constant and revenue growth continues on its current trend, will the company make it to profitability on the money it has left? "Or to put it more dramatically, by default do they live or die?" [1]

The startling finding: half the founders Graham talks to do not know the answer [1]. Trevor Blackwell built a [calculator](http://growth.tlb.org/#) to help founders find out.

This question determines the entire conversation. If the company is default alive, you can talk about ambitious new things. If it is default dead, you need to talk about how to save it [1]. See [[Default Alive or Default Dead]] for the full framework.

## The Fatal Pinch

The most dangerous scenario is what Graham calls the "fatal pinch": default dead + slow growth + not enough time to fix it [1]. Founders end up in it by not realizing that is where they are headed.

A common death spiral [1]: A startup makes something moderately appealing and has decent initial growth. They raise their first round fairly easily because the founders seem smart and the idea sounds plausible. But growth is only ok, not great. The founders convince themselves that hiring a bunch of people will boost growth. Their investors agree. But the growth never comes because the product is only moderately appealing. Now they are rapidly running out of runway. They hope further investment will save them, but with high expenses and slow growth, they are unappealing to investors. They are unable to raise more, and the company dies.

The correct action: address the fundamental problem -- that the product is only moderately appealing. Hiring people is rarely the way to fix that. More often it makes it harder. "At this early stage, the product needs to evolve more than to be 'built out,' and that's usually easier with fewer people" [1].

## The Right Way to Spend Money by Stage

The YC Group Partners provide stage-specific spending guidance [6]:

### Pre-Seed (Day Zero)

"Don't spend any dollar" [6]. The only necessary expenses are a laptop and a place to live. Most companies YC funds have spent close to zero before being funded. Many do not even have bank accounts or exist as entities yet [6].

### Post-Seed ($500K - $2M raised)

Hire one or maybe two engineers -- people you already know and have worked with. Use contractors for other roles. Founders should do sales themselves. "Don't hire for sales or marketing until product market fit" [6].

Pete Koomen describes a common office hour: "The founder says, 'I think this person would be better than me at selling my product, that I'm the only one in the world that understands.' We have to say, 'I'm so sorry, no one is coming to save you. You are on your own'" [6].

The money in the bank is time to find [[Product-Market Fit]]. Every hire reduces that time. "The more people, the slower you get to product market fit" [6].

### Post-Series A (PMF achieved)

Once genuine product-market fit exists, hiring for sales becomes appropriate. Gustaf Alströmer: "If you have actual product market fit, you can create a sales team. That's a good way to spend the money as long as you know that anyone you hire is going to make the company more money than you spend on them" [6].

The framework for hiring: each function should be monitored for when the person is overwhelmed by inbound demand. When someone is approaching their breaking point, plan the hire -- understanding that hiring takes time, so anticipate rather than react [6].

Monitor revenue per employee over time. That number should go up as the company benefits from scale [6].

### Post-Series B

Revenue quality becomes critical. Companies that fail at this stage "don't actually understand the quality of their revenue" [6]. They may have fooled themselves -- and their investors -- about net dollar retention, revenue retention, and customer retention. "The ones that I've seen where it doesn't work are the ones that don't actually understand their own retention" [6].

If the revenue is genuinely high quality, "the money becomes just fuel in the engine" [6].

## Spending Traps

### The "Miniature Big Company" Fallacy

Brad Flora identifies the core mistake: founders treat startups as miniature versions of big companies, assuming every function that exists in a big company should exist in their startup [6]. "Startups only exist to reach product-market fit. Most of the things you end up spending money on at big companies -- there is no equivalent in a smaller startup. It just doesn't exist at all" [6].

Founders get offices, hire chiefs of staff, build departments -- all of which feel validating but are irrelevant pre-PMF. "It makes you feel better more than it actually makes any difference whatsoever" [6].

### Premature Advertising

Spending on ads pre-PMF is one of the most common and pernicious mistakes [6]. It becomes addictive: ads produce growth numbers that impress investors, but the underlying business does not work. When the ad budget runs out, there is nothing left [6].

Worse, ad spending prevents founders from learning. "You don't learn anything when you get a customer from an ad at an early stage" [6]. Founders miss the opportunity to discover capital-efficient customer acquisition channels. One YC company was told to turn off AdWords for two weeks and match their numbers through any means necessary. "They ended up finding some really clever ways to scrape government documents and permits and reach out to these people" [6].

The only legitimate ad spend categories: (1) experimental and small, or (2) ROI-positive with known payback period [6].

### Branding Agencies Pre-PMF

"Sometimes the first thing founders do after raising is hire a branding agency for $50K to rebrand their company. A B2B company that nobody's going to find on their website anyway. Just such a waste of money" [6].

### The ZIRP Hangover

[[Dalton Caldwell]] and [[Michael Seibel]] describe the multi-year unwinding of ZIRP (Zero Interest Rate Policy) era spending habits [7]. During 2020-2022, companies that raised $2M at seed could quickly raise $10-15M Series A rounds and then burn $1M/month while stuck at $1M ARR. "They weren't honest with themselves. They somehow had the money and pushed the gas in all of the spending and just thinking that the revenue will catch up. But it almost never does" [6].

Signs of ZIRP recovery -- which are good signs for the company's future -- include executive turnover, founders becoming more hands-on, return to office, elimination of vanity projects, and reduction of excessive perks [7].

## Tactics for Financial Discipline

Two specific tactics from Gustaf Alströmer [6]:

1. **Send monthly investor updates.** Accountability forces discipline. Having someone else look at the numbers monthly prevents self-deception.
2. **Use two bank accounts.** Put half the money in a separate account and pretend it does not exist. "You look at your bank account and think you only have half the money that you raised."

## Overhiring: The Biggest Killer

"Don't hire too fast. Hiring too fast is by far the biggest killer of startups that raise money" [1].

Three reasons founders overhire [1]:
1. **There is so much work to do.** Naive founders think if they can hire enough people, it will all get done.
2. **Successful startups have lots of employees**, so it seems like that is what you do to be successful. But large staffs are probably more the effect of growth than the cause.
3. **They don't want to face the real reason for slow growth**: the product is not appealing enough.

VCs often push founders to overhire because kill-or-cure strategies are optimal for portfolios. "VCs want to blow you up, in one sense of the phrase or the other. But as a founder your incentives are different. You want above all to survive" [1].

Sam Altman reinforced this in a note on a draft of the essay: "I think it's roughly correct to say that YC's most successful companies have never been the fastest to hire, and one of the marks of a great founder is being able to resist this urge" [1].

Airbnb, by contrast, waited 4 months after raising money at the end of YC before hiring their first employee. The founders were overworked but were spending that time evolving Airbnb into the astonishingly successful organism it became [1].

## Plan A and Plan B

It is not safe to be default dead even if you think you can count on investors saving you [1]. Investors' interest is a function of growth. If you have steep revenue growth -- over 5x a year -- you can start to count on investor interest even if you are not profitable. But investors are fickle [1].

Graham's rule: "You should always have a plan B as well: you should know (as in write down) precisely what you'll need to do to survive if you can't raise more money, and precisely when you'll have to switch to plan B if plan A isn't working" [1].

## Raising Too Little, Too Much, or Just Right

Graham's 2006 essay identifies three fundraising-related mistakes that kill startups [2]:

**Raising too little (#11):** Startup funding is measured in time -- runway. You must take enough to get to the next step [2].

**Spending too much (#12):** The classic way to burn cash is hiring a lot of people, which bites you twice: it increases costs and slows you down [2].

**Raising too much (#13):** Taking a lot of money changes the company's atmosphere and makes it harder to change direction. "When you raise a lot of money, your company moves to the suburbs and has kids" [2].

## The Equity Equation

When trading stock for anything -- money, employees, deals -- Graham provides a formula for whether to do it: you should give up n% of your company if what you trade it for improves your average outcome enough that the (100 - n)% you have left is worth more than the whole company was before [3]. See [[Equity Equation]] for the full treatment.

## Poverty as Advantage

Graham identifies poverty as a genuine advantage for young founders [5]. Nearly every startup that fails, fails by running out of money, and a low burn rate gives more opportunity to recover from the many mistakes startups make at first [5].

Even more important than living cheaply is thinking cheaply. The Apple II was popular partly because it was cheap -- Woz designed it for himself and couldn't afford anything more. Viaweb's top-level service was $300/month, an order of magnitude below the norm, because $300 seemed like a lot to them. "Like Apple, we created something inexpensive, and therefore popular, simply because we were poor" [5].
