# Financial Survival

Graham claims that half the founders he talks to do not know whether their company is default alive or default dead. He considers this the single most important question for any startup past roughly 9 months old.

## Default Alive or Default Dead

The framework is simple: given current expenses and current revenue growth trend, will the startup reach profitability on the money it has left? If yes, the company is default alive. If no, it is default dead.

Default dead does not mean the company will die — it means the company must either raise more money or cut costs. But founders who do not know their status cannot make informed decisions about hiring, spending, or fundraising timing.

## The Fatal Pinch

The worst position is what Graham calls the fatal pinch: default dead, with slow growth, and not enough time or money to fix either. By the time founders recognize they are in a fatal pinch, it is often too late. The solution is to monitor the default alive/dead status continuously and act early.

## Plan A / Plan B

Graham recommends always having a written-down plan for surviving if the next fundraise fails. This is Plan B — typically cutting to a skeleton crew and reaching ramen profitability. The plan should include a specific date by which the founder switches from Plan A (growth mode) to Plan B (survival mode) if fundraising has not succeeded.

## Premature Scaling

Graham attributes to Paul Buchheit the observation that many startups scale a business with bad unit economics to chase impressive growth numbers. This "premature scaling" accelerates burn without improving the underlying business.

## The Hiring Trap

Hiring too fast is "by far the biggest killer of startups that raise money." Founders hire to solve slow growth, but the real problem is usually that the product is not good enough. Each hire increases burn rate. Graham argues there is "surprisingly little connection between how much a startup spends and how fast it grows."

Airbnb waited four months after raising before making their first hire. The founders did all the work themselves until they were certain what needed to be done and that they could not do it alone.
