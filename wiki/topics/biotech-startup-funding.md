# Biotech Startup Funding

[[Jared Friedman]], YC partner, argues that biotech startup fundraising in 2019 looks like tech startup fundraising looked in 2005, and the same forces that transformed tech fundraising are now transforming biotech [1]. The parallel is structural: new infrastructure has collapsed the cost to start, which enables new funding models, which shifts power to founders.

## How Tech Fundraising Changed (2005-2019)

In 2005, when YC started, venture capital access was limited. VCs preferred companies that already seemed like a sure bet, favored MBAs with executive experience over unproven technical founders, asked for onerous financial terms, and often replaced founders with favored executives. The only model of institutional seed funding was the "business incubator" where VC firms funded well-connected founders and incubated them in their offices [1].

Then the cost to start a tech company plummeted. Open source software, modern web frameworks, SaaS developer tools, cloud hosting, and better distribution channels meant technical founders could launch a product and get users with minimal funding. Once they proved the idea had merit, they could use traction to raise capital [1].

These companies needed only a small amount of money to get started, but no institutional investors made small investments. This insight led to the creation of YC and hundreds of institutional seed funds. Easy access to flexible institutional seed funding led to an explosion of tech startups [1].

Because founders raised VC only when they were further along and had leverage, the balance of power shifted. Founders increasingly retained control. Investors lost the power to fire founders and bring in executives. And when founders did run their companies, investors discovered something surprising: "despite their inexperience, the founders were often the right people to run the company" [1]. (Friedman notes this trend was accelerated by institutional seed funding, building on the logic Ben Horowitz [articulated in 2010](https://a16z.com/2010/04/28/why-we-prefer-founding-ceos/) about preferring founding CEOs.)

## The Biotech Parallel

Early-stage biotech funding in 2019 is dominated by the "venture creation model." The VC firm creates the company, assembles a team of favored executives (often from their entrepreneur-in-residence pool), incubates the startup out of the VC's offices, invests a large amount of money upfront, and takes a controlling ownership stake [1].

This model made sense when the cost to start a biotech company was high -- when no one could make progress before a VC wrote a $10M check. But new infrastructure has brought down the cost of doing biology dramatically. Founders can now make real progress proving a concept for as little as $100K [1].

### Infrastructure That Lowered Costs

Friedman catalogs the new infrastructure [1]:

- **Low-cost CROs** (contract research organizations): [WuXi Biologics](http://wuxibiologics.com), [Evotec](http://evotec.com), [ChemPartner](http://chempartner.com)
- **CRO access platforms**: [Science Exchange](https://www.scienceexchange.com/) for instantaneous, cost-effective access to CROs and scientific supplies
- **Lab space by the bench**: [MBC BioLabs](https://mbcbiolabs.com/), [Berkeley BioLabs](http://www.berkeleybiolabs.com/)
- **Lab management**: [Quartzy](https://www.quartzy.com/), [Happilabs](https://www.happilabs.org/)
- **Lab automation**: Affordable lab robots from [OpenTrons](https://opentrons.com/) for automating batch experiments
- **Computational drug discovery**: [Atomwise](https://www.atomwise.com/) for in silico experiments
- **Patent filing**: [Cognition IP](https://www.cognitionip.com/) for lower-cost patents
- **FDA submission**: [Enzyme](https://www.enzyme.com/) for streamlined regulatory submission

### YC Bio Companies as Evidence

Bio companies routinely clear major scientific hurdles during YC's short program. Therapeutics companies show effectiveness in animal models. Diagnostic companies show success with human samples. Synthetic biology companies successfully engineer cell lines [1].

**Shasqi** (2015): Jose Mejia Oneto, an MD/PhD who left orthopaedic surgery residency, had developed a technique for localizing chemotherapy delivery in academia but had not tried it in animal therapeutics. Using just YC's funding, he showed in less than three months in a breast cancer mouse model that localized delivery outperformed conventional chemo [1].

**Athelas**: Founders Tanay Tandon and Deepika started the company while still in college, making a device for at-home blood tests for oncology patients using computer vision. They built a working prototype with $40K in investment. During YC they ran a 350-patient initial study showing very good results. The device is now FDA-cleared and serves thousands of patients [1].

Friedman notes that running clinical trials for drugs remains very expensive, and biotech companies ultimately need to raise large amounts. But this is not different from top tech companies: the biggest YC software companies each raised over $1B. The key is that these companies could get started with less than $100K and de-risk enough to raise more [1].

## Predictions

Because startups can start cheaply, the tech startup model is now available for biotech: raise money incrementally, keep control, and work on your own idea rather than VC-originated ideas [1].

This path has drawn a new kind of biotech founder. Many are grad students or postdocs whose previous options were staying in academia or joining big pharma. Starting a company is now a viable third option [1].

If the pattern plays out as it did in tech [1]:
- An explosion of funding options for biotech companies
- Traditional biotech investors' controlling legal terms will go out of vogue, as happened in tech in the early 2000s
- A new crop of biotech and tech/biotech crossover seed funds will create a vibrant seed ecosystem (YC bio companies already typically raise $1-5M seed rounds after each batch)
- More biotech companies will be run by founders who care about their ideas, not by VCs and hired executives
