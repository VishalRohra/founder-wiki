# Handling Investor Rejection

YC group partners [[Michael Seibel]], [[Harj Taggar]], and Brad Flora state that one of their most common activities is "pumping belief back into founders who have had their confidence shaken by investor rejections" [1]. The YC mantra for handling rejection is: **"Believe the no. Don't believe the why."**

## Why Founders Overweight Investor Feedback

Taggar describes the asymmetry: founders are the genuine experts -- they think about their product, market, and customers day in and day out. Yet a single 30-minute Zoom call with an investor, "while like on Slack, doing email, maybe not the most engaged," can shake everything [1].

The lies founders tell themselves after a rejection [1]:

1. **"This investor is an expert."** They follow the founder on Twitter, they tweet about the market, they worked at a company in the space. "Therefore, they know everything about what I'm doing, and whatever they told me in that rejection email, that must be the reason and the problem with my startup" [1].

2. **"If I address their concern, they'll invest."** Founders believe they can convince an investor to change their mind with a follow-up email or another call. Taggar is blunt: "It's never up for debate really" [1].

3. **"The reason they gave is the real reason."** When an investor says the market is too small, the founder fixates on market size -- even when the real issues are more fundamental: no technical co-founder, negative margins, about to run out of money, no growth since launch, or building a non-software product for a software-focused investor [1].

## The Danger of Acting on Investor Feedback

Flora highlights the paradox: an investor spends two minutes writing a rejection email. Later, the founder has "pivoted your entire company because of it. Not a huge signal of conviction" [1].

Seibel describes a worse scenario: a founder comes to YC saying an investor suggested they pursue a different market, so they are pivoting. The partners' reaction: "I wish I could explain to you how this is gonna make you less attractive. Investors are aware they're not experts. If a founder actually acts on your feedback in a material way, it makes you less likely to want to invest. You don't want to invest in someone who is relying on you for product insights" [1].

## When the Same Feedback Comes from Everyone

When 23 out of 25 investors give the same reason for rejection, the advice remains nuanced. Flora: "The takeaway still isn't 'believe that.' It's 'think about it. Assess it. Dig into it'" [1]. Do not take it at face value, but do not ignore it either. Write it down. But "don't internalize it and absorb it into your mindset for the company. You should know about it, but not soak it in" [1].

Taggar describes his personal algorithm: "The first time they tell me there's a rejection and a why, I ignore it completely. If they've gotten 15, 20, 25 rejections, I then ask, 'Are the whys consistent?' And only if then the whys are consistent do I even think this is a data point to be considered at all" [1]. This requires pitching many investors -- one or two rejections produce no useful signal.

## The Real Reasons Investors Say No

The partners identify the actual (usually unspoken) reasons investors reject founders [1]:

**The founder does not seem like a leader.** Flora: "You sit across from the person, and in the course of the conversation, you think, 'I have a really hard time seeing this person building and running and leading a large organization'" [1]. This does not mean the founder must be that person today, but they must seem like they are aspiring to become one [1].

**Pattern matching and stack ranking.** Seibel makes the underlying mechanics explicit: investors do two things. First, they pattern match: "Do you pattern match to founders I've had success with in the past?" Second, they stack rank: "Of all the founders I've worked with and are currently pitching me, where do you rank?" [1]. The honest but brutal reason is often "you might be like the kind of founders I've done well with, but you're just not as good as the other ones I'm talking to right now" [1].

**Communication failures.** Taggar notes that the reason can be as simple as "you're not communicating clearly. I don't really understand what you're saying" [1]. Flora adds: "I don't believe your numbers. You gave me conflicting numbers and cast some doubt" [1]. These are self-inflicted wounds that are fixable.

## Founders Change Too

Seibel gives investors credit: founders improve between meetings. "You might reject a company when the founders haven't actually gone out and spoke to any users, or they're two weeks into it. But then a month of real work, they come back, and the same founders can just seem different. They've got real insights. They see more. Their confidence has grown" [1].

This is why 60% of the companies that do YC were rejected at some point in the past [1]. Seibel doubts any other fund, school, or selective institution looks back at failed applicants and gives them another try [1].

## The Practical Takeaway: Change the Facts

The partners converge on a single piece of advice: change the facts of the business, not the words of the pitch [1].

Taggar advises founders to keep a list of investors who said no but seemed reasonable, and send them monthly updates. "Shockingly, if things start going well, those same investors will reach out, change their mind" [1]. But founders must understand: "You can't convince them to change their mind with words, but you can with actions" [1].

Seibel illustrates the disconnect: "The founder says, 'What do I say to this investor to get them to change their mind?' And we say, 'Tell them about the 10 new customers you just closed.' And it takes a second for that to land, realizing that they need to go get 10 more customers and then tell them about it. Not just write something different back to the investor" [1].

Flora frames it as the secret behind YC's model: "With multiple applications, we can see progress. And progress is exciting to invest in" [1].
