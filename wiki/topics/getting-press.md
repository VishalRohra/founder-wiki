# Getting Press

[[Michael Seibel]] describes the press strategy he advises for YC companies, built on advice he received from Mike Arrington and M.G. Siegler at an SV Angel CEO conference [1]. The core principle is four words:

**"Treat PR like biz dev."**

Before hearing this advice, Seibel wasted mental energy on presumed special rules for press: catchy subject lines, blasting lists of a hundred reporters, writing press releases. Most of these efforts failed. Treating PR like business development means developing relationships with reporters as people, getting introduced through warm connections instead of cold emails, and creating a fair value exchange [1].

## Step One: Generate News

Founders commonly pitch reporters profile pieces and stories that could be written at any time. Reporters do not find these valuable. What makes a valuable story is news -- something timely, something that makes more sense to talk about today than tomorrow [1].

Four categories of startup news [1]:

**Product Launches and Features**: Things coming out today, not yesterday or tomorrow. User-facing features. At Socialcam, Seibel would reach out to the press for video filters, soundtrack capability, or user profiles.

**Product/Sales Milestones**: Hitting a million users, $100K MRR, a million guests per night (as with Airbnb). The number should be as big as possible relative to the startup's size. Seibel recommends sandbagging: wait until 25% past the milestone to announce, so the next milestone is that much closer.

**Significant BD Deals or Customers**: Landing a team at Airbnb or Instacart, or partnering with GM to build self-driving cars. If you have their permission, consider announcing it.

**Fundraising**: For better or worse, fundraising is conflated with traction and success. Angel rounds or Series A/B/etc. are considered news by most reporters and noticed by potential employees.

## Step Two: Make Your Own Press Contacts

Reporters want to talk to CEOs and cofounders, not PR people. As an early-stage founder, building reporter relationships and making pitches directly produces a higher success rate and costs less time and money. Seibel's team spent over $100,000 on PR agencies and PR people as an early-stage startup before learning this [1].

The best way to meet a tech reporter is through a warm intro from the CEO or a cofounder. For YC founders, the YC network is helpful because the target reporter has almost certainly written about someone in the network. Proper etiquette is to ask your friend for an intro to only one reporter at a time [1].

The intro message should be readable and reply-able in thirty seconds [1]:
- One sentence describing the news
- For an exclusive: say you are offering an exclusive
- For an embargo: include the date and time of the announcement
- A request for a twenty-minute call

On the call, have three to five reasons ready for why the news and the company are important. At the end, offer notes and relevant collateral (screenshots, graphs, logos, pictures). This provides reporters with the language you prefer while being courteous [1].

## Step Three: Pitch an Exclusive

Almost all startups should offer their story as an exclusive to one tech reporter at a time. The target audience -- investors, potential employees, motivated customers, early adopters -- is concentrated in a few tech outlets. Getting blanket coverage across many tech blogs is often not worth the struggle and can create problems when embargoes are broken [1].

## Step Four: Consistent Cadence

The PR industry philosophy of maximizing press mentions per story (packing all news into one big announcement) is not optimal for startups. Seibel compares PR benefits to funding basic research in science: "you can't predict the advances but they do happen when you invest consistently over time" [1].

One story every month or every other month produces better results than one huge story every year. Spread news across multiple stories instead of combining everything. Maintain a queue of three to five pieces of news to announce in the coming six months. This removes pressure if one piece does not get picked up and helps keep a regular cadence [1].

## Expectations and Limits

A 25-50% success rate on pitches is reasonable when doing everything right. After six to twelve months of good cadence and real news, a founder should be relatively friendly with two to five reporters who will reply to most emails [1].

At a later stage, when press organically pays attention (Dropbox and Airbnb are examples), it becomes strategic to create distance between founders and the press. This is when PR people become helpful as a buffer for strategic announcements [1].

PR should occupy no more than 5% of a CEO's time. Founders should not expect PR to create observable miracles (tons of users, customers, or investment offers). Most benefits only become obvious weeks or months after a story is posted [1].
