# Selling to Startups

Selling to startups has been a formula for success at YC. Stripe started as a payments processor for YC companies. PagerDuty's early customers were startups, still referenced in their S-1 filing years later [1]. But the strategy fails when founders cargo cult it without understanding why it works for certain businesses and not others.

## When Selling to Startups Does Not Work

### The Long Sales Cycle Escape

The most common mistake: a product naturally belongs in large enterprises, but the sales cycle seems long, so the founder sells to startups instead [1]. Brad Flora describes the pattern: a founder who spent four years at a large company, saw a problem only large companies have, was funded because of that enterprise insight, and then in the first office hours says "I'm going to sell this enterprise-grade DevTools system to startups for $100 a month" [1].

Snowflake only makes sense with massive data volumes. Workday's early customers were doing six and seven-figure deals because comprehensive HR suites are only needed with hundreds of employees. Trying to sell either product to startups would have been a waste [1].

### Startups Are Not Easier Customers

Founders assume startups will be easier to sell to than enterprises. Flora pushes back: "Why would it be easier to sell to a customer who doesn't have the problem than to sell to a customer who has the problem but takes a long time to make decisions?" [1]. A startup willing to talk about a problem it does not actually have is not evidence of an easier sale -- it is evidence of misdirection [1].

Furthermore, startups can be high-maintenance while paying very little. Enterprise end users may be equally demanding, but the contract size justifies support resources [1].

### The Churn Problem

The most dangerous assumption: that customers acquired as startups will stay as they grow. In some categories, churn is nearly guaranteed [1]:

- **HR tools**: Almost every early-stage HR product gets churned as customers scale
- **Heroku-type products**: Customers churn once engineering teams outgrow simplified platforms
- **CRM**: Startups pick lightweight CRM tools, then switch to Salesforce the moment a VP of Sales wants to scale to 500 people
- **Triplebyte's experience**: The product was built for engineers screening engineers, but every company eventually hires a recruiting team with completely different needs, requiring a brand new product [1]

The underlying pattern: when a product scales with people rather than revenue, the buyer changes as the company grows. The CTO or technical founder who evaluated the tool is replaced by a non-technical department head with entirely different requirements [1].

### When Churn Does Not Happen

Stripe does not churn because payments do not scale with people in the same way. Companies do not want to manage payments in-house regardless of size. The product grows seamlessly from two people to IPO. The test: will the core buyer and their needs remain the same as the company scales? [1]

### Avoiding Sales

Sometimes founders target startups not because the strategy makes sense but because they are afraid to sell. Flora calls this the "Civilization fallacy": "I just want to turn my startup into a real-time strategy game where I can sit at my computer and click on things and watch numbers go up" [1].

The manifestation: building a self-serve flow and waiting. "MVP self-serve flow, dot dot dot, riches" [1]. Founders ping accessible founders in their YC batch but never develop the actual sales skills -- how to get replies to cold emails, what messaging works, how to change behavior. "Building a great self-service interface is not necessarily a strategy for getting customers" [1].

## When Selling to Startups Works

### Grow-With Strategy (Stripe Model)

Get companies when they are two people, and the product sticks with them through IPO and billions in revenue. This requires that the product does not need to be rebuilt as the company scales [1].

### Bridge to SMB (Gusto Model)

Gusto used startups to build a great payroll product, then expanded into the broader small business market. The product was similar enough across segments that startup learnings transferred directly [1].

### Bottoms-Up Enterprise (AWS Model)

Selling to startup engineers creates a path to large enterprises. [[Harj Taggar]] explains: Amazon did not have Microsoft's existing enterprise relationships, so they sold to smart engineers at startups who loved the product. Those engineers later moved to larger companies and advocated for AWS. Amazon was training an entire generation of developers on cloud software [1].

The key caveat: bottoms-up adoption is a booster, not a replacement for enterprise sales. AWS, Stripe, and similar companies still built real enterprise sales teams. "Walmart's not just gonna walk into your self-serve flow" [1].

## Know Which Game You Are Playing

The biggest-picture takeaway: founders must know which strategy they are executing and how selling to startups fits into it [1]. The Stripe game (grow with customers forever), the Gusto game (use startups as training wheels for SMB), and the AWS game (bottoms-up into enterprise) are all viable. "The game of 'I'm gonna sell to big companies by first selling to people that have nothing in common with big companies' -- that's not a good game" [1].

These strategies are well-documented. Founders do not need to invent them -- they can study Gusto, AWS, and Stripe directly. "You don't have to learn all the hard mistakes that they did. You can learn from them" [1].
