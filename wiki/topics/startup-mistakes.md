# Startup Mistakes

[[Paul Graham]] identifies 18 mistakes that kill startups [1]. The unifying theme: "In a sense there's just one mistake that kills startups: not making something users want. If you make something users want, you'll probably be fine, whatever else you do or don't do" [1]. The 18 mistakes are all ways startups fail to make something users want.

## 1. Single Founder

"Have you ever noticed how few successful startups were founded by just one person? Even companies you think of as having one founder, like Oracle, usually turn out to have more" [1]. See also [[Cofounders]].

A single founder is a vote of no confidence -- it suggests the founder could not convince any friends to join. Friends know the founder best [1]. But even if they are wrong, a solo founder is at a disadvantage: "Starting a startup is too hard for one person. Even if you could do all the work yourself, you need colleagues to brainstorm with, to talk you out of stupid decisions, and to cheer you up when things go wrong" [1].

The last point may be the most important. "The low points in a startup are so low that few could bear them alone. When you have multiple founders, esprit de corps binds them together in a way that seems to violate conservation laws. Each thinks 'I can't let my friends down'" [1].

## 2. Bad Location

"Startups prosper in some places and not others. Silicon Valley dominates, then Boston, then Seattle, Austin, Denver, and New York" [1]. The falloff is sharp -- in Houston, Chicago, and Detroit, the number of startups per capita is "too small to measure" [1].

The same dynamic exists in every industry: "What's the sixth largest fashion center in the US?" [1]. Startups prosper where the experts are, standards are higher, people are sympathetic, hires want to live, and chance meetings yield useful connections [1].

## 3. Marginal Niche

Choosing a small, obscure niche to avoid competition [1]. Graham compares it to an 8-year-old outfielder closing his eyes when a fly ball comes: "if you make anything good, you're going to have competitors, so you may as well face that. You can only avoid competition by avoiding good ideas" [1].

This shrinking from big problems is mostly unconscious. A remedy: "What would be a great idea for *someone else* to do as a startup?" [1]. See also [[Startup Ideas]].

## 4. Derivative Idea

Imitating an existing company is "one source of ideas, but not the best" [1]. Successful startups usually originate from "some specific, unsolved problem the founders identified." Apple happened because Wozniak wanted a computer, Google because Larry and Sergey could not find things online, Hotmail because Bhatia and Smith could not exchange email at work [1].

"Instead of starting from companies and working back to the problems they solved, look for problems and imagine the company that might solve them" [1].

## 5. Obstinacy

"The stick-to-your-vision approach works for something like winning an Olympic gold medal, where the problem is well-defined. Startups are more like science, where you need to follow the trail wherever it leads" [1]. Most successful startups end up doing something different than they originally intended -- "often so different that it doesn't even seem like the same company" [1].

But constant switching is equally fatal. A test: "If in each new idea you're able to re-use most of what you built for the previous ones, then you're probably in a process that converges" [1].

## 6. Hiring Bad Programmers

Not a problem when founders are programmers. But business-guy-led startups of the 1990s e-commerce era were killed by this [1]. "Business guys can't tell which are the good programmers. They don't even get a shot at the best ones, because no one really good wants a job implementing the vision of a business guy" [1].

The result: "their startup lumbers along like a World War II bomber while their competitors scream past like jet fighters" [1]. See also [[Hiring]].

## 7. Choosing the Wrong Platform

Hotmail was still running on FreeBSD years after Microsoft bought it "presumably because Windows couldn't handle the load" [1]. PayPal's Max Levchin showed their software "scaled only 1% as well on Windows as Unix" -- the company nearly died when a new CEO wanted to switch. "Fortunately for PayPal they switched CEOs instead" [1].

How to choose: let good programmers decide, or "visit a top computer science department and see what they use in research projects" [1].

## 8. Slowness in Launching

"Software is always 85% done" [1]. One reason to launch quickly: "Nothing is truly finished till it's released." Another: "it's only by bouncing your idea off users that you fully understand it" [1].

Delays manifest as: working too slowly, not understanding the problem, fear of users, fear of judgment, working on too many things, excessive perfectionism [1]. The remedy: force yourself to launch something fairly quickly. See also [[Do Things That Don't Scale]].

## 9. Launching Too Early

Less common than launching too slowly ("probably killed a hundred times more startups"), but possible [1]. The danger: ruining your reputation with early adopters who never come back [1].

The minimum viable launch: "identify a core that's both (a) useful on its own and (b) something that can be incrementally expanded into the whole project" [1]. Early adopters "don't expect a newly launched product to do everything; it just has to do *something*" [1].

## 10. Having No Specific User in Mind

"You can't build things users like without understanding them" [1]. The most successful startups often started by solving the founders' own problems. "Perhaps you create wealth in proportion to how well you understand the problem you're solving" [1].

If building for others: "You're flying on instruments" -- you must shift gears, stop relying on intuition, and measure user responses empirically [1]. "If you're going to make something for teenagers or 'business' users or some other group that doesn't include you, you have to be able to talk some specific ones into using what you're making" [1].

## 11. Raising Too Little Money

"Startup funding is measured in time" -- runway [1]. "Too little money means not enough to get airborne." You must raise enough to reach the next milestone: if you have an idea, a working prototype; if a prototype, a launch; if launched, significant growth [1]. See also [[Fundraising]] and [[Default Alive or Default Dead]].

Graham advises: "spend practically nothing, and make your initial goal simply to build a solid prototype. This gives you maximum flexibility" [1]. Take 50-100% more than you think you need "because software takes longer to write and deals longer to close than you expect" [1].

## 12. Spending Too Much

"The classic way to burn through cash is by hiring a lot of people. This bites you twice: in addition to increasing your costs, it slows you down" [1]. Three suggestions: "(a) don't do it if you can avoid it, (b) pay people with equity rather than salary, not just to save money, but because you want the kind of people who are committed enough to prefer that, and (c) only hire people who are either going to write code or go out and get users, because those are the only things you need at first" [1].

## 13. Raising Too Much Money

"Once you take several million dollars of my money, the clock is ticking" [1]. Taking too much money changes the company: you move to proper offices, hire employees (not founders), and encounter office politics. "When you raise a lot of money, your company moves to the suburbs and has kids" [1].

More dangerously, it becomes harder to change direction. "The more people you have, the more you stay pointed in the same direction" [1]. See also [[Financial Survival]].

Graham advises: "take the first reasonable deal you get" from a reputable firm at a reasonable valuation. "Who cares if you could get a 30% better deal elsewhere? Economically, startups are an all-or-nothing game" [1].

## 14. Poor Investor Management

"You shouldn't ignore them, because they may have useful insights. But neither should you let them run the company" [1]. If investors had the vision to run companies, "why didn't they start them?" [1]

"Pissing off investors by ignoring them is probably less dangerous than caving in to them" [1]. With VC-scale money, investors get significant control. When things go well, they leave you alone. When they don't, they can cause serious harm -- even Apple's board "made a nearly fatal blunder in firing Steve Jobs" [1].

## 15. Sacrificing Users to (Supposed) Profit

"Making something people want is so much harder" than making money from it [1]. Business models should be left for later, like "some trivial but messy feature for version 2." The core problem is creating wealth (how much people want something times the number who want it), not converting wealth into money [1].

"It *is* irresponsible not to think about business models. It's just ten times more irresponsible not to think about the product" [1].

## 16. Not Wanting to Get Your Hands Dirty

Larry and Sergey initially tried to get someone to buy their search algorithm rather than start a company. "There's not much of a market for ideas. No one trusts an idea till you embody it in a product and use that to grow a user base" [1]. See also [[Do Things That Don't Scale]].

"If you're going to attract users, you'll probably have to get up from your computer and go find some" [1]. In YC's first batch (summer 2005), the startup whose founder spent half his time talking to cell phone executives was "the most successful of that group by an order of magnitude" [1].

## 17. Fights Between Founders

"About 20% of the startups we've funded have had a founder leave" [1]. YC now advises founders to vest so there is an orderly way for people to quit. A founder leaving does not necessarily kill the startup -- "Blogger got down to one person, and they bounced back" [1]. See also [[Cofounders]].

Most disputes "could have been avoided if they'd been more careful about who they started a company with" [1]. Graham advises: "Don't suppress misgivings. It's much easier to fix problems before the company is started than after." Do not include your housemate because he would feel left out, or someone you dislike because they have a skill you need [1].

## 18. A Half-Hearted Effort

"The most common type is not the one that makes spectacular mistakes, but the one that doesn't do much of anything -- the one we never even hear about" [1]. Statistically, most founders of failed startups do not quit their day jobs, and most founders of successful ones do [1].

Graham estimates the number of people who could have succeeded if they had taken the leap is "probably an order of magnitude larger than the number who do make it" [1].

"Starting startups is just like everything else. The biggest mistake you can make is not to try hard enough" [1].
