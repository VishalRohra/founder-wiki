# Silicon Valley Fundraising

[[Michael Seibel]] explains why fundraising in Silicon Valley operates under fundamentally different dynamics than in other locations. The difference is not merely about the number of investors -- it is about their psychology [1].

## The Investor Psychology Gap

Investors are not spread evenly around the country or the world. The critical difference between Bay Area investors and investors elsewhere comes down to one thing: how often they have been wrong when saying no [1].

**Bay Area investors**: If you are an early-stage investor in the Bay Area for long enough, you will have said no to companies that went on to be very successful. "That gives you pause every time you meet with a new company. That lets you think, 'Hmm, this company might be a good one. I should pay extra special attention. I should really dig in. I should really learn a lot before I make a decision'" [1].

**Investors outside strong startup communities**: They see fewer deals and fewer good deals. "It might be the case that almost every time you've said no, you've been right" [1].

The result: Bay Area investors are much more likely to give you the benefit of the doubt on your idea and focus on execution -- how you plan to execute, monetize, and scale. Investors outside strong startup communities are more likely to try to evaluate whether the idea itself is good, and dismiss it out of hand [1].

## Execution Over Ideas

Seibel draws on reviewing "literally hundreds and hundreds of thousands of YC applications" to make a broader point: the ability to evaluate ideas is much less reliable than the ability to evaluate whether a team is executing [1]. "I much rather use the 'is this team executing' bar to choose companies than the idea bar" [1].

Bay Area investors have learned this lesson through painful experience -- by passing on companies that seemed like bad ideas but turned out to be great. Investors elsewhere have not had enough of those experiences to develop the same humility [1].

## Practical Implication for Founders

If you have been discouraged from fundraising in your local startup community, that does not necessarily mean you have a bad idea or should quit [1]. "Maybe you should consider trying to do that same fundraise in the Bay Area or in a larger community with investors who might be a little bit more afraid to say no without digging deep into what you're working on" [1].

Seibel specifically warns against overcorrecting based on feedback from investors outside of strong startup ecosystems. One YC company from North Carolina had tried hard to fundraise locally and gotten nowhere. They were "overcorrecting based on the feedback they got from their angel investors in the local community" [1]. The feedback may not have been representative of how the idea would be received in a more active ecosystem.
