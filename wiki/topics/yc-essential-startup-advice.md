# YC Essential Startup Advice

This is YC's compilation of its most important, most transformative advice for startups [1]. It synthesizes principles from multiple YC partners into a single canonical reference.

## Launch Immediately

"The first thing we always tell founders is to launch their product right away; for the simple reason that this is the only way to fully understand customers' problems and whether the product meets their needs" [1]. Launching a mediocre product and iterating is better than waiting for the "perfect" product, as long as the product contains a "quantum of utility" whose value overwhelms any rough edges [1].

## Do Things That Don't Scale

After launch, [[Do Things That Don't Scale|do things that don't scale]] (per [[Paul Graham]]'s essay [Do Things That Don't Scale](http://paulgraham.com/ds.html)) [1]. Many advisors push premature scaling, which wastes time building technology and processes that may not be needed. Instead, get the first customer by any means necessary, "even by manual work that couldn't be managed for more than ten, much less 100 or 1000 customers" [1].

The Airbnb example: founders offered to "professionally" photograph homes of their earliest customers, then went and took the photos themselves [1]. Entirely unscalable, but proved essential for learning how to build a vibrant marketplace.

## The 90/10 Solution

[[Paul Buchheit]] advises looking for a way to accomplish 90% of what you want with only 10% of the work [1]. "If you search hard for it, there is almost always a 90/10 solution available." A 90% solution available right now beats a 100% solution that takes ages to build [1].

## Write Code, Talk to Users

The most important tasks for an early-stage company [1]. All other activities (conferences, dinners, VC meetings, press) are distractions from this core loop. [[Jessica Livingston]] created a list of wrong things to focus on in [How Not To Fail](https://blog.ycombinator.com/how-not-to-fail/) [1]. [[Sam Altman]] calls non-essential activities "fake work" because "it tends to be more fun than real work" [1].

Altman also notes that "it is nearly always better to take the more ambitious path" when choosing between directions [1].

## Choosing Customers

A small group of customers who love you is better than a large group who kind of like you [1]. "Recruiting 10 customers who have a burning problem is much better than 1000 customers who have a passing annoyance" [1].

Sometimes startups need to fire their customers. [[Justin.tv]]/[[Twitch]] only became a breakout success when they focused on video game broadcasters and stopped serving people trying to stream copyrighted content (per [[Michael Seibel]]'s [Users You Don't Want](http://www.michaelseibel.com/blog/users-you-don-t-want)) [1].

## Growth and Product-Market Fit

Growth is the natural result of building something users want, talking to them, and iterating quickly [1]. Growth is not always the right choice: without [[Product-Market Fit|product-market fit]], growth leads to poor retention. With unprofitable unit economics, growth drains cash [1].

[[Paul Buchheit]]: "it never makes sense to take 80 cents from a customer and then hand them a dollar back" [1].

Founders' intuition is always to do more; "usually the best strategy is almost always to do less, really well" [1]. Choose one or two key metrics early, and base decisions nearly exclusively on those metrics [1].

## Deals with Large Companies

"Deals between large companies and tiny startups seldom end well for the startup. They take too long, cost too much, and often fail completely" [1]. Avoid chasing big company validation at the expense of building product.

Also: avoid corporate development conversations. [[Paul Graham]] wrote about this in [Don't Talk to Corp Dev](http://www.paulgraham.com/corpdev.html) [1].

## Competition

"Startup companies always die of suicide not murder" [1]. Spending time worrying about competitors is nearly always a bad idea in the first year or two [1].

## Founder Psychology

Nearly every startup has deep, fundamental issues, even those that become billion-dollar companies [1]. "Your job as a founder will often seem to be continuously righting a capsized ship. This is normal" [1].

Companies most often fail because founders fall out, not because they run out of money [1]. "The relationships with your cofounders matter more than you think and open, honest communications between founders makes future debacles much less likely" [1]. See also [[Cofounders]].

[[Paul Graham]]: "one of the best things you can do to make your startup successful, in fact, to be successful in life, is to simply be nice" [1].

## The Pocket Guide

- Launch now
- Build something people want
- [[Do Things That Don't Scale|Do things that don't scale]]
- Find the 90/10 solution
- Find 10-100 customers who love your product
- All startups are badly broken at some point
- Write code, talk to users
- "It's not your money"
- Growth is the result of a great product not the precursor
- Don't scale your team/product until you have built something people want
- Valuation is not equal to success or even probability of success
- Avoid long negotiated deals with big customers if you can
- Avoid big company corporate development queries
- Avoid conferences unless they are the best way to get customers
- Pre-[[Product-Market Fit|product market fit]]: do things that don't scale, remain small/nimble
- Startups can only solve one problem well at any given time
- Founder relationships matter more than you think
- Sometimes you need to fire your customers
- Ignore your competitors, you will more likely die of suicide than murder
- Most companies don't die because they run out of money
- Be nice! Or at least don't be a jerk
- Get sleep and exercise, take care of yourself
