# Why Now

"Why now?" is the question great investors ask themselves when they meet a startup [1]. [[Garry Tan]] identifies three categories of "why now" that explain why certain startups can exist at certain times: new technology, behavior change in society, and new regulation [1]. [[Sam Altman]] reinforces that identifying exponentially growing markets is one of the most important things a startup can do [2].

## Technology Curves

Technology progresses on curves -- linear, superlinear, and exponential [1]. Tan uses Ray Kurzweil's work to illustrate: Kurzweil pioneered optical character recognition in the 1970s and waited until the 1980s to tackle speech recognition because it required more computation. "He was thinking through timing when he was developing those things" [1].

The principle: orders-of-magnitude improvement, not incremental improvement, is the key to breakthrough. "The most powerful things you could work on come on the back of not a 50% or 2x improvement, but on 16,000x better, 600x. Look for the 10x. Look for the 100x" [1].

### General Magic vs. the iPhone

Tan uses the General Magic / iPhone comparison as the canonical example of timing [1]. In 1994, General Magic released a smartphone-like device costing $1,400 with 1 megabyte of RAM and a 4,800 baud modem ($400/month data fee). Thirteen years later, Apple released the iPhone 1 at $499 with 16 GB of storage and a 300 kilobytes/second network -- 16,000x the storage, 600x faster network, one-third the price [1].

General Magic employed people who would go on to shape the smartphone industry. The technology was not ready. The "why now" had not arrived [1].

## Behavior Change

Once a technology becomes broadly available, the "why now" shifts to human mass behavior change [1]. Tan uses smartphone adoption rates to illustrate: around 2012, smartphone penetration crossed a threshold that made mobile-workforce businesses viable for the first time [1].

### Instacart vs. Webvan

Instacart is Tan's primary case study for behavior-driven timing [1]. Webvan, which attempted grocery delivery in 1997, raised over $396 million from Sequoia, Benchmark, and SoftBank, lost over $800 million, and shut down in June 2001 [1].

When Instacart launched in 2012, the "why now" was smartphone penetration. As the Instacart founder described it: "What has changed today and why this is the first time in history where a company like Instacart is actually possible is because smartphone penetration. There hasn't been a time where smartphones are so prevalent that everyone has them. And as a result of that, we're able to utilize the people who have them to pick and deliver the groceries" [1].

The same behavior shift drove Uber, Lyft, and [[DoorDash: Creating a Market from Scratch|DoorDash]] -- "all multi-billion-dollar startups today" [1]. Tan funded Instacart while at Y Combinator. "I saw the future literally right before my eyes in my iPhone" [1].

### Zimride to Lyft

Tan's partner Kim-Mai Cutler wrote the first TechCrunch article in 2012 about Lyft's pivot from Zimride (a long-haul ridesharing app) into the first citizen driver service, even before Uber launched UberX. "Zimride becoming Lyft was the pivot of a century" [1].

## Regulation

The third "why now" is government regulation [1]. Tan acknowledges this may be less intuitive for technologists but calls it "the largest, hairiest, and most difficult to handle 'why now' for any startup" [1].

### Uber and Regulatory Battles

Tan cites Bradley Tusk's account of Uber's DC launch in 2012. When the taxi industry pushed legislation to outlaw Uber, the company emailed its customers asking for help. In one week, 50,000 people contacted the city council. The anti-Uber bill was defeated, and Uber's bill passed unanimously -- "including voted for it by the sponsor of the bad bill" [1].

### Zenefits and the ACA

Tan recalls meeting [[Parker Conrad]] at Y Combinator when Conrad was working on Zenefits. The key insight in the YC application: the Affordable Care Act (Obamacare) was about to mandate that millions of small and medium businesses offer health insurance. "That turned out to be a mega trend need that drove huge adoption of not just Zenefits, but Gusto and yes, even Parker Conrad's new decacorn startup, Rippling" [1].

### Climate Change

Tan identifies climate change regulation as the biggest enduring regulatory "why now" today [1]. The Inflation Reduction Act is "the first of its kind, the largest climate package the United States has ever done" and is "going to lead to lots of different incentives around decarbonizing our entire economy" [1].

## Real Trends vs. Fake Trends

Altman provides a complementary framework for evaluating timing [2]. A real trend is one where early adopters use a new technology "obsessively and tell their friends how much they love it." A fake trend is one where people may buy the product but do not use it -- "or at least not enough" [2].

The iPhone was a real trend: early owners "used it for hours every day, it became central to their lives." VR (as of 2018) was a fake trend: "most people that I know that own a VR headset use it never or very rarely" [2].

## Exponentially Growing Markets

Altman argues that one of the biggest mistakes investors make is evaluating a startup's market by its current size rather than its growth rate [2]. "11 years ago, the market for iPhone apps: absolute $0. It's not huge. And I think if you only think about the TAM today, you'll make a big mistake. What you really wanna do is identify a market that's gonna grow every year and be able to ride that up elevator" [2].

## Platform Shifts and Startups

Both Tan and Altman identify platform shifts as the primary mechanism through which startups beat big companies. Altman explains that most large companies operate on annual planning cycles. When a huge platform shift occurs, "the battleship just turns too slowly, whereas a startup can say, 'Wow, woke up this morning. The world is fundamentally different than it was six months ago. We're gonna go all in on this new direction'" [2]. See [[Competing with Big Companies]] for more on this dynamic.
