# Early-Stage Mistakes

In a sense there is just one mistake that kills startups: not making something users want [1]. [[Paul Graham]] argues that if you make something users want, you will probably be fine, whatever else you do or don't do. And if you don't, you are dead, whatever else you do. His 2006 essay catalogues 18 specific mistakes, nearly all of which funnel through that one root cause [1].

## The Root Cause: Not Making Something Users Want

Graham states this is so much harder than it sounds that most startups fail at it. "I don't know why it's so hard to make something people want. It seems like it should be straightforward. But you can tell it must be hard by how few startups do it" [1].

The pattern across his essays is consistent: the biggest mistake is not trying hard enough [1], not understanding users deeply enough [1], or building something nobody actually needs (the "sitcom startup idea" problem described in [[Startup Ideas]]) [3].

## The 18 Mistakes

Graham's complete taxonomy from October 2006 [1]:

### Team Mistakes

**1. Single Founder.** Few successful startups were founded by one person. Having a single founder is "a vote of no confidence" -- it probably means the founder couldn't talk any friends into joining, which is alarming because friends know the founder best [1]. The emotional dimension may matter most: "The low points in a startup are so low that few could bear them alone. When you have multiple founders, esprit de corps binds them together in a way that seems to violate conservation laws. Each thinks 'I can't let my friends down'" [1].

**6. Hiring Bad Programmers.** This is primarily a problem for non-technical founders. "Business guys can't tell which are the good programmers. They don't even get a shot at the best ones, because no one really good wants a job implementing the vision of a business guy" [1]. The result: the startup is in the same position as a big company but without the advantages [1].

**17. Fights Between Founders.** About 20% of YC-funded startups have had a founder leave [1]. Most disputes are not due to the situation but the people, meaning they are inevitable. "Don't include your housemate in your startup because he'd feel left out otherwise. Don't start a company with someone you dislike because they have some skill you need" [1]. YC now advises founders to vest so there will be an orderly way for people to quit [1].

### Idea and Product Mistakes

**3. Marginal Niche.** Choosing a small, obscure niche to avoid competition is the startup equivalent of a child closing his eyes when a fly ball comes his way [1]. "If you make anything good, you're going to have competitors, so you may as well face that" [1]. See [[Startup Ideas]] for more on this.

**4. Derivative Idea.** Imitating existing companies is one source of ideas but not the best. Apple happened because Wozniak wanted a computer. Google because Larry and Sergey couldn't find stuff online. "Instead of starting from companies and working back to the problems they solved, look for problems and imagine the company that might solve them" [1].

**5. Obstinacy.** Startups are more like science than the Olympics: you need to follow the trail wherever it leads, not stick to a rigid plan [1]. Most successful startups end up doing something different than they originally intended, often so different it doesn't even seem like the same company. "But openness to new ideas has to be tuned just right. Switching to a new idea every week will be equally fatal" [1]. The test: ask whether the ideas represent some kind of progression, and ask your users.

**7. Choosing the Wrong Platform.** The scary thing about platforms is that some seem like fine, responsible choices yet will destroy you. Java applets "probably killed just about 100% of the startups who believed that" [1]. PayPal nearly died because the post-merger CEO wanted to switch to Windows, even after Max Levchin showed their software scaled only 1% as well on Windows as Unix. "Fortunately for PayPal they switched CEOs instead" [1].

**10. Having No Specific User in Mind.** "Perhaps you create wealth in proportion to how well you understand the problem you're solving, and the problems you understand best are your own" [1]. If you are building for a group that does not include you, you must shift gears: you are "flying on instruments" and must be empirical, finding real users and measuring their responses [1].

### Launch and Execution Mistakes

**8. Slowness in Launching.** Software is always 85% done. Several distinct problems manifest as delays: working too slowly, not truly understanding the problem, fear of users, fear of being judged, working on too many things, excessive perfectionism [1]. "I've seen a lot of startups die because they were too slow to release stuff, and none because they were too quick" [2].

**9. Launching Too Early.** Less common than launching too slowly by a factor of a hundred, but possible. The minimum: "a core that's both (a) useful on its own and (b) something that can be incrementally expanded into the whole project" [1]. Early adopters are fairly tolerant -- "it just has to do something" [1].

**16. Not Wanting to Get Your Hands Dirty.** Programmers would rather write code than sell. Larry and Sergey initially tried to get another company to buy their search algorithm [1]. In the first YC batch (summer 2005), one founder spent half his time talking to cell phone company executives. "Can you imagine anything more painful for a hacker? But it paid off, because this startup seems the most successful of that group by an order of magnitude" [1].

### Financial Mistakes

**11. Raising Too Little Money.** You must raise enough to get to the next step. Rule of thumb: take 50-100% more than you think you will need [1].

**12. Spending Too Much.** The classic way: hiring a lot of people, which increases costs and slows you down. "Don't do it if you can avoid it" [1]. See [[Financial Survival]] for the full treatment.

**13. Raising Too Much Money.** Changes the company's atmosphere and makes it harder to pivot. "When you raise a lot of money, your company moves to the suburbs and has kids" [1].

**14. Poor Investor Management.** Founders should neither ignore investors nor let them run the company. Apple's board nearly killed the company by firing Steve Jobs [1].

**15. Sacrificing Users to Supposed Profit.** Leave business models for later. Google made search work first, then monetized. "It is irresponsible not to think about business models. It's just ten times more irresponsible not to think about the product" [1].

### Commitment Mistakes

**18. A Half-Hearted Effort.** The most common type of failure is not the spectacular flameout but the startup that never gets going -- a side project that was gradually abandoned. Most founders of failed startups don't quit their day jobs; most founders of successful ones do [1]. See [[Founder Mindset]] for the full treatment of determination and commitment.

**2. Bad Location.** Silicon Valley dominates, then Boston, then Seattle, Austin, Denver, and New York. In towns like Houston and Chicago and Detroit, the number of startups per capita is "too small to measure" [1]. The reasons compound: experts, higher standards, sympathetic people, potential hires who want to live there, supporting industries, and serendipitous encounters [1].

## Playing House

Graham coined the term "playing house" for a pattern he saw repeatedly at YC [4]: young founders who go through all the outward motions of starting a startup -- make up a plausible idea, raise money, rent a cool office, hire people -- while neglecting the one essential thing: making something people want [4].

The reason is training. Students have spent their whole lives learning to game systems. "The next step after rent a cool office and hire a bunch of people is: gradually realize how completely fucked they are" [4]. Startups are where gaming the system stops working: "There is no boss to trick, only users, and all users care about is whether your product does what they want" [4].

## The Class Project Problem

Graham identifies a specific failure mode in young founders: they build things that look like class projects [5]. Two things are missing from class projects:

1. **An iterative definition of a real problem.** In class, problems are predefined. In startups, you discover the real problem through a process of evolution. The very implementation exists to refine the idea -- often the only value of what you build in the first six months is proving your initial idea was mistaken [5].

2. **Market-standard measurement.** Professors judge the distance from starting point to where you are now. Customers judge the distance remaining between where you are and what they need. "The market doesn't give a shit how hard you worked" [5].

## Premature Scaling

Paul Buchheit identifies a related pattern: founders take a small business that isn't really working (bad unit economics, typically) and then scale it up because they want impressive growth numbers [3]. This is similar to overhiring in that it makes the business much harder to fix once it is big, plus they are bleeding cash fast [3]. See [[Default Alive or Default Dead]] for the full framework.

## The Self-Destruction Pattern

Graham argues consistently that competitors are not the primary threat. "Way more startups hose themselves than get crushed by competitors" [2]. The three main ways startups destroy themselves:

1. **Internal disputes** -- fights between founders, usually traceable to choosing the wrong cofounder
2. **Inertia** -- not launching, not iterating, not responding to what users need
3. **Ignoring users** -- the worst of the three. "A couple of founders who have some great idea they know everyone is going to love, and that's what they're going to build, no matter what" [2]

Almost everyone's initial plan is broken. Microsoft would be selling programming languages and Apple would be selling printed circuit boards if they had stuck to theirs. In both cases their customers told them what their business should be -- "and they were smart enough to listen" [2].
