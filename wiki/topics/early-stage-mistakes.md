# Early-Stage Mistakes

In a sense there is just one mistake that kills startups: not making something users want [1]. [[Paul Graham]] argues that if you make something users want, you will probably be fine, whatever else you do or don't do. And if you don't, you are dead, whatever else you do. His 2006 essay catalogues 18 specific mistakes, nearly all of which funnel through that one root cause [1]. [[Michael Seibel]] and [[Jessica Livingston]] add additional patterns from having seen thousands of YC companies.

## The Root Cause: Not Making Something Users Want

Graham states this is so much harder than it sounds that most startups fail at it. "I don't know why it's so hard to make something people want. It seems like it should be straightforward. But you can tell it must be hard by how few startups do it" [1].

The pattern across his essays is consistent: the biggest mistake is not trying hard enough [1], not understanding users deeply enough [1], or building something nobody actually needs (the "sitcom startup idea" problem described in [[Startup Ideas]]) [3].

## First-Time Founder Mistakes (Seibel)

[[Michael Seibel]] identifies a distinct set of mistakes specific to first-time founders [7]:

**Choosing a problem you don't care about.** While not fatal on its own, many startups that fail do so because founders lose motivation. "They were trying to pick a problem that they thought people might want, or they thought could grow quickly, or they thought might be cool, but it wasn't actually something they were willing to devote a significant amount of time -- like, you know, five years plus -- of their life to solving" [7].

**Helping users you don't care about.** Seibel uses Justin.tv as his example: the founders were excited by the general idea of democratizing live video but were not in love with their initial users. Only when Emmett Shear refocused the company around video game streaming -- "Emmett was a gamer and he loved gamers" -- did the company find its stride as Twitch [7].

**Choosing co-founders you don't know well.** Pre-existing relationships matter because startups are so hard that founders need to know whether they can survive hard times together. That relationship can be a friendship, co-worker relationship, or school project -- "that you didn't just find them on the street yesterday" [7].

**Not having transparent conversations with co-founders.** The typical drama topics: performance ("Is my co-founder working as hard as I am?"), goals ("Are we trying to accomplish the same goal?"), and roles ("Whose job is it to do what?"). "Oftentimes, founders just don't have these completely transparent, honest conversations about these topics. And as a result, resentment builds up and the relationship degrades" [7].

**Not launching.** Seibel sees founders who spend months or even years building before shipping anything to users, out of fear that the product is not ready [7].

**Working on non-essential problems.** Many first-time founders get distracted by things that feel productive -- PR, conferences, partnership deals -- but do not move the core metrics [7].

## The 18 Mistakes (Graham)

Graham's complete taxonomy from October 2006 [1]:

### Team Mistakes

**1. Single Founder.** Few successful startups were founded by one person. Having a single founder is "a vote of no confidence" -- it probably means the founder couldn't talk any friends into joining, which is alarming because friends know the founder best [1]. The emotional dimension may matter most: "The low points in a startup are so low that few could bear them alone. When you have multiple founders, esprit de corps binds them together in a way that seems to violate conservation laws. Each thinks 'I can't let my friends down'" [1].

**6. Hiring Bad Programmers.** This is primarily a problem for non-technical founders. "Business guys can't tell which are the good programmers. They don't even get a shot at the best ones, because no one really good wants a job implementing the vision of a business guy" [1].

**17. Fights Between Founders.** About 20% of YC-funded startups have had a founder leave [1]. YC now advises founders to vest so there will be an orderly way for people to quit [1].

### Idea and Product Mistakes

**3. Marginal Niche.** Choosing a small, obscure niche to avoid competition is the startup equivalent of a child closing his eyes when a fly ball comes his way [1].

**4. Derivative Idea.** Imitating existing companies is one source of ideas but not the best. "Instead of starting from companies and working back to the problems they solved, look for problems and imagine the company that might solve them" [1].

**5. Obstinacy.** Startups are more like science than the Olympics: you need to follow the trail wherever it leads, not stick to a rigid plan [1].

**7. Choosing the Wrong Platform.** Java applets "probably killed just about 100% of the startups who believed that" [1].

**10. Having No Specific User in Mind.** If you are building for a group that does not include you, you are "flying on instruments" and must be empirical [1].

### Launch and Execution Mistakes

**8. Slowness in Launching.** "I've seen a lot of startups die because they were too slow to release stuff, and none because they were too quick" [2].

**9. Launching Too Early.** Less common than launching too slowly by a factor of a hundred [1].

**16. Not Wanting to Get Your Hands Dirty.** Programmers would rather write code than sell [1].

### Financial Mistakes

**11. Raising Too Little Money.** Rule of thumb: take 50-100% more than you think you will need [1].

**12. Spending Too Much.** The classic way: hiring a lot of people [1]. See [[Financial Survival]] for the full treatment.

**13. Raising Too Much Money.** "When you raise a lot of money, your company moves to the suburbs and has kids" [1].

**14. Poor Investor Management.** Founders should neither ignore investors nor let them run the company [1].

**15. Sacrificing Users to Supposed Profit.** "It is irresponsible not to think about business models. It's just ten times more irresponsible not to think about the product" [1].

### Commitment Mistakes

**18. A Half-Hearted Effort.** The most common type of failure is the startup that never gets going [1].

**2. Bad Location.** Silicon Valley dominates [1].

## The Jessica Livingston Seven-Point Framework

After seeing over 1,000 companies go through YC, [[Jessica Livingston]] distills the avoidance of failure into seven points [8]:

1. **Make something people want.** "Nothing else you do will matter if you are not making something people want" [8].
2. **Stay focused.** The most successful founders are "totally focused on their product and their users, to the point of being fanatical" [8]. Distractions that look productive but are not: grabbing coffee with investors, talking with acquirers, networking, recruiting boards of advisors, doing partnerships, spending time on PR before PMF, arguing on social media, going to conferences [8].
3. **Don't worry about being a woman.** "While I'll tell you that it is going to be harder for you as a woman, it's not going to be so much harder that it will make the difference between success and failure" [8].
4. **Measure your growth.** "You make what you measure." At least 10% per month growth means you are on the right track [8].
5. **Know if you're default alive.** See [[Default Alive or Default Dead]] [8].
6. **Keep expenses low.** "Overhiring is the big mistake in the second phase of a startup, as making something no one wants is in the first phase" [8].
7. **Fundraising gets harder.** "Series A investors have a totally different attitude from seed investors. Seed investors are looking for promise. Series A investors are looking for performance" [8].

## Playing House

Graham coined the term "playing house" for a pattern he saw repeatedly at YC [4]: young founders who go through all the outward motions of starting a startup -- make up a plausible idea, raise money, rent a cool office, hire people -- while neglecting the one essential thing: making something people want [4].

## The Self-Destruction Pattern

Graham argues consistently that competitors are not the primary threat. "Way more startups hose themselves than get crushed by competitors" [2]. The three main ways startups destroy themselves:

1. **Internal disputes** -- fights between founders, usually traceable to choosing the wrong cofounder
2. **Inertia** -- not launching, not iterating, not responding to what users need
3. **Ignoring users** -- the worst of the three [2]
