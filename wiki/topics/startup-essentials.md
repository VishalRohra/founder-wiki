# Startup Essentials

Y Combinator distills its most fundamental, most transformative advice for startups into a set of core principles [1]. These are not tactical tips for day-to-day operations but structural beliefs about what makes startups succeed or fail. Many of these principles are reinforced across dozens of YC partners and alumni.

## Launch Immediately

The first thing YC tells founders is to launch right away. This is the only way to fully understand customers' problems and whether the product meets their needs. Launching a mediocre product as soon as possible and then talking to customers and iterating is much better than waiting to build a "perfect" product. The threshold is a "quantum of utility" -- the product must contain enough value for customers that its usefulness overwhelms whatever warts it has [1].

## Do Things That Don't Scale

After launching, YC advises founders to do things that cannot scale, following [[Paul Graham]]'s essay of the same name [1]. Many startup advisors push startups to scale too early. Building technology and processes to support premature scaling wastes time and effort and often leads to failure.

The right approach is to get the first customer by any means necessary, even by manual work that could not be managed for more than ten customers, let alone a hundred or a thousand. The Airbnb founders offered to "professionally" photograph the homes and apartments of their earliest customers to make listings more attractive to renters, then went and took the photographs themselves. The listings improved, conversions improved, and they had "amazing conversations with their customers." This was entirely unscalable but proved essential in learning how to build a vibrant marketplace [1].

## The 90/10 Solution

When talking to users yields a long, complicated list of features to build, Paul Buchheit advises looking for the "90/10 solution": a way to accomplish 90% of what you want with only 10% of the work, effort, or time [1]. If you search hard, there is almost always a 90/10 solution available. A 90% solution to a real customer problem that is available right away is much better than a 100% solution that takes ages to build [1].

## Write Code and Talk to Users

As companies grow, distractions multiply: conferences, dinners, meetings with VCs, corporate development inquiries, press coverage. Jessica Livingston created a comprehensive list of wrong things to focus on [1]. [[Sam Altman]] points out that it is nearly always better to take the more ambitious path. He calls the avoidance of hard problems "fake work" because it tends to be more fun than real work [1].

The most important tasks for an early-stage company are to write code and talk to users. For great companies, the cycle of launching, talking to users, taking feedback, and iterating never ends [1].

## Choose Customers Carefully

Founders often do not realize they get to choose customers as much as customers choose them. A small group of customers who love you is better than a large group who kind of like you. Recruiting 10 customers who have a burning problem is much better than 1,000 customers who have a passing annoyance [1].

Some customers cost way more than they provide in revenue or learning, and startups sometimes need to fire their customers. Justin.tv/Twitch only became a breakout success when they focused on video game broadcasters and stopped serving people trying to stream copyrighted content, as [[Michael Seibel]] describes [1].

## Growth Follows Product, Not the Reverse

Growth is a natural result of building what customers want and iterating quickly. But growth is not always the right choice. If you have not found [[Product-Market Fit]], growing merely produces poor retention. If your product is unprofitable, growth drains cash. As Paul Buchheit says, "it never makes sense to take 80 cents from a customer and then hand them a dollar back." [[Sam Altman]] reinforces that unit economics really matter, despite too many startups forgetting this [1].

## Do Less, Really Well

Founders' intuition is always to do more, but the best strategy is almost always to do less, really well. Deals between large companies and tiny startups seldom end well for the startup: they take too long, cost too much, and often fail completely [1].

Early on, a startup should choose one or two key metrics to measure success, then make decisions based nearly exclusively on how each task impacts those metrics. When the early product is not working, the temptation is to build new features for every problem. The right move is to talk to customers and focus only on the most acute problem they have [1].

## Every Startup Is Broken

Nearly every startup has deep, fundamental issues, even those that become billion-dollar companies. Success is not determined by whether the startup is broken at the beginning but by what the founders do about the inevitable problems. "Your job as a founder will often seem to be continuously righting a capsized ship. This is normal" [1].

## Ignore Competitors

Spending time worrying about competitors is nearly always a bad idea. "Startup companies always die of suicide not murder." Competitive dynamics will eventually matter, but it is highly unlikely in the first year or two [1].

## Fundraise Fast, Then Get Back to Work

The best fundraising advice is to raise money as quickly as possible and get back to work. Growth curves visibly flatten when a company is fundraising. Valuation is not equal to success or even probability of success. Some of YC's best companies (Airbnb, Dropbox, Twitch) raised on tiny initial valuations [1].

The money raised is not the founder's money. Founders have a fiduciary and ethical duty to spend it only to improve the company's prospects [1].

## Cofounder Relationships and Personal Health

Most companies fail fast because founders fall out. The relationships with cofounders matter more than founders think. Open, honest communication makes future debacles much less likely. [[Paul Graham]] argues that being nice is one of the best things you can do to make a startup successful [1].

YC also tells founders to take breaks, spend time with friends and family, get enough sleep, and exercise between bouts of intense, focused work [1].

## The Pocket Guide

YC's essential advice, condensed [1]:

- Launch now
- Build something people want
- Do things that don't scale
- Find the 90/10 solution
- Find 10-100 customers who love your product
- All startups are badly broken at some point
- Write code, talk to users
- "It's not your money"
- Growth is the result of a great product, not the precursor
- Don't scale your team/product until you have built something people want
- Valuation is not equal to success or even probability of success
- Avoid long negotiated deals with big customers if you can
- Avoid big company corporate development queries
- Avoid conferences unless they are the best way to get customers
- Pre-product market fit: do things that don't scale, remain small/nimble
- Startups can only solve one problem well at any given time
- Founder relationships matter more than you think
- Sometimes you need to fire your customers
- Ignore your competitors, you will more likely die of suicide than murder
- Most companies don't die because they run out of money
- Be nice! Or at least don't be a jerk
- Get sleep and exercise, take care of yourself
