# Product Development

The YC canon on product development is dominated by one principle: release early and iterate. Graham writes, "I've seen a lot of startups die because they were too slow to release stuff, and none because they were too quick."

## Release Early, Iterate Fast

Graham argues that the primary job of a startup is to pump out features, collect user feedback, and iterate. Waiting for perfection is a form of procrastination. The product will always feel unfinished — the question is whether users find it useful enough to keep coming back.

## The Two-Week Cycle

Shear describes the development process at Twitch (then Justin.tv): strict two-week cycles where no item could last into the next cycle. If a feature was too large, it was broken into smaller chunks. This created constant shipping cadence and prevented scope creep.

Within each cycle, the team set three goals (e.g., increase content creation, acquire new users, improve retention) and selected features that mapped to those goals. Features were graded Easy/Medium/Hard for engineering effort, and the team used consensus-based prioritization.

## Analytics-First

Shear argues that no feature should be released without releasing the analytics for it. Before building, the team defined the specific measurable result they wanted. This prevented building features that "felt right" but had no impact.

## Need-to-Have vs. Nice-to-Have

Shear separated feature requests into need-to-have and nice-to-have. Non-technical team members, once they understood engineering effort estimates, "often got better at thinking up easier and easier MVPs of their ideas." This pushed the team toward simpler solutions that delivered the core value.

## No Roadmap Beyond the Current Cycle

Shear's team maintained no roadmap beyond the current two-week cycle. This kept the team responsive to user feedback and prevented committing to features that might not matter by the time they were built.
