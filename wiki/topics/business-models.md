# Business Models

A business model is how a company makes money. [[Aaron Epstein]], YC Group Partner and founder of Creative Market, analyzed the YC Top 100 companies list and found that nearly every billion-dollar company uses one of nine proven business models. Rather than inventing a new way to make money, founders should copy a proven model and innovate on their product [1].

## The Nine Business Models

### SaaS (Software as a Service)

Cloud-based subscription software. Customers pay monthly or annually. Primary metrics: MRR/ARR, growth rate, net revenue retention, and customer acquisition cost (CAC). SaaS benefits from recurring revenue predictability and is usually sold to businesses, ideally on annual contracts [1].

### Transactional

Companies that facilitate transactions and take a cut. Often fintech and payments businesses. Primary metrics: gross transaction value (GTV), net revenue, user retention, CAC. The best transactional businesses have extremely consistent revenue from high repeat usage. One-time transactions rather than recurring, often high volume with a low fee (1-3% is common) [1].

### Marketplaces

Facilitate transactions between buyers and sellers. Primary metrics: gross merchandise value (GMV), net revenue, growth rate, user retention. Hard to get off the ground due to the chicken-and-egg problem of needing both supply and demand simultaneously. But when they work, [[Network Effects]] at scale drive exponential growth and they often become dominant winner-take-all players [1].

### Subscription

Products or services sold on a recurring basis, usually to consumers. Similar metrics to SaaS (MRR/ARR, growth rate, user retention, CAC) but typically lower price points from a higher volume of customers. Growth driven by scalable, self-serve acquisition channels [1].

### Enterprise

Large fixed-term contracts sold to big companies (5,000+ employees). Primary metrics: bookings, recognized revenue, annual contract value (ACV), and pipeline (top of funnel to demo to close). Very few customers, much larger deals ($100K+/year). Long sales cycles with many gatekeepers. The buyer is not always the end user. Growth is lumpy -- measuring month-over-month growth rate is less meaningful [1].

### Usage-Based

Pay-as-you-go based on consumption. Primary metrics: monthly revenue (not recurring), growth rate, revenue retention, gross margin. Epstein warns: do not confuse usage-based revenue with recurring revenue. Charges per API request, number of records, data usage, or similar. Products and pricing scale from tiny startups to large enterprises [1].

### E-Commerce

Selling products online. Primary metrics: monthly revenue, growth rate, gross margin/unit economics, CAC. Includes D2C brands and Shopify stores. Not marketplaces -- the company keeps 100% of each sale. Higher COGS means lower margins. Products are often commoditized. Must excel at user acquisition and operations [1].

### Advertising

Sell ads to monetize free users. Primary metrics: DAU, MAU, user retention, CPM/CPC. Typically consumer social products with huge scale. The customer is the advertiser, not the end user. "Users are the product being sold." Need billions of impressions each month. Registered users is a vanity metric [1].

### Hard Tech / Bio / Moonshots

Hard businesses with lots of technical risk and long time horizons. Primary metrics: milestones (progress toward vision), signed contracts, letters of intent (LOIs). Often take years to reach a live product because of technical or regulatory risk. Revenue is often years away, so LOIs are the best way to show customer interest [1].

## Lessons from the YC Top 100

Epstein matched each of the top 100 YC companies to its primary business model and found several patterns [1]:

### SaaS Dominates the List

31% of the top 100 are SaaS businesses -- nearly a third. Recurring revenue makes them great businesses: customers keep paying until they explicitly stop, producing predictable, compounding growth [1].

### Transactional Businesses Are in the Flow of Funds

22% of the top 100 are transactional. They outperform because they are directly in the flow of funds. Stripe processes money; Brex is the corporate card companies use to spend. "If you're a company like Stripe that literally processes money for companies, then you're directly in that flow of funds. And so it's really easy to take your cut" [1].

Epstein received this advice during his own YC batch in 2010: "Get as close to the transaction as possible." Companies far from the transaction (affiliates, for example) depend on multiple intermediary steps before getting paid [1].

Transactional businesses become critical infrastructure. "The thought of ripping that out sounds terrible" -- which is why churn is low [1].

### Marketplaces Create the Most Value per Company

14% of the top 100 are marketplaces, but they create 30% of the overall value. Five of the top 10 YC companies by value are marketplaces: Airbnb, Instacart, DoorDash, OpenSea, and Fair. Once marketplaces hit their inflection point, network effects make them dominant -- each new user increases the value for everyone else [1].

### Advertising Businesses Rarely Make the List

Only 3% of the top 100 use advertising as their primary model. They need organic virality to win -- catching lightning in a bottle. "You should not use ads as your primary business model unless you expect to be a top 10 site on the internet" [1].

### The Power Law Applies Within the Top 100

50% of the overall value of the top 100 comes from just the top 10. The power law means the biggest winners outperform all others by orders of magnitude [1].

## What Is Not on the List

Several business types are absent from the top 100 [1]:

- **Services/consulting**: Non-recurring revenue, scales with people not software, low margins. Starting with consulting to learn is fine, but consulting businesses are not venture scale.
- **Affiliate businesses**: Too far from the transaction. Multiple things must happen before you get paid.
- **Hardware businesses**: Require lots of capital, have low margins. Hard to start and scale.
- **Platform-dependent businesses**: High platform risk. "If your business is built on top of another big successful platform and your business starts to work, then it's in the interest of that platform to shut you down."

## Recurring Revenue Wins

Recurring revenue consistently creates winners because [1]:
- It is highly predictable
- Higher customer lifetime values vs. one-off transactions
- Lower customer acquisition costs (no need to re-acquire existing customers)

But recurring revenue only works with strong retention. Epstein illustrates: with 95% monthly retention, starting with 100 customers, only 54 remain after one year. With 90% monthly retention, only 28 remain. "Just that five percent difference in monthly retention can actually be the difference between life and death for a startup" [1]. See [[Cohort Retention]] for how to measure and improve this.

## Moats Build Winners

The biggest winners are built with defensible moats [1]:
- **Network effects**: Each new user increases value (marketplaces)
- **Lock-in and switching costs**: Stripe as payment processor; customer data on SaaS platforms
- **Recurring revenue**: Customers keep paying; inertia works in your favor
- **Technical innovation**: Cruise (self-driving), Boom (supersonic jets) -- years of development create barriers
- **Economies of scale**: DoorDash, Instacart have driven costs down at scale that new entrants cannot match
- **Organic distribution**: Virality and word of mouth. Companies growing organically can outpace competitors who must pay for every customer

## The Core Formula

Epstein summarizes: "The best businesses generate recurring revenue, have high retention, build defensible moats, are as close to the transaction as possible, they scale with software not people, and they're proven and use business models that are familiar to customers." Innovate on the product, copy the business model [1].

## Pricing Insights

See [[Pricing and Monetization]] and [[Startup Pricing]] for detailed guidance. Epstein contributes five insights from the YC top companies [1]:

### 1. You Should Charge

The most common mistake: founders afraid to charge. Charging teaches you whether users will pay, which segments are most willing, and how much value they see. Even universal refusal is valuable information [1].

**The Stripe example**: Early Stripe set prices at 5% per transaction -- nearly double competitors charging 3% -- to test how much value customers placed on one-click signup and developer-friendly API documentation. "Rather than trying to undercut the competition, they did the exact opposite and set a really high bar" [1].

### 2. Price on Value, Not Cost

Cost-plus pricing (cost to serve + markup) ignores the full value customers perceive. The gap between price and perceived value is the opportunity to raise prices. If cost exceeds price, margins are negative. If price exceeds value, customers will not buy [1].

To find value, ask users: "What problem were you hoping our product could solve?" Their answers typically fall into four categories: make more money, reduce costs, move faster, or avoid risk [1].

Alternatively, keep incrementally raising prices until customers complain but still pay. "The ideal scenario is when you tell the customer a price, they say they have to think about it, they go back, and then they come back a week later and say, 'All right, that seems good'" [1].

### 3. Most Startups Are Undercharging

Lower prices are not a sustainable advantage. "All that means is that your large competitor can underprice you even way lower than your cost because they have way more money." Pricing implies value: if your price is lower than competitors, customers may assume your product is less valuable [1].

Raising prices is the easiest way to grow revenue: "Changing a number on the website" vs. acquiring the same number of customers again [1].

### 4. Pricing Is Not Permanent

Common fear: nailing the price the first time. Strategies for raising prices: exclude existing customers (grandfather them), or give advance notice. Netflix has raised prices repeatedly across 221 million subscribers. "If Netflix is able to find a way to increase their prices on 221 million customers, you should be able to figure out how to do it on your handful of early customers" [1].

### 5. Keep It Simple

Complex pricing pages with five buy buttons and crossed-out prices create friction that decreases conversion. GitLab's three clear plans with simple pricing is the model to follow [1].

**The Segment Story**: Segment started free, then charged $120/year. Customers responded: "I hope you would charge me more than that. Otherwise, I'm worried about keeping my customer data with you." A sales advisor told them to charge $120,000/year. Their CEO nervously quoted that price; the customer countered at $12,000; they settled at $18,000. A 150x price increase because they asked. They continued growing deal sizes to six figures and beyond, leading to a $3 billion acquisition by Twilio [1].
