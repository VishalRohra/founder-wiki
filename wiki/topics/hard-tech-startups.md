# Hard Tech Startups

Hard tech startups -- those building physical products, aerospace technology, medical devices, energy systems, or other complex engineering -- face distinct challenges around demonstrating progress, raising capital, and navigating regulatory environments. Multiple YC founders and partners have shared lessons on how to make hard tech work within the accelerator model and beyond. The 2025 era has seen renewed interest in hard tech driven by AI's demand for energy, space infrastructure, and climate technology.

## Why Founders Matter in Hard Tech

[[Blake Scholl]], founder of [[Boom Technology]] (YC W16), argues that all the big breakthroughs in aviation came from founder-led companies. The first airplane came from bicycle entrepreneurs. The first practical airliner (DC-3) and the first jetliner (De Havilland Comet) were created by companies run by their founders. The last new commercial aircraft company was founded in 1921. The last founder retired from the industry in 1958 -- "which, coincidentally or not, was also the year we had the first jetliner" [1].

Since then, Boeing and Airbus have optimized: making existing designs more efficient and safer, but not improving human capability. Boeing's CEO publicly said their policy is "no more moonshots." Scholl identifies what founders bring that managers do not: "Founders have vision. Founders start things to make the world a better place. Founders take risks. They make bold bets" [1].

## Demonstrating Progress in Hardware

Hardware companies at YC must figure out what they can build and show at demo day. Scholl's advice: "Figure out really early on something that both is and looks like actual, substantive progress towards the product" [1]. Avoid two traps: meaningful but unappreciable progress (simulation results no one understands), and progress that is just for show (a flight simulator that looks impressive but is empty) [1].

Boom's solution: they completed the high-level aerodynamic design, 3D-printed a model of the jet engine, and physically brought to demo day one of the three actual engines that would power the first airplane. "People, of course, were like, 'OMG, jet engine.'" The key was that it was real [1].

### The Athelas Path

Tanay Tandon describes how [[Athelas]] went from a proof-of-concept at YC Hacks 2014 (a smartphone microscope using a toilet paper roll) to shipping $250 medical devices with FDA Class 2 510(k) approval [2]. The path required automated blood processing, deep learning computer vision, clinical validation (100% 5-class inter-rater agreement at 350 patients), and regulatory navigation [2].

## Selling Before Building

Scholl's experience challenges the assumption that hard tech companies cannot sell before the product exists. YC's advice: "Get your ass out of stealth mode" and "Go sell some airplanes" [1]. Scholl pursued Richard Branson through two channels simultaneously, crashed a Virgin Galactic event for a breakfast meeting, and used demo day as a deadline. The email from Virgin arrived 24 hours before Boom's demo day slot: "You can announce the following -- We'll take the first 10 airplanes." Boom set a record with $5 billion in LOIs at demo day [1].

## Fusion Energy

[[Gustaf Alströmer]] details the state of fusion energy, which has gained urgency because of AI's massive electricity demands [4]. Helion Energy is taking a unique approach: a linear, pulsed reactor using deuterium and helium-3 fuel (avoiding tritium's radioactivity), with direct energy recovery that can reach 90%+ conversion efficiency. Helion has a partnership to power a Microsoft data center by 2028 [4].

Helion's innovation is organizational as much as technical: they build, test, and design in parallel. "While operating one machine, they were already building the next and prototyping the factories needed to mass-produce future reactors" [4]. This hardware-first, iterate-fast approach helped Helion move from early prototypes to its seventh-generation Polaris system in under a decade [4].

## Reusable Rockets

The reusable rocket startup ecosystem continues to grow, building on SpaceX's demonstration that reusability transforms launch economics [5]. New entrants are tackling specific niches -- small satellite launches, orbital debris removal, and space-based AI infrastructure [5] [6].

The pattern across these companies mirrors the broader hard tech playbook: demonstrate real hardware progress early, sell before the product is complete (LOIs and pre-orders), and use demo day deadlines as forcing functions.

## Carbon Capture

Mobile carbon capture devices represent a new category of climate technology startup [7]. The challenge parallels other hard tech domains: the physics works, but commercializing at scale requires solving engineering, manufacturing, and unit economics problems simultaneously.

## The Motivation Advantage of Big Ideas

Scholl argues that bigger ideas can actually be easier because they attract better talent. "The team we have around us at Boom is phenomenal, and we wouldn't have it if we were building an app or a small airplane, even. Everyone looks at what we're building, and they're like, 'I can tell my grandkids about this'" [1].

His advice for choosing what to work on: "Find something in the world that you desperately want to exist." All startups go through hell; the question is whether it is worth it [1].
