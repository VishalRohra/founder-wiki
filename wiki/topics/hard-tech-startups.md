# Hard Tech Startups

Hard tech startups -- those building physical products, aerospace technology, medical devices, or other complex engineering -- face distinct challenges around demonstrating progress, raising capital, and navigating regulatory environments. Multiple YC founders and partners have shared lessons on how to make hard tech work within the accelerator model and beyond.

## Why Founders Matter in Hard Tech

[[Blake Scholl]], founder of [[Boom Technology]] (YC W16), argues that all the big breakthroughs in aviation came from founder-led companies. The first airplane came from bicycle entrepreneurs. The first practical airliner (DC-3) and the first jetliner (De Havilland Comet) were created by companies run by their founders. The last new commercial aircraft company was founded in 1921. The last founder retired from the industry in 1958 -- "which, coincidentally or not, was also the year we had the first jetliner" [1].

Since then, Boeing and Airbus have optimized: making existing designs more efficient and safer, but not improving human capability. Boeing's CEO publicly said their policy is "no more moonshots" -- from the company that was literally in the moonshot business. "I believe very strongly that if we want more human capability, we need entrepreneurs, we need founders, and we need new companies" [1].

Scholl identifies what founders bring that managers do not: "Founders have vision. Founders start things to make the world a better place. Founders take risks. They make bold bets" [1].

## Demonstrating Progress in Hardware

Hardware companies at YC must figure out what they can build and show at demo day. Software companies can ship an MVP relatively quickly; hardware has longer lead times. The product may not be done, in market, or even have a working demo [1].

Scholl's advice: "Figure out really early on something that both is and looks like actual, substantive progress towards the product" [1]. This means avoiding two traps:

1. **Meaningful but unappreciable progress**: Boom's simulation results hitting critical lift-drag ratios were technically significant but "no one's gonna understand it or believe it or get excited about it" [1].
2. **Progress that is just for show**: Building a flight simulator would have been impressive-looking but empty [1].

Boom's solution: they completed the high-level aerodynamic design, built a model of the airplane, 3D-printed a model of the jet engine, and physically brought to demo day one of the three actual engines that would power the first airplane. "People, of course, were like, 'OMG, jet engine.'" The key was that it was real -- they had found and purchased the engine they would fly [1].

### The Athelas Path: Hackathon to Shippable Product

Tanay Tandon describes how [[Athelas]] (YC S16) went from a proof-of-concept built overnight at YC Hacks 2014 to shipping medical devices to patients and hospitals [2].

The first version used a rubber piece and spherical magnifier attached to a smartphone camera with a blood sample held by a toilet paper roll -- similar in design to a [van Leeuwenhoek microscope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antonie_van_Leeuwenhoek). "While functioning and a neat trick, someone needed to be physically holding the camera in place, the slide had to be moved around, with the lighting often being hard to fix. At the end of the day it was a nice experimental toy" [2].

The progression from hackathon prototype to shippable product required [2]:
- An automated blood processing mechanism for stained peripheral smears
- More robust computer vision for different cell types (deep learning plus traditional CV)
- Automated imaging without manual slide manipulation
- Clinical validation (350 patients tested against gold-standard Beckman Counter)
- FDA Class 2 510(k) submission

The path was slow because of college workload and rising hardware iteration costs. "Finals often meant days going by without any tangible progress whatsoever" [2]. During YC (summer batch, full-time), clinical validation was completed at the FEMAP family hospital, demonstrating high inter-rater agreement (100% 5-class inter-rater agreement) [2].

The device shipped at $250 per unit for point-of-care locations, homes, and clinical settings [2].

## Selling Before Building

Scholl's experience challenges the assumption that hard tech companies cannot sell before the product exists. YC's advice to Boom: "Get your ass out of stealth mode, it's not helping you" and "Go sell some airplanes" [1].

Scholl's reaction: "I don't know how 10 guys in a basement who say they're building supersonic jets are going to sell anything to airlines, but I could try. And it turns out you can" [1].

The group partners' mandate: "Show up on demo day with sales or your goose is pretty cooked." Scholl assessed his sales pipeline (United, Delta, Lufthansa, Air China) and concluded none could close in eight weeks. He focused instead on startup airlines and Virgin [1].

### The Virgin Deal

Boom pursued Richard Branson directly, ensuring two different avenues to him so neither could be blocked: friends inside Virgin Galactic, and astronaut Mark Kelly on Boom's advisory board. The team crashed a Virgin Galactic spaceship rollout to get 15 minutes with Branson over breakfast with his mother [1].

The critical ask: "Look, we're not asking for your money, we're asking, 'When this flies, do you want a Virgin logo on it?'" Scholl emphasizes: "Ask for the right thing. When you have a deal that's probably gonna be hard to close -- ask for what's really gonna help you" [1].

Using demo day as a deadline was equally critical. Virgin cared about its brand and publicity. "We are launching the company on demo day, and either you can be part of it or not" [1]. Virgin took demo day more seriously than expected.

The email from Virgin arrived 24 hours before Boom's stage slot: "You can announce the following -- We'll take the first 10 airplanes, and we've got options on them. And through Virgin Galactic, we're gonna help you build it" [1].

Boom went from potential embarrassment to a record-setting $5 billion in LOIs at demo day [1].

### Sales Lessons for Hard Tech

Scholl distills three principles:
1. **Be persistent beyond comfort**: "Be so persistent that you're sure you're being annoying. And you'll feel like you must be annoying, but when you have that feeling that means you're probably doing it right" [1].
2. **Have a deadline**: demo day provided a believable, external forcing function [1].
3. **Make the deal sweet for the other side**: when in a hurry, maximize the value proposition for the counterparty [1].

## The Motivation Advantage of Big Ideas

Scholl argues that bigger ideas can actually be easier because they motivate the founder, attract better talent, and energize everyone involved. "The team we have around us at Boom is phenomenal, and we wouldn't have it if we were building an app or a small airplane, even. We're able to get these people because everyone looks at what we're building, and they're like, 'I can tell my grandkids about this! It's either we're going to fail or we're going to change the world. There's no in between'" [1].

His advice for choosing what to work on: "Find something in the world that you desperately want to exist." All startups go through hell; the question is whether it is worth it. "If you haven't found that, don't start a startup just to start a startup. I did that my first time and it was a horrible idea" [1].

## Regulatory and Validation Challenges

Hard tech startups often face regulatory hurdles that software companies do not. Athelas needed FDA approval (Class 2 510(k)) to ship its blood diagnostics device [2]. [[Jared Friedman]] notes that for hardtech and biotech companies, [[Series A Fundraising]] traction sections should focus on technology efficacy, patents, LOIs, and specific regulatory milestones like 510(k) or IND filings [3].
