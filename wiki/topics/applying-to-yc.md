# Applying to YC

[[Garry Tan]], YC President and CEO (and former YC partner who worked with 700+ companies), provides three core principles for a stronger YC application. These principles also apply broadly to any pitch situation [1].

## 1. Show, Don't Tell

If there is a story about a specific user and a specific problem, start with that [1]. Three things to highlight, in ascending order of impressiveness:

1. You have actually created something real
2. Users are using it in real life
3. Someone is paying for it [1]

YC receives applications from every part of the spectrum -- from people who have not yet quit their jobs to companies doing $1-2 million in annual revenue. The most important thing is to establish where you are on that spectrum [1].

### Demonstrate Market Size

Tan asks: "Is there a way you can show that your business or your problem set that you're trying to solve can be worth a billion dollars or more?" In concrete terms, this means: "In the next five to ten years, is there some real way for you to get to a hundred million dollars a year in revenue?" [1].

This is a high bar, but it is what venture investors look for. Show the path through stories: real customers, why they choose you over competitors, and what special insight or advantage ("special sauce") you have that nobody else has figured out [1].

## 2. Don't Use Buzzwords

"Plain English is best because Y Combinator uses plain English to figure out whether or not you know your stuff" [1].

Tan draws a distinction between two investor cultures. Elsewhere in the world, buzzwords signal insider status. "Great investors don't use buzzwords to figure out who to invest in. They use their brains" [1].

Avoid terms like "hyper-verticalization" or "unbundling." Instead, communicate plainly about the problem, the solution, and why your solution will beat all comers [1].

Tan's test for a great pitch: "If I sit down with someone and just have a conversation about what they're doing, do I get excited about what they're doing? And do I believe that this needs to be manifest in the world?" [1].

He provides a useful reframe of what a great pitch actually is: "A great pitch is not something incredibly complicated that impresses the listener. A great pitch is actually very simple, understandable statements put side by side that, when you zoom out, you realize that is a really big idea" [1].

This aligns with [[Michael Seibel]]'s advice on [[Cold Emailing Investors]], where simple language is prioritized over jargon.

## 3. Use the Inverted Pyramid

Tan credits his high school newspaper with teaching him this structure [1]. In standard academic writing, you build to a conclusion. In startup communication, invert it:

1. **Put the conclusion first.** Lead with your most important claim.
2. **Follow with proof.** Stack evidence below the claim.

"When someone actually hears it or when they read it on your homepage, they can decide very quickly whether or not what you say makes sense. Right after it enters their brain, there's a bunch of proof that makes it more and more real" [1].

This technique respects the reader's time and front-loads the most important information. It applies to YC applications, pitch decks, startup homepages, and investor emails [1].

## Why Apply

[[Dalton Caldwell]] provides three reasons [2]:

1. **It forces you to think about your startup.** Going through the application questions helps organize and clarify thinking. "This is evergreen advice regardless of YC" [2].
2. **If you're watching this, you're likely a fit.** The odds that YC is appropriate for someone already watching YC content are reasonably high [2].
3. **The cost-benefit is heavily skewed.** The application is intentionally fast and easy. Downside: a small time investment. Upside: potentially transformational. "Man, that's a pretty good trade-off" [2].

## Creating Luck Through Application

Caldwell connects applying to YC with his broader framework on [[Creating Luck]]. "Certain people are luckier than others, but one of the ways you can create luck is to put yourself out there more" [2]. Avoiding situations that involve potential rejection -- like applying to YC -- reduces the surface area for lucky outcomes [2].

Applying to YC is itself a luck-creating action. The mentality extends to all startup activities: finding ideas, learning sales, recruiting employees [2].

## Bad Reasons Not to Apply

Caldwell catalogs the common excuses [2]:

**"I am too early."** Many great YC companies pivoted after getting in. Many founders had not quit their jobs or written any code. "There's honestly no such thing as too early" [2].

**"I am too far along."** Companies with $500K+ ARR and companies that have already raised over $1 million have been accepted [2]. If you do not have product-market fit, "there's a good chance that you are not too far along for YC" [2].

**"I've watched the videos -- that's basically the same as being at YC."** It is not. YC partners are real people who give full attention. There are secrets told only to batch companies. "There's a whole breadth and depth of knowledge that you will only hear if you're at YC" [2]. Internal tools (fundraising data, Series A tools) are not available publicly [2].

**"Someone discouraged me."** Caldwell suggests asking pointed questions of the discourager: If it is an investor, "So are you going to invest? On what terms?" If the investor will not invest, "they're simply saying they don't want to see you raise" [2]. If it is someone local saying "stay small, stay in this city," ask: "Are they discouraging you from actually dreaming as big as you can dream?" [2]. If someone proposes a theoretical alternative ("maybe you'll raise a Series A"), ask: "Are you discouraging me from seizing an opportunity right in front of me for some theoretical thing that might happen?" [2].

**"My company is too unique for YC."** Look at the top companies list. YC funds companies in every vertical, from every country [2].

**"YC already funded a startup in my space."** "It's pretty common -- especially if it's a great startup idea -- to have multiple companies in the same space" [2].

**"I applied before and didn't get in."** Reapplying is a plus, not a negative. "Most of the folks in the most recent YC batches are not first-time applicants. There's some folks that applied five times, six times, seven times" [2].

**Good reasons not to apply:** You only plan to work on the company briefly, you do not want venture capital, or your business is not a technology startup [2].

## Application Tips

The application is on the website. No permission, no emails, no decks, no calls needed. "It really is that simple" [2].

**Fill the whole thing out.** Include founder biographies. Weak applications often simply were not filled out [2].

**Follow directions exactly.** The founder video has specific instructions. "We definitely take into account if you follow them in our selection criteria. This is one of the ways to tell how detail-oriented someone is" [2].

**Be clear and concise.** "Don't write a novel. More words is not better" [2].

## How Applications Are Read

Caldwell shares the questions he asks himself as a reader [2]:

1. Who are the founders? What is their backstory? How do they know each other?
2. Who is doing what on the team?
3. What is the idea? Do I understand it? Does it seem interesting? Have I heard it before?
4. What do they have today? Is it built? Are there users?
5. Is there evidence people want this?
6. Are the founders serious? (Left a job? Applied multiple times with progress?)
7. What is impressive, unique, or special?

From this data, Caldwell constructs a mental story: "This is the story of two friends. They were both programmers working on infrastructure at Facebook, and they quit their jobs three months ago to build a new cloud hosting company. They built a basic demo and have one first customer" [2]. If he can tell this kind of story, the application is good.

Weak applications "obfuscate everything" -- the reader cannot tell what the idea is, whether it is built, or whether there are customers [2]. Caldwell frames this as a startup-wide metaphor: "If I can't even understand basics of what you're trying to communicate to me, you're not even in the ballpark of convincing me" [2].

**GitLab's application** is the example of clarity: concrete about what it is, what it does, who uses it. "They say a hundred thousand organizations use it. They say programmers at Apple use it. Crystal clear" [2].

## Honesty Is Mandatory

"Our entire system rewards honest people acting in good faith" [2]. Any hint of intentional misrepresentation -- inflated revenue, fake traction, misleading founder backgrounds -- is disqualifying. "It's pretty common to see people try to misrepresent revenue or make it look like monthly revenue is really annual revenue. So just don't do things like this" [2].

Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence. If you claim a major company is a customer, be prepared to demonstrate it [2].

## What Affects Interview Odds

The biggest variable: technical talent on the founding team. "If there is a team that's applying, or at least one of the founders has a skill set level to be hired into a technical role at a top YC company, they have five times better odds" [2]. Internships count (e.g., interning at Stripe or Airbnb) [2].

The lowest odds: teams without technical founders working on an unlaunched [[Startup Ideas|tar pit idea]] [2].

To improve odds: add technical talent via co-founder matching [2].

The entire YC model requires no networking. "We built a system that is optimized for funding complete strangers that fill out a form on our website." No warm intros, no pitch decks, no calls [2]. Beware of people who claim they can help you get into YC in exchange for equity -- "they're basically scammers" [2].

## Interview Tips

The interview is a 10-minute Zoom call with all founders on one side and 2-4 YC partners on the other. The application is open on the interviewers' screens [2].

**Questions are basic, not tricks.** They are context-dependent: pre-launch companies get asked when they will launch; regulated companies get regulatory questions; crowded markets get differentiation questions [2].

**Do not treat it as adversarial.** "The people on our side of the Zoom call when you interview are likely the people that are going to be working with your company if you're accepted." Acting adversarial backfires completely [2]. The interviewer asks: "Do I believe I can have a productive working relationship with these founders?" [2].

Three behaviors that get low scores [2]:
1. Reciting memorized speeches
2. Not listening to the actual question asked
3. Stretching the truth

**Be yourself.** "We want you to show up in an authentic way and not act like you're on a Shark Tank episode or a pitch competition" [2].

A successful interview: all basic questions answered well, founders demonstrate mastery of their business, understand their numbers, know the risks, and have a crisp view of what to work on next. "They're honest. They're self-aware about what the real challenges are, but they still are confident" [2].

## After Rejection

Rejection emails include quick feedback focused on the most important factors. "Internalizing this feedback and demonstrating that you've addressed it in a future application is heavily weighted in your favor" [2]. YC tracks progress between applications [2].
