# MVP

The minimum viable product (MVP) is the simplest version of a product that can be put in front of users to start the learning cycle. [[Michael Seibel]] emphasizes that the MVP should be built in weeks, not months, and that founders consistently overestimate what needs to be included in a first version [1] [2].

## Order of Operations

Seibel describes the order of operations for going from nothing to a launched MVP [2]:

1. **Pick a problem you care about.** The blocker for most talented technical people is that they do not have an idea. Seibel argues this is because they learned that "every great startup started with a great idea" -- a belief he considers wrong. His first startup (Justin.tv) started with the idea of livestreaming someone's life 24/7. Airbnb started as a site for finding an airbed during sold-out conferences [2].
2. **Find co-founders.** Ideally people you already know well and have worked with.
3. **Build the simplest possible version.** Not a complete product -- just enough to start learning from real users.
4. **Launch and talk to users.** The MVP exists to generate feedback, not revenue.

## What an MVP Should Look Like

Seibel's core principle: the MVP should be embarrassingly simple. If you are not embarrassed by your first version, you launched too late [1].

The Poppy example from Avni Patel Thompson illustrates this vividly. Thompson, a non-technical solo founder, built an on-demand childcare service with her tech stack being: Squarespace for the landing page, Typeform for signups and feedback, Stripe for payments, Google Calendar for scheduling, SMS for communication, and Excel for her database. She had $200 in her business bank account [3]. Her first product was literally a day planner where she manually tracked bookings in handwritten circles. She gave parents her personal phone number and manually texted sitters to match availability [3].

With this zero-code MVP, Thompson grew 10-20% per week. "I can't tell you how different that felt versus my first startup where the whole time I was trying to push this boulder up the hill. And this one it was just parents telling other parents" [3]. She eventually outgrew the day planner and moved to Excel, then found a technical co-founder to build real software [3].

## The Hair on Fire Standard

[[Michael Seibel]] connects MVP thinking to [[Product-Market Fit]]: "In a good market, an MVP is all you need to get your initial customers in the door" [4]. If users have a problem so dire they are willing to try half-baked solutions, the MVP does not need to be polished -- it just needs to address the core pain [4].

## Common MVP Mistakes

1. **Building too much.** Founders want to build the complete vision before shipping anything. Seibel argues this wastes months and prevents learning.
2. **Optimizing before validating.** Building analytics, scaling infrastructure, or polishing UI before knowing whether anyone wants the product.
3. **Refusing to launch without a technical co-founder.** Thompson's Poppy example shows that even complex marketplace products can be validated with no code at all [3].
