# Pivoting

A huge number of the best startups had to pivot. [[Dalton Caldwell]] and [[Michael Seibel]] address when to pivot, how to pivot, and the psychological traps that make pivoting either too hard or too easy [1]. YC group partners share pivot stories across multiple billion-dollar companies, illustrating that working on the wrong thing before finding the right thing is not a failure mode but the standard path [2].

## Defining a Pivot

Aaron Epstein offers a useful analogy from basketball: when you pivot, you keep one foot stationary [2]. You may keep the type of product but change the audience, or build a different product for the same audience. A good pivot builds on the learnings from prior ideas [2].

## When to Pivot

The decision to pivot is one of the hardest a founder faces because there is no clean signal. Indicators that a pivot may be warranted [1]:

- The product has been launched and iterated multiple times but growth remains flat
- Users do not retain -- they sign up but do not come back
- Every conversation with users reveals that the problem is not urgent enough
- The founding team has lost genuine excitement about the problem

The critical distinction: a pivot is warranted when the market is telling you the current approach will not work, not when the founders are simply bored or discouraged by normal startup difficulty [1].

Tom Blomfield's signal was quantitative: four founders cold-calling for two weeks and getting one user [2]. The Brex founders needed three to four weeks of talking to hardware experts [2]. The GOAT founders needed two years of poor retention [2].

[[Brad Flora]] offers a KPI-based test: "If you don't immediately know what the thing is that you're chasing when you wake up every morning, then you probably need to pivot" [2].

## How to Pivot

Caldwell and Seibel recommend a structured approach [1]:

1. **Go back to your users.** Talk to the people who did engage and ask what they actually need. Often the pivot idea comes from users themselves.
2. **Look for what's working.** Even in a failing product, there may be one feature or use case generating disproportionate engagement.
3. **Move fast.** Once the decision is made, execute quickly.
4. **Keep the team.** A pivot should not mean losing co-founders unless the relationship is already broken.

## Billion-Dollar Pivot Stories

### Brex: VR Headsets to Corporate Cards

[[Harj Taggar]] describes Brex's application to YC: the idea was a virtual reality headset. The founders had no experience with hardware. What they did have was experience building a payments processing business in Brazil as high schoolers [2]. Three weeks into the YC batch, they went back to payments and built Brex, one of YC's largest companies [2].

Taggar notes a pattern: smart people feel that worthy things need to be really hard, so they ignore what seems easy to them [2].

### GOAT: Group Dinners to Sneaker Marketplace

[[Dalton Caldwell]] describes GOAT, funded as "Grub with Us" -- group dinners. Awful retention. Two years later, the founders realized they were really into sneaker collecting. In 2015, pivoting into sneaker collecting was "so far outside the norm of conventional wisdom" [2]. Sometimes founders need their morale to be so low that they think "I'm going to fail, so I might as well do something cool" -- and that lets them take the blinders off [2].

### GoCardless: Bill-Splitting to Recurring Business Payments

[[Tom Blomfield]]: a "classic fintech tar pit" for group payments. They hassled friends into using it but nobody retained. Paul Graham told them to stop building features and try to get users. They took the core payments infrastructure and applied it to businesses. Twelve years later, GoCardless is a multi-billion dollar company [2].

Blomfield reflects: "When you're young, you think of the entire world as consumer, and the older you get, the more you're like, 'Oh, I guess businesses buy software'" [2].

### Clipboard Health: Indeed for Nurses to Staffing Platform

[[Gustaf Alströmer]]: founder Wei Deng discovered that when a nurse called in sick, hospitals had to go to an agency. This transformed Clipboard from a generic job board to one of the biggest software-enabled skilled nursing staffing platforms. Deng adopted "doing things that don't scale" better than almost anyone [2].

### Twitch: General Live-Streaming to Game Streaming

Started as Justin.tv, a general live-streaming platform. Only when Emmett Shear refocused on video game streaming did it find PMF and sell to Amazon for nearly $1B [1].

## Pivot Hell

A company can pivot too much. YC calls this "pivot hell" [2]. [[Jared Friedman]] describes it as when every week the founders are working on a totally new idea [2].

The cause: perfectionism. No startup idea is ever perfect, and searching for the perfect one means you keep switching as soon as you see problems [2].

### Escaping Pivot Hell

Friedman describes a company that went through pivot hell all batch, deferred Demo Day, and kept doing office hours on new ideas. About six months post-batch, they discovered a regulatory change that created a new opportunity. The key: to arrive at this idea, they had to go through all the bad ideas first [2].

## The Founders Are the Constant

The group partners emphasize that the most important factor is the founders, not the idea [2]. YC has seen the same founders knock a company "down to the studs," rebuild on a totally different idea, and succeed. Often the founders even act like different people when working on an idea they are excited about [2].
