# Pivoting

A pivot is changing what your startup works on. YC group partners define it simply: "If you're not making something people want, you usually need to change your idea or your startup will fail" [1]. A huge number of the most successful startups had to pivot, and the process of working on the wrong thing is often what teaches founders enough about the space to find the right thing [1].

## Pivoting Is Normal, Not a Failure

Multiple YC group partners independently reinforce that pivoting is not a sign of weakness. [[Harj Taggar]], [[Dalton Caldwell]], [[Michael Seibel]], [[Jared Friedman]], Gustaf Alstromer, [[Tom Blomfield]], Brad Flora, and Aaron Epstein all share pivot stories from billion-dollar YC companies [1]. The message: many of the biggest YC successes started as completely different companies.

## Pivot Stories from Billion-Dollar Companies

### Brex: VR Headsets to Corporate Cards

Brex's founders, Henrique Dubugras and Pedro Franceschi, applied to YC with an augmented reality headset idea. The two founders had no hardware experience -- they had built a Stripe-like payments business in Brazil as high schoolers [1]. Taggar recalls: "I thought there was no way that that idea would stick." Three or four weeks into the batch, after consulting hardware experts, they realized they were not the team to build a VR headset. They went back to payments, the domain where they had deep expertise [1].

The pivot illustrates what Taggar calls the "smart person trap": smart people feel that worthy problems must feel hard. An idea in their area of expertise -- payments -- felt too easy, too "been there, done that." The harder, shinier VR challenge was more appealing but a poor fit [1].

### GOAT: Group Dinners to Sneaker Marketplace

Dalton Caldwell describes GOAT's pivot. YC funded the team over ten years ago as "Grub with Us," a site for meeting local people through group dinners. The concept worked within niche communities -- San Francisco's investor community loved it -- but retention was awful [1].

Two years after YC, the founders realized they were personally obsessed with collecting sneakers and that the secondary market for limited-edition sneakers was growing. In 2015, they pivoted to a sneaker marketplace [1].

Caldwell emphasizes the timing: "In 2015, this was so far outside the norm of conventional wisdom to decide to pivot your company into sneaker collecting." The really great pivots often come when founders are "years ahead of consensus" [1].

The psychological dynamic matters: founders sometimes need morale to drop low enough that they stop optimizing for what sounds impressive and start working on what genuinely interests them. "They've internalized, 'I'm gonna fail. So I might as well do something cool on the way down.' That's what lets them take the blinders off" [1].

### GoCardless: Bill-Splitting App to Recurring Payments

[[Tom Blomfield]] describes his first company. He and his co-founders applied to YC with Group.io, a bill-splitting and group payments app -- "a classic fintech tar pit. Every college kid thinks this is the best idea in the world" [1]. Retention was terrible. After Paul Graham told them to stop building features and just try to get users, four founders spent two weeks cold-calling hundreds of sports club treasurers in the UK at 3-4 AM Pacific time. They got one new user [1].

The pivot: they took the core payments infrastructure and applied it to businesses instead of consumers. Direct debit for small businesses collecting recurring payments from customers turned out to be a perfect use case. Twelve years later, GoCardless is a multi-billion dollar company doing the same thing [1].

Blomfield reflects: "When you're young, you think of the entire world as consumer, and the older you get, the more you're like, 'Oh, I guess businesses buy software'" [1].

### Clipboard Health: Nurse Job Board to Staffing Platform

Gustaf Alstromer describes Clipboard Health's journey. Founder Wei Deng came in with a hiring platform for nurses -- "almost like an Indeed but for nurses." The idea was common and had no clear "why now" [1].

Deng spent years selling to hospitals and talking to everyone in the ecosystem. The key insight came from observing that when a nurse called in sick, hospitals always turned to staffing agencies. This was the real pain point -- not job placement but shift filling [1].

Alstromer praises Deng's commitment to "doing things that don't scale": she manually coordinated getting nurses to hospitals before writing any software. "She literally spent time trying to understand exactly what is the problem that I need to solve -- and like maybe I can just manually solve this problem" [1]. Only after doing this repeatedly did she build software to automate it.

### Azure Reality: AR Marketing to AR SDK

Diana (co-founder of Azure Reality) describes starting with AR technology and no clear market. They were "a cool technology in search of a problem" [1]. Their first customers were marketers doing splashy AR campaigns. It took getting into YC to realize that marketers were just being polite -- "that wasn't real feedback" [1]. Through user interviews, they discovered that game developers were the real market. Azure Reality built an AR SDK for game developers and was eventually acquired by Niantic (creators of Pokemon Go) [1].

### Creative Market: Design Community to Design Marketplace

Brad Flora describes pivoting from Color Lovers, a creative design community with over a million members, to Creative Market, a marketplace for graphic design assets. For a year and a half after YC, they tried multiple monetization approaches (software sales, ads, contests) without a clear primary KPI. "The reason it took so long was because we didn't have a main KPI that we were focused on" [1].

Once they pivoted to a marketplace, the KPI became obvious: are people buying? "I became obsessed with it. I was like refreshing it every 15 minutes" [1].

Flora's advice: "Make sure you have a main KPI that you're focused on, and make sure that you're tracking it on a regular basis." If you do not immediately know what your main KPI is -- the thing you chase when you wake up every morning -- you probably need to pivot [1].

## When to Pivot

Several signals suggest it is time [1]:

1. **Retention is terrible** -- users try the product but do not come back (Group.io, Grub with Us)
2. **Users are being polite, not enthusiastic** -- they say nice things but do not actually use or pay for the product (Azure Reality's marketers)
3. **No clear primary KPI** -- the founders cannot identify the single metric that defines whether the business is working (Color Lovers)
4. **Sustained inability to acquire users despite effort** -- four founders cold-calling for two weeks and getting one user (Group.io)

The basketball analogy, attributed to Aaron Epstein: "When you pivot you have to keep one foot stationary. You may keep the type of product that you're building, but maybe the audience is going to change. Or you might build a different product for the same audience." Good pivots build on learnings from prior ideas [1].

## Pivot Hell

A company can pivot too much. YC has a term for it: **pivot hell** -- changing to a completely new idea every week. Not a slightly different idea, but an entirely new startup concept each time [1].

[[Jared Friedman]] explains the root cause: perfectionism. "If you're too much of a perfectionist up front and you want everything planned out perfectly... I think that keeps you in this loop of constantly iterating instead of just saying, 'Look, there's a bunch of unknowns, and I know that, but the only way I'll truly learn is if I sink my teeth into one idea and push it forward'" [1].

The paradox: no startup idea is ever perfect. Founders searching for the perfect idea keep switching because every idea reveals flaws, and they mistake those flaws for fatal problems [1].

Friedman shares a story of a company so deep in pivot hell that they had to defer demo day because they had nothing to present. But six months later, they hit on a high-quality idea: a regulatory change had created an opportunity, and their time exploring the industry -- working through bad ideas -- had given them the domain knowledge to spot it [1].

The lesson: "In order to have this idea, they had to go through a whole bunch of bad ideas first. But in the process of doing this, they got to know the industry really well" [1].

## The Founders Are the Location

The analogy from real estate -- "location, location, location" -- maps to startups as "the founders, the founders, the founders." Everything else can change. The same founders working on a different idea can look like a completely different company. "Oftentimes, the founders even act like different founders. They become more formidable when they're working on an idea that they're excited about and they're well suited for" [1].

YC group partners describe their role as helping founders reach the moment of clarity faster -- sometimes in the interview, sometimes early in the batch. The realization that the current idea does not work is not the end; it is the prerequisite for finding the right idea [1].

## The Process

Seibel frames the meta-lesson: "If you're already a builder, the process is truly about finding a problem you can solve, and often you won't know what that problem exactly is until you've actually made contact with real customers. That's the knowledge you're looking for" [1].

Alstromer adds: "One of the big awesome questions that founders should always ask themselves is: 'What do I understand or what do I know, and what do I believe that nobody else knows or believes?' That's actually the core of a lot of the pivots that really work incredibly well" [1].
