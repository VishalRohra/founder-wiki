# Cold Emailing Investors

[[Michael Seibel]] receives and responds to large volumes of cold emails from founders seeking investment. His advice centers on one principle: the goal of a cold email is not to close an investment. It is to get a reply [1].

## The Do's

### Make It Short

If a cold email takes two to five minutes to read, Seibel will set it aside for later, and "it's not clear when I'll get back to it" [1]. If the email can be read in 60 seconds or less, he can guarantee he will read it. The critical insight: "You need me not to sell you on writing a check and investing in your company. You need me to just read what you're working on and understand what you're working on enough to want to reply back" [1].

The test: "Can I see your email, be interested, and reply all within the same 60 to 120 seconds? That's what you're looking for" [1]. If there is any confusion about length, record yourself reading the email aloud. If it takes too long, cut it down [1].

### Include the Right Information

In that short email, Seibel wants to know [1]:

1. The problem you are trying to solve
2. What your solution is
3. Whether you have launched, and any growth metrics
4. How big the market could be
5. Whether you have co-founders
6. Whether you have the ability to write code
7. Whether you know something controversial about the problem or market that others do not know or would not agree with

### Use Plain Language

Jargon is a common failure mode. There is a difference between a customer pitch and an investor pitch. Customers know the jargon of their industry. Investors may not. "Make sure you're using simple language, language that you can use with any friend that you have, regardless of whether they're in your industry" [1]. See also [[Garry Tan]]'s advice on plain English in [[Applying to YC]].

### Send From a Company Email

Use a company email address with your name in it. Seibel notes that emails from personal addresses or generic "info@" addresses "just kind of set things off the wrong way" [1]. Most investors use tools like Rapportive or Superhuman that surface information about the sender. A personal email address prevents this from working [1].

### Track Opens

Use email tracking to confirm whether investors have actually seen the email. This data informs whether to follow up [1].

### Attach a Deck (Optional)

Attaching a deck is fine but not required. If you do, use the standard Silicon Valley format. Seibel recommends Googling "Airbnb's fundraising deck" as a template. Decks from other industries (finance, marketing) often look completely different and will confuse investors [1]. See [[Seed Fundraising]] for the full deck template.

## The Don'ts

### Don't Make It Long

Seibel repeats this because it is the single most common mistake. Founders try to accomplish too much in the first email -- they want a meeting, a phone call, an investment. "In reality, what you want is a conversation. Once we're in a back-and-forth, that's when I can ask questions" [1].

### Don't Request an In-Person Meeting Immediately

Different investors have different preferences for first engagement. Pushing for an in-person meeting can backfire: "If you are perceived as just pushing for an in-person meeting, it's more often than not that investors are going to think, 'Oh, well, I don't want my arm to be twisted in a meeting where I don't know whether it's going to be a high quality meeting'" [1].

### Don't Send Multiple Follow-Ups Quickly

If you have open tracking and the investor has read the email, they have made a choice about whether to reply now or later. "You don't need to hit them over the head with multiple emails" [1].

### Don't Withhold What Your Company Does

The most important don't is a double negative: "Don't not describe what your company does" [1]. Seibel is shocked by how many emails he receives saying essentially: "Michael, I'm coming to town. I'd love to meet in person for an hour so I can tell you what I'm working on" [1].

This is the wrong order of operations. The correct approach: describe the interesting thing you are working on, get the investor to want to know more, and let the investor come to you. "You want me to be the one seeking more information from you. You want to kind of put something enticing out there and have me come to you. That's what gives you leverage" [1].
