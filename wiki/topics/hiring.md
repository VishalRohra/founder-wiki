# Hiring

Hiring is one of the highest-leverage activities for a startup founder. [[Aaron Epstein]] states the principle bluntly: "hiring well is the most important thing you can possibly do to positively impact your business and your team" [3]. [[Harj Taggar]], cofounder of Triplebyte, has observed enormous variance in how effectively companies hire, with offer acceptance rates ranging from 10% to 90% across companies using the same platform [2].

The importance of personal networks for early hiring is one of the most reinforced principles in the YC library, cited by [[Harj Taggar]] [1][2], [[Aaron Epstein]] [3], and [[Ali Rowghani]] [5] across 5 sources. The consensus: for your first hires, exhaust your personal network before turning to any other channel.

## Hiring Your First Engineer

[[Harj Taggar]] describes hiring the first engineer as "incredibly hard" because founders are already stretched thin [1]. The process involves a lot of dead ends and frustration, and "isn't the kind of work that provides you constant dopamine hits."

### Define What You Are Looking For

List all specific criteria for the ideal hire, both technical (engineering skill) and non-technical (productive working relationship) [1]. Mark each candidate against all criteria and "rigorously debate if you think they have enough strengths in some areas to make up for weaknesses in others."

Hiring decisions always involve tradeoffs: quality vs. speed (rejecting solid candidates to wait for the perfect one) or money vs. time (paying above market to close now) [1].

### Sourcing Channels, Ranked by Effectiveness

Taggar ranks sourcing strategies from most to least effective [1]:

**1. Personal Networks (Best)**
The most important source [1]. Hiring someone you have already worked with is optimal because you already know the working relationship. You also have a better chance of convincing someone you know to take the risk of joining.

Taggar is surprised by how often founders do not fully explore their networks [1]: "It's easy to be quick in assuming that none of your friends are available before even asking them." His recommended process:

1. List the best engineers you know, whether you think they are available or not. Go through Facebook and LinkedIn to jog memory.
2. Invite them to lunch or dinner to talk about the startup.
3. Make the ask: "Would you consider joining us?"
4. Whatever they answer, follow up: "If you did join us, which engineers would you most want to hire?"
5. Ask for introductions to those people.
6. Repeat with each introduction.
7. "Repeat ad infinitum. I know public company founders who still do this. Expect to be spending at least a third of your time on this alone."

For the first three engineering hires, focus exclusively on personal network hiring [1]. After that, supplement with other channels to improve team diversity.

**2. Hiring Marketplaces (Second Best)**
Platforms like Triplebyte and Hired operate as two-sided marketplaces [1]. They provide quick access to vetted, skilled engineers who are actively looking. Fees are usually around $25K per experienced engineer. Candidates are speaking with multiple companies, so competition is high. Success depends heavily on pitch quality and process speed.

**3. Generating Inbound**
Post on sites with engineering audiences: [Stack Overflow Jobs](https://stackoverflow.com/jobs), the monthly Hacker News "Who is hiring?" thread, and [AngelList](https://angel.co/recruiting) [1]. Most job sites produce volume, not quality.

Content creation helps: blog about technical choices, unusual stack decisions, or engineering challenges [1]. Taggar advises: "Set a goal of writing an article that's Hacker News front page worthy at least once a month." Interactive content like coding challenges can work but is high-risk.

Make job postings stand out: write in first person as a founder, use informal tone, move away from "corporate boilerplate" [1].

**4. Cold Outreach**
Message engineers on LinkedIn, Hacker News, and GitHub [1]. The challenge is standing out from the overwhelming volume of messages good engineers receive. Personalization is critical. Use tools like Connectifer to find email addresses. Follow up 2-3 times. Expect this approach to take up to 6 months to result in a hire.

**5. Recruiters**
Contract-based recruiters who can send more messages per day, but unlikely to achieve higher response rates than messages from a founder [1]. Train them thoroughly on how to pitch the company: "give them all the information they need, give them a day to prepare and then ask them to pitch it back to you."

**6. Technical Meetups**
High variance [1]. Small, informal, deeply technical meetups can be good for network building but unlikely to produce a quick hire. Only effective if you are either an engineer who can earn technical respect or a highly charismatic personality.

**7. Recruiting Agencies (Worst)**
"Bad adverse selection bias on the candidates they can engage. Most good engineers won't work with them" [1]. Taggar cannot think of a startup that made their first engineering hire through a recruiting agency.

## Convincing Engineers to Accept Offers

Generating an offer is expensive [2]. It takes roughly 5 onsite interviews to produce a single offer (industry standard 20% onsite-to-offer rate), consuming about 30 hours of engineering productivity. Increasing the offer acceptance rate provides direct economic value.

### Speed Matters

Being fast and responsive is a huge advantage startups have over larger companies [2]. Use software to track candidates: a spreadsheet or Airtable initially, then a real ATS like [Lever](http://lever.co/) as you grow. Make clear who reviews and acts on candidates daily.

Taggar warns against rationalizing lost candidates: "It's easy to believe that candidates who drop out because you were slow weren't interested in your company anyway. This is bad reasoning" [2].

### The First Call (25 minutes)

Taggar recommends this agenda [2]:
1. Introduce yourself, the company, and what you work on
2. Ask the candidate what is important to them in their next role (move this earlier, not to the end, so you can tailor your pitch)
3. Ask about skills and experience
4. Pitch the work and why it is an exciting time to join
5. Answer questions and explain next steps

At early-stage companies (<20 people), a founder should always do this call [2]. Taggar recommends the sales/non-technical founder so the technical founder gets uninterrupted building time (per [[Paul Graham]]'s [maker vs manager schedule](http://www.paulgraham.com/makersschedule.html)).

When recruiters handle these calls, set up monthly reporting on call volume and opt-out rates [2]. Assign an engineer to train the recruiter on technical challenges. Provide specific technical questions in multiple-choice format so the recruiter can ask them confidently.

### The Onsite Interview

Standard agenda: welcome (5 min), 4 technical interviews (1 hour each), lunch (1 hour), behavioral/culture interview (45 min), closing Q&A (25 min) [2].

Top three complaints from engineers about bad onsite experiences (from Triplebyte data) [2]:
1. Interviewers being unprepared and knowing nothing about the candidate
2. Interviewers not being familiar with the technical questions they asked
3. Interviewers being determined to prove their own intelligence

Make good interviewing part of company culture [2]. Write an interviewing guide emphasizing being on-time, prepared, and friendly. Create a centralized question bank. Only select the friendliest, most representative engineers as interviewers.

Lunch tip: announce the candidate to the team, have everyone join the lunch table, do a lighthearted icebreaker [2]. "We've had candidates tell us this experience was the deciding factor in joining."

### Making the Offer

**Step One**: Call the same day as the onsite to tell them they will receive an offer [2]. Mention specific reasons from their interview performance. Have multiple team members on the call if possible.

**Step Two**: Call the next day with offer details [2]. Before discussing equity, ask how familiar they are with concepts like stock options or RSUs. Do not overwhelm the unfamiliar. See also [[Equity and Compensation]].

Formal offer letter should include: salary, benefits, signing bonus, health insurance (with policy document links), 401(k), vacation policy, and equity details (total shares, shares outstanding, percentage ownership, exercise price, vesting schedule) [2].

### Closing

Get the entire team involved [2]. Everyone who met the candidate should send a follow-up email with specific reasons they enjoyed the interaction. Describe what the first two weeks would look like. "Nitty gritty details will make the offer feel more real."

Include investors in every engineering offer at early stage [2]. Choose the investor most likely to sell that specific candidate.

Think about the candidate's personal circumstances: other decision makers (spouse, family), relocation concerns [2]. Taggar describes a recruiter who researched career opportunities for a candidate's wife who was reluctant to relocate, gathered contact information and compensation data, and offered to speak with her. "The candidate accepted the offer shortly afterwards."

### Competing Against Big Companies

Arguments that work for startups competing against Facebook and Google [2]:

1. **Learning**: "You learn the fastest by being given real decision making responsibility. Large companies won't give you this kind of responsibility for years."
2. **Career progression**: "Silicon Valley and the technology industry are unique because you can rise up through the ranks of a company faster than anywhere else (e.g. the Chief Product Officer of Facebook ($500bn market cap) is 36 years old)."
3. **Opportunity cost**: "At any point in time there will always be the safe option of working at a public technology company. The names might change but the experience of working at one is fungible."
4. **Mentorship**: If you have experienced engineers who can mentor, make sure to tell candidates [2].

### Exploding Offers

Taggar advises against aggressive deadlines: "good candidates with multiple options will push back" [2]. A softer alternative is an expiring signing bonus: "We want you to take all the time you need to make the right decision. If you are able to make a final decision by X date, we'd be able to offer you a $X,000 signing bonus."

## Hiring Philosophy

[[Aaron Epstein]] encapsulates the core principle: "Hire slow, fire fast. It's the thing everyone says and few have the discipline to do" [3]. Great people want to work with other great people, so resist the temptation to fill positions with mediocre candidates. "You should be thrilled about each new hire" [3].

Epstein also advises hiring for hunger over specific skills: "passion for your mission, business, and team will do more to drive an employee to make a big, long-term impact than any specific skills they may have" [3].

At the senior level, [[Ali Rowghani]] describes how [[Patrick Collison]] of Stripe "made it a point to meet with the 'best-in-the-world' in each field so he could get a sense of what a great candidate looks like" [5]. Executive hiring takes extraordinary time and should be staged rather than done all at once. Rowghani recommends using an executive search firm for the first couple of searches despite high cost [5]. Expect that at least 25% of leadership hires will not work out. See also [[Management]] and [[The Second Job of a Startup CEO]] for more on executive hiring.
