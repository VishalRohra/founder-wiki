# Hiring

Hiring is where many startups first encounter the tension between growth ambition and survival. Graham calls hiring too fast "by far the biggest killer of startups that raise money." The YC canon treats hiring as a high-stakes decision that should be approached with caution, not speed.

## When to Hire

Graham argues that founders often hire to solve slow growth, when the real problem is that the product is not appealing enough. "There is surprisingly little connection between how much a startup spends and how fast it grows." He cites Airbnb, which waited four months after raising money before hiring employee #1.

The right time to hire is when there is clearly more work than the founders can do and the type of work is well-understood. Hiring into ambiguity — before the founders know what needs to be built — creates more problems than it solves.

## Your First Engineering Hire

Taggar recommends focusing the first three engineering hires exclusively on personal network hiring. His 7-step pipeline: list everyone you know who codes, invite them to try your product, ask if they would consider joining, follow up, ask for introductions to engineers they respect, and repeat.

He frames hiring as sales: "Asking anyone to join as your first hire is asking for an order of magnitude greater commitment than pitching an investor to invest." The pitch to candidates is fundamentally different from the pitch to investors — candidates care about growth opportunity, technical challenge, and the team they will work with, not market size.

Taggar ranks hiring channels in order of effectiveness: personal networks, marketplaces (Triplebyte, AngelList), inbound applications, cold outreach, recruiters, meetups, agencies.

## Hire for Hunger

Epstein argues in "Advice for New Managers" that startups should hire for hunger — passion for the mission will drive more long-term impact than any specific skill set. The "hire slow, fire fast" principle applies: take time to find the right person, but act quickly when a hire is not working.

## The Danger of Overhiring

Graham's "Default Alive or Default Dead" makes the case most forcefully: hiring is the primary way startups burn through their runway without improving their trajectory. Each hire increases burn rate but may not increase growth. Founders should model their path to profitability with and without each hire.

In "The 18 Mistakes," Graham lists hiring bad programmers (#6) and spending too much (#12) — both of which frequently manifest as overhiring.
