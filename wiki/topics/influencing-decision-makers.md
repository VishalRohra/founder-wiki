# Influencing Decision Makers

Many of the most important decisions in a founder's life -- fundraising decisions, promotions, budget approvals, partnerships -- are made in rooms where the founder is not present. [[Dalton Caldwell]] and [[Michael Seibel]] discuss what founders can do to influence those decisions beyond making strong factual arguments [1].

## Table Stakes: A Coherent Argument

A strong logical case is the starting point, not the differentiator. "Let's just assume you've made an amazing factual argument. That's table stakes" [1]. The question is what else influences the decision when you are not in the room.

## Attitude and Demeanor

Long-term demeanor matters more than any single interaction. Seibel notes that people develop personal brands, sometimes based on a single incident: "It's like how you get a nickname. You do one thing once when you're 18, and for the rest of your life that's your nickname" [1].

Negativity is particularly damaging. The partners distinguish between being realistic and being "always negative about everything." Even when negative people are making excellent factual points, they can tip over the line. The question decision makers ask is: "Do we want other people on the team to act like this person?" [1].

## The "More Time" Test

When decision makers are in the room, they are implicitly deciding whether they want to spend more time with the person. If someone is doing a Series A, the investor is signing up for board membership and years of interaction. If someone is getting promoted, it may mean more meetings and closer collaboration [1].

This does not mean founders must be nice. But decision makers think about what they are signing up for [1].

## Being Irreplaceable

Seibel introduces the concept of irreplaceability as the strongest form of influence. The thought experiment: "If this person disappeared tomorrow, how hosed are you?" [1].

He applies this test in hiring: 90 days after a new hire starts, put a calendar event asking "How would you feel if that person said today is my last day?" Anything other than distraught means the person did not make themselves irreplaceable [1].

"If you really want to influence the room where it happens, you can't be a B player. You can't be like, 'Well, they get some stuff done.' Irreplaceable is really where it happens" [1].

The same test applies to companies and [[Product-Market Fit]]: "If your company went away tomorrow, how sad would people be and how hosed would their business be?" If AWS disappeared, websites go down. That is irreplaceability at the product level [1].

## Heart vs. Mercenary

Caldwell identifies another factor: whether someone gives more to an organization than they take, or the reverse. Decision makers try to assess motivation: "Is this a mercenary? If I give them something, are they going to try to figure out how to take more?" versus someone who cares about customers, direct reports, or the product [1].

Mercenaries are easy to detect: "Man, mercenaries are easy to sniff out" [1].

## The Lobbying Trap

Both partners warn strongly against aggressive lobbying or political maneuvering. The margin for error is razor-thin: "It's a hair trigger -- if you go over the line of doing things, oh man, you lost. You lost the decision makers completely. In a very memorable way. Often it's disqualifying" [1].

Caldwell notes that weird lobbying attempts for YC admission never work: "There's never a case where we're like, 'Wow, this is a really good email, and I'm definitely going to take this seriously'" [1].

The risk is environment-dependent. In some organizations, politics may be the only way to get seen. But in every environment, going over the line is "worse than nothing. Way worse than nothing. If you just don't do it, you're ahead" [1].

## The Desperation Signal

When founders become obsessed with getting one particular investor to say yes, they tend to get rejected. Caldwell describes a Zen-like pattern: "When you're overly attached to some particular thing happening in your life, it tends to not happen" [1].

Seibel connects this to why YC changed its standard deal. When YC gave companies $125,000, founders approached Demo Day feeling desperate, and investors could sense it. With $500,000 and eighteen months of runway, the same founders had a "take it or leave it" attitude. "Investors rightly pattern match that attitude with being effective" [1].

People who seem fine regardless of the outcome -- "Hey, I'll figure it out whichever way this goes" -- tend to be rewarded. Frantic, anxious people tend to struggle [1].

## How You Handle Rejection Matters

Seibel closes with a point about the repeated-game nature of influence: how you respond to a decision not going your way is itself a factor in future decisions. "Don't go all in necessarily in situations that don't require it" [1]. The reaction to a loss becomes part of the reputation that follows you into the next room [1].
