# Company Culture

Culture is rarely the most imminent risk of death for a startup. It will not kill you this week. But Kathryn Minshew, co-founder of The Muse (YC W12), argues it is one of the most important things to think about at every stage -- when you are 2 people, 10 people, 25 and beyond [1]. The Muse grew from 12 to over 80 employees in two years, and the lessons she extracted center on a principle: culture is not what you say it is. Culture is how you behave when no one is watching [1].

[[Patrick Collison]] of Stripe frames culture as a solution to a bandwidth problem. At two people, a founder can be involved in every decision. By five or ten, this is already impossible. By fifty, it is "completely hopeless." Culture is "what the strands are that you want to have, the invariances that you want to maintain, as you can get specifically involved in fewer and fewer decisions over time" [3].

## The Four Dimensions of Culture (Patrick Collison)

Collison defines culture along four dimensions [3]:

1. **Who you hire and what those people value**
2. **What you do every day and why**
3. **What you choose to communicate**
4. **What you choose to celebrate** (and its converse: what you choose to punish)

"Running a company based on what you celebrate is more exciting than what you punish" [3].

## Culture Is Tough Choices

The core of culture is not perks, office design, or stated values on a wall. It is the willingness to make hard decisions that align with those values [1].

Minshew describes the test: finding a candidate who has every skill the company needs, a pedigree that would make investors swoon, and who "kind of might be a jerk." The culture decision is not to hire that person and to keep looking, "because you realize that the impact of one really bad person is far more important than their behavior, and that toxic behavior spreads to the rest of your team" [1].

The Muse codified six core values when they reached 25 people. Every interview candidate is tested against all six. The most well-known value: "No assholes" [1]. Minshew acknowledges the skepticism -- "A lot of companies say that" -- but argues that the difference is whether you enforce it when it costs you something.

## Culture Is Behavior, Not Words

"Culture is how the founders behave, how the executives behave" [1]. Putting values on a wall is not culture. Living them under pressure is.

Minshew tells a cautionary story: a company that served steak and sushi at Friday lunches, then gradually downgraded to hot dogs during the 2008 recession. When an employee asked if the company was cutting costs, the office head replied: "No, it's Americana themed." Minshew's assessment: "That's bullshit, and everybody knows it" [1].

The alternative: be honest. "When you treat your employees like kids, they're going to behave as such, versus being open and honest, even if the answer is, 'The economy's tough, so we're going to be a little bit more careful'" [1].

## Transparency as a Cultural Practice

### The Muse's Approach

The Muse practices transparency through several mechanisms [1]:

- **Board deck sharing**: The entire company sees board decks at All Hands after every board meeting
- **Full budget review**: A 20-slide presentation walking through every line in the budget -- total spend, revenue, burn, payroll, health insurance, office supplies, marketing expenses, and the rationale for each allocation
- **Open trade-off discussions**: After the budget review, the company discussed trade-offs openly ("Why aren't we providing free lunch? What did we decide to spend that money on instead?")

The budget review produced an unexpected benefit: executives self-edited their requests. After seeing the full picture, several said "I asked for this much, but actually, I can do what I need with less" [1]. Transparency generated buy-in for scrappiness without mandating it from above.

### Stripe's Radical Email Transparency

Stripe placed extraordinary emphasis on transparency as a core cultural value. Collison explains the reasoning: if everyone believes in the mission and has "really good access to information and a good picture of the current state of Stripe, then that gets you a huge amount of the way there in terms of working productively together. And it kind of forgives a lot of the other things that tend to break as you grow a startup" [3].

In the early days, Stripe BCC'd every other person at the company on every outgoing email unless the sender opted out [3]. The rationale: fewer meetings needed if everyone could stay abreast of what was happening. As the company grew past 50 people, this created problems -- new employees had 2,000 emails in their inbox on the first week and Gmail itself broke at one point from the volume [3].

Stripe evolved the system through increasingly sophisticated tooling: mailing lists, Gmail filter generation programs, weekly all-hands with decks, and a tool called Hackpad (described as "Google Docs but with a news feed") where all documents were public by default [3].

The challenge of transparency at scale: when someone contacts a partner with an idea and 170 people can see it, "maybe a person sitting across the way from you thinks that's the stupidest idea they've ever heard." Stripe developed cultural norms around when it is appropriate to jump into discussions and how to give drive-by feedback without discouraging sharing [3].

Collison's assessment: "I can't give a much stronger endorsement of it than it has worked so far pretty good" [3].

## Scrappiness After Raising Money

A common warning in the startup world: "Don't raise too much money, because you will spend it frivolously." Minshew pushes back: "That takes away the agency from the founders, from the leadership, and I think that's a little bit silly" [1].

The Muse raised $10 million for their Series A when they had targeted $5-6 million. Rather than letting the extra capital erode their scrappy culture, they used the budget review to maintain discipline. They surveyed employees to understand which benefits mattered most, ensuring money went where it counted [1].

The lesson: raising more money than planned does not automatically destroy culture if leadership is intentional about communicating how the money will be used and why discipline still matters.

## Perks Are Not Culture

"Say no to ball pits. I think that there's a lot of these startup floor things, and frankly, I think if you need something like this to recruit, you probably have other problems" [1].

## The First Ten Hires Set Everything

The first ten employees are disproportionately important because each one effectively brings ten more people with them. Collison frames it as a compounding decision: "You're not just hiring those first ten people. You're actually kind of hiring a hundred people because you should think of each one of those people as bringing along sort of another ten people with them" [3]. See [[Hiring at Early Stage]] for specific tactics on finding and evaluating early hires.

## Hiring for Culture: What Silbermann, Collison, and Collison Look For

### Ben Silbermann (Pinterest)

[[Ben Silbermann]] describes culture as more like gardening than architecture: "You plant some seeds, and then you pull out weeds that aren't working, and they sort of expand" [3].

His filter for early hires [3]:
- **Hard work, high integrity, low ego**
- **Creativity expressed through curiosity**: "Some of our first employees are probably some of the quirkiest people I've ever met." One had created his own board game with elaborate rules. Another was into magic tricks and had coded a magic trick app for iPhone and shot the production video himself.
- **Desire to build something great without arrogance**

In the early days, the horrible office and zero pay were natural selection mechanisms: "There was no external reason other than being excited about building something to join. In fact, there was every reason not to" [3].

For evaluating talent in unfamiliar domains, Silbermann developed a preparation ritual: before interviewing candidates for a role he did not understand (head of finance, head of marketing), he would first talk to people he knew were world-class in that discipline. He asked them: "What are the key traits? What questions do you ask? If you're looking for the next person as good as you, where are they working right now? And what's their phone number?" [3].

### Patrick and John Collison (Stripe)

The Collisons identified three common traits in their first ten hires [3]:

1. **Genuine and straightforward**: People others trust. Intellectual honesty in how they approach problems.
2. **Enjoyed finishing things**: "There's a lot of people who are really excited about tons of things. Only a subset of those are actually excited about completing things." Collison is skeptical of hiring based on GitHub activity, because it rewards starting many things over going deep on one.
3. **Cared intensely about quality**: "It was offensive to them when something was just a little bit off." Early Stripe routed every single API error to all inboxes and phones. Team members copied each other on all outgoing emails and pointed out grammar or spelling mistakes.

For finding early talent, the Collisons describe thinking like value investors: "You're looking for the human capital that's significantly undervalued by the market." Their first designer was 18 and in high school in Sweden. Their CTO was in college. "You can relax one constraint -- relax the fact that it's apparent that they're talented" [3].

A practical test for talent: "Is this person probably the best out of all of their friends at what they do?" [3].

### Work Trial Before Hiring

Collison recommends working with candidates before committing, especially for the first ten hires. For a majority of Stripe's first ten, the team worked with them for about a week before extending an offer. "It's pretty hard to fake it for a week" [3].

[[John Collison]] adds that founders should have the confidence to interview in ways that work for them, not cargo-cult standard processes. When Stripe hired its first engineer, they flew the candidate out and spent a weekend coding with him -- "the only way we could really tell" [3].

## Onboarding

### The Small-Team Version

Silbermann describes Pinterest's early onboarding: "Here's your computer. We already set up your environment. This is the problem that we have to solve together." Because the team worked from a tiny two-bedroom apartment, relationship-building happened automatically [3].

The one critical addition at every stage: remind new hires of the long-term vision. "It's really easy when you first join to drop someone into a problem, and then they think the whole world is like this little problem in front of them. We'd always say, 'Someday we want to do for discovery what Google did for search'" [3].

### The Scaled Version

As Pinterest grew, onboarding became a week-long program with function-specific tracks. They measure two output metrics: new hire satisfaction (surveyed immediately and at 30 days) and manager/peer assessments of ramp-up speed. A critical rule: if a team is not doing a good job bringing new people up to speed, that team should not be hiring more people [3].

Silbermann emphasizes learning about the person: "What are their aspirations? What's their working style? How do they like to be recognized? Do they really prefer being in total silence? Are they a morning person or night person?" [3].

### Push Off the Cliff (Stripe)

John Collison advocates getting new hires doing real work immediately: engineers committing code on day one, business hires in real meetings on day one. "Sometimes there's a tendency to be tentative and help ease people in. We're much more of the 'push people off the cliff' school" [3].

The second priority is rapid cultural feedback. When Stripe's culture was more written (people sitting next to each other with headphones on, emailing each other), new hires needed explicit coaching on these norms. "Everything from high level -- how you're doing at your job -- to minor cultural pointers, the more feedback you can give them, the better they'll do" [3].

## Scaling Culture

### Maintaining a "Startup of Startups" (Silbermann)

Silbermann describes Pinterest's goal as making the company feel like "a startup of many startups, rather than this monolithic organization with a set of foreign processes that cut horizontally through it" [3]. Each team should control the resources it needs, know what the most important thing is, and know how it is measured. This makes the management problem "somewhat tractable" [3].

The challenge at Pinterest: each team needs a strong designer, a strong engineering leader, often a writer, and often a community manager -- all self-contained. This cross-functional structure is harder to build but supports the company's product philosophy of putting curious people with diverse skills together, anchoring them on a single project, and removing barriers to speed [3].

### Time Horizons Shift (Patrick Collison)

As a company ages, the time horizon for planning lengthens correspondingly. After one month, you plan one month ahead. After one year, you plan one year ahead. After four years, four years ahead [3].

This affects hiring: "In the really early days, you kind of have to hire people who will be productive essentially immediately. You don't have the luxury of hiring people who are really promising but aren't quite going to be up to speed for another year or two." After two or three years, making longer-term investments in people becomes not just reasonable but necessary [3].

### Growing Early Hires into Leaders

Collison states that several of Stripe's first ten people now hold leadership roles. The key: "People don't necessarily come out of the womb being good at managing or being good at leadership. And being able to develop that in people is a lot of work at a time when everyone is running around with their hair on fire. But it's also sort of damaging if the company can't develop that skill" [3].

John Collison adds nuance: some people try management and succeed. Others try it and say "I'm really glad I tried it because I never want to do that again." The company must ensure both paths -- management and individual contribution -- offer equal impact. "My strong preference is that you give as many people a shot as possible" [3].

## Referencing: The Quantitative Back-Channel

Silbermann describes reference checks as one of the most important and underused hiring tools [3]. The key principles:

- **Test self-awareness**: "If I asked Jonathan what you're best at, or what you'd be most proud of, what would he say?" This creates social accountability because the candidate knows you will actually talk to the reference.
- **Force scarcity**: Ask references to rank the candidate: "Top 1% of people you've ever worked with, top 5%, or top 10%?" Without this forcing function, references default to generic positives.
- **Spend real time**: 15 minutes on the phone, not a quick confirmation call.
- **Use references as a recruiting source**: "References are also a tremendous source of new recruits" [3].

## Authenticity in Recruiting

All three founders reject whitewashing difficulties when recruiting. Silbermann shares his interview technique: when a candidate says "I'm really passionate about what you're doing," he asks "Where else are you interviewing?" If they list seven unrelated companies at the same stage, "that's a sign that they're probably not being authentic about what they care about. Those folks, often when things get really hard, they won't stick it out" [3].

Collison cites the PayPal and iPhone recruiting stories as models: PayPal told recruits "Visa and MasterCard want to kill us and we might be doing something illegal. But if you succeed, you'll redefine payments." The iPhone team told recruits "You won't see your families for three years, but when you're done, your kids' kids will remember what you built" [3].

The principle: be transparent about why the opportunity is amazing and lay out in detail why it will be hard. "The right people select in, or they select out" [3].

## Culture Is What You Make Surface Area (Sarah Nahm)

[[Sarah Nahm]] introduces the concept of "cultural surface area" -- the boundaries of what a company considers part of its culture. At Lever, dish duty was not part of the culture until someone pointed out that a disproportionate group of people (mostly women) did the dishes. The team built a Slackbot for rotational dish duty [2].

Similarly, Lever's holiday calendar was not cultural surface area until the team pushed back on not including Martin Luther King Day. "We realized in that moment that our holiday schedule was surface area to our culture" [2].

## Inclusion Before Diversity

Nahm argues: "What's the point of hiring all this quote diverse talent if when they get there they're not ready to succeed?" Start with an inclusive culture; diverse hiring follows [2]. See [[Diversity and Inclusion]] for a detailed treatment of DNI at early-stage startups.

## The Technical/Non-Technical Divide

Lever rejected Silicon Valley's dichotomy with a training called "I'm Technical and So Are You" because the divide was holding both sides back: engineers did not understand customers, and non-engineers did not think in scalable systems [2].

## Employee Resource Groups with Policy Power

At 65% participation, Lever's ERGs were asked to help build company policies -- the parents ERG co-designed the parental leave policy [2].

## It Does Get Easier

Minshew challenges the common advice that "it doesn't get easier." In the early days of The Muse, she cold-emailed 800 student groups for the first users, pitched over 150 VC firms, and got 148 rejections in six months [1]. People told her constantly that it only gets harder.

Her rebuttal: it never gets easy, but the risk of death recedes. With a team of executives, a single departure does not kill the company. "Yes, the challenges keep going, and yes, it's always going to be hard. But it does, in some ways, get a little bit easier" [1]. The early stage is "blood, sweat, tears and Ramen. And now, sometimes, I eat in restaurants" [1].

## Focus Changes Over Time

Minshew describes a critical transition at The Muse. The company was beloved by users -- people emailed daily saying the product changed their lives. But growth had slowed. Revenue was flat. "We had to have a really tough moment where we said, 'If we don't start focusing on the things that matter, on moving our growth, on figuring out revenue, we're going to be the most loved startup that nobody ever gets to use anymore, because it's dead'" [1].

The shift: hiring a sales leader, figuring out pricing and revenue model, while continuing to build the user experience. The lesson: what is most important changes over time, and culture must support the willingness to make those shifts honestly.

## Remember Why You Started

Minshew's advice for sustaining motivation over the long haul: remember why you started [1]. For her, it was the experience of being in a job she did not love, going online to look for a new one, and thinking "This is the best there is? I'm almost offended by how bad that experience is" [1].

When energy flags at four and a half years in, she reads user emails or talks to someone who found their job through The Muse. "Being a startup can be one part one of the most meaningful experiences of your life, and one part a total slog. But I think when you remember why you got into it, you keep your focus on building for the long haul" [1].

## Airbnb: Culture as Competitive Moat (Brian Chesky and Alfred Lin)

[[Brian Chesky]] and [[Alfred Lin]] provide one of the most detailed treatments of how culture is built from day one and how it influences strategic decisions at scale [3].

### Three Things Nobody Tells You About Culture

Chesky identifies three reasons culture is systematically neglected [3]:

1. **Nobody talks about it.** "There's tons of articles about building a great product. There's very few things about culture."
2. **It's hard to measure.** "Things that are hard to measure often get discounted."
3. **It doesn't pay off in the short term.** "If you wanted to build a company in one year and sell it as quickly as possible, the number one piece of advice I give you is forget the culture." Culture makes you hire slowly and be deliberate about decisions that slow near-term progress. "It's really about building a company for the long term."

### Culture Must Be Designed

Chesky studied companies that endured -- Apple, Amazon, Nike -- and concluded they all had a clear mission and clear values. "Culture needs to be designed" [3]. He and Gebbia wrote Airbnb's core values before hiring their first employee, spending four to five months finding that first engineer. "I kind of felt like your first engineer was like bringing in DNA to your company. There were going to be a thousand people just like him or her" [3].

### Core Values Interviewers

Airbnb uses dedicated core values interviewers who are never from the candidate's function. An engineering candidate is assessed for cultural fit by non-engineers, to prevent technical skill from biasing the values assessment. "We've said no to a lot of really great people because we just didn't feel right about them being with us long term" [3].

Chesky's hiring bar: "If you could hire anybody in the world, would you hire the person sitting across from you?" Every hire should raise the standard [3].

### Missionaries vs. Mercenaries

In 2011, the Samwer brothers cloned Airbnb, raised $90 million, and hired 400 people in 30 days. Airbnb had 40 employees and $7 million. Multiple advisors recommended acquiring the clone. Chesky refused: "I felt like we were missionaries and they were mercenaries. I didn't think they were doing it for the beliefs" [3].

His logic: "I believed that missionaries would outlast and out-endure mercenaries." He also observed that "the best revenge against an internet clone was just to make them run the company long term." Today, more than 50% of Airbnb's revenue comes from Europe [3].

### Brand Is Culture Turned Outward

Chesky argues that culture and brand are "two sides of the same coin." Culture is the internal principles and beliefs. Brand is the external promise. "Whatever happens inside the company eventually comes out" [3].

He cites Steve Jobs's 1997 Think Different campaign as the model: instead of talking about bits and bytes, Apple declared "We believe people with passion could change the world." The campaign preceded Apple's renaissance into the most valuable company in the world. "If that doesn't happen, you're a utility. And utilities get sold at commodity prices" [3].

### The CEO's Three Jobs

Chesky distills the CEO role to three functions [3]:
1. Articulate the vision
2. Develop the strategy
3. Hire people who fit the culture

"If you do those three things, you basically have a company" [3].

## Alfred Lin's Culture Framework

[[Alfred Lin]] provides the analytical scaffolding for Chesky's stories [3].

### Culture Defined

Lin defines company culture as: "Every day, the [core values] and [actions] of each member of the team in pursuit of our company [mission]" [3].

### Why Culture Matters: The FIRST Framework

Lin organizes benefits into an acronym [3]:
- **F**irst principles for decision-making
- **I**dentity alignment on values
- **R**esilience and stability
- **S**howing what not to do (more important than showing what to do)
- **T**alent retention of the right employees

### The Evidence: Stock Returns

Lin cites research showing Fortune 100 Best Companies to Work For returned 11.8% annually from 1997-2003, nearly double the S&P 500 (6.8%) and Russell 3000 (6.04%) [3].

### Culture as Daily Habit

Lin compares culture to fitness: "You just can't be fit if you don't do it as a daily habit. Eventually you get out of shape, then you get fat." Culture must be integrated into hiring (interview for culture fit, not just skills), performance reviews, and daily operations [3].

### Zappos: One Year to Write Ten Values

At Zappos, Lin and Tony Hsieh asked all employees what core values they identified with. They received 37 submissions and spent a year whittling them to 10. The year was necessary because single words are meaningless: "If you just come up with the word 'honesty,' I mean, give me a break. Everybody wants the culture to be honest" [3].

Two Zappos examples [3]:
- **"Deliver WOW through service"**: Specifically committed to a "wow experience," supported by a paragraph defining what "wow" meant for employees, customers, partners, and investors.
- **"Be humble"**: Derived from the founders not liking arrogant people. Flip the negative to find the value.

### Patrick Lencioni's Five Dysfunctions

Lin references "The Five Dysfunctions of a Team" to illustrate how culture failures cascade [3]:
1. Absence of trust prevents honest debate
2. Fear of conflict prevents arriving at right answers
3. Lack of commitment follows from unclear answers
4. Avoidance of accountability follows from weak commitment
5. Inattention to results follows from no accountability

## Mission Follows Product-Love (Sam Altman)

[[Sam Altman]]'s 2016 *Before Growth* essay names a structural observation about mission that cuts against the standard cultural-values-first playbook: "Startups that don't first figure out a product some users love also seem to rarely develop the sense of mission that the best companies have" [5]. The ordering is load-bearing — mission, in this framing, is downstream of product-love, not upstream. Chesky and Lin's argument that culture must be set deliberately in the first year assumes the product side is functional; Altman's observation is that culture statements authored before the team has experienced users loving the product tend to be performative. The synthesis: build the thing users love first, then codify the culture that produced the love. See [[Product-Market Fit]] and [[Growth]].

## Defining Culture Is a Founding-Team Role (Sam Altman)

Altman's 2017 profile of Greg Brockman frames culture-definition as one of the non-technical founder-level responsibilities, alongside making offers, organizing offsites, letting everyone work out of his apartment, and cleaning up after meals [6]. "It's important to have someone great in this role at a small startup -- many people gloss over it" [6]. The structural claim is the same as Alfred Lin's — culture is not downstream of the first engineering hire, it is set by the founder who does the role and the behaviors that role models early. See [[Co-Founders]] for the Chief Optimist framing of the same role.
