# Company Culture

Company culture is how a startup's team behaves when no one is watching. Multiple YC speakers argue that culture is not a nice-to-have -- it is a structural decision that determines hiring quality, retention, and the company's ability to scale. Kathryn Minshew, co-founder of The Muse (YC W12), and Alfred Lin, former COO of Zappos, provide complementary perspectives: Minshew from the operational trenches of building culture at a growing startup, Lin from the theoretical foundations of what makes culture a competitive advantage.

## Culture Is Behavior, Not Posters

Minshew is blunt: "Culture is how you behave when no one's watching. Culture is how the founders behave, how the executives behave" [1]. She distinguishes between stated values and actual culture. Companies that put values on walls but do not live them create cynicism.

Her example: a friend's company served fancy free lunches every Friday. When the 2008 recession hit, the food degraded to hot dogs. When an employee asked if the company was cutting back, the office head replied, "No, it's Americana themed." "That's bullshit, and everybody knows it. When you treat your employees like kids, they're going to behave as such" [1].

## When to Define Culture

Minshew argues culture matters from the very beginning -- when you are 2 people, 10 people, 25 people [1]. The Muse grew from 12 to over 80 people in two years, and Minshew learned that the investment in culture must scale with headcount.

At about 25 people, The Muse wrote down six core values. Every interview candidate is tested against all six values. Minshew warns against the fake culture screen of "Would I want to get a beer with this person?" -- this is overly simplistic and causes founders to miss candidates who are different from them [1].

## The "No Assholes" Rule

One of The Muse's best-known values is "No assholes." Minshew acknowledges many companies claim this but few enforce it. "Culture is tough choices. Culture is needing that hire, looking for that one person who's going to solve the biggest problem for your company's existence, and finally finding someone who has every skill you need, who has a pedigree and a background that will make your investors swoon, and that kind of might be a jerk. And deciding not to hire them and keep bleeding and keep looking, because you realize that the impact of one really bad person is far more important than their behavior, and that toxic behavior spreads to the rest of your team" [1].

## Transparency as Culture

The Muse practices radical transparency [1]:
- Shares board decks with the entire company after every board meeting
- Ran all employees through the full company budget in exhaustive detail
- After raising $10M Series A, created a 20-slide presentation showing every budget line item, explaining trade-offs

The result: executives voluntarily reduced their budget requests. "People were self-editing their requests, because they bought in to where we were going, and why it was so important for us to stay thrifty, stay scrappy and stay lean" [1].

## Culture and Scrappiness

Minshew pushes back on the common advice that raising more money inevitably leads to wasteful spending: "I think when people say, 'If you raise more, you will spend it,' that takes away the agency from the founders" [1]. The Muse raised $10M when they expected $5-6M and used the budget transparency exercise to maintain their scrappy ethos despite the larger war chest.

Her summary: "Say no to ball pits. If you need something like this to recruit, you probably have other problems" [1].

## It Does Get Easier

Minshew challenges the common startup wisdom that "it doesn't get easier." After 148 VC rejections out of 150 pitches post-YC, she learned that "sometimes, it actually kind of does. It never gets easy, but the risk of death is not quite so imminent" [1]. As a company builds an executive team, individual crises become less existentially threatening.
