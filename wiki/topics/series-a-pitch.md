# Series A Pitch and Deck

[[Janelle Tam]], YC's Series A Program Manager, compiled feedback from hundreds of hours working 1:1 with founders to workshop pitches and decks [1]. "Every single piece of feedback given here is something we've told one of our founders internally" [1].

## Purpose of a Series A Pitch

"The purpose of a Series A pitch is to show investors why they should invest in your business" [1]. It provides a clear overview of what you have built, then paints a picture of where it could go and why raising money will help [1].

Six principles unpacked [1]:

1. **"Why they should invest"**: This is an investor pitch, not a customer pitch. Good pitches explain the problem customers face, show how the product solves it, and demonstrate why this is a good business.

2. **"Clear and concise"**: "Think of investor attention as a limited resource." Use plain English. Avoid jargon. Where jargon is necessary, define it on first use.

3. **"Overview"**: The goal is to "tantalize investors with enough information to keep them interested." Leave room for questions. Pitches should be 15-20 minutes, setting up a 40-45 minute discussion. "If investors aren't asking questions or engaging at all, that's a red flag -- it likely means your slides are too complicated."

4. **"Of the business you've built"**: Start with what exists. "It's harder to raise an A based purely on promise."

5. **"Paint a picture of where it's going"**: Show how present reality trends toward the vision. "Once you've established that you've already built something impressive, then you have the credibility you need to show how it could grow into a massive company."

6. **"Why raising money will help"**: The deck should make clear "that the obstacle to your growth is actually money (not incompetence, poor product quality, a lack of product-market fit, etc.)."

## The Starting Exercise: Fundraising Vertebrae

1. Write your narrative as 10-15 bullet points -- the "fundraising vertebrae" [1].
2. Under each bullet, list data points as evidence [1].
3. Build the deck: each bullet becomes one slide. The bullet is the title; the data points are the content. "If you're trying to make more than one point per slide, your slide is too complicated" [1]. This means 10-15 slides, excluding appendix.
4. Write out all possible investor questions and prepare clear, data-backed answers [1].

See also [[Pitch Deck Design]] for [[Kevin Hale]]'s visual design principles (legible, simple, obvious).

## Deck Structure

### Title (1 slide)
Company name/logo and a one-sentence description [1]. Requirements:
- **Concrete:** "We build and operate robot greenhouses" not "Reinventing agriculture."
- **Layperson-friendly:** "Help people quit smoking" not "Creating behavior change among nicotine addicts."
- **Specific to what YOU do:** "We make plant-based cheese" not "Plant-based dairy."

### Traction Teaser (1 slide)
Place this early. "Putting this slide early grabs the investor's attention and convinces them that your presentation is worth listening closely to" [1]. Provides a smooth transition from one-liner to problem statement.

### Problem (1 slide)
How the world currently works for customers and what is wrong with it [1].

- **Solve YOUR specific problem.** Bad: "Americans spend $400B on mental health problems." Good: "x Americans need therapy but can't pay for it."
- **Present from customer's perspective.** The customer is whoever is paying. In healthcare, that is usually insurers, not patients. In employee benefits, employers (HR departments).
- **Show, not tell.** Bad: "Medical billing is broken." Good: "Here's how medical billing works today: patients need to do X steps, which takes Y months of delay in reimbursement and results in only Z% of claims being approved."

### Solution (1 slide)
What you do and how it changes the world [1].

- **Side-by-side comparison** with the status quo.
- **Quantify impact.** "Don't tell me you have the fastest/easiest/cheapest product, actually show me numbers."
- **Focus on what you do now**, not future plans. "Investors will discount these hand-wavy hypotheticals to zero."
- **Explain how customers use it**, not just what it can do. "Our database allows customers to verify someone's identity with X% accuracy in Y minutes" rather than "We have a database of 100k entries."

### Traction (a few slides)
Your numbers should tell a story, starting with the most important metric [1]. If you have revenue, lead with revenue and explain the business model.

**Enterprise narrative:** Revenue growing fast, solid business model (margins trending over time), great at acquiring customers profitably (sales cycle, ACV, salesperson efficiency), customers love the product (time to onboard, adoption rate, churn) [1].

**Consumer narrative:** Revenue growing fast, profitable acquisition (organic vs. paid breakdown, CAC/LTV, distribution channels), strong engagement (DAU/WAU/MAU, retention curves, time on platform) [1].

**Hardtech/biotech:** Technology efficacy data, patents filed, LOIs/partnerships, milestone achievements (510(k), IND application) [1].

**Show trends.** Monthly or quarterly graphs, not cumulative annual numbers [1]. Need at least 4-6 months of trend data. "The longer you've been around, the more you have to have done." YC's [benchmarks](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/1k-benchmarks) assume 18-24 months post-seed [1].

**Three presentation traps** [1]:
1. **Too many numbers:** Information overload. Identify "hero facts" -- headline numbers that stick in investors' minds.
2. **Too few numbers:** "It feels like you're hiding your numbers because you think they're weak."
3. **Unclear numbers:** Cumulative numbers are almost always used to hide weak monthly numbers. Double-axis graphs waste time clarifying axes. Graphs should be "understandable at first glance."

### Market (1 slide)
Bottoms-up calculation: *number of prospective customers x value of each customer to you* [1]. Use real numbers from your business, not top-down reports. For new categories, show analogies to massive companies that did similar things [1].

### Competition (1 slide)
Show why you are 10x better [1]. Convince investors your moat is defensible.

### Vision (1 slide)
"Show how you become a $10B company" [1]. This is the thought exercise YC used in the Series A program. After building credibility with present-tense data, "now when you talk about the future, investors are more likely to believe you" [1].

### Team (1 slide)
Move this to slide 2-3 if the team is a comparative advantage (successful prior exit >$100M, or one of the few people in the world uniquely equipped -- PhD, former senior executive, ex-professor) [1]. Otherwise: show why founders are right for this company and that you can recruit needed skills. If you have been lean (accomplished everything with 4 people) or capital efficient ($1M spend to $1.2M ARR), highlight it [1].

**Signaling risk:** Be cautious listing existing Series A fund investors. "If you include a Series A fund on the list of existing investors, you'll be asked whether or not they're leading your round" [1].

### Use of Funds (1 slide)
"The ask is the climax of your whole deck" [1]. Many decks omit this. Cover: how much you are raising, what you will use it for, and where it gets you in 18-24 months in terms of traction [1].

### Appendix
Ammunition for Q&A [1]. Financial projections, detailed use of funds, slides answering anticipated investor questions. "This section will expand significantly once you start pitching" [1].

### Design
"Optimize for clarity and understanding, not beauty" [1]. Keep it simple and bare-bones. Avoid fancy diagrams and decorative images that do not illustrate your point. See [[Pitch Deck Design]] for detailed visual guidance [1].

## Pitch Practice

Set up practice pitches with angels or founders who have raised Series As [1]. Format: 20-minute pitch, 20-minute conversation, 20 minutes of feedback.

During pitch: Time yourself. Does it feel natural? Are you confident? [1]

During conversation: What did you want them to ask? What did they ask that you were unprepared for? [1]

Feedback questions (from YC Demo Day prep) [1]:
- Were you confused? When?
- Were you bored? When?
- Did my deck help or hurt?
- What are a couple hard questions I need great answers to?

**Slide test:** Flash a slide to a friend who has never seen it. Ask what point it makes. "If it isn't immediately understandable, you have some work to do" [1].

## Video Pitch Tips

Most initial pitches happen over video [1]. Key adjustments:
- Turn on video. Ensure good lighting (ring lights work), attractive camera angle, non-distracting background.
- One person should pitch. Switching speakers "is usually confusing and hard to coordinate."
- Record and watch yourself. "You're going to have to overcompensate in energy and verve."
- Have a bullet-pointed script available (invisible to investor on video call). For long-winded speakers, write a full script, but do not read from it.

## Iterating the Deck

Spend 1-2 hours daily during the fundraise refining based on each meeting [1]:
- What excited investors most? Emphasize it.
- What caused them to zone out? Clarify or restructure.
- What objections were raised? Pull data, create slides, talk to people who can strengthen your answers.
