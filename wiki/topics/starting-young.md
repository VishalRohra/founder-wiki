# Starting Young

The question of when to start a startup -- and particularly whether to start one in or immediately after college -- is one [[Paul Graham]] has revisited multiple times, with his position evolving from encouragement (2005-2007) to more nuanced caution (2014). The tension between the advantages of youth and the dangers of starting too early runs through several of his most important essays.

## The Sweet Spot: Mid-Twenties

Graham's considered view, expressed in his 2006 student guide, is that the sweet spot for startup founders is the mid-twenties [1]. The question is what advantages 25-year-olds have over 21-year-olds, and why the answer is not older still.

**Advantages of young founders [1]:**

1. **Stamina.** Long hours are universal in successful startups. Younger founders need this even more because they are probably less efficient than they will be later.

2. **Poverty.** This sounds counterintuitive, but poverty means you can live cheaply, which is critically important because nearly every startup that fails does so by running out of money. A low burn rate gives more opportunity to recover from mistakes. "The guys with kids and mortgages are at a real disadvantage" [1]. Even more important than living cheaply is thinking cheaply. The Apple II was popular partly because Woz designed it for himself and couldn't afford anything more [1].

3. **Rootlessness.** Young people are more mobile -- no house, less stuff, fewer serious relationships. Many startups involve someone moving, and a startup hub matters enormously. Houston, Chicago, and Miami have microscopically small numbers of successful startups per capita [1].

4. **Colleagues.** The number one question at YC is "Where can I find a cofounder?" At 30, your school friends have either lost touch or are tied down by jobs. At a good college, you are concentrated with ambitious, technical people more than you will ever be again [1]. "Co-founders really should be people you already know. And by far the best place to meet them is school" [1]. Google, Yahoo, and Microsoft were all founded by people who met in school [1].

5. **Ignorance (innocence).** Jessica Livingston's interviews with startup founders revealed a pattern: "One after another said that if they'd known how hard it would be, they would have been too intimidated to start" [1]. Steve Wozniak put it strongly: "All the best things that I did at Apple came from (a) not having money and (b) not having done it before, ever" [1].

**Disadvantages of young founders [1]:**

The core problem is that young founders build things that look like class projects -- lacking (1) an iterative definition of a real problem and (2) market-level intensity [1]. See [[Early-Stage Mistakes]] for the full analysis.

The 24-year-old's real advantage over the 20-year-old: "they know what they're trying to avoid" [1]. To the average undergrad, getting rich means buying Ferraris. To someone who has learned about the relationship between money and work, it means opting out of the brutal equation governing 99.9% of people's lives. "Someone who gets this will work much harder at making a startup succeed -- with the proverbial energy of a drowning man" [1].

## The Excuses for Not Starting

Graham's 2007 essay systematically dismantles 16 common reasons people give for not starting a startup [2]. His goal is explicitly to increase self-confidence, but with the constraint that he is motivated to be honest -- encouraging people who shouldn't start startups makes his own life worse [2].

**Excuses that are mostly bogus:**

- **Too young.** The most successful founder YC had funded at the time, Sam Altman, was 19. But Altman is an outlier: "he seemed like he had a 40-year-old inside him" [2]. The real test: whether you have crossed the adult threshold, not your chronological age.

- **Too inexperienced.** "The best way to get experience if you're 21 is to start a startup" [2]. The Kiko founders started right out of college, made a lot of mistakes, and by the time YC funded their second startup a year later, they had become "extremely formidable" [2]. Getting a normal job may actually make you less able to start a startup by "turning you into a tame animal who thinks he needs an office to work in and a product manager to tell him what software to write" [2].

- **Not smart enough.** "If you're smart enough to worry that you might not be smart enough to start a startup, you probably are" [2]. Starting a startup does not require exceptional intelligence. Enterprise software companies are sales companies, and sales depends mostly on effort [2].

- **Know nothing about business.** The coefficient of this variable should be zero. "You don't need to know anything about business to start a startup. The initial focus should be the product" [2]. Pretty much 100% of startups that make something popular manage to make money from it. Acquirers buy strategic value, meaning something people want [2].

- **No idea.** In the average YC startup, 70% of the idea is new at the end of the first three months, sometimes 100%. YC is "so sure the founders are more important than the initial idea" that they accept applications with no idea at all [2].

- **No room for more startups.** There is no economic limit on the number of startups. "Startups make wealth, which means they make things people want, and if there's a limit on the number of things people want, we are nowhere near it. I still don't even have a flying car" [2].

- **Fear of uncertainty.** "If you start a startup, it will probably fail. Seriously, though, this is not a bad way to think about the whole experience." No employer will hold it against you. "In America, companies, like practically everything else, are disposable" [2].

**Excuses that are real:**

- **Not determined enough.** Determination is probably the single best predictor of success. But most hackers underestimate their own determination [2].

- **No cofounder.** "A startup is too much for one person to bear." All investors, without exception, are more likely to fund you with a cofounder. If you don't have one, get one -- "It's more important than anything else" [2].

- **Family to support.** Graham won't advise anyone with a family to start a startup. An alternative: start a consulting business and gradually turn it into a product business, or join an existing startup as an early employee [2].

- **Not ready for commitment.** If a startup succeeds, it will consume at least three or four years. This is a legitimate reason to wait, though Graham notes you will probably spend just as long at a regular job with less spare time than you expect [2].

## The 2014 Recalibration

By October 2014, Graham had shifted to more cautionary advice for students [3]. His Stanford lecture argues explicitly: "Do not start a startup in college" [3].

The reasoning: starting a startup at 20 is "like a brutally fast depth-first search. Most people should still be searching breadth-first at 20" [3]. You can do things in your early 20s that you cannot do before or after -- "plunge deeply into projects on a whim and travel super cheaply with no sense of a deadline" [3].

Mark Zuckerberg will never get to bum around a foreign country. "Success has taken a lot of the serendipity out of his life. Facebook is running him as much as he's running Facebook" [3].

The key insight: there is not even a tradeoff. You are not sacrificing anything by waiting, because you are more likely to succeed if you do [3]. The usual way startups take off is for founders to make them take off (see [[Doing Things That Don't Scale]]), and "it's gratuitously stupid to do that at 20" [3].

What students should do instead: "just learn" [3]. Become good at things that matter. Work on problems that interest you. The optimal preparation is not a vocational version of college focused on entrepreneurship but "the classic version of college as education for its own sake" [3].

Graham flags university entrepreneurship programs as misguided. "They go out of their way to ensure their students are well supplied with contraceptives, and yet they're setting up entrepreneurship programs and startup incubators left and right" [3]. The university cannot teach the essential thing -- understanding user needs -- because that requires actually starting the company, at which point you are no longer really a student [3].

## The Google Trap

Graham observes that while Google seems like the ideal place to work, "zero startups come out of there. In that respect it's a black hole" [1]. People like working there too much to leave. If you hope to start a startup one day, a pleasant employer can lull you into staying indefinitely even if leaving would be a net win. You need "activation energy" to start a startup, and a comfortable job suppresses it [1].

The best place to work if you want to start a startup is probably a startup. It is the right sort of experience, and one way or another it will be over quickly: you will either end up rich, or the startup will get acquired and start to suck, or it will blow up and you will be free [1].

## The Default Shift

Graham sees the shift from employment as default to startups as an option in historical terms. "Getting a job" is only about a hundred years old as the default way to make a living -- before that it was farming [2]. The shift from farming to manufacturing would have seemed insanely risky to medieval serfs [2].

"How grim it would be to commute every day to a cubicle in some soulless office complex, and be told what to do by someone you had to acknowledge as a boss -- someone who could call you into their office and say 'take a seat,' and you'd sit! Imagine having to ask permission to release software to users" [2].

Graham believes startups may represent another such historic shift in how wealth is created [2].
