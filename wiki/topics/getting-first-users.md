# Getting First Users

Getting a new product off the ground requires finding people willing to use a product from a startup they have never heard of. Ankit Gupta, YC General Partner, frames this as a search problem rather than a persuasion problem: "You're looking for the Gustafs and Ankits of the world, the ones who try new things or have a burning need that you can solve" [1].

Most people are not early adopters. For most people, the number of products they used as one of the first 10 users is zero. Almost no one wants to be a startup's first paying customer. Yet every great product manages to find people willing to take that leap [1].

## Finding Early Adopters Is a Search Problem

Two types of people become first users [1]:

1. **Habitual early adopters.** Gupta describes his colleague Gustaf (formerly at Airbnb) who enjoyed trying products from startups and bringing them into the company [1].

2. **People with a burning need.** When Gupta's team needed to ship their first inference API quickly without dealing with billing or public endpoints, he found and paid a startup within three days whose product solved the problem. "Their size or reputation didn't matter. Our problem did, so we took a chance on them, and they delivered" [1].

## Counterintuitive Principles

Understanding that user acquisition is a search problem leads to several non-obvious strategies [1]:

### Charge Real Money Early

Early adopters and people with burning problems are rarely price sensitive. The goal is not revenue -- it is feedback. "Paying customers give sharper feedback than free users ever will. You're more likely to get feedback from an angry customer paying a lot of money than a nobody who isn't willing to pay" [1].

### Use Targeted Personal Outreach

The ways you find early adopters do not look like how you find normal people. "A billboard is way less likely to reach them versus something like a targeted cold email or a knock on their door" [1]. See [[Doing Things That Don't Scale]] for more on manual, unscalable acquisition tactics.

### Launch Early

In the early days, you do not know much about who your early users are. Launching early creates a wide surface area for them to find you [1]. This is one of YC's longest-standing principles.

### Study Your Early Users Like an Anthropologist

"You should be like an anthropologist that's discovered a hidden civilization. How do they make decisions? Why would they make the strange choice to trust you?" [1]. Understanding how early users think and what they want is essential input for product evolution.

### Experiment Fast and Don't Fear Churn

Run constant experiments: pricing, landing pages, onboarding, features. Talk to early users and try to make them love the product, but do not stress if you lose one. "If you annoy someone, you can usually fix it because the relationship is personal. And if they churn, that's fine, too. There are plenty of others who haven't even heard of you yet" [1].

Startups have an advantage over big companies here: "When you run a bad experiment, no one writes about it. You're fighting irrelevance, not headlines" [1].

## B2B vs. Consumer in the Early Stage

The dynamics of finding first users shape who many founders choose to build for in the beginning. The average person's personal software spend is small (Gupta estimates his at about $150/month total), while his corporate card carries multiple tools that each cost more than that [1].

In the AI era, this gap matters more. Consumer apps can struggle because ads often do not cover AI costs and subscriptions must squeeze into small personal budgets. This is why many AI founders start by selling to prosumers, businesses, or targeting users with high advertising value (like doctors) [1].

## The Minimum Evolvable Product

Gupta updates the classic MVP concept. When starting out, you do not just need a minimum viable product. You need a **minimum evolvable product** -- something that can adapt to the needs of critical early customers [1].

The earliest version of the product only needs to do one thing: survive contact with a tiny group of people who might actually try it. "You're not building the final form. You're building something that can evolve" [1].

## Early Users Steer Product Evolution

Early users do not just give feedback. They steer how the product evolves over time [1]. Gupta uses the metaphor of a phylogenetic tree: every product on the market has run an evolutionary process, morphing from an amoeba (basic functions, exposed to external pressures) to a complex organism (millions of users, refined sales pitch, clear value). The founders run "an evolutionary search through the tree of potential future directions" [1].

### Tesla as Case Study

The Tesla Roadster illustrates how early users shape product evolution. Tesla's first product was an impractical $150,000 car that did not go far, did not fit much, could not publicly charge anywhere, and looked strange. The conventional narrative is that Tesla needed a high-margin product to fund capex for the Model S and eventually the Model 3 [1].

Gupta offers a second interpretation: Tesla was searching for early adopters -- "the people crazy enough to buy an impractical $150,000 car" [1]. What those early adopters cared about shaped the entire product line.

The Tesla Model Y, a mass-market vehicle, has a faster 0-to-60 time than a Lamborghini and better tech than a BMW, but worse suspension and comfort than a Toyota. Why? Because early adopters cared more about tech and acceleration than comfort [1]. "If early adopters were willing to pay $150,000 for a slow, plush vehicle, I bet Tesla's cars would look very different today" [1].

Product evolution is path-dependent on what early adopters want. This is why the choice of initial users is so consequential -- it determines the trajectory of the product itself [1].

## It Doesn't Have to Be Perfect

The minimum evolvable product framework is freeing: "It's freeing to know that the product will change a lot, so it doesn't have to be perfect from the start. Ultimately, what it becomes will depend on where you begin and who you begin it with" [1].
