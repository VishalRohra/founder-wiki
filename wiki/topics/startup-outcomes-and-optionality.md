# Startup Outcomes and Optionality

[[Sam Altman]] addresses a common but rarely discussed situation: the startup that is successful but is not on a trajectory to be a billion-dollar company. Using a metaphor attributed to David Weiden, he describes starting a startup as being pushed off a cliff with a bag of aerospace parts [1].

## The Airplane vs. Spaceship Framework

Sometimes the parts make a spaceship -- huge market, great product, natural monopoly, credible path to being the winner. Plenty of advice exists for that case. Sometimes the parts make nothing at all. But "a reasonable amount of the time, it turns out you have the parts for an airplane" [1].

An airplane is a company with profitability within reach, on track to be worth $100 million with several more years of hard work. "This is nothing to be ashamed of -- quite the opposite, obviously. This is a triumph by almost any metric." It is what happens to "most very lucky, very smart, very determined people who decide to start a startup and have everything go their way except happening upon a perfect market" [1].

The founder has two choices: try to get a new bag of parts (new market, new product, very different strategy) and keep trying to build a spaceship, or decide to have a really good airplane [1].

## Founder-Investor Misalignment

The most important thing to understand: this is where founders and investors often get misaligned. Investors are looking for spaceships; airplanes do not matter much to them, though they matter quite a bit to founders [1].

Existing investors will usually be willing to take almost any amount of risk to turn an airplane into a spaceship. The typical advice offered is little more than "point your plane at the moon, burn more fuel, and let's see what happens" [1].

Altman's recommendation: "Be frank and realistic with your existing investors; you will eventually persuade them that a small multiple is better than a loss." The best investors understand how venture investing works and do not fight over such outcomes -- many treat founders extra-generously hoping to fund their next company. The longer founders delude their investors, the worse shape they end up in [1].

## The Overvaluation Trap

Raising too much money at too high a valuation severely limits optionality. "Very often I've seen cases where founders know in their hearts they have an airplane but are able to convince good investors it might still be a spaceship. This really causes a lot of heartache, and often precludes your opportunity for a good acquisition later" [1].

The answer is simple in theory but difficult in practice: do not raise a lot of money at a high price when you think the company will probably not increase 10x in value and you would be happy selling soon. This may require changing how the company is run and finding a path to profitability with current capital. "It's far less painful to deal with this now rather than putting the company in a position that will preclude the option you think is best" [1].

## Positioning for Acquisition

Companies get bought, not sold. It is very difficult to force an acquisition, and nearly impossible if the company has not built something legitimately valuable. "Somehow, this fact surprises founders who decide 'we'd like to get acquired' almost every time" [1].

What founders can do: begin developing relationships with potential acquirers early. It usually takes a long time, and personal relationships help [1].

The outcome is still worth pride. "Silicon Valley is filled with people who had one or several mid-success companies before eventually hitting it out of the park" [1].

## The Investor Power Law That Drives the Misalignment

Altman's 2020 *How to Invest in Startups* essay states the investor-side arithmetic that makes the airplane-vs-spaceship misalignment structural rather than cultural. "You should try to limit yourself to opportunities that could be $10 billion companies if they work (which means they have, at least, a fast-growing market and some sort of pricing power). The power law is that powerful. This is easy to say and hard to do, and I've been guilty of violating the principle many times. But the data are clear -- the failures don't matter much, the small successes don't matter much, and the giant returns are where everything happens" [2]. Founders reading this should recognize the second half as why even sympathetic investors push spaceship bets onto airplane companies — the failure mode is not malice but rational behavior under the return distribution. A $100M airplane is categorically a "small success" by this math, worth roughly nothing to a venture fund even though it is a life-changing outcome for a founder.

This is also why Altman's "be frank with investors" advice works: once both parties are honest about the underlying outcome distribution, the investor has no rational reason to keep forcing a spaceship strategy onto an airplane company. The pretense that it might still be a spaceship is what sustains the bad advice; dropping it aligns incentives immediately.
