# Growth

Growth is the defining characteristic of a startup. [[Paul Graham]] argues that a startup is not merely a newly founded company or one that works on technology -- it is a company designed to grow fast [1]. Everything else associated with startups -- venture funding, exits, technology focus -- follows from this single property. [[Sam Altman]] adds a critical caveat: growth is not step one. Founders who focus on growth before building something people love are unlikely to succeed [2].

## Growth as the Defining Property of a Startup

Graham draws a sharp distinction between startups and ordinary businesses. A barbershop is not a startup. A search engine is. The difference is not about technology but about growth potential. For a company to grow fast, it must (a) make something lots of people want and (b) reach and serve all those people [1]. Most businesses are constrained in one or both dimensions. Startups are the rare companies that are not.

This distinction matters because it shapes founder behavior. "Startup is a pole, not a threshold," Graham writes. Starting one is a declaration of ambition -- a commitment to search for one of the rare ideas that generates rapid growth [1].

The growth of a successful startup follows an S-curve with three phases: an initial period of slow or no growth while the startup figures out what it is doing; a period of rapid growth as it finds [[Product-Market Fit]] and reaches its audience; and an eventual slowdown as internal limits and market saturation set in [1]. The second phase -- the ascent -- is what defines the startup. Its length and slope determine how big the company becomes.

He sees startup founding as analogous to scientific research: "A startup founder is in effect an economic research scientist. Most don't discover anything that remarkable, but some discover relativity" [1].

## Growth Rate as a Compass

Graham advocates using a target growth rate as a decision-making compass. At YC, growth is measured weekly, partly because the batch is short and partly because early-stage startups need frequent feedback [1].

Benchmarks during YC:
- **5-7% per week**: a good growth rate
- **10% per week**: exceptionally well
- **1% per week**: a sign you have not yet figured out what you are doing [1]

The best metric to measure growth is revenue. For startups not yet charging, active users serve as a reasonable proxy because revenues will likely be a constant multiple of active users [1]. Getting the pricing right is itself a growth lever -- see [[Pricing and Monetization]] for when and how to start charging.

The compounding effect of small differences in weekly growth rate is dramatic:

![Growth rate compounding](../raw/posts/images/8s-startup-growth/03a34df22371.png)

| Weekly Growth Rate | Annual Multiple | 4-Year Outcome (from $1K/mo) |
|---|---|---|
| 1% | 1.7x | $7,900/month |
| 5% | 12.6x | $25 million/month |

Graham notes: "Our ancestors must rarely have encountered cases of exponential growth, because our intuitions are no guide here" [1].

Setting a weekly growth target converts the bewildering problem of starting a startup into an optimization problem. "Anything that gets you the growth you need is ipso facto right," Graham writes -- should you attend a conference, hire a programmer, focus on marketing, court a big customer? Whatever hits the number [1]. This narrow focus works because founders' intuitions about which hill to climb are usually better than they realize, and the maxima in the space of startup ideas are not spiky and isolated -- most fairly good ideas are adjacent to even better ones [1].

## The Power of Compound Growth

Founders often dismiss manual user acquisition because the absolute numbers seem so small. Graham counters with simple math [5]: if you have 100 users and grow 10% a week, after a year you have 14,000 users. After two years, 2 million. The mistake founders make is to underestimate the power of compound growth [5].

Patrick Collison described the transition at Stripe: "At some point, there was a very noticeable change in how Stripe felt. It tipped from being this boulder we had to push to being a train car that in fact had its own momentum" [5].

[[Sam Altman]] adds a structural claim that distinguishes durable compounding from mere growth: "Compounding exponentials are magic. In particular, you really want to build a business that gets a compounding advantage with scale" [11]. The addition is "with scale" -- the math of weekly growth is necessary but not sufficient, because a business that scales cost as fast as it scales revenue is a leaky bucket ([2], see above). The business worth building is the one that gets *harder to compete with* as it grows. Altman pairs this with an observation from both OpenAI and the network-product era: "Scale often has surprising emergent properties" [11]. At sufficient model size, language models exhibited capabilities nobody had predicted from smaller systems; at sufficient user density, marketplaces flip from demand-limited to supply-limited; at sufficient content volume, platforms spawn content categories that did not exist in the smaller version of themselves. Founders underwrite the growth curve and get the emergent behavior as a byproduct -- or not, depending on whether the compounding is structural or cosmetic.

## Do Not Grow Before the Product Is Loved

Altman is explicit: focusing on growth before you have a product people love leads to the "leaky bucket" problem. Users come in the door but do not stay and likely will not return [2].

Facebook had a product people talked about obsessively from the start. Airbnb slogged for 1,000 days before discovering how to make their product loved. Once they had that, it grew exponentially by word of mouth [2].

When the product is loved, users become "a free marketing and sales force." Word-of-mouth growth is powerful even for enterprise and hard tech companies -- the sales process becomes easier when word has spread that what you are making is great [2].

The danger of growing on a mediocre product is that reliance on inorganic means (ads, marketing, PR) becomes unsustainable. The larger the company grows on a weak foundation, the harder it becomes to course correct. "Once your company is a big moving battleship, it becomes really hard to be nimble" [2].

Altman's test for readiness: "When they actively recommend your product to friends, you're in the right place." Net Promoter Score can be a useful measurement [2].

## Growth vs. Conversion

The industry term for turning casual visitors into users is "conversion" [4]. Graham frames it simply: either your site is catching on or it isn't, and you must know which. "If you have decent growth, you'll win in the end, no matter how obscure you are now. And if you don't, you need to fix something" [4].

The metaphor: users are a fickle wind, but more powerful than any other. "If they take you up, no competitor can keep you down" [4]. To catch this wind, do two things: (1) explain, as concisely as possible, what your site is about -- a startup should be able to explain in one or two sentences exactly what it does [4]; (2) give people everything you have, right away -- put the impressive stuff on the front page because that is the only one most visitors will see [4].

## Release Early, Keep Pumping

Graham's most repeated advice to startups: get a version 1 out fast, then improve it based on users' reactions [4]. "By 'release early' I don't mean you should release something full of bugs, but that you should release something minimal. Users hate bugs, but they don't seem to mind a minimal version 1, if there's more coming soon" [4].

Continuous improvement is itself a form of marketing. "Users love a site that's constantly improving. In fact, users expect a site to improve" [4]. If you improve in response to user comments, you generate fanatical loyalty because customers are used to companies ignoring them [4].

The test for whether your product is finished: "If your product seems finished, there are two possible explanations: (a) it is finished, or (b) you lack imagination. Experience suggests (b) is a thousand times more likely" [4].

## Growth vs. Spending: A False Dichotomy

One of the most dangerous misconceptions is that growing fast and operating cheaply are opposed [6]. Graham argues there is "surprisingly little connection between how much a startup spends and how fast it grows" [6].

When a startup grows fast, it is usually because the product hits a nerve -- hitting some big need straight on. When a startup spends a lot, it is usually because the product is expensive to develop or sell, or simply because they are wasteful [6]. These are independent variables.

The implication: if growth is slow, hiring more people is almost never the answer. The real problem is usually that the product is not appealing enough [6]. The right approach is to seek growth "by [[Doing Things That Don't Scale]], or by redesigning the product in the way only founders can" [6].

## Premature Scaling

Paul Buchheit warns about premature scaling: founders take a small business that is not really working (bad unit economics, typically) and then scale it up because they want impressive growth numbers [6]. This is similar to overhiring -- it makes the business much harder to fix once it is big, and they are bleeding cash fast [6].

The common death pattern: a startup makes something moderately appealing, raises easily because the founders seem smart, then hires aggressively to try to boost growth. Growth never comes because the product is only moderately appealing. With high expenses and slow growth, they become unappealing to investors for the next round. The company dies [6].

## The Contained Fire

The [[Doing Things That Don't Scale]] essay introduces the "contained fire" strategy: focus on a deliberately narrow market first, get it really hot, then expand [5]. Facebook started with only Harvard students. Microsoft started with only Altair owners. The narrowness is not the goal -- depth is. But in practice narrowness comes as a byproduct of optimizing for depth and speed [5].

Any startup that could be described as a marketplace usually has to start in a subset of the market [5]. Among companies, the best early adopters are usually other startups -- they are more open to new things and grow fast, pulling you with them [5]. See [[Selling to Startups]] for a detailed treatment of when and how to use startups as your initial customer base.

## Big Launches Don't Work

Graham explicitly warns against relying on a "Big Launch" for growth [5]. There may even be an inverse correlation between launch magnitude and success -- the only launches he remembers are famous flops like the Segway and Google Wave [5].

All you need from a launch is some initial core of users. How well you are doing a few months later depends more on how happy you made those users than how many there were [5]. Partnerships also usually fail as growth strategies: "Six months later they're all saying the same thing: that was way more work than we expected, and we ended up getting practically nothing out of it" [5].

## Building a Growth Team

[[Anu Hariharan]], with contributions from Gustaf Alströmer, synthesized advice from 25 growth experts at companies including Facebook, Airbnb, Uber, Stitch Fix, Square, Slack, and Instagram [3].

### When to Invest

The consensus is clear: do not build a growth team before you have proven Retention. Investing in growth before nailing retention wastes money and jeopardizes the company's future [3]. Stitch Fix, for example, hired a retention-focused PM to run experiments improving retention before investing in new customer acquisition [3].

### Retention Benchmarks

To determine if retention is good enough to justify growth investment, three checks apply:

![Retention - Cohort Analysis](../raw/posts/images/59-how-to-set-up-hire-and-scale-a-growth-strategy-and-team/f39d6a8e86db.png)

1. **Stable long-term retention**: the retention curve should flatten and run parallel to the x-axis after an initial dip
2. **In line with vertical benchmarks**: stable 10% long-term retention is poor for a social network
3. **Newer cohorts perform better**: improving cohorts signal product improvement [3]

Retention benchmarks by vertical:

| Vertical | Period | Long-Term Target | Median |
|---|---|---|---|
| Social Network | Monthly (Month 12) | 45-65% | 55% |
| On-Demand | Monthly (Month 12) | 20-30% | 22% |
| Travel | Annual (Year 2) | 20-35% | 29% |
| E-commerce | Monthly (Month 12) | 10-25% | 16% |
| Subscription | Monthly (Month 12) | 25-35% | 33% |

### The Year 1 Growth Team

The initial team composition: 1 growth-focused PM + 2-3 growth engineers + 1-2 growth data scientists [3].

Timing: most companies made their first growth hire when they had about 15 engineers on the product team. The growth PM is typically the 3rd or 4th PM. The most common mistake CEOs make is waiting too long before hiring a growth-focused PM [3].

100% of growth experts said the first few hires are "magnets" for scaling the team. Eric Colson (former VP of data science at Netflix) was one of Stitch Fix's early hires and attracted accomplished data scientists to the company [3].

### Setting Goals and Metrics

The growth team's first priority is setting an **absolute goal** -- not a percentage change. For example, "achieve 5M first-time room nights this year" rather than "improve conversion by 10%" [3]. Casey Winters (former Growth Product Lead at Pinterest) wrote extensively about this approach [3].

The absolute goal breaks into sub-goals. Facebook's growth accounting equation: Monthly Active Users = New MAUs + Retained MAUs + Resurrected MAUs. Airbnb's: Room Nights = Room nights from new users + Room nights from existing users [3]. Jonathan Hsu (Partner at Social Capital, former Facebook data scientist) published this [growth accounting equation](https://medium.com/swlh/diligence-at-social-capital-part-1-accounting-for-user-growth-4a8a449fddfc) [3].

100% of experts said the CEO must be aligned on the absolute goal and that it must be communicated to the entire company [3].

### Growth Channels

The most common framework for identifying channels is based on existing user behavior. Aatif Awan (Head of Growth at LinkedIn) mapped user behaviors to channels [3]:

| User Behavior | Channel | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Product requires connecting with another user | Product itself | Facebook, PayPal, Slack |
| Existing users talk about the product | Referrals, Community | Uber, Airbnb, Dropbox |
| Users search for a solution | SEO, SEM | Airbnb |
| Users look for expert inspiration | Affiliate bloggers, Partnerships, Content | Stitch Fix, Glossier, Intercom |
| High LTV users | Paid acquisition | Airbnb, Expedia, Uber |

Most products find 1-2 relevant channels early on. Approximately 70% of experts said referrals were the top channel in the first year [3].

### Experimentation at Scale

The four essential elements for a growth team: a clean data set, segmentation tools, a rigorous experiment dashboard, and a peer review process [3].

A company at scale typically runs **1 experiment per growth engineer per week**. Growth teams running 100+ experiments per year report only a third turn out positive. The success rate of 20-30% is expected -- the point is to encourage engineers to take more risks [3].

A common heuristic: "Don't test things you wouldn't ship to everybody" [3].

100% of experts emphasized the decision to build their own internal tools at scale. Early on, tools like [Mixpanel](https://mixpanel.com/), [Optimizely](https://www.optimizely.com/), and [Chartio](https://chartio.com) suffice [3].

### Where the Growth Team Sits

This remains the biggest source of debate. Facebook pioneered the separate growth team -- Growth as its own department, with the Head of Growth reporting to the CEO. The rationale: if no one owns MAU growth, no one drives it. Facebook grew from 50 million to 2 billion MAUs under this model [3].

Other companies (Uber, Airbnb, Slack) started with separate teams but later merged them with product. Because growth experiments often require subtle product changes, advocates argue product and growth must be in the same org [3].

The least-favored option among experts is housing growth under marketing [3].

At scale, a growth team can exceed 100 people, roughly composed of: 50% engineers, 10% PMs, 10-15% data scientists, 10% product marketing, 10-15% designers, and ~5% researchers [3].

## There Is Always Room

A common fear among would-be founders is that the market for startups is saturated. Graham dismisses this [4]. "At every point in history, even the darkest bits of the dark ages, people were discovering things that made everyone say 'why didn't anyone think of that before?'" [4].

The argument that there are only so many startups Google and Yahoo can buy conflates the limit on acquisitions with the limit on wealth creation. "There is no limit on the amount of newly created wealth consumers can absorb, any more than there is a limit on the number of theorems that can be proven" [4]. The only limit on the number of startups is the pool of people willing to work that hard.

## Growth, Fundraising, and Acquisitions

Growth explains why VCs invest in startups rather than ordinary businesses. The returns when startups succeed are so high that the ratio of return to risk passes any investment test. But VCs also prefer startups because capital gains are easier to manage than dividends from private companies, where owners can funnel revenues to themselves [1].

Raising money lets founders "choose their growth rate." The most successful startups take VC money even when profitable because it lets them grow faster and fend off competitors, especially in businesses with network effects [1].

Growth also explains why successful startups get acquisition offers. A rapidly growing company is not merely valuable but dangerous to incumbents. "Most product acquisitions have some component of fear," Graham writes. Acquirers often pay more than ordinary investors would because a startup is doubly valuable -- for what it is and for what it could become in a competitor's hands [1].


## Dropbox: Viral Growth Mechanics (Drew Houston)

[[Drew Houston]] built Dropbox's growth engine on Dave McClure's AARRR framework [8]. Four of five new signups did not put a file in their Dropbox or install the client. Houston brought five people off Craigslist and watched them try to install -- zero succeeded [8].

The referral program (both sides get free storage) drove 30% of all signups. Shared folders drove another 20%. Half of all Dropbox signups were viral [8]. The demo video posted on Hacker News stayed at the top for two days and became a repeatable tactic [8].

## Meesho: Growth-Quality Cycles (Vidit Aatrey)

[[Vidit Aatrey]] describes Meesho's growth in six-month cycles alternating between expansion and quality improvement. Growth was driven by progressively expanding the addressable market: first existing WhatsApp sellers, then new resellers (housewives), then expanding categories, then cross-border supply chains from China [9].

## Rippling: Growth Beyond Sales (Parker Conrad)

[[Parker Conrad]] argues software sales and marketing is fundamentally broken: "Public companies are spending 50% more on sales and marketing but they're adding 10% less in new ARR" [10]. The compound startup thesis reduces dependency on expensive acquisition by serving more of a customer's needs [10].
