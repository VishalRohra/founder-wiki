# Growth

Growth is the defining characteristic of a startup. [[Paul Graham]] argues that a startup is not merely a newly founded company or one that works on technology -- it is "a company designed to grow fast." Everything else associated with startups -- venture funding, acquisitions, the high failure rate -- follows from growth.

## Growth as the Defining Metric

Graham draws a sharp distinction between startups and ordinary businesses. A barbershop is not a startup because it cannot scale: it serves customers in person, and few will travel far for a haircut. A startup must (a) make something lots of people want, and (b) reach and serve all those people. Most businesses are tightly constrained in one of these two dimensions. "The distinctive feature of successful startups is that they're not."

Software is a particularly good way to solve (b), but you can still be constrained in (a). Graham uses the example: "If you write software to teach Tibetan to Hungarian speakers, you'll be able to reach most of the people who want it, but there won't be many of them. If you make software to teach English to Chinese speakers, however, you're in startup territory."

The growth of a successful startup typically follows three phases, producing an S-curve:

1. An initial period of slow or no growth while the startup figures out what it is doing.
2. A period of rapid growth as the startup discovers how to make something people want and how to reach them.
3. A slowdown as the company becomes large and bumps against the limits of its markets.

The second phase -- the ascent -- defines the startup. Its length and slope determine how big the company will be.

## Growth Rates and Compounding

Graham states: "If there's one number every founder should always know, it's the company's growth rate. That's the measure of a startup. If you don't know that number, you don't even know if you're doing well or badly."

He emphasizes that what matters is the ratio of new customers to existing ones, not the absolute number. "If you're really getting a constant number of new customers every month, you're in trouble, because that means your growth rate is decreasing."

During Y Combinator, growth is measured per week. Graham provides specific benchmarks:

- **5-7% per week**: A good growth rate during YC.
- **10% per week**: Exceptionally well.
- **1% per week**: A sign you have not figured out what you are doing.

The best metric to measure is revenue. The next best, for startups not yet charging, is active users, which Graham calls "a reasonable proxy for revenue growth because whenever the startup does start trying to make money, their revenues will probably be a constant multiple of active users."

Small variations in growth rate produce qualitatively different outcomes over time: "A company that grows at 1% a week will grow 1.7x a year, whereas a company that grows at 5% a week will grow 12.6x." A company making $1,000/month growing at 1%/week will be making $7,900/month in four years. At 5%/week, it will be making $25 million/month in four years. Graham observes: "Our ancestors must rarely have encountered cases of exponential growth, because our intuitions are no guide here."

## Growth as Compass

Graham argues that founders should pick a target growth rate and then use it to make every decision: "anything that gets you the growth you need is ipso facto right." Should you spend two days at a conference? Hire another programmer? Focus on marketing? Court a big customer? Add a feature? "Whatever gets you your target growth rate."

This turns starting a startup into an optimization problem. Graham compares it to optimizing code: "You don't have to think about what the program should do, just make it faster." For founders, the narrow focus creates a similar clarity.

The approach also helps discover better ideas. "If you start out with some initial plan and modify it as necessary to keep hitting, say, 10% weekly growth, you may end up with a quite different company than you meant to start. But anything that grows consistently at 10% a week is almost certainly a better idea than you started with."

Graham cautions that within YC, "ipso facto right" excludes "trickery like buying users for more than their lifetime value, counting users as active when they're really not, bleeding out invites at a regularly increasing rate to manufacture a perfect growth curve."

## Don't Grow Before Product-Market Fit

[[Sam Altman]] argues that growth is not step one: "If you focus on trying to grow before you make a product people love, you are unlikely to succeed." Focusing too heavily on growth too early leads to the "leaky bucket problem" -- users come in the door but do not stay and will not return.

Altman points to specific examples. Facebook had a product people "talked about obsessively from the start." Airbnb "slogged for 1000 days before discovering how to make their product loved. Once they had that, it grew exponentially by word of mouth."

When a product is loved, users become "a free marketing and sales force for you." The chances of building a large company are "much higher when you have a product that spreads by word of mouth." This applies to enterprise and hard tech companies too -- "your sales process becomes much easier when word has spread that what you're making is great."

Altman warns against papering over a mediocre product with capital: "Don't try to avoid the problem by raising capital for growth -- the problems will still be there, with higher expectations." He recommends using [[Net Promoter Score]] as one measure of user love.

If a company is already growing around a mediocre product, Altman advises fixing it immediately. The larger a company grows, "the harder it becomes to course correct. Once your company is a big moving battleship, it becomes really hard to be nimble."

## The Growth Ecosystem: VCs, Founders, and Acquirers

Graham explains why VCs are interested specifically in high-growth companies, not just high-return-to-risk businesses. VCs make returns through capital gains (IPOs or acquisitions), not dividends. Private companies can too easily funnel revenues to insiders while showing little profit, making dividend-based investing impractical without close oversight. With growth-stage startups, "The founders can't enrich themselves without also enriching the investors."

Founders take VC money because growth creates competitive pressure: "If you have such an idea and don't grow fast enough, competitors will. Growing too slowly is particularly dangerous in a business with network effects." The most successful startups view fundraising as buying insurance and choosing their growth rate: "Money to grow faster is always at the command of the most successful startups, because the VCs need them more than they need the VCs."

Acquirers want startups because a rapidly growing company "is not merely valuable, but dangerous." Most product acquisitions have a component of fear -- the acquirer worries about what a competitor could do with the startup. Graham cites eBay's purchase of PayPal: "Paypal is now responsible for 43% of their sales and probably more of their growth."
