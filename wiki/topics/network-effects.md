# Network Effects

Network effects occur when a product becomes more valuable as more people use it. The concept is central to understanding why certain startups achieve winner-take-most dynamics. [[Anu Hariharan]]'s case studies of Chinese technology companies provide some of the most detailed analyses of network effects in the YC canon.

## Types of Network Effects

Hariharan draws a distinction between Metcalfe's law and Reed's law in her analysis of [[Pinduoduo]]:

**Metcalfe's law** holds that the value of a network grows proportionally to the square of the number of users. This applies to one-to-one connection networks like telephones or messaging platforms [1].

**Reed's law** holds that the value of a group-forming network grows exponentially with the ease of group formation. Hariharan identifies Pinduoduo as having "one of the most powerful network effects in the world" because it taps into groups of social relationships rather than one-to-one connections [1]. The team purchase mechanic means every user has an incentive to recruit others, and the recruiting happens through existing social groups.

## Network Effects in Practice

### WeChat

[[WeChat]]'s network effects compounded across layers. The messaging platform created a base network effect, then the Moments social feed added a content layer, then WeChat Pay added a financial layer, then Mini Programs added an application layer. Each layer reinforced the others, making switching costs enormous [2]. WeChat grew from 0 to 889 million MAUs by stacking network effects on top of each other.

### Toutiao

[[Toutiao]] demonstrates data network effects: more users generate more engagement data, which improves the recommendation algorithm, which makes the content more addictive, which attracts more users [3]. Algorithms without data are useless, and data without an addictive product is unavailable. The virtuous cycle compounds product quality over time.

### Pinduoduo

Pinduoduo's team purchase model creates network effects at the group level. Users recruit friends and family into purchasing groups to unlock lower prices, which brings new users to the platform, who then form their own groups [1]. The social graph becomes the distribution channel.

## Network Effects and Growth

[[Paul Graham]] notes that raising money lets founders "choose their growth rate," and the most successful startups take VC money even when profitable because it lets them grow faster and fend off competitors, "especially in businesses with network effects" [4]. Network effects create a strategic imperative for fast growth: the first company to reach scale often locks in its advantage.
