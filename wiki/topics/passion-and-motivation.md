# Passion and Motivation

The advice to "follow your passion" is among the most common and least useful counsel given to aspiring founders. [[Dalton Caldwell]] and [[Michael Seibel]] dissect what passion actually means in the context of startups, when it helps, when it misleads, and what to do when you do not have a burning problem to solve [1].

## The Solution in Search of a Problem

The failure case that prompted this discussion: a YC founder wrote, "We chose to create a company around a hot technology we were really excited about. We eventually raised funds because there were enough investors excited about it too, but now looking back, we had it backwards. We were the classic Silicon Valley startup in search of a problem" [1].

A solution in search of a problem (SISP) is when founders build something and then try to find someone who wants it. "As long as technology has existed, this has been an issue" [1]. The pattern is amplified by the school metaphor: students are trained to accomplish specific tasks and are rewarded when doing what their smart friends are doing. Applied to startups, this produces founders who treat investor approval as the equivalent of a good grade, and a "cool" idea as the equivalent of getting into a prestigious school [1].

The diagnostic: "Because you don't understand, really deeply understand, the product you're copying, you miss some obvious things." Founders optimizing for superficial signals -- fundraising, press, advisor names -- while the user "is not essential" [1].

## When Passion Works

Passion is valuable when it is genuine and specific -- when the founder would work on the problem even without money or approval from authority figures [1].

Caldwell traces Microsoft and Apple back to their origins: people who were "really into this weird niche thing" of microcomputers. "No one told them to go work on microcomputers. They probably would be working on that stuff even if there was no money in it" [1].

Seibel's definition: "Would you be doing this stuff even if you didn't make any money doing it?" The "no money" test is easy. The harder test: no approval from authority figures. "You can't imagine yourself becoming Elon Musk solving this problem. You can't imagine the press loving you" [1].

YC has funded founders who encountered real problems -- banking transactions failing two out of five times in their country -- and fixed them. These founders' passion was grounded in lived experience, not theoretical global problems [1].

## When Passion Misleads

The problem with being 19 or 20: "A lot of people aren't passionate about many things, and they all tend to have the same startup ideas" [1]. The canonical bad idea: "I want to find a way to coordinate going out with my friends. It's too hard to plan activities." This has been tried for 20 years. The founders believe they are experts, so they do not Google it [1].

When your passions are the same as everyone else's, passion becomes a poor decision criterion. "To the extent that the stuff that you care about the most is the same as everyone else, you might want to zoom out and not have passion be your primary mode of making a decision" [1].

## Passion Through Traction

The key insight: passion can follow from a business working, rather than preceding it [1].

"Guess what gives you passion? That damn thing working. Success, users, revenue numbers. That makes a lot of these folks that have no particular ideas suddenly care a lot more when they actually are running a business that appears to be working" [1].

Seibel uses Twitch as the example. The Justin.tv founders were not passionate about live video streaming. But users wanted to stream, traffic was high (30 million monthly visitors even when the company was not doing well), and the positive feedback loop kept people engaged for years until the business broke through [1].

Caldwell draws a sports analogy: the person who becomes the best at a sport is often not the one who was nerding out about it earliest, but the one who was naturally good and generated a positive feedback loop. "Oh, I'm pretty fast. Oh, I can jump higher than my friends. This is fun because I'm doing well" [1].

"Running a startup that is not working is not fun. Running something that's working, where you're getting customers, and every day you see your Stripe account inching up -- is awesome. And you might actually develop passion for the thing you're working on" [1].

## Other Motivators Beyond Problem Passion

Not every co-founder will be equally passionate about the problem. Seibel identifies alternative motivators that sustain founders through years of hard work [1]:

- **Working with friends**: "Working with friends is so much fun compared to a job at Google"
- **Autonomy**: "Not having a boss feels like winning"
- **Consequences**: "Having consequences feels like winning. If I'm playing in a sandbox and everything is controlled, it doesn't feel like winning. If I can lose, winning feels much more real"
- **Revenue as scorekeeping**: Money is a number that goes up. "Your brain will release chemicals when you make money. And you'll work hard another day"

The advice: "Intentionally set up motivators, psychological motivators around you, to intentionally game yourself" [1]. Do not rely on amorphous, ill-defined goals. Choose a metric (revenue), track it daily, and let the feedback loop generate the motivation.

## Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic Motivation

The failure case (SISP on hot technology, funded by excited investors, surrounded by impressed friends) is driven by extrinsic motivation: money, status, approval. The success case is driven by intrinsic motivation: solving a problem that bothers you, working with people you enjoy, competing against yourself [1].

"95 percent of your friends doing startups are extrinsically motivated, and their startups are gonna die in the next two or three years. Some of them will raise money and hire people and get offices. But almost all of them are gonna die. And the person who everyone thinks is a little too weird, not working on the normal thing, not growing as fast -- those disproportionately end up surviving and building something people want" [1].

Seibel's framing: "Extrinsic motivation is great for getting to a good high school or a good college. Not so good for the whole startup game" [1].
