# Fundraising

Startup fundraising is the process of raising external capital to fund [[Growth]]. Nearly all startups require outside funding because the capital needed to reach profitability exceeds what founders and their networks can provide. [[Geoff Ralston]] describes fundraising as "brutal" but a path "almost all companies and founders must walk" [1]. The core YC advice on fundraising, per [[YC Essential Startup Advice]], is to "raise money as quickly as possible and then get back to work" [7].

Raising quickly and returning to building is one of the most reinforced principles in the YC library, cited by [[Geoff Ralston]] [1], [[Paul Graham]] [8][9], [[Sam Altman]] [15], and the YC team [7] across 16 sources. The consensus is clear: fundraising is a necessary distraction, not the main event.

## Why Raise Money

Cash provides a competitive advantage in [[Hiring]], PR, marketing, and sales [1]. High-growth companies almost always need to burn capital to sustain growth before reaching profitability. A few startups successfully bootstrap, but they are the exception. As [[Geoff Ralston]] writes, "Without startup funding the vast majority of startups will die" [1].

One critical reminder from YC: "the money you raise IS NOT your money. You have a fiduciary and ethical/moral duty to spend the money only to improve the prospects of your company" [7].

## When to Raise

Founders should raise when they have identified the market opportunity, the customer, and have a product that is being adopted at a rapid rate [1]. [[Geoff Ralston]] sets a bar: "a rate of 10% per week for several weeks is impressive" [1]. For founders who can convince investors without traction, the advice is simply: congratulations. For everyone else: "work on your product and talk to your users" [1].

Valuation is not equal to success or even probability of success [7]. [[Michael Seibel]] has emphasized this, and YC notes that "some of Y Combinator's very best companies raised on tiny initial valuations (Airbnb, Dropbox, Twitch, are all good examples)" [7].

## How Much to Raise

The ideal amount gets a startup to profitability so it never needs to raise again [1]. Failing that, raise enough for 12-18 months of runway to reach the next fundable milestone. [[Geoff Ralston]] provides a rule of thumb: an engineer costs roughly $15K/month all-in, so 5 engineers for 18 months requires about $1.35M [1].

Dilution guidance: 10% is wonderful, up to 20% is typical, and founders should try to avoid more than 25% [1]. Seed valuations tend to range from $2M-$10M, but "the goal is not to achieve the best valuation, nor does a high valuation increase your likelihood of success" [1].

Most first seed rounds cluster around $600K, though these have been increasing. The range is typically $500K to $1.5M [1].

## Financing Instruments

### SAFEs (Simple Agreements for Future Equity)

The safe, created by YC, has almost completely replaced convertible debt for seed rounds [1]. A safe has no interest rate, no maturity date, and no repayment requirement. Negotiable terms are typically just the amount, the cap, and the discount. [[Geoff Ralston]] recommends reading the [safe primer](http://www.ycombinator.com/documents/) by [[Carolynn Levy]] [1].

### Convertible Debt

A loan that converts to equity at the next priced round [1]. Has a principal amount, interest rate (usually minimum 2%), maturity date, and typically a cap or discount. Convertible debt may be called at maturity. Largely replaced by SAFEs in Silicon Valley.

### Equity Rounds

Setting a valuation and selling new shares [1]. More complicated, expensive, and time-consuming than SAFEs or convertible notes. Example math from [[Geoff Ralston]]: raising $1M on a $5M pre-money valuation with 10M shares outstanding means selling shares at $0.50, issuing 2M new shares, for a post-money of $6M and dilution of 16.7% (not 20%, because dilution is calculated on the post-money share count) [1].

## Types of Investors

[[Geoff Ralston]] draws a clear distinction: "The difference between an angel and a VC is that angels are amateurs and VCs are pros. VCs invest other people's money and angels invest their own on their own terms" [1]. Angels decide faster, with more emotional input. VCs require more time, more meetings, and multiple partners in the decision.

The ecosystem also includes micro-VCs / super-angels that target very early stage companies, AngelList Syndicates that let angels pool resources, and platforms like FundersClub that let angels become LPs [1].

The best way to meet investors is via warm introduction [1]. If no introduction is possible, send a "brief, but compelling summary of your business and opportunity."

## The Seed Pitch Deck

[[Aaron Harris]] provides YC's seed deck template [2]. The key principle: "founders should strive for clarity and concision. This is not the right place to write a treatise on your market or world philosophy." The structure:

1. **Title page** - Company name and one-line description (the only slide limited to exactly 1 slide) [2]
2. **Problem** - State the problem clearly with real-world impact [2]
3. **Solution** - What you do, in as few words as possible, with concrete benefits [2]
4. **Traction** - Clear, meaningful numbers (charts, metrics) [2]
5. **Insights** - What makes this work, what your unique insight is [2]
6. **Business model** - How you make money [2]
7. **Market** - Size and growth, how much money you will make [2]
8. **Team** - Why founders are well-suited; "nobody cares about your advisors" [2]
9. **The Ask** - How much money you need and what it gets you; if it makes you Series A ready in a year, that is powerful [2]

Each section can use 1-3 slides, but try to keep it to 1. Harris emphasizes: "Focus on narrative. The rest is commentary" [2].

For deck design principles, see [[Pitch Deck Design]], based on [[Kevin Hale]]'s framework of legible, simple, and obvious [3].

## Meeting Investors

From [[Geoff Ralston]]'s advice on investor meetings [1]:

- Know your audience: research what they invest in and why
- Simplify your pitch to the essential: great product, right team, dream of a gigantic company
- "If you can get the investor to talk more than you, your probability of a deal skyrockets"
- Strike a balance between confidence and humility. "Never cross over into arrogance, avoid defensiveness, but also don't be a pushover."
- Never leave a meeting without an attempted close or absolute clarity on next steps

### What Not to Do

Ralston provides a detailed list of don'ts when communicating with investors [1]:
- Don't be dishonest, arrogant, or overly aggressive
- Don't talk so much they can't get a word in
- Don't create detailed financials at seed stage
- Don't use ridiculous market size numbers without justification
- Don't ask for an NDA
- Don't try to negotiate in real-time
- Don't over-optimize your valuation
- Don't take a "No" personally

[[Paul Graham]] captures the psychological reality of rejection: "If the soda is empty, stop making that awful sucking sound with the straw" [8].

## Negotiating and Closing

Deals have momentum [1]. Getting the first investor in is the hardest part; each subsequent close gets faster and easier. [[Geoff Ralston]] advises a "breadth-first search weighted by expected value" approach: talk to as many investors as possible, prioritizing those most likely to close [1].

Once an investor says yes, use a [handshake deal protocol](https://www.ycombinator.com/handshake/) to close rapidly [1]. Get signatures and cash as soon as possible. One reason SAFEs are popular is that closing can take "no longer than a few minutes" via platforms like [Clerky](https://www.clerky.com/) or [Ironclad](https://ironclad.ai/) [1].

## Series A

### The Term Sheet

[[Jason Kwon]] and [[Aaron Harris]] published a standard, clean Series A term sheet through YC's Series A program [4]. The most important insight: "founders don't know what 'good' looks like in a term sheet" because it is often literally the first time they have seen one [4]. See also [[Series A Term Sheet]] for the full framework.

Key points about a standard Series A [4]:
- The lead investor generally wants 20% of the company, but pricing flexes based on leverage
- A founder-friendly board structure is 2-1 (2 founders, 1 investor). Founders most often lose control with a 2-2-1 structure (2 founders, 2 investors, 1 independent), which means "the founders can be fired from their own company." See also [[Board Governance]].
- The term sheet fits on a single page in modern practice

**Dirty terms to watch for** (absent from a clean term sheet) [4]:
- Liquidation preference greater than 1x
- Participating preferred (double-dipping)
- Cumulative dividends (compounding liquidation preference yearly)
- Warrant coverage (extra ownership without paying agreed valuation)

These terms signal that the investor is "sort of afraid of losing my money" [4]. As Kwon and Harris write: "when an investor says that they're committed to partnering with you for the long-term - or that they're betting everything on you - but then tells you something else with the terms that they insist on, believe the terms" [4].

Good investors address economic risk by negotiating valuation, not by adding structure [4].

The Series A documents set a foundation and precedent for future rounds. "Unwinding bad terms is difficult, and oftentimes impossible." But: "No one ever built an enduring company just by winning their Series A negotiation" [4].

### Series A Diligence

[[Jason Kwon]] and [[Aaron Harris]] also published a Series A diligence checklist [5]. Having all documents ready in a data room before signing a term sheet "will cut as much as a week off of your closing process" [5]. Founders often do not realize closing can take more than a month, much of it spent tracking down documents. See also [[Series A Diligence Checklist]] for the full list.

The checklist covers [5]:
- **Corporate records**: minutes, certificates of incorporation, bylaws, org charts
- **Business plan and financials**: current plan, projections, recent financial statements
- **Intellectual property**: trademarks, patents, copyrights, domain names, assignments
- **Security issuances**: stockholder list, option holders, vesting schedules, voting agreements
- **Material agreements**: terms of service, contracts >$25K, leases, debt, insurance, IP licenses
- **Litigation**: pending or threatened actions, IP infringement allegations
- **Employee information**: employee list with titles/salaries/classifications, offer letters, benefit plans, equity grants, handbook

**Critical note on equity grants**: Complete all pending or promised equity grants before starting the Series A process [5]. Once a term sheet is received, many believe the current 409A valuation can no longer be used. Employees hired earlier would then receive equity at a higher strike price, reducing their upside and potentially creating morale issues. See also [[Equity and Compensation]].

### Series A Pitch and Deck

[[Janelle Tam]] provides a complete guide to building a Series A pitch and deck [13]. The Series A deck typically runs 10-15 slides and tells a traction story rather than just a vision story. See [[Series A Pitch and Deck]] for the full framework.

## Fundraising Rules Summary

From [[Geoff Ralston]] [1]:
- Get fundraising over as soon as possible, and get back to building
- But don't stop too soon if it is difficult: keep fighting and stay alive
- Be "greedy": breadth-first search weighted by expected value
- Once someone says yes, get docs signed and money in the bank immediately
- Always hustle for leads
- Never screw anyone over. "The Valley is a very small place, and a bad reputation is difficult to repair."
- Develop a style that fits you and your company
- Stay organized; co-founders should split tasks

## Convincing Investors: Graham's Framework

[[Paul Graham]] frames the fundraising challenge differently from the mechanics-focused approach above [8]. In "How to Convince Investors," he argues that the core problem is not pitch technique but truthful self-evaluation.

"When people hurt themselves lifting heavy things, it's usually because they try to lift with their back. The right way to lift heavy things is to let your legs do the work. Inexperienced founders make the same mistake when trying to convince investors. They try to convince with their pitch. Most would be better off if they let their startup do the work" [8].

### The Three Requirements (Graham)

Investors need three things [8]:

1. **Formidable founders.** "Most investors decide in the first few minutes whether you seem like a winner or a loser." What formidable means: "A formidable person is one who seems like they'll get what they want, regardless of whatever obstacles are in the way. Formidable is roughly justifiably confident." Winners get questions like "yes, and you could also do x." Losers get "but what about y?" Don't fake swagger: "If you try to act like something you're not, you'll just end up in an uncanny valley." See also [[Founder Mindset]].

2. **A promising market.** Investors think of startups as markets. "If there are x customers who'd pay an average of $y per year, the TAM is $xy" [8]. The market doesn't have to be big yet, but there must be "some plausible sequence of hops that leads to dominating a big market." The "why now?" question is critical: ideally something recently changed and no one else has noticed.

3. **Evidence of success.** At Demo Day (3 months), a growth graph is "mostly evidence that the founders are effective" [8]. At Series A (2 years), "it has to be evidence of a promising market and a company tuned to exploit it."

### Truth as Strategy

Graham's core advice: "Convince yourself that your startup is worth investing in, and then when you explain this to investors they'll believe you" [8]. This requires being a domain expert. "If you're not a domain expert, you can be as convinced as you like about your idea, and it will seem to investors no more than an instance of the Dunning-Kruger effect."

School trains everyone to present regardless of substance [8]. Founders approach fundraising the same way, spreading thin truth across a fixed presentation. Graham's corrective: "The time to raise money is not when you need it, or when you reach some artificial deadline like a Demo Day. It's when you can convince investors, and not before."

The recipe for inexperienced founders [8]: (1) Make something worth investing in. (2) Understand why it's worth investing in. (3) Explain that clearly. "If you're saying something you know is true, you'll seem confident when you're saying it."

[[Alfred Lin]] notes that startup explanations must be clear and concise because they convince at one remove: "it has to work not just on the partner you talk to, but when that partner re-tells it to colleagues" [8].

### Handling Rejection

Getting rejected is common at all levels [8]. "A lot of VCs would have rejected Microsoft. Certainly some rejected Google." All East Coast VCs passed on Dropbox; weeks later it raised a Series A from Sequoia. The best response to rejection: talk candidly about what scared investors. The best investors "already believe most other investors are conventional-minded drones" [8].

### Don't Get Your Hopes Up

From "The Hardest Lessons," Graham's deal psychology advice: when someone says "we want to invest," assume they won't [9]. "The reason I warn startups not to get their hopes up is not to save them from being disappointed. It's to prevent them from leaning their company against something that's going to fall over." VCs and corp dev are professional negotiators who exploit perceived weakness. The only real leverage: genuinely not needing the deal [9].

### The YC Interview

In "Billionaires Build," Graham explains YC interviews are explicitly not pitches [10]. Ten-minute conversations requiring random access, not sequential presentations. "It is so annoying when people try to [turn it into a pitch]." The best preparation: talk to your users. The most important single question: **"How do you know people want this?"** [10]

### The Equity Equation for Investment Decisions

Graham's [[The Equity Equation]] provides a mathematical framework: give up n% of the company only if the trade makes the remaining (100-n)% worth more than the whole was before [8]. The formula: 1/(1-n). For YC's 7%, that means 1/.93 = 1.075 -- so YC needs to improve outcomes by more than 7.5% for the deal to be worthwhile. For Sequoia at 30%, 1/.7 = 1.43, meaning a 43% improvement is needed. Graham argues that for most startups, "it would improve the average startup's prospects by more than 43% just to be able to *say* they were funded by Sequoia" [8]. The catch: Sequoia receives ~6,000 plans/year and funds ~20.

### The Valuation Trap

[[Sam Altman]] warns against raising at too high a valuation: "Don't raise at too high a valuation when you have an airplane, not a spaceship" [15]. A high valuation creates expectations the company may not be able to meet, making the next round harder or triggering a damaging down round.

### Common Fundraising Mistakes

[[Paul Graham]] identifies several fundraising-related mistakes that kill startups [14]: raising too little money, spending too much, raising too much (creating unrealistic expectations), and poor investor management. See [[Startup Mistakes]] for the full list.

### Don't Assume More Money Is Coming

From [[Default Alive or Default Dead]], Graham warns: "Don't assume it will be easy to raise more; the more you depend on it, the falser it becomes" [12]. See [[Financial Survival]] for the full framework on managing runway.
