# Fundraising

Fundraising is one of the most written-about topics in the YC library, covered from seed mechanics to Series A diligence to the psychology of convincing investors.

## When to Raise

Graham argues that the time to raise money is not when you need it — it is when you can convince investors. Fundraising when desperate is a losing position. Ralston recommends raising seed when the startup has a story that is "interesting enough to get an investor to open their checkbook."

## How Much to Raise (Seed)

Ralston's "Guide to Seed Fundraising" recommends raising enough to get to the next milestone, typically 12-18 months of runway. The amount depends on the type of business. In Graham's framing, both raising too little (#11 in "18 Mistakes") and raising too much (#13) are dangerous — too little leaves no margin for error, too much creates complacency and overspending.

## Financing Instruments

Ralston covers three main options at seed:

**SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity).** YC's preferred instrument. Simpler than convertible notes, no interest, no maturity date. Converts to equity at the next priced round.

**Convertible Notes.** Debt that converts to equity. Simpler than a priced round but more complex than SAFEs. Include interest and maturity dates.

**Priced Equity Round.** Standard stock purchase. More expensive and complex at seed stage. More common for larger seed rounds.

## Convincing Investors

Graham identifies three things investors need to see: formidable founders, a promising market, and evidence of success so far.

**Seeming formidable.** Not just confident — justifiably confident. Graham argues the best way for inexperienced founders to do this is to stick to the truth. "Convince yourself first, then explain." Investors can sense the difference between genuine conviction from domain expertise and the Dunning-Kruger effect.

**Market sizing.** Investors think in markets, founders think in ideas. Framing the opportunity as a large addressable market, and answering "why now?" (what changed to make this possible), are essential.

**Handling rejection.** Graham recommends being candid about why investors turned you down and why they are mistaken. The best investors already believe most other investors are conventional-minded.

## Pitch Decks

Hale argues that a pitch deck should answer three questions: What do you do? How big is the market? What is your traction? He recommends keeping decks to 10-15 slides and leading with the clearest possible statement of the problem and solution.

Tam's Series A deck guide emphasizes that Series A pitches must show metrics and traction, not just vision. The shift from seed to Series A is from "promising" to "delivering."

## Series A Mechanics

Harris's diligence checklist covers what founders need to have ready: corporate records, business plan and financials, IP documentation, securities, material agreements, and employee/benefits records. Having a complete data room before signing a term sheet can cut a week off closing.

A critical detail: all pending equity grants should be completed before starting the Series A process. Once a term sheet exists, the old 409A valuation is no longer usable, which hurts employees hired under the prior valuation.
