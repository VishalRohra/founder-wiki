# Startups for Students

When Y Combinator launched in 2005, starting a startup was an unusual career path for recent graduates. It was hard to be taken seriously by investors without an MBA or years of business experience. TechCrunch published about 40 company launches per month in 2005, and YC received only a couple hundred applications for its first batches [1]. By 2021, YC received tens of thousands of applications per year, and on any given day Product Hunt shows dozens of new company launches [1].

## Why Start a Startup

### The Three Self-Assessment Questions

[[Michael Seibel]] recommends asking three questions to determine if a startup is right for you [1]:

1. **Do I like being the underdog?**
2. **Do I seek hard challenges most people shy away from?**
3. **Do I thrive taking personal responsibility for success or failure?**

Seibel writes: "There is a certain type of person who only works at their peak capacity when there is no predictable path to follow. The odds of success are low, and they have to take personal responsibility for failure" [1]. If you answer yes three times, a startup may be right for you.

### Find a Problem You Are Obsessed With Solving

Some of the most successful YC companies formed when founders confronted real-world issues [1]:

- **Airbnb** (2007): Brian Chesky and Joe Gebbia were having trouble paying rent, so they bought air mattresses and charged people $80/night during a San Francisco convention
- **Stripe** (2010): Patrick and John Collison started a side project after Patrick became frustrated with how hard it was to accept financial payments online
- **Gusto**: Founded because the founders were frustrated by payroll inefficiencies for small businesses

The advice: look at your own life, identify a problem, and build a solution [1].

## Why Not to Start a Startup

### Legitimate Reasons

- **You are still in school.** Startups demand enormous time and energy. Grades and social life will suffer. Aaron Feurer, co-founder of Panorama Education, spent over 40 hours a week building the company as a Yale undergraduate and had amassed hundreds of thousands of dollars in revenue by graduation — but this is "very difficult to achieve and highly uncommon" [1].
- **It is difficult.** Nine out of 10 startups fail in their first year. Building a company is mentally and emotionally taxing [1].
- **You have no co-founder.** Only 7.9% of YC's top companies were solo-founded. See [[Co-Founders]] [1].
- **You are not ready for the commitment.** Exits and IPOs typically take years — prepare for up to a decade of work [1].
- **You need structure.** Large corporations provide managers as mentors and collaborative teams [1].
- **You hate uncertainty.** There is no clear linear path to success at a startup [1].

### Bad Reasons Not to Start

- **"I don't have an idea."** YC puts more emphasis on the team than the idea. Reddit, Brex, and Segment were all accepted as completely different concepts before pivoting [1].
- **"My parents want me to get a real job."** YC notes that *The Social Network* (2012) caused a massive spike in applications and may have been the cultural inflection point where parents realized startups could be a viable career. Point skeptical parents to companies founded by people in their 20s: Brex, Stripe, Embark, Scale AI — all now worth billions [1].
- **"I have student loans."** Student debt makes a well-paying corporate job more enticing, but startup experience is a force multiplier for earning potential even if the company fails [1].

## What to Do in College

### Skills to Develop

1. **Coding.** Learn to code and befriend technical people. You cannot build a software company without software [1].
2. **Communication.** Learn to convey ideas clearly and simply — even enormously complex ones should be explainable to grandparents [1].
3. **People skills.** Build your network, meet potential [[Co-Founders|co-founders]], and develop deep understanding of people's needs. Talking to users is a critical founder skill [1].

### How to Spend Time

College is one of the best places to meet a potential co-founder. Work on projects and extracurricular activities with classmates. Experts in technology, business, and science are usually happy to help college students [1].

YC tells most students to finish their degree. The exception: "if the only thing you can do is think about your startup and you're spending all your free time working on it, it might be worth considering dropping out" [1].

### Non-Technical Founders

YC has funded startups without a technical co-founder. Roughly 10% of companies in each batch are founded by non-technical founders. But among YC's top companies, only three were founded by non-technical founders: Flexport, [[Snapdocs]], and UpKeep. YC strongly recommends finding a technical co-founder for software companies. Even non-technical founders should learn the basics of coding [1].

## Working at a Startup Instead

For those not ready to found, working at a startup — especially a YC startup — provides experience building products, talking to customers directly, and transparent views of the business [1].

### Choosing the Right Company

- Find a startup solving a problem interesting to you
- For internships, a post-Series A company is recommended for more structure and mentorship
- For self-directed learners, a seed-stage company could work

### Questions to Ask Founders

- What will I work on during this internship? (Bonus if the project ships)
- Will I get to see how the business is doing? How much interaction with customers?
- What will I learn from the people I am working with?
- For full-time roles: ask about runway and burn rate [1]

## Getting First Users as a Student

1. **College campus** — easy access to a large pool of potential customers likely to try something new [1]
2. **Friends and family** — helps determine ideal demographic [1]
3. **Charge as soon as possible** — even small amounts help validate how much value you are creating [1]

## Applying to YC

YC accepts applications year-round. No specific school, degree, or business plan is required. The application is free. About half of the Summer 2021 batch was internationally based [1].

What YC looks for [1]:
- **Technical ability** — teams that can build, usually meaning a technical founder
- **A drive to build** — about 60% of funded companies do not have a launched product, but founders show inclination for action and fast progress
- **Persistence and resilience** — about 50% of funded companies have applied more than once; showing progress between applications signals seriousness
