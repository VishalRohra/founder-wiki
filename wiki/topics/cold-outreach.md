# Cold Outreach

Aaron Epstein, YC Group Partner and founder of Creative Market, provides a comprehensive framework for cold email outreach -- covering the full funnel from targeting to conversion [1]. His opening advice: the single best outreach hack is to get a warm intro, which yields 2-3x higher conversion rates than cold email [1]. But when warm intros are not possible, cold outreach done well can still be effective because "most cold emails are actually terrible, so the bar is really low for you to stand out" [1].

## The Conversion Funnel

Epstein maps out a typical B2B email funnel with sample conversion rates [1]:

| Stage | Conversion Rate | Volume Needed |
|---|---|---|
| Emails Sent | -- | 800 |
| Emails Opened | 50% | 400 |
| Responses | 10% of opens | 40 |
| Demos Booked | 25% of responses | 10 |
| Customers | 10% of demos | 1 |

The implication: getting one customer may require sending 800 emails. Founders who send a handful per week will not see results. Dozens per day -- potentially 50 -- are needed [1].

Critical rules: start writing manually and personalized before automating. The goal of the first email is not to close the sale but to advance to the next step in the funnel. Track conversion rates at each step to identify where to improve [1]. Conversion rates decrease as outreach scales, so fix problems at small scale first [1].

## Increasing Open Rates

**Better targeting is the highest-leverage lever.** It is better to send 100 targeted emails with high open rates than 1,000 untargeted emails that get deleted [1]. Targeting improves iteratively as founders learn to qualify leads better.

The recipient sees three things: sender name, subject line, and a preview of the first sentence. The "from" field should be a personal name (not a company name). Effective subject lines are short and conversational: "Hey quick question," "Can I get your advice," "Hello from [company name]" [1].

Email is generally better than LinkedIn for cold outreach, though this varies by industry. Text messaging can be more personal but should only be used with explicit permission [1].

Tools for finding email addresses: [Apollo.io](http://Apollo.io), [Hunter.io](http://Hunter.io), Clearbit, LinkedIn Sales Navigator, guessing based on company email format [1].

## Seven Principles of Effective Email Copy

### 1. Focused, Specific Goal

Each email should drive toward a single outcome -- respond, click, request an intro. Multiple asks create a paradox of choice. "Every word counts. If there's anything that doesn't move the recipient towards that goal, you should delete it" [1].

### 2. Be Human

Talk about emotions: "I'd love to," "It would mean a lot," "Thanks." Use informal language -- "hey" instead of "Hello Mr. Epstein." Typos and lack of capitalization signal humanity. Consider creative touches like personalized GIFs or videos [1].

The core rule: "Write how you talk to a friend." Compose the email with a specific person in mind, not as a template in a Google Doc. Then read it out loud. If anything feels awkward to say, rewrite it [1].

### 3. Personalize

Generic personalization ("love what you're doing at [company]") is transparent. Specific personalization ("I'm a huge Creative Market fan ever since you launched the photos category") shows genuine research [1].

**Uncommon commonalities** are the most powerful personalization tool: shared experiences that are not obvious. "We went to the same college and went to class in the same buildings" creates instant connection. Finding these requires real research -- LinkedIn, their product, their history [1].

### 4. Keep It Short

Wall-of-text emails get deleted. Short emails can be read and responded to on a phone in the moment. Anything requiring significant cognitive overhead gets archived or forgotten [1].

### 5. Establish Credibility

YC affiliation opens doors. Without it, use schools, former employers, impressive accomplishments, well-known customer logos, or industry data. Shared connections create baseline trust [1].

### 6. Make It About the Reader

Reframe "I" statements as "you" and "yours." Tell the story as solving the reader's problems. Use the language customers use to describe the product, not the company's internal language. Ask users: "How would you describe my company?" and use their exact phrase [1].

Do not make demands. The founder is in the recipient's inbox on their time [1].

### 7. Clear Call to Action

End with a concrete next step as its own standalone sentence: "Reply to let me know," "Click here to get started." Make it clear what action is requested and make that action easy enough to do immediately [1].

## Follow-Up Strategy

One email is often not enough. Plan to follow up 2-4 times, spacing emails a few days apart. Get creative with follow-ups -- Epstein once sent an email with subject line "Free donuts," then delivered donuts to the recipient's office [1].

If someone does not respond, do not get angry. "Nobody owes you anything." Move on and check back in a few months [1].

## The Founder Advantage

Founders should send these emails personally, not delegates. Recipients take emails from founders and CEOs far more seriously. "You may be small, but that is your advantage because you can give your personalized attention to making these dedicated emails feel special" [1].

In the early days, the founder is the brand. The level of effort, personalization, and care in the outreach represents the company. Offering a personal cell phone number and 24/7 availability signals genuine commitment that reduces the risk of working with a startup [1].
