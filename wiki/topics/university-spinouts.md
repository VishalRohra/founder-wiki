# University Spinouts

[[Jared Friedman]], YC partner, provides advice for people who have done scientific research at a university and are considering starting a company to commercialize it. YC has funded more than 75 companies in this situation. YC also conducted a [Bio Tour](https://blog.ycombinator.com/yc-bio-tour-2020/) visiting research universities to talk with hundreds of students and professors in the life sciences about commercialization [1].

## Deciding Who Should Spin Out

In a typical spinout, several people worked on the research -- a mix of students, post-docs, and faculty. The first decision is who works on the company and who stays at the university. Friedman identifies four common misconceptions [1]:

### Misconception 1: You Can Start a Company While Continuing Your Academic Career

"Your company has little chance of success unless someone who worked on the original research is willing to leave their university role to start this company" [1]. Students graduating soon can wait until graduation -- a perfect time to start. Otherwise, an intentional decision to leave must be made at some point [1].

### Misconception 2: You Should Find a CEO to Run the Company

Scientists who remain in academia and try to find a CEO to build a company around their invention almost always fail. Great CEOs are scarce and have better opportunities than running an idea-stage startup with no funding. Most scientists either never find a CEO or settle for a mediocre one. It is even worse if the university tries to find a CEO [1].

Even with any person in the world available, an outside CEO would likely be a bad choice. The people who did the original research are far more invested and far more qualified -- their domain knowledge is more valuable than general business skills. The common belief that "the research is done and all that's left is commercialization" rarely holds. Usually the market wants something different from what was invented, requiring more research. Original inventors can adjust; an outside CEO will be stuck [1].

### Misconception 3: You Need Someone With Business Experience on the Founding Team

"In the first couple of years, there is typically very little 'business' to be done, and whatever business skills you need you will pick up along the way" [1]. Most scientists YC funds have no prior business experience. "People who work in business like to make it sound hard, as if business were like quantum physics, a field that needed to be studied for years to master. The fact is, it's not even close" [1].

### Misconception 4: You Should Raise Money First, Then Leave the University

Scientists often pitch to local VCs hoping for validation and a smooth transition. VCs occasionally fund spinouts this way but usually do not. Founders who get rejected conclude the idea is bad and give up, when the actual issue is that it is too early for VC money [1].

Typically founders need to work full-time for 1+ years before a multi-million dollar VC round is feasible. In the meantime, they sustain themselves through savings, government grants, friends and family, or small pre-seed rounds from angels, accelerators, or seed funds [1].

Founders who refuse to quit before raising face a catch-22: "They are waiting for an investor to take a bet on them before they quit their job. But the investors are waiting for the founders to believe enough in their own company to quit their job" [1].

### The Recommended Approach

The ideal situation: two or more people from the lab who did the work leave together as cofounders. One full-time founder is also acceptable. One of the people who leave should be CEO. Others who remain at the university can serve as "academic cofounders" or "scientific cofounders" and contribute, but the full-time founders are the most important [1].

## Deciding When to Spin Out

In the early stages of developing a new technology, progress is faster at the university, taking advantage of university resources. At some point that flips, and being at the university slows things down because universities are not set up to commercialize technologies [1].

Leaving too early is possible, but the far more common mistake is waiting too long. Most founders delay because leaving is scary. "No one is pressuring you to leave and leaving seems risky so the natural thing to do is to keep delaying it" [1]. The temptation is to make the technology perfect before spinning out, with always "one more experiment" to do. Programs like NSF I-Corps can test market demand from inside the university [1].

After leaving, founders often realize that much of the last year's work was wasted because assumptions about market needs were wrong, and that they are now moving so much faster that they could have saved months by spinning out a year earlier [1].

## Splitting Equity

Friedman's framework has two rules [1]:

1. **Full-time founders get equal or nearly equal equity.** (He references [[Michael Seibel]]'s [essay](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/5x-how-to-split-equity-among-co-founders) on this.)
2. **Full-time founders get much more than academic cofounders.** Academic cofounders should typically own no more than 10% unless they remain hands-on.

The biggest conceptual mistake: thinking equity allocation rewards past contributions, when it actually anticipates future ones. "If you are going to make a new company successful, you will probably have to work on it for 7-10 years post spin-out" [1].

Friedman uses a marathon analogy: at spinout, founders may feel halfway done, but they are actually on mile 2 of 26. Academic founders may have been instrumental in mile 1, but full-time founders take the other 25. The equity split must reflect expected contributions over the entire marathon [1].

This means equity in the new company may have no relation to academic seniority. Junior people who leave often get much more equity than their former senior faculty boss. "This can be an awkward conversation, but it's entirely sensible" [1].

## Negotiating with Tech Transfer Offices

Commercializing university research requires negotiating intellectual property rights with the technology transfer office (TTO) [1].

TTOs historically had a bad reputation for being slow, bureaucratic, and forcing onerous terms that strangled young startups. Things have improved. TTOs at universities in major startup hubs (Harvard, MIT, Stanford) now give startups reasonable terms, though they still take too long. At universities with fewer successful spinouts, quality is hit-or-miss. Some universities now use "[express license agreements](https://ctl.cornell.edu/entrepreneurs/fasttrack/)" -- preset agreements requiring little to no negotiation [1].

### Four Key Terms

1. **Equity**: University gets equity in the company. 3-5% is typical and acceptable. Above 10% causes problems [1].
2. **Royalty**: Percentage of revenue or profits paid to the university. If too high, it affects the company's ability to raise money and operate. Ideally zero; keep it as low as possible [1].
3. **Milestone payments** and **sublicensing terms** (details truncated in source) [1].
