# University Spinouts

[[Jared Friedman]] provides advice for people who have done scientific research at a university and are considering starting a company to commercialize it [1]. YC has funded more than 75 companies in this situation and conducted a [Bio Tour](https://blog.ycombinator.com/yc-bio-tour-2020/) visiting research universities and talking with hundreds of students and professors in the life sciences [1].

## Deciding Who Should Spin Out

In a typical spin-out, several people worked on the research -- a mix of students, postdocs, and faculty [1]. The first decision: who works on the company and who stays at the university.

### Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: You can start a company while continuing your academic career.**

"Your company has little chance of success unless someone who worked on the original research is willing to leave their university role to start this company" [1]. Students graduating soon can wait, but otherwise "at some point you will have to make an intentional decision to leave" [1].

**Misconception 2: You should find a CEO to run the company.**

"Almost always a bad idea" [1]. Great CEOs are scarce and "usually have much better opportunities available to them than running an idea-stage startup with no funding." Most scientists who try this either never find a CEO or settle for a mediocre one. "It's even worse if your university tries to find a CEO for you" [1].

Even with a perfect external CEO: "The people who did the original research will be far more invested in the success of the venture than any outsider. They are also far more qualified to build a company around it because their domain knowledge of the field is much more valuable than whatever general business skills an outside CEO would bring" [1].

A related fallacy: believing the research is done and only commercialization remains. "Usually you find that the thing the market wants is not quite the thing that you've invented, and that more research needs to be done" [1].

**Misconception 3: You need someone with business experience on the founding team.**

"In the first couple of years, there is typically very little 'business' to be done, and whatever business skills you need you will pick up along the way" [1]. Most scientists YC funds have no prior business experience. "People who work in business like to make it sound hard, as if business were like quantum physics, a field that needed to be studied for years to master. The fact is, it's not even close" [1].

**Misconception 4: You should raise money first, then leave the university.**

Scientists often pitch local VCs to validate their idea before leaving [1]. When turned down, they conclude the idea is bad and give up. Actually: "it's just too early at this point to raise money from VCs." Founders typically need 1+ years full-time before a multi-million dollar VC round is feasible [1]. See also [[Fundraising]] and [[Biotech Startup Funding]].

The catch-22: "They are waiting for an investor to take a bet on them before they quit their job. But the investors are waiting for the founders to believe enough in their own company to quit their job!" [1]

In the interim, sustain yourself through savings, government research grants, friends and family, or a small pre-seed round from angels, accelerators, or seed funds [1].

### The Ideal Spin-Out Structure

Two or more people from the lab who did the work leave together as [[Cofounders|cofounders]] [1]. One full-time founder is also acceptable. One of them should be the CEO. Others who want to stay at the university can be "academic cofounders" or "scientific cofounders" and remain helpful, but the full-time founders are the most important [1].

## Deciding When to Spin Out

In early stages, the university is the ideal place: resources for initial experiments, programs like NSF I-Corps for market testing [1]. "At some point, though, that will flip, and being at a university will start to slow you down, because universities are not set up to commercialize technologies" [1].

"The far more common mistake is to wait too long" [1]. Founders delay because leaving is scary: "Academia is a comforting environment. No one is pressuring you to leave." There is always "one more experiment." After leaving, most founders realize "a lot of the work they did in the last year was wasted, because some of their assumptions about what the market wanted were wrong." They also find they move much faster as a company [1].

## Splitting Up Equity

Friedman's framework has two rules [1]:

1. **Full-time founders get equal or nearly equal equity.** (See [[Michael Seibel]]'s essay on [equity splits](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/5x-how-to-split-equity-among-co-founders).) See also [[Equity and Compensation]] and [[The Equity Equation]].

2. **Academic cofounders get much less.** "Founders who will be leaving their job to work on the company full-time should get much more equity than founders who are going to remain in academia. Academic cofounders should typically own no more than 10% unless they are going to continue to be hands-on" [1].

The biggest conceptual mistake: "they think the purpose of allocating equity is to reward past contributions, when actually it's mainly to anticipate future ones" [1].

The marathon analogy: "If you are going to make a new company successful, you will probably have to work on it for 7-10 years post spin-out" [1]. If you are just spinning out, "you might feel like you're halfway done, but actually you are on mile 2 of a 26 mile marathon." The equity split must reflect expected contributions over the whole marathon [1].

One consequence: "your equity split in the new company will not necessarily have any relation to your seniority within the original academic team" [1]. Junior lab members who leave may get much more equity than their former boss who stays. "This can be an awkward conversation, but it's entirely sensible" [1].

## Negotiating with Tech Transfer Offices

The technology transfer office (TTO) manages IP licensing from the university to the startup [1].

Historically, TTOs had "a well-deserved bad reputation" for being slow, bureaucratic, and imposing onerous terms that "strangled the very companies they were trying to create" [1]. Things have improved. Universities in major startup hubs (Harvard, MIT, Stanford) now give reasonable terms, though they still take too long. Some universities use "[express license agreements](https://ctl.cornell.edu/entrepreneurs/fasttrack/)" requiring little negotiation [1].

### Four Key Terms

1. **Equity.** University gets equity in the company. 3-5% is typical. "Above 10% will cause problems" [1].
2. **Royalty.** Percentage of revenue/profits to the university. "If this is too high, it can affect the viability of the company to raise money and operate." Aim for zero; keep it low if zero is not possible [1].
3. **License scope.** Whether the license is exclusive or non-exclusive, and to which fields it applies [1].
4. **Milestone requirements.** Conditions the company must meet to keep the license (e.g., raising funding, hiring, filing patents) [1].

Friedman emphasizes that these negotiations should be completed before or shortly after spinning out, not delayed indefinitely [1].
