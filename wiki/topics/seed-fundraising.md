# Seed Fundraising

Seed fundraising is the process by which startup founders raise initial outside capital to fund operations, growth, and product development. Most startups require external capital because the cost of reaching profitability exceeds what founders and their personal networks can finance [1]. Geoff Ralston, drawing on his experience at YC, describes fundraising as "brutal" but a path "almost all companies and founders must walk" [1]. This article covers the complete landscape of seed fundraising: when to raise, how much to raise, the mechanics of financing instruments, how to build a pitch deck, and how to close investors.

## When to Raise

Investors write checks when three conditions align: the idea is compelling, the founding team appears capable of executing, and the opportunity is real and sufficiently large [1]. For most founders, this requires having a product with measurable traction. Ralston specifies that a growth rate of 10% per week for several weeks is "impressive" and sufficient to attract investor interest [1]. For founders who lack traction, the advice is direct: "work on your product and talk to your users" [1].

The general principle is that when you can raise money, you should. Cash provides competitive advantage in hiring, marketing, sales, and public relations [1].

## How Much to Raise

The ideal amount is enough to reach profitability, eliminating the need for future rounds entirely [1]. When that is not realistic (as with hardware companies or capital-intensive businesses), the target should be enough to reach the next "fundable" milestone, typically 12 to 18 months away [1].

Ralston offers a concrete formula for calculating the amount: an engineer costs approximately $15,000 per month all-in. For 18 months of operations with five engineers, the calculation is 15k x 5 x 18 = $1.35 million [1]. Most seed rounds cluster around $600,000, though rounds have been increasing in size [1].

On dilution, founders should aim to give up no more than 10% of the company in a seed round, though most rounds require up to 20% dilution. Exceeding 25% dilution should be avoided [1]. The amount raised must be tied to a believable plan, and founders should prepare multiple scenarios showing different growth rates at different funding levels [1].

Seed valuations tend to range from $2 million to $10 million, but Ralston cautions against over-optimizing on valuation: "the goal is not to achieve the best valuation, nor does a high valuation increase your likelihood of success" [1].

## Financing Instruments

### SAFEs (Simple Agreement for Future Equity)

The SAFE has almost completely replaced convertible debt at YC [1]. A SAFE has no interest rate, no maturity date, and no repayment requirement. The negotiable terms are simply the investment amount, the valuation cap, and the discount (if any) [1]. SAFEs are popular because closing mechanics are minimal: signing a document and transferring funds, which can happen in minutes via services like Clerky or Ironclad [1].

### Convertible Notes

Convertible debt is a loan that converts to equity at a future priced round. It carries a principal amount, interest rate (usually minimum 2%), and maturity date [1]. Key terms include the valuation cap (the maximum effective valuation the note holder pays, regardless of the round valuation) and a discount (a percentage off the round valuation) [1]. Investors view these terms as their "seed premium" [1].

### Equity Rounds

Equity rounds involve setting a per-share price and issuing new shares. They are more complicated, expensive, and time-consuming than SAFEs or notes, which is why they are now the exception for seed rounds [1]. Ralston provides a worked example: raising $1 million on a $5 million pre-money valuation with 10 million shares outstanding results in selling 2 million new shares at $0.50 each, yielding 16.7% dilution (not 20%, because dilution is calculated on post-money shares) [1].

Whatever financing form is used, Ralston advises using well-known documents like YC's SAFE: "These documents are well understood by the investor community, and have been drafted to be fair, yet founder friendly" [1].

## Types of Investors

The difference between angels and VCs, as Ralston characterizes it, is that "angels are amateurs and VCs are pros" [1]. Angels invest their own money and can make decisions quickly with a larger emotional component. VCs invest other people's money, require more meetings, and involve multiple partners in the decision [1].

The seed ecosystem also includes "super-angels" or "micro-VCs" that explicitly target very early stage companies, as well as platforms like AngelList Syndicates (which let angels pool resources behind a lead angel) and FundersClub (which invests selectively like a traditional VC) [1].

The best way to meet investors is through warm introductions. Demo days provide a concentrated opportunity to meet motivated seed investors [1].

## Building the Seed Pitch Deck

[[Aaron Harris]] provides YC's canonical seed deck template, emphasizing that "founders should strive for clarity and concision" [2]. The core insight is that most seed-stage companies do not have meaningful detail to explore, and when founders pretend otherwise, "their stories get muddled, and the investors get lost" [2].

Harris distills the philosophy to a single line: "Focus on narrative. The rest is commentary" [2].

### The YC Seed Deck Template

The template consists of these slides, each of which can expand to a set of up to 3 slides if needed (except the title, which must be exactly 1 slide) [2]:

1. **Title** - Company name and one-line description of what you do [2]
2. **Problem** - Clearly state the problem with specifics on how it impacts real people or businesses [2]
3. **Solution** - Explain what you do in as few words as possible, describing concrete benefits [2]
4. **Traction** - Show metrics with clear, meaningful numbers. Revenue is better than other metrics [2]
5. **Additional Metrics** - If available [2]
6. **Insights / Unique Advantage** - What makes this work, what your insights are [2]
7. **Business Model** - How you make money. "You probably don't know all the details yet, but you should know a lot of them" [2]
8. **Market** - Market size and growth. "Convince the investor that they're going to make lots of money with you" [2]
9. **Team** - What makes the founding team well suited to the problem. "This should be about founders. Nobody cares about your advisors" [2]
10. **The Ask** - How much money you need and what it buys. "If you can lay out where you'll be inside of a year, which should make you Series A ready, that's powerful" [2]

## Meeting and Closing Investors

### Meeting Strategy

The goal of an initial meeting is not to close but to get the next meeting [1]. Investors rarely commit on the first day. Ralston's rules for meetings:

- Research the investor's preferences and investment history [1]
- Simplify the pitch to essentials: great product (with demo), right team, huge opportunity [1]
- Listen more than you talk. "If you can get the investor to talk more than you, your probability of a deal skyrockets" [1]
- Strike a balance between confidence and humility. "Never cross over into arrogance, avoid defensiveness, but also don't be a pushover" [1]
- Never leave without an attempted close or absolute clarity on next steps [1]

### Closing Strategy

The hardest part is getting the first money in [1]. Once one investor commits, each subsequent close gets faster and easier. Ralston advises a "greedy" approach: "breadth-first search weighted by expected value" [1]. This means talking to as many people as possible while prioritizing those most likely to close.

When an investor says yes, close immediately using a handshake protocol. "If you fail at negotiating from this point on, it is probably your fault" [1].

### Negotiation Principles

- Do not negotiate in real-time. Take requests away and consult advisors or legal counsel [1]
- Most terms from credible investors tend to be reasonable [1]
- Ask investors to explain precisely what they are asking for and why [1]
- "Get the best deal you can get--but get the deal!" The valuation at this early round will seldom determine the company's success or failure [1]

### Documents Required

Ralston cautions against over-preparing documents for seed rounds: "If an investor is asking for too much due diligence or financials, they are almost certainly someone to avoid" [1]. The required documents are:

- **Executive Summary**: One page (two at most) covering vision, product, team, traction, market size, and minimum financials [1]
- **Slide Deck**: A coherent leave-behind with graphics, charts, and screenshots rather than walls of text [1]

### What Not to Do

Ralston lists specific behaviors to avoid when communicating with investors, including: being dishonest, being arrogant, talking so much investors cannot speak, being slow to follow up, creating detailed financials at seed stage, using unjustified market size numbers, asking for NDAs, trying to play investors against each other, and taking a "no" personally [1].

As [[Paul Graham]] advises: "If the soda is empty, stop making that awful sucking sound with the straw" [1].

## Key Rules for Seed Fundraising

Ralston consolidates his advice into a set of rules [1]:

- Get fundraising over as soon as possible and return to building
- But do not stop raising too early if fundraising is difficult
- Once someone says yes, get documents signed and money in the bank immediately
- Always hustle for leads
- "Never screw anyone over. Hold yourself and others on your team to the highest ethical standards. The Valley is a very small place, and a bad reputation is difficult to repair"
- Stay organized; co-founders should split tasks
- Have thick skin but balance confidence with humility
