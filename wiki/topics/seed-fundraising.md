# Seed Fundraising

Seed fundraising is the process by which startup founders raise initial outside capital to fund operations, growth, and product development. Most startups require external capital because the cost of reaching profitability exceeds what founders and their personal networks can finance [1]. Geoff Ralston, drawing on his experience at YC, describes fundraising as "brutal" but a path "almost all companies and founders must walk" [1].

## When to Raise

Investors write checks when three conditions align: the idea is compelling, the founding team appears capable of executing, and the opportunity is real and sufficiently large [1]. For most founders, this requires having a product with measurable traction. Ralston specifies that a growth rate of 10% per week for several weeks is "impressive" and sufficient to attract investor interest [1].

## How Much to Raise

The ideal amount is enough to reach profitability, eliminating the need for future rounds entirely [1]. When that is not realistic, the target should be enough to reach the next "fundable" milestone, typically 12 to 18 months away [1].

Ralston offers a concrete formula: an engineer costs approximately $15,000 per month all-in. For 18 months of operations with five engineers, the calculation is 15k x 5 x 18 = $1.35 million [1]. Most seed rounds cluster around $600,000, though rounds have been increasing in size [1].

On dilution, founders should aim to give up no more than 10% in a seed round, though most rounds require up to 20% dilution. Exceeding 25% should be avoided [1].

## Why Silicon Valley Is Different

[[Michael Seibel]] explains why fundraising in Silicon Valley differs from other locations [3]. The key factor: concentration of investors who have each missed at least one big deal. Every Silicon Valley investor has a story of passing on a company that became a unicorn. This creates a fundamentally different psychology -- investors in the Valley are more willing to take risks because the cost of missing out is visceral and personal [3].

Outside Silicon Valley, investors are spread thin and have fewer reference points for massive outcomes. Their feedback tends to be more risk-averse, and founders who overcorrect based on this feedback may damage their prospects [3].

## How to Cold Email Investors

Seibel provides a specific framework for cold investor emails [4]:

**Do's:**
- **Make it short.** "If you can make your email something that I can read in 60 seconds or less, you can pretty much guarantee that I'll read it" [4].
- Include in that short email: the problem you are solving, your solution, whether you have launched and any growth, market size, co-founders, ability to write code, and any controversial insight you have about the opportunity [4].
- Use simple language. "There's a difference between a customer pitch and an investor pitch. When talking to a customer, they know the jargon. When talking to an investor, you have no idea whether they know" [4].

**Don'ts:**
- Don't send a wall of text
- Don't lead with a long narrative about how you came up with the idea
- Don't use industry jargon [4]

## Fundraising as a Woman Founder

Amber Atherton (Zyper, YC W18) and Iba Masood (TARA, YC W15) describe their contrasting fundraising paths [5]. Atherton raised a $1.2M seed in London before YC, then raised a $4M round pre-Demo Day from Forerunner. Her Series A process took about 60 days. Masood's seed process was painful -- the first $100K was very difficult -- but her pivot from a careers platform to an AI-powered product management tool attracted $2.8M from investors who "doubled down" [5].

Key lessons from both founders [5]:
- **Write an investment memo.** Atherton wrote a memo doing "the VCs' jobs for them" -- explaining the point of the company, problem, market size, and why perceived risks were not real risks. This went out with the deck after first meetings.
- **Focus on the partner, not the fund.** Masood emphasizes researching specific partners who have led rounds in companies you admire.
- **End conversations early when fit is missing.** Atherton wishes she had been more assertive ending conversations with investors who did not understand her space, rather than being seduced by brand-name fund names.

Masood's Series A closed in nine days from first coffee to term sheet, while the legal process took 60 additional days [5].

## Financing Instruments

### SAFEs (Simple Agreement for Future Equity)
The SAFE has almost completely replaced convertible debt at YC [1]. No interest rate, no maturity date, no repayment requirement. Negotiable terms: investment amount, valuation cap, and discount [1].

### Convertible Notes
A loan that converts to equity at a future priced round. Carries principal, interest rate (usually minimum 2%), and maturity date [1].

### Equity Rounds
More complicated, expensive, and time-consuming than SAFEs or notes, which is why they are the exception for seed rounds [1].

## Meeting and Closing Investors

The goal of an initial meeting is not to close but to get the next meeting [1]. Once one investor commits, each subsequent close gets faster and easier. Ralston advises a "greedy" approach: "breadth-first search weighted by expected value" [1].

## Key Rules

- Get fundraising over as soon as possible and return to building
- Once someone says yes, get documents signed and money in the bank immediately
- "Never screw anyone over. The Valley is a very small place, and a bad reputation is difficult to repair" [1]
