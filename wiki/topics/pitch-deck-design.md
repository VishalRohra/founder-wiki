# Pitch Deck Design

[[Kevin Hale]], YC partner, teaches a presentation to YC startups before they create their Demo Day slide decks [1]. His core argument is that founders already have everything they need to create a great presentation. The challenge is not finding things to say but communicating them so investors can understand and remember.

## The Fundamental Constraint

There are probably 100 reasons why a company is great, but people can only remember a few after a short presentation. YC helps each startup identify the five to seven most important ideas investors should know. On Demo Day, with over 100 companies presenting, investors will be lucky to remember even one or two points from any given company [1].

The standard is not creativity or flair. If a founder just communicates their points clearly, they will do better than 99% of startups. Before anyone can remember, they have to understand [1].

## The Three Principles: Legible, Simple, Obvious

Hale's framework for making ideas easy to understand is the [[Legible Simple Obvious]] framework [1]:

![Legible, Simple, Obvious](../raw/posts/images/4T-how-to-design-a-better-pitch-deck/35131887eed3.png)

- **Legible**: Even old people sitting in the back row with bad eyesight can read it.
- **Simple**: Each slide expresses one idea.
- **Obvious**: Can be understood at a glance.

The inverse produces failure: illegible, complicated, and subtle slides [1].

![Illegible, Complicated, Subtle](../raw/posts/images/4T-how-to-design-a-better-pitch-deck/7f82892467cb.png)

### Legibility

At Demo Day, the room fills with over 500 people. They cannot all sit in the front row, and many are older with imperfect eyesight. Legible slides use large type, bold text, a simple font, and good contrast from the background. Text at the top of the slide is easier to read from the back [1].

![Legibility principles](../raw/posts/images/4T-how-to-design-a-better-pitch-deck/fb3a8a6a0309.png)

Hale shows an evolution of the same slide through three versions. The first has text with poor contrast and bad positioning:

![Slide with poor contrast](../raw/posts/images/4T-how-to-design-a-better-pitch-deck/6e447d5bfc3f.png)

The second improves but is still constrained by a screenshot:

![Improved but constrained](../raw/posts/images/4T-how-to-design-a-better-pitch-deck/fa95fa8743e4.png)

The third strips away the screenshot and makes the text dominant and readable from anywhere in the room [1]:

![Final legible version](../raw/posts/images/4T-how-to-design-a-better-pitch-deck/bf6463530e43.png)

He uses an example from Shred Video (YC company): their Prototype Day slide had text that was "too small and too skinny," with the explanation of what they actually do hidden "in light gray at the bottom of the slide." Their technology -- software that automatically edits hours of raw video footage into professional-looking edits in seconds -- was buried:

![Shred Video Prototype Day slide](../raw/posts/images/4T-how-to-design-a-better-pitch-deck/ac4165ec4f6c.png)

By Demo Day, the description was simplified and the text was "large and in charge" [1].

### Simplicity

The word "simple" shares an origin with "complex." The root "plex" means braid, twist, or fold. Simple ideas are "one fold" -- one idea. A simple slide expresses one idea. Crowding a slide with multiple ideas makes it complex [1].

![Simple vs Complex etymology](../raw/posts/images/4T-how-to-design-a-better-pitch-deck/c05df6011960.png)

![Plex means braid, twist, fold](../raw/posts/images/4T-how-to-design-a-better-pitch-deck/8aaef1a06fa4.png)

Since there are only five to seven key ideas, there should be only about five to seven slides:

![5-7 slides](../raw/posts/images/4T-how-to-design-a-better-pitch-deck/25c73d797368.png)

Hale shows a slide from Afrostream (YC company) that tried to pack all the nuances of their business onto one slide, resulting in too much text that no one could easily remember:

![Afrostream early version](../raw/posts/images/4T-how-to-design-a-better-pitch-deck/f43a60dfdede.png)

Their Demo Day version was "short, sweet, and easy to recollect" [1]:

![Afrostream Demo Day version](../raw/posts/images/4T-how-to-design-a-better-pitch-deck/3394a3d6fc83.jpg)

### Obviousness

Obvious slides can be understood at a glance. The test: show it to a stranger and ask them what it means. If they don't immediately say your idea, you lose [1].

![Obvious slides test](../raw/posts/images/4T-how-to-design-a-better-pitch-deck/375759af9166.png)

Speed of understanding matters because Demo Day presentations are 2 minutes and 30 seconds, and investors are easily distracted. "If they don't get your point right away, they will check their email" [1].

To make slides obvious, Hale advises making them legible and simple first, then making the idea explicit. He shows a growth graph without labels -- the viewer can guess it represents growth but it is not immediately clear:

![Growth graph without labels](../raw/posts/images/4T-how-to-design-a-better-pitch-deck/9531057ae80a.png)

Adding axis labels helps:

![Growth graph with labels](../raw/posts/images/4T-how-to-design-a-better-pitch-deck/93712f10cafe.png)

But adding a direct caption like "10x growth in 6 months" makes it unmistakable. "It's like I put CliffsNotes right on the slide" [1]:

![Growth graph with caption](../raw/posts/images/4T-how-to-design-a-better-pitch-deck/0f3b663f4851.png)

Another variation from a YC startup, where the caption eliminates the need to study the graph:

![Graph with explanatory caption](../raw/posts/images/4T-how-to-design-a-better-pitch-deck/8d68036b1cb5.png)

Hale references [Hick's Law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hick%27s_law): each piece of information added to a problem increases the time to make a decision logarithmically. A Dropbox diagram illustrates excessive information:

![Dropbox diagram](../raw/posts/images/4T-how-to-design-a-better-pitch-deck/e297b4cf14ba.png)

Each piece of information in the diagram creates comprehension overhead:

![Information overload annotated](../raw/posts/images/4T-how-to-design-a-better-pitch-deck/d2bf3d3fa7dd.png)

Diagrams are "little mazes for ideas" that make the path to understanding too long [1].

**Distractions to avoid** [1]:
- Too much text
- Excessive explanations and caveats
- Excessive branding per slide
- Photos with no titles or captions
- Animations, transitions
- Memes
- Subtle humor, accidental humor

"Investors invest in teams not slides. Your slides should make your ideas more clear. Don't let your slides distract investors from what you're saying out loud" [1].

![Investors invest in teams not slides](../raw/posts/images/4T-how-to-design-a-better-pitch-deck/9da12d19d674.png)

### The Exception: Deliberate Complexity

One exception exists: when the point is to show complexity or overwhelm the audience, usually to illustrate how hard a problem is. Hale uses Magic (YC company) as an example. Magic's software abstracts thousands of on-demand services into a single interface:

![Magic single interface](../raw/posts/images/4T-how-to-design-a-better-pitch-deck/99c0944f2327.png)

Their slide showed a grid of every on-demand service they could find across every industry, market, and vertical. Each logo represented a separate account a user would need, illustrating the discovery and usability problem:

![Magic grid of services](../raw/posts/images/4T-how-to-design-a-better-pitch-deck/0c0085e4610a.png)

"It's overwhelming for a reason and that's usually when it's okay to bend the rules" [1].

## On Screenshots

Hale states he "usually hates screenshots in Demo Day presentations." Screenshots are almost always illegible (interface text is too small), complex (interfaces do multiple things), and non-obvious (take longer than a glance to understand). "They break all 3 rules" [1].

A Google image search for "screenshots" demonstrates the problem:

![Screenshots search](../raw/posts/images/4T-how-to-design-a-better-pitch-deck/0124f324ff52.png)

Instead of showing what a product does through screenshots, Hale recommends showing the most simplified version. A bulleted list of steps is his favorite technique. He cites SparkGift's slide explaining their product through three simple steps as "far better at explaining the ease of use" than any workflow from the actual interface:

![SparkGift simplified steps](../raw/posts/images/4T-how-to-design-a-better-pitch-deck/5bfdee5c72af.png)

Meadow's two-point explanation of their product was "far better than any combination of buttons, links and chrome they could have put up there":

![Meadow simplified explanation](../raw/posts/images/4T-how-to-design-a-better-pitch-deck/38414710dbcc.png)

The same caution applies to screencasts and videos [1].
