# Startup Management

Most startup founders begin managing teams with zero management experience. Building the right [[Company Culture]] early is closely tied to effective management -- both shape how people behave when no one is watching. As Aaron Epstein, founder of Creative Market, describes it: "One of the most difficult things about starting a company is that you have to create both a product that people love and a company where people want to work at the same time" [1]. This article compiles practical management advice for founders who are building and leading teams for the first time.

## The Core Tension

The dual mandate of startups creates a fundamental challenge: "Great people build great products, and great people won't tolerate a bad environment for very long" [1]. The commonly cited observation that "people leave managers, not companies" applies with particular force to startups, where many first-time founders "raise a bunch of money and start building a team without any management experience at all" [1].

## Delegation and Scaling

Delegation is the foundational management skill for founders. Epstein frames it as a necessary evolution: "As a founder you're used to doing it all, and it can be scary to shed responsibilities you've always owned, but you can't scale yourself and focus on the highest leverage opportunities until you get things off your plate" [1].

The practical signal for when to delegate: "If you think you're going to have to work especially late or can't get to the things you need to in a given day, take that as a signal to delegate to your team" [1].

The flip side of delegation is growth opportunity creation. Great employees often value personal growth more than compensation: "Don't hoard [new responsibilities] all for yourself" [1]. This connects to the broader principle from [[Startup Leadership]] that a CEO's role transitions from Doer-in-Chief to Company-Builder-in-Chief.

## Investing in Star Employees

Epstein draws an analogy to customer retention: "Similar to how it's much easier to retain an existing customer than to acquire a new one, it's much easier to keep an existing star employee happy than find a new star" [1]. The advice is to go "above and beyond" to make stars feel valued, appreciated, and rewarded.

## Setting Standards and Accountability

### High Bar

"Great people want to do big, meaningful things. Push your team with aggressive goals, and you might be surprised what they will accomplish" [1].

### Deadlines and Follow-Through

When assigning tasks, always ask "by when?" and add a calendar note to follow up [1]. When standards or deadlines are not met, give direct feedback to change future behavior.

### Verbalizing Feedback

Epstein emphasizes the power of simply stating feelings: "'I'm disappointed in this work' is extremely powerful in helping people directly understand where you stand. People aren't mind-readers, so rather than just jumping into solutions when giving feedback, state your feelings out loud" [1].

## Leading by Example

"Teams embody the characteristics of their leaders, and the things you care about will be the things your team cares about" [1]. If a founder pays attention to details, the team will learn to do the same. This extends to credit and blame: "Use 'I' when talking about a screw-up, and use 'we' (or better yet, specific contributors' names) when talking about successes" [1].

## Communication

### Sharing the Big Picture

Employees need to understand how their work fits into the company's larger goals. "It's easy to take it for granted that your team is clear on this, but this is one of those things where you want to constantly make that connection" [1]. This reinforces the [[Mission-to-Metrics Framework]] that [[Ali Rowghani]] describes as one of a CEO's three non-delegable tasks.

### Repetition

"You should feel like you sound like a broken record about the things that are important to your company -- your mission, your vision, your KPIs, etc." [1]. The test for adequate repetition: "every single person on your team will be able to explain them to a complete stranger exactly how you would've said it" [1].

## Hiring

### Hire Slow, Fire Fast

"It's the thing everyone says and few have the discipline to do" [1]. The reasoning: "Great people want to work with other great people, so resist the temptation to fill open positions with mediocre candidates, and don't settle. You should be thrilled about each new hire" [1].

### Hire for Hunger

Skills can be learned; passion cannot be manufactured. "Passion for your mission, business, and team will do more to drive an employee to make a big, long-term impact than any specific skills they may have" [1].

### Onboarding

"It's unfair to expect even the best people to hit the ground running on day one without being properly onboarded" [1]. Managers must invest early in setting expectations, showing the ropes, teaching the business, and providing clear direction.

### Earning Trust

New employees should not be given full autonomy immediately: "Make them prove themselves first by working more closely with them and frequently reviewing their work until they've earned your full trust" [1].

## Adapting Management Style to Individuals

Epstein's favorite management interview question is "How would you describe your management style?" and "the best possible answer is 'it depends on the employee'" [1]. Each person has a unique combination of experience, motivations, and personality. The assumption that everyone is self-motivated like the founder is a common mistake: "Get curious to figure out what drives and motivates each individual on your team, rather than assuming everyone's like you" [1].

## Decision-Making

### Including the Team

"If you've hired great people, you'd be crazy not to include them in important decisions" [1]. The key is defining the process upfront: will the decision be made by committee, delegated to someone, or made by the founder after gathering input? "Even if your team disagrees with the final call, by including them in the process they will better understand the decision, feel like their opinion was valued, and be able to get on board" [1].

### Showing Your Work

"Explain your decision-making process" [1]. When taking controversial actions or making difficult decisions, explaining the rationale earns buy-in and teaches the team how to make similar decisions.

### Admitting Uncertainty

"There's nothing wrong with saying you'll need some time to think or learn more before making an important decision" [1].

## Healthy Tension

"Differing perspectives help make teams and products better, so a little professional tension can be really valuable" [1]. The limit: tension must be net-positive. If it forces decisions to stall or shifts from professional to personal, "you need to take action to eliminate it quickly before it drags down the team or blows up" [1].

## One-on-Ones

"Have consistent 1-on-1s every week" [1]. These meetings are for the employee's benefit, so they should drive the agenda. The manager's role is to ask open-ended questions: "How are things going?", "How are you feeling?", "What's your opinion on X?" Prioritizing these meetings and refusing to cancel them "will send a signal to your team that you care about their happiness" [1].

## The Maker-to-Manager Transition

As organizations grow, the best individual contributors are promoted to managers. Epstein warns that "just because someone is an outstanding individual contributor doesn't mean they'll be an outstanding manager right off the bat, because it requires a completely different set of skills and experience" [1].

The common failure mode: new managers try to do both their old maker work and their new management duties, "spending 75% effort on making and 75% effort on managing -- a 150% workload resulting in less than 100% output in each area" [1]. The solution is to "put structure in place to allow them to focus on being a manager first and foremost, and take maker duties off of their plate" [1].

## Career Development

"Every 6 months, ask your employees where they want to be in their career in the next 2-3 years. Work with them to put together a plan, help them get the skills and experience they need, and guide them on a course to get there" [1].
