# Enterprise Sales

[[Ryan Junee]] reframes enterprise sales for technical founders: "being a great enterprise salesperson requires the same skill set as being a great hacker -- you just need to reframe how you think about sales." Where a computer system is a complex network of hardware and software, a large enterprise is a complex network of people and processes that can be understood and influenced in the same way.

This advice focuses on early-stage companies in the "wild west" stage -- those without an established repeatable sales process, selling $1M+ deals to Fortune 500 companies. Junee assumes approximate [[Product-Market Fit]].

## The Enterprise as a System to Hack

"Enterprise sales involves understanding and influencing the components (people) of the system in order to produce the desired behavior (buying your product.)" Every system (company) is different, requiring a systematic approach.

At mature companies, sales processes are scripted -- what Junee calls "[script kiddies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Script_kiddie)" rather than hackers. Early-stage startup sales is fundamentally different: you are exploring uncharted systems. "The more complex the system, the more satisfaction the hacker feels when he or she pwns it."

Enterprise systems are harder than computer systems for two reasons: the components (people) "don't always operate according to structured, rational rules" because they have emotions, motivations, hopes, and fears; and the feedback cycle is much slower, meaning "you need to be much more methodical in your approach -- brute force is generally not an option."

## Key Players in the Enterprise System

### Champion
Your ally inside the target company. "He or she will have deep familiarity with the pain your product solves, and will hope that your product is the solution. When you give your pitch, you will find the champion nodding along and completing your sentences for you."

Champions may become so excited they want to quit and join your company (Junee says this has happened to him multiple times). They act as if they are your employees -- "your guy/gal on the inside." Find them early and build strong relationships. Line-level champions are useful, but you also need director-level and above.

"These people are gold."

### Detractor
The opposite of a champion. "Maybe it threatens their job. Maybe they are already invested in a competing product. Maybe they built the solution that is currently being used." Strategy: identify them early, stay off their radar, and rally enough support from champions to overpower them.

### IT
Two types: risk-avoidant (perceives change as threatening) and forward-thinking (embraces change). The former is far more common. "It's usually a good strategy to avoid talking to IT" unless your product's users are in IT. IT meetings can lead you astray -- Junee was "burned by this in the past, building features that IT asked for in order to get a deal done only to discover when putting the product in front of actual users that it wasn't what they wanted."

Strategy: avoid IT until required for security reviews and approvals. By that time, the business units should be "pulling" you so strongly that IT cannot slow things down for too long.

### Procurement
Once a decision maker agrees to buy, procurement negotiates the deal. "Usually these folks are incentivized to get the lowest price possible." Strategy: "make sure you are prepared to 'give' them something -- once they extract their pound of flesh they will get out of the way." This is why much enterprise software has a "ridiculously high 'list price', but discounting by 80% or more is standard practice."

### Legal
Their weapon is exhaustion: "sending back endless redlines over clauses in the contract that in reality won't have much impact." Strategy: decide upfront what you really care about, give in on what does not matter, then have "a frank and authentic discussion" listing those things. Combine this with the champion pushing legal from the inside to hurry.

### Finance
May not interact directly, but important to understand their motivations: budget cycles (they may prefer to pay more upfront to use this quarter's budget), and capex vs. opex preferences (lump sum purchase vs. monthly license fees), which affects your pricing model and sales team compensation structure.

## Tactical Advice for Hacking the Enterprise

**Uncover the motivations of every player.** "Map out the people in your target company, draw a diagram." Spend significant time listening and learning before attempting to influence the system, like a hacker exploring how a system works before exploiting it.

**Build strong relationships with champions.** "When doing a large enterprise deal it will fall through before it closes, maybe more than once." You need champions who will revive deals. "You should be able to text them any time of night and ask for their help."

**Hire the right type of salespeople.** "Optimize for skills and raw characteristics over experience." The most common mistake is hiring for the wrong stage -- "hiring a 'coin operated' script kiddie salesperson out of a large company when you really need a hacker." Look for people "comfortable with uncertainty, who can learn quickly, and have strong intuitive and intellectual intelligence."

**Keep your map front and center.** Constantly re-evaluate the people, their motivations, and their interactions. "Position your product in the right way to the right people at the right time."

**Engage on many fronts.** Create pairs of relationships: your executives with their executives, your engineers with their IT and security, your product people with their business unit leaders. Always have one person (usually the account executive) coordinating all interactions.

**Instrument your process.** Measure your sales process as you would use "a packet sniffer or debugger when hacking."

**Companies are people.** "Decisions are made by people, and people don't always act rationally." Friendships lead to partnerships, and friendships transcend employers. "By nurturing these relationships they will pay off across multiple companies."
