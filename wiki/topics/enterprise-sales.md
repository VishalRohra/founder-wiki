# Enterprise Sales

Ryan Junee argues that enterprise sales requires the same skill set as hacking -- the founder just needs to reframe the problem. Where a computer system is a network of hardware and software, a large enterprise is a network of people and processes. The emergent behavior of a corporation results from incentives and structured rules among its constituent people, much like the output of a computer system results from structured rules and data flows [1].

Enterprise sales, then, involves understanding and influencing the components (people) of the system to produce the desired behavior (buying your product). Every system (company) is different, requiring a systematic approach [1].

This article focuses on early-stage companies selling to Fortune 500 companies in high-touch, complex sales with $1M+ deals -- often called "elephant hunting" [1]. It assumes the company has found [[Product-Market Fit]] or is close to it. For the contrasting approach of starting with smaller customers, see [[Selling to Startups]].

## The People in the System

Junee maps out the typical roles a founder will encounter inside a target enterprise:

### Champion

A champion is an ally within the target company who has deep familiarity with the pain the product solves. "When you give your pitch, you will find the champion nodding along and completing your sentences for you" [1]. Champions will do everything they can to get the product deployed. Their influence depends on seniority -- line-level champions are useful, but director-level and above are necessary. Champions may become so excited they want to quit and join the startup (Junee reports this happening multiple times). "These people are gold. Find them early and build strong relationships quickly" [1].

### Detractor

The opposite of a champion. Detractors oppose deployment for various reasons: it threatens their job, they are invested in a competing product, or they built the current solution. The strategy is to stay off their radar and rally enough support from champions and other parts of the organization to overpower them [1].

### IT

Two types exist: the risk-avoidant organization that perceives change as threatening, and the forward-thinking one that embraces change. The former is far more common at large enterprises. The general strategy is to avoid IT until required for security reviews and approvals. By that time, business units should be "pulling" so strongly that IT cannot slow things down [1].

Junee warns against building features that IT requests without validating with actual users: "I've been burned by this in the past, building features that IT have asked for in order to get a deal done only to discover when putting the product in front of actual users that it wasn't what they wanted" [1].

### Procurement

After a decision maker agrees to buy, procurement negotiates the deal. They are typically incentivized to get the lowest price. The strategy: "make sure you are prepared to 'give' them something -- once they extract their pound of flesh they will get out of the way" [1]. This dynamic is why much enterprise software has a high list price with 80% or more discounting as standard practice [1].

### Legal

Legal's weapon of choice is "exhaustion -- sending back endless redlines over clauses in the contract that in reality won't have much impact" [1]. The strategy: decide upfront what matters and what you will concede, then after a round or two of redlines, have a frank discussion listing your non-negotiables and giving in on the rest. Combined with pressure from your champion telling legal to hurry, this closes the negotiation [1].

### Finance

Finance thinks about budget cycles. They may prefer to pay more upfront to use a quarter's budget rather than spacing payments over time. Whether they prefer capex or opex affects pricing model and sales team structure. Understanding their preference for lump sum purchase vs. monthly license fees is important [1].

## Tactics for Hacking the Enterprise

### Map and Understand the System

Spend significant time listening and learning before attempting to influence. Map out the people in the target company, draw a diagram. "Just like a great hacker spends a lot of time poking around, exploring and understanding how a system works before attempting to influence it, you need to spend a lot of time really understanding the organization, motivations and interactions" [1].

### Build Champion Relationships

Large enterprise deals will fall through before they close, maybe more than once. Champions revive the deal and keep it moving. "You should be able to text them any time of night and ask for their help" [1].

### Hire the Right Salespeople

In early-stage companies, Junee optimizes for skills and raw characteristics over experience. The most common mistake is hiring the wrong fit for the stage -- a "coin-operated" salesperson from a large company when you need someone comfortable with uncertainty who can learn quickly and has the intelligence to understand the system well enough to influence it. The large-company salesperson is, to use the analogy, a [script kiddie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Script_kiddie) rather than a hacker [1].

### Engage on Multiple Fronts

Create pairs of relationships: executives with executives, engineers with IT and security, product people with business unit leaders. One person (usually the account executive) should oversee and coordinate all interactions [1].

### Instrument the Process

Measure the sales process like using a packet sniffer or debugger. Collecting data and generating reports is essential to improving the process over time [1].

### Remember That Companies Are People

"Decisions are made by people, and people don't always act rationally." Friendships lead to partnerships, and friendships transcend the employer. Nurturing relationships pays off across multiple companies [1].


## The Lighthouse Customer Problem (Harry Zhang / Lob)

[[Harry Zhang]] describes enterprise sales as a chicken-and-egg problem: "You need a lighthouse customer to get other big customers. Getting the first one is always the hardest" [2]. Once you succeed with one enterprise customer, take "that exact same story to a similar customer." By that point, the founder should understand "the language of the customer" [2].

Lob's first major enterprise customer came from a cold email. Zhang's team maps organizations before making contact: "Who are the key stakeholders? Decisions aren't made by just one individual" [2]. They combine cold outreach with conference attendance and marketing designed to make Lob visible everywhere the target company looks [2].

## Selling Ahead vs. Over-Selling

Zhang draws a careful distinction. The Booking.com lesson: Lob was transparent about not yet having European operations, negotiated a specific rollout timeline, and turned the deal into a partnership. "If the customer really wants to work with you, they're going to find a way to make it work" [2].
