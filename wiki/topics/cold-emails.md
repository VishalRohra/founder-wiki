# Cold Emails

[[Aaron Epstein]], YC Group Partner and founder of Creative Market, provides a systematic framework for writing cold emails that convert strangers into customers, partners, or collaborators. The good news: most cold emails are terrible, so the bar for standing out is low [1].

## Start with a Warm Intro

The single best email outreach hack: get a warm introduction. Conversion rates are two to three times higher than cold outreach. Turn over every rock: LinkedIn contacts, friends of friends, current and former coworkers, school alumni networks, employer alumni networks [1].

## Map Your Funnel

Before sending a single email, work backwards from your goal [1]:

Epstein provides a sample B2B funnel with illustrative conversion rates:
- **Goal**: 1 new customer
- **Demo-to-customer conversion**: 10% → need 10 demos
- **Response-to-demo conversion**: 25% → need 40 responses
- **Open-to-response conversion**: 10% → need 400 opens
- **Sent-to-open conversion**: 50% → need 800 emails sent

The math: 800 emails to get one customer. "If you're expecting to just send a handful of emails every day, every week, every month and see results, that's not going to cut it. You're going to need to send dozens of emails per day, possibly even 50 per day" [1].

Start manually and personalized. Do not automate until you have learned what works. The goal of any single email is not to close the sale -- it is to advance to the next step in the funnel [1].

Track conversion rates at each step to identify where to focus improvement. Conversion rates decrease as you scale, so fix poor rates before trying to scale [1].

## Increasing Open Rates

### Targeting Is the Highest-Leverage Activity

Better targeting beats better copywriting. "It's better to send 100 targeted emails that are going to have a much higher open rate rather than a thousand untargeted emails that are mostly going to be deleted" [1].

Epstein shares a cautionary example: he received an email pitching a shipping optimization service. His company, Creative Market, sold digital design assets downloaded from the internet. No amount of brilliant copywriting could have converted him -- the targeting was simply wrong [1].

### The From and Subject Lines

The "from" field should be a personal name, not a company name -- it feels like it comes from a human [1].

Subject lines should be short, relevant, and non-salesy: "Hey quick question," "Can I get your advice," "Help a fellow founder," "Hello from [company name]." It should feel like a friend could have written it [1].

### Channel Selection

Email is usually better than LinkedIn. LinkedIn can feel overwhelming for some recipients and special for others -- adapt to the industry. Text messaging is more personal but only appropriate with explicit permission [1].

### Finding Email Addresses

Ask mutual connections, check LinkedIn profiles and personal blogs/websites, use LinkedIn Sales Navigator, guess based on company email format (firstname.lastname@company.com), or use tools like Apollo.io, Hunter.io, and Clearbit [1].

## Seven Principles of Effective Email Copy

### 1. Have a Focused, Specific Goal

One email, one ask. Multiple asks create a paradox of choice: "If they have too many things they can do, or it's not clear which is important, they're just going to delete your email." Every word should drive toward the single goal. Delete anything that does not [1].

### 2. Be Human

Use emotional language: "I'd love to," "It would mean a lot," "Thanks -- you have no idea how much this helped." Be informal -- "hey" instead of "Hello Mr. Epstein." Typos are acceptable. No capitalization is fine. "Humans talk about emotion, and bots or LLMs are not good at this type of thing" [1].

The test: read the email out loud to your cofounder or yourself. If anything feels awkward to say to a person's face, rewrite it [1].

### 3. Personalize

Everyone can smell spam. The difference between "hi there" (generic) and "hey Aaron" (personal) is substantial. But personalization must be specific. "Hey, love what you're doing at Creative Market" feels like a placeholder variable. "I'm a huge Creative Market fan ever since you launched the photos category -- it's now my go-to resource" shows genuine research [1].

**Uncommon commonalities**: Find things uniquely in common. "We both went to the same college and had classes in the same buildings" -- instantly builds connection. "We're both men" -- weird and useless. Finding uncommon commonalities requires real research: look up the person on LinkedIn, use their product, understand their history [1].

### 4. Keep It Short

A wall of text is an instant delete. The email should be easy to read and respond to on a phone in under 60 seconds. If it requires more cognitive overhead, recipients will archive it and forget [1].

### 5. Establish Credibility

If in YC, mention it. If not, use former schools, notable companies, or impressive achievements. Share well-known customer names as social proof. Publish interesting industry data to position as an expert. Mention shared connections to create baseline trust [1].

### 6. Make It About the Reader, Not About You

Reframe every "I" as "you" and "yours." Tell your story as the quest to solve the reader's problems. This works especially well if you have felt the problem yourself: "I'm dedicating the next ten years of my life to solve this problem that I felt so deeply that I know you feel too" [1].

Use the language your users use to describe you, not your internal terminology. Ask users: "How would you describe my company?" Use their exact phrasing in outreach and on your website [1].

Do not make demands. "You're on their time. You're in their inbox. It's not their responsibility to get back to you" [1].

### 7. Have a Clear Call to Action

End with a concrete next step: "Reply to let me know," "Click here to get started," "Can you intro me to [name]?" Make it a standalone sentence in its own paragraph before the sign-off. Recipients scan to the end to figure out what is being asked -- make it unmistakable [1].

## Follow Up

One email is often not enough. Follow up two, three, four times manually. Give it a few days between emails. Get creative: Epstein once used the subject line "Free donuts," then delivered donuts to the recipient's office. If someone does not respond, do not get angry. "Nobody owes you anything here. Just move on. You can check back in in a couple months" [1].

## Examples of Bad Emails

Epstein critiques real emails he received [1]:

**Bad targeting**: A shipping optimization pitch sent to a digital asset company. "This could have been the most amazingly written, most personalized email, and nothing they could do would make me their customer."

**No personalization, no name, no sender**: "Hey there, hope you are doing great... do you partner with companies to make extra bucks?" No name used, nothing specific, unclear ask, unsigned, and includes a P.S. saying "if you don't want to hear from me anymore, just let me know" -- effectively admitting it is spam.

**All about the sender**: "My name is so-and-so... I'm a freelancer... I have been helping companies since 2014... I can help assist you..." Every sentence starts with "I." Nothing about the recipient.

## Examples of Good Emails

**The uncommon commonality**: Subject line "Go Terps" on LinkedIn. The sender references Van Munching Hall (a specific building at the University of Maryland), establishes credibility (ex-Googlers), makes a clear ask (review YC application), notes they have already had friends review it (not wasting time), and ends with a clear call to action. Epstein responded -- something he rarely does on LinkedIn [1].

**The templated-but-personal**: Epstein's own Creative Market seller recruitment email. Entirely templated except for the name and URL, but uses tricks to feel special: "special link," "super early," "I'd love your feedback," "handpicked sellers only," "welcome to the club." Multiple smiley faces. Written in conversational tone [1].

**The before-and-after**: A smoothie machine company's original email focused on their demo day goal. The rewritten version: "I'd love to throw a free smoothie party for the whole team at your office. It's like happy hour but smoothies. And free." A near no-brainer for the recipient [1].

## Final Advice

Founders should send these emails personally -- recipients take them more seriously from the CEO than from an account executive. Block off hours per day for this work. "You may be small, but that is your advantage because you can give your personalized attention to making these dedicated emails feel special to the recipient" [1].
