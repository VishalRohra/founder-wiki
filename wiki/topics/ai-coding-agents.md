# AI Coding Agents

AI coding agents -- tools like Cursor, Windsurf, Claude Code, and Replit Agent -- are reshaping how software is built. [[Tom Blomfield]], YC partner, estimates that in the current YC batch (2025), a third to a half of companies primarily write code using AI agents, up from 25% one batch earlier and approximately zero two batches before that [1]. The consensus among YC partners, [[Andrej Karpathy]], [[Michael Truell]] (Cursor CEO), and Amjad Masad (Replit CEO) is that software engineering jobs as they exist today will be fundamentally different within five to ten years [1] [2] [3] [4].

## The State of AI Coding in 2025

Blomfield describes his personal experience: he rebuilt his blog in 90 minutes on a train using Claude Code -- setting up hosting, writing new blogging software, and migrating 15 years of posts [1]. He then built recipes.ai, a 35,000-line application with an interactive voice agent, writing zero lines of code himself. After the first 5,000 lines, he stopped even reading the code -- "I'd prompt, auto-accept, go make a coffee, and come back and a new feature was built" [1].

Blomfield, a former professional software developer who had not written code in ten years, reports that AI tools made him "ten times more productive than I was when I was current in software engineering ten years ago" [1]. The question he raises: what happens when current software engineers, semi-technical people, and non-technical people all start using these tools?

## The Combine Harvester Analogy

Blomfield compared software engineers to "highly paid organic farmers, tending their crops by hand" and AI coding agents to the combine harvester [1]. The combine harvester increased food production massively while reducing the number of farmers per kilogram of food by orders of magnitude. He argues the effect will be more pronounced in software: demand for software will increase (potentially 10-100x), but the amount automatable by AI will increase even faster [1].

The counterargument -- Jevons paradox, where reduced cost increases demand enough to grow total employment -- is one Blomfield partially accepts but ultimately rejects for software: "I don't think it's even productivity per person because I think the person just goes to zero and you're dividing by zero" [1]. The future may involve on-demand custom software: "You come to ChatGPT with a problem and it spins up a little piece of code to help you solve the problem. It's like an ephemeral program with a user interface just designed for you and that problem. You solve your problem, it kind of goes away" [1].

## What Humans Still Bring: Taste and Obsession

Multiple speakers converge on taste and product obsession as the enduring human contribution:

Blomfield: "If you look at the best software that you use in your life, very likely there is a single human being behind that team that built that, and that person obsesses over making that product excellent for the user" [1]. Whether AI can develop that obsession is "a big stretch" given current methodologies [1].

[[Michael Truell]], who co-founded Cursor (Anysphere) at age 23, argues that taste is the differentiator in a world where code generation is cheap [2]. Cursor's hiring prioritized people with "exquisite taste in what good software feels like" -- the first 10 hires were chosen for their aesthetic judgment about user experience as much as their technical ability [2]. Truell sees a future where "programming as we know it" is replaced, but the creative act of imagining what to build and evaluating whether it is good becomes more important, not less [2].

Lieb (YC partner) adds that smaller teams will improve product quality because the ownership gaps between teams disappear: "If you look at bad design or bad experiences, a lot of it comes from interfaces between people or between teams where ownership isn't clear. One person or a very small group of people can really be the owners of a high quality experience" [1].

## Cursor: The Fastest-Growing Developer Tool

Cursor (by Anysphere) went from idea to one of the fastest-growing startups of all time. Truell describes the founding journey: the team initially worked on AI-powered CAD tools, then pivoted to reinventing how software is written after recognizing that code was the domain where LLMs could have the most leverage [2].

Key insights from Cursor's development:
- **Years of false starts**: The team went through multiple rewrites and product directions before the breakthrough moment when the models became good enough [2].
- **First 10 hires matter enormously**: Truell selected for taste and aesthetic judgment, not just coding ability [2].
- **The goal is beyond code**: Cursor's long-term vision is a world where programming languages are an intermediate representation, not the primary interface. The human specifies intent; the machine handles implementation [2].

## Replit: Democratizing Software Creation

Amjad Masad started Replit to make programming accessible to anyone. The platform has evolved from "teach a billion people to code" to "let anyone build software," recently surpassing $100M in ARR [4].

Masad's perspective on the future of work: "The dystopian view of AI taking all our jobs is not correct. The future of work is more human, more interactive, more multimodal. Once the making of things gets easier, the bottleneck goes back to how many ideas you can have" [4]. His advice: "I wouldn't put learning to code as the top thing. I would put learn to make things. Learn to make things with code. Learn to make things with video. Learn to make anything with AI" [4].

## Software 3.0

[[Andrej Karpathy]] places AI coding agents in the context of a broader paradigm shift he calls Software 3.0 [3]. In Software 1.0, humans write explicit code. In Software 2.0 (neural networks), humans provide data and the weights are learned. In Software 3.0, the programming interface is natural language. Prompts are the new programs.

This means the people closest to the problem -- domain experts, not software engineers -- can directly instruct computers. The technology diffusion pattern has reversed: LLMs reach everyday users before they reach expert developers, because natural language is universally accessible [3].

## Implications for Founders

The consensus across speakers -- Blomfield, Lieb, Truell, Masad, Karpathy -- is that now is "the best time in the history of humanity to build something from scratch" [1]. Small teams of 2-4 engineers can build what once took 40 [1]. Companies can reach profitability on a seed round without needing Series A or B [1]. Industries that were not big buyers of software -- law, education, medicine -- will be transformed in the next five years [1].

The skill that matters most for founders in this environment is identifying human problems to solve. "If you overindex and get really good at understanding people and seeing problems, figuring out how you talk to people to understand what their problems actually are, I think that skill relative to all the other skills needed to be a good founder is going to be the one that is more important in this future where it's pretty easy to build stuff" [1].
