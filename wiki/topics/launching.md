# Launching

Launching is one of the most over-thought activities in startup building. Kat Manalac, head of YC's Outreach team who has seen over 3,500 companies launch through YC, argues that founders should stop thinking about launching as a single moment in time and instead treat it as something done continually [3]. [[Michael Seibel]], [[Harj Taggar]], and [[Brad Flora]] reinforce this: the advice to launch early is "painfully obvious" and "YC [[Paul Graham]] 101," yet founders consistently resist it [4].

## Launch Now

The answer to "when should I launch?" is ASAP -- probably right now [3]. Founders are good at lying to themselves. Putting the product out there, even in its earliest state, helps determine whether the problem is big enough that someone is willing to pay or use the product [3].

### Worst Case Scenarios Are Survivable

If you launch too early [3]:
- People might think the product is ugly or bad
- Investors might hear about it before it is ready
- Competitors might see it
- No one will see it or care

All of these are survivable. Airbnb launched three times before it started to get real users [3].

### The Parallel Universe Problem

Brad Flora identifies the core psychological barrier: founders develop a parallel universe in their head of what the launch reaction should look like [4]. Seibel describes the image founders have: it is like the Oscar ceremony, with hordes of people treating you like a celebrity. The reality is that often no one cares [4].

Using the definite article -- "the launch" -- bakes in a fallacy that it is a singular event [4]. Launching is not precious. You launch over and over again [4].

## Big Launches Don't Work

Graham explicitly warns against relying on a "Big Launch" for growth [2]. There may even be an inverse correlation between launch magnitude and success -- the only launches he remembers are famous flops like the Segway and Google Wave [2].

All you need from a launch is some initial core of users. How well you are doing a few months later depends more on how happy you made those users than how many there were [2].

## Launch Continuously

Paul Buchheit, creator of Gmail, says it is better to make a few people really happy than to make a lot of people semi-happy [3]. With even 10 users who love you, all you have to do is expand that number [3].

Segment provides a case study in continuous launching. They did five launches on Hacker News in about a month, each time adding features users had requested. This iterative launching helped them find [[Product-Market Fit]] and eventually led to a $3.2 billion acquisition by Twilio [4].

## Types of Launches

Manalac identifies several types of launches [1] [3]:

1. **Silent launch**: Ship to a small group with no fanfare. Every founder should have at minimum: a domain name, company name, short description, contact method, and call to action [3].
2. **Friends and family launch**: Broader distribution within personal networks. But do not stay in this phase too long [3].
3. **Stranger launch**: First time in front of people with no personal connection to the founders. DoorDash's founders discovered their delivery insight from Chloe, a macaroon store manager in Palo Alto [3].
4. **Online community launch**: Hacker News, Product Hunt, relevant subreddits. Robinhood got 10,000 signups the first day and 50,000 the next week after being posted on HN on a Friday night [3].
5. **Press launch**: Only when the product is strong enough to survive scrutiny [1].
6. **Ongoing launches**: New features, major milestones, and pivots each deserve their own launch moment [1].

## The One-Sentence Pitch

Before any launch, you need a clear one-sentence description [3]. Lead with what, not with why. Start with the company name and what you do.

**Example**: Pave: "Pave lets companies plan, communicate, and benchmark your compensation in real time" [3].

**Anti-example**: "IndieCloud is a know-how and synergy platform." [[Garry Tan]]: "Meaningless jargon is the number one issue I spend time trying to fight when helping startups" [3].

## Why Founders Resist Launching Early

1. **Speed calibration.** If you have only ever worked at a big company, startup fast is another level [4].
2. **Perfectionism.** Magic launched two days after the idea with just a phone number: "Text us and we'll do whatever you like" [4].
3. **"I'm the exception" thinking.** Founders who believe their situation is different [4].

### The Exception: When Slow Launches Work

Rippling took close to two years to officially launch. But Parker Conrad had already built a billion-dollar company (Zenefits) doing the exact same thing [4]. "If you're not Parker Conrad -- you haven't already built a billion-dollar company doing this exact same thing -- then you should ignore all the advice about slow launches" [4].

## Press Is Not a Launch Strategy

For early-stage companies that have not raised $1M+, landing press stories is extremely difficult [3]. Even when it happens, press is not a silver bullet -- it is not a scalable way to get users and cannot be counted on for sustained growth [3].
