# Technical Co-Founders

[[Dalton Caldwell]] and [[Michael Seibel]] state that based on the thousands of companies YC has funded, "companies lacking a technical co-founder underperform" [1]. The video's title answers its own question with a single word: Yes.

## The Core Argument

Caldwell frames the key insight: "The best business founders recruit amazing technical co-founders, and that is how they distinguish themselves. That is the number one distinction" [1]. It is not what is on the resume, the prior executive role, or the quality of the idea. The distinguishing act is the recruiting [1].

Seibel draws two overlapping sets: "There's the set of people that are great business founders. And there's the set of people that can recruit amazing technical co-founders. You want to be in the intersection of those two sets" -- and "there's not many people" in that intersection [1].

## Why Founders Resist This Advice

Caldwell identifies the most common pattern: "I can't find this person, so I'm just going along" [1]. Business founders frame going ahead without a technical co-founder as entrepreneurial spirit -- "no barrier can prevent me from getting my product to market." They hire engineers, use dev shops, or build with no-code tools [1].

Caldwell's response: "One, the percentage chances of death just went through the roof. But two, I bet if you would put the same amount of effort you put into getting a product to market without a great technical founder into finding a great technical founder, at least half of people would be able to do it" [1].

## The Rocket Ship Analogy

Seibel offers a vivid comparison: "I'm going to build a rocket to the moon. But no one here knows anything about physics. I have such a big heart. I have such hustle. I'm going to the moon" [1]. He notes the absurdity is clearer in other domains: a five-foot-four person who cannot jump or run rarely claims they will make the NBA. "Whereas we see the equivalent of that in startups all the time" [1].

## The "Tech-Enabled" Objection

A common retort is: "We're not building a software business. Software is in there, but it's a tech-enabled marketplace / a simple DoorDash clone / we can use white-label software" [1].

Seibel acknowledges this is hard to win in theory. But the facts are clear: "Literally DoorDash or literally Airbnb, they lived and died by their speed of shipping software. And if what they did was buy white label software or have some dev shop in a foreign country making edits to the website, there's a zero percent chance those companies would have worked" [1].

## The Compounding Effect

Seibel draws from his personal experience at Justin.tv and Twitch. Even when competitors raised more money, had nicer offices, more employees, or cooler users, "having better engineering is the gift that gives you more shots every day. Even if you need to pivot, no matter what happens, good or bad, you're in a better position to react to these changes" [1].

The compounding works in the negative direction too. A team of business people assessing engineers will "naturally start by hiring worse engineers" -- and that quality cascades through every subsequent engineering hire [1].

## Even Technical Founders Benefit

Seibel was a "technical CEO" but still benefited enormously from having three additional technical co-founders. "Without my three other co-founders, none of that happens. Ideas are a dime a dozen" [1]. The advice is not only for non-technical founders.

## How to Find a Technical Co-Founder

### Stop Disqualifying People

Caldwell describes a common failure: "I obviously need a CTO with ten years of experience, who've managed teams of at least fifty to one hundred." He asks: "You're twenty-three years old with no years of experience. Why do you need that?" [1]. The founder imagines premature scale. In reality, a talented engineer who cares about the mission is far more valuable than a seasoned executive expecting a large team to manage [1].

### Change Your Network

Sometimes the right move is not to start the company yet. "Go get a job at a startup. Go change the people you know" [1]. Taking a sideways step to build the right network may be "the easiest and best path to starting the journey with the highest likelihood of success" [1].

### The Visualization Exercise

Caldwell recommends a thought experiment: "Visualize the best person you have ever worked with, either in school or in your job. The best engineer that everyone knows is the best person. Can you get that person to be your co-founder?" [1].

Founders immediately negotiate against themselves: "Oh, they would never. They're making lots of money." The response: "Have you talked to them?" Almost always, the answer is no [1].

### Pitch Adventure, Not Ideas

The biggest recruiting mistake: pitching the idea as a finished plan and offering the technical person the role of builder. "Here's my idea for a social network for dogs, will you build my website?" This is offering employment, not partnership [1].

The correct pitch: "Let's build a company together" [1]. Seibel notes: "You're offering adventure. You're offering the unknown." The best engineers do not have "six adventures on the table." Being pitched a genuine co-founding adventure is rare and compelling [1].

Seibel uses Justin Kan as an example. Kan recruited Seibel, Emmett Shear, and Kyle Vogt to co-found a startup where Kan wore a camera on his head, streaming his life 24/7, on a site named after himself, having raised only $50,000. "How did he build that team? He promised adventure, not that the idea was going to work" [1].

Ask the target engineer what startup ideas they have. Come together on the idea as co-owners. "It's shocking how often that appears to have never occurred to someone. They think finding a tech co-founder is to find someone who will basically submit to their whims" [1].
