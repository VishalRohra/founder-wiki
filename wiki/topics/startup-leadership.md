# Startup Leadership

Leadership in startups differs from leadership in established organizations because the role of the CEO changes dramatically as the company scales. [[Ali Rowghani]], drawing on his experience working closely with Ed Catmull, Steve Jobs, John Lasseter, and Bob Iger at Pixar, argues that leadership is measurable through three foundational traits and that a startup CEO's job shifts from building product to building a company. These two essays together form a comprehensive framework for understanding, measuring, and improving leadership in the startup context.

## Leadership Is Built on Trust

The central claim in Rowghani's framework is that all great leadership reduces to trust. Despite observing leaders with radically different styles (an introverted scientist, an extroverted artist, a college dropout infamous for brash behavior, and a career executive who was "exceptionally genteel and diplomatic"), Rowghani found they all shared the ability to "create an extraordinary amount of trust in the people around them" [1].

Trust is both a science and an art [1]:
- **The science**: Trust accrues when a leader's predictions about products, investments, and competitive landscapes prove accurate, and when their people decisions prove correct. Over time, the track record becomes known.
- **The art**: Trust is built when leaders "say the right thing at the right time, and show empathy and good judgment." It grows when leaders "stand for ideals bigger than yourself rather than caring primarily about your personal success, wealth, fame, or position."

This is why leadership styles cannot be copied: "You can't try to copy Steve Jobs or Ed Catmull in your quest to be a great leader. You can only be yourself" [1].

## The Three Foundational Traits

See [[Three Traits of Great Leadership]] for the full framework. In summary:

### 1. Clarity of Thought and Communication

Great leaders think clearly and describe a compelling vision [1]. Rowghani cites Jeff Bezos's retail strategy as an exemplary case: it was built on three enduring customer preferences (lower prices, bigger selection, faster delivery). As Bezos said: "You can build a business strategy around the things that are stable in time....when you have something that you know is true, even over the long-term, you can afford to put a lot of energy into it" [1].

Clarity of thought always precedes clarity of language. The practical advice: "spend more time thinking about what you believe is truly important for your business. Once you've crystallized what's important for everyone to understand, practice expressing it in simple terms" [1].

As companies scale, preparation becomes critical. Tobi Lütke of Shopify and his senior team spend hundreds of hours preparing for their annual employee Summit. As Lütke describes it: "We want to be a loosely coupled, highly aligned company. The Summit is the main enabler of this because it is a grand sync. We spend countless hours preparing because if we communicate well at the Summit, we achieve great alignment by the end" [1].

### 2. Judgment About People

Great leaders have strong intuition about who to give power and responsibility to. They "are able to see hidden potential in people and detect cases where ambition exceeds ability" [1]. When hiring or promotion mistakes happen, they have the courage to act.

The stakes are high: "Nothing does more damage to an organization or to the standing of a leader than picking the wrong leaders or failing to correct these mistakes when they happen" [1]. However, "leaders who fire too many of their own also lose a lot of credibility and trust" [1].

To improve people judgment, Rowghani recommends gathering more data: meet the best people in every field to sharpen pattern recognition. He cites the example of Uber CTO Thuan Pham being interviewed by Travis Kalanick for "30 hours straight, one-on-one, over two weeks" including over Skype while traveling. Pham recalled: "Throughout those 30 hours, I actually forgot it was an interview. It was just like a discussion between two colleagues" [1].

Reference checks are essential, particularly for behavioral evidence of good judgment and integrity, which are "hard to test in an interview" [1].

### 3. Personal Integrity and Commitment

Integrity means "standing for something meaningful beyond oneself rather than being motivated by narrow personal interests" [1]. It includes admitting mistakes, receiving critical feedback with humility, and avoiding behaviors that erode trust (favoritism, conflicts of interest, inappropriate relationships).

Rowghani proposes a demanding test: "if your team had full transparency into your private communications and behavior towards employees, would you be embarrassed by anything you have done or said? This is a high bar, but one that great leaders strive to meet" [1].

Commitment goes beyond working long hours. Great leaders "make their work into their core life mission in ways that inspire others" [1].

## Measuring Leadership

Rowghani argues that measuring leadership through bottom-line company performance fails because "timing and luck can be confounding factors" and because performance metrics offer "no clues as to how someone can improve as a leader" [1].

Instead, he proposes systematic employee surveys organized around the three traits. The full survey framework appears in [[Three Traits of Great Leadership]]. Key elements include asking both the CEO and employees parallel questions about clarity of mission, quality of people decisions, and integrity of leadership behavior [1].

He recommends startups begin gathering this data systematically at approximately 50 people [1].

## The CEO's Evolving Role

The CEO's job changes dramatically across three phases of company growth. See [[Startup Leadership]] for the complete framework.

### Phase 1: Doer-in-Chief

The CEO's first job is to "build a great product and find a small group of people who love it and use it enthusiastically" [2]. In this phase, the CEO must be deeply involved in building the product, acquiring users, and conserving money. "Delegation should not be a word in your vocabulary" [2].

### Phase 2: Company-Builder-in-Chief (20-500 employees)

When demand exceeds the small team's capacity, the CEO must shift to company-building [2]. This is the critical transition that determines the startup's long-term success. In practice, Phase 2 begins around 20-25 employees and ends around 400-500 [2].

The transition is psychologically difficult: "When you've been a successful Doer-in-Chief, it's hard to stop. It's hard to stop coding, designing product specs, and interacting with customers on a daily basis" [2]. It can also create confusion with the team, who may wonder why the CEO is suddenly delegating tasks they had always done [2].

The key insight: "once your startup reaches 20-30 people, you'll have to spend more time leading (i.e., directing the activities of others). And since time is finite, the only way to lead more is do less" [2].

David Rusenko, co-founder of Weebly, illustrates successful delegation: "Often, the first time I find out about a product feature is reading about it on our blog. It shocks most founders to hear this, but I know I've done my job well because I've yet to see a feature that was built poorly. You should aspire to build a team that's so good that you don't have to be involved in the product details" [2].

### Phase 3: Investment in Transformation

At the end of Phase 2, the leadership team operates at a high level with minimal CEO involvement. The CEO can then shift to taking profits from the core business and investing in transformative new products [2]. Facebook exemplifies this: it built its senior management team in Phase 2 while operating at roughly breakeven, then used Phase 3 profits to fund Messenger as a separate product and acquire Instagram, WhatsApp, and Oculus [2].

## Three Non-Delegable CEO Tasks

Rowghani identifies three tasks that only the CEO can perform. See [[Startup Leadership]] for full detail.

### 1. Hiring and Managing the Leadership Team

"Only the CEO can hire the company's senior leadership team and make sure that they work well together" [2]. The hiring decisions must be the CEO's because "the consequences are yours as well" [2]. Patrick Collison made it a point to meet with the "best-in-the-world" in each field to calibrate what great looks like [2].

Key metrics for this task:
- Expect at least 25% of leadership hires not to work out [2]
- Most CEOs take too long to fire their first executive [2]
- "The longer you leave an under-performing executive in place, the more credibility you lose with everyone else on your team" [2]
- Do not be surprised if 50% of time goes to hiring and managing the senior team [2]

### 2. Creating Purpose and Alignment (Mission-to-Metrics)

See [[Mission-to-Metrics Framework]]. The CEO defines the Mission (purpose), Strategy (direction), and Metrics (pace and performance) [2]. Rowghani illustrates perfect alignment with the SpaceX employee who, when asked "What is your job?", traced his specific task (designing a steering system) back through the company's strategy (reusable rockets) to the mission (colonize Mars) [2].

Key principles:
- Write your strategy in long form to catch flaws in thinking [2]
- Do not equate metrics with top-line results like revenue growth. Instead, find the drivers. Facebook discovered that connecting a new user to 10 friends within 14 days correlated with retained usage, so they set that as their key product metric [2]
- Communicate Mission-to-Metrics constantly. "You have to reiterate the Mission-to-Metrics much more than what feels reasonable" [2]

### 3. Nurturing Company Culture

Unlike the other two tasks, culture is not solely the CEO's job; it is everyone's responsibility [2]. The CEO should not write cultural values alone. Instead, "encourage co-founders and early employees to work together to codify a set of values and behavioral norms that feel authentic and aspirational to everyone" [2].

Rowghani tells the story of Toy Story 2 at Pixar as the defining cultural moment. With seven months to release, creative leadership felt the film was not working. Disney argued it was too late to start over. Pixar refused, tearing up the story and rewriting from scratch, pushing the studio "to the brink of collapse" to complete the new version on time [2]. The film was a success, and afterward, the executive team closed the studio for two months to let everyone recuperate.

Ed Catmull called it "the most intense and important period in the studio's history: 'Toy Story 2 defined us. It said that we couldn't be a studio that produced both great work and average work. Everything we made had to be great'" [2].

## The Measure of Success

Rowghani cites Steve Jobs at a Pixar management meeting: "When I'm at my best, 50% of my time is unscheduled. That's the time I use to think, drop in on the people I want to speak with, and let my curiosity roam. It's my time to be creative. Without this free time, I would never be able to stay ahead of the company. To lead a company, you've always got to be two steps ahead. There's no way to lead a company from behind" [2].

Having significant unscheduled time is "perhaps the clearest sign of success for a Phase 2 CEO" because it signals successful delegation, a strong leadership team, and well-codified Mission-to-Metrics [2].
