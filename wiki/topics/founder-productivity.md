# Founder Productivity

[[Dalton Caldwell]] and [[Michael Seibel]] address founder productivity across two complementary perspectives: how successful founders organize their time and get work done [1], and how they maintain the physical and psychological infrastructure to sustain performance over years [2]. Both draw on patterns observed across hundreds of YC companies.

## Maker Schedule vs. Manager Schedule

The framework comes from [[Paul Graham]]'s essay "Maker's Schedule, Manager's Schedule" [1]. The core insight: programmers (makers) and managers organize time differently, and most companies are set up around the manager schedule -- a day packed with meetings. This is destructive for makers [1].

### The Maker's Perspective

Caldwell, a developer-founder, explains: when programming, the more of the program you can keep in your head at any one time, the easier it is to make changes and fix bugs. Loading context into "RAM" takes one to two hours. If interrupted, you must restart your state every time [1]. A great maker schedule is an eight-hour uninterrupted block. Programming in hour increments is disastrous [1].

[[Emmett Shear]] explained it to Seibel by saying: "Imagine that I'm doing ridiculously complicated word problems and you're interrupting me in the middle of them." Once Seibel heard that, it clicked -- writing email in twenty-minute increments is fine, but deep technical work cannot be sliced that way [1].

Caldwell recalls that at his startup, nothing productive happened before lunch. Email and meetings consumed the morning. The entire trick was making post-lunch time free of everything [1].

### Maker Mode Applies to Sales Too

The maker schedule concept applies to sales and customer work, not just programming [1]. Scheduling eight-hour blocks for sales, rather than squeezing it into slivers between other things, produces dramatically better results. "It's really hard to get anything at a high level done in twenty-minute blocks" [1].

## Productivity Practices

### To-Do Lists Over Notebooks

Seibel argues that a well-managed to-do list using software is essential [1]. "When founders write something down in a notebook, it's gone forever. But they look cool with their fountain pen." Any of the many software products works -- the important thing is adopting one [1].

### To-Do List Before Inbox

When in manager mode, to-do list comes first [1]. If work is inbox-driven, other people control your time. "Horrible, horrible" [1].

### Write Everything Down

The worst thing is having another meeting because no one wrote down the decisions from the first meeting [1]. Even if you think you don't need it, write down the agenda and decisions [1].

### Know Your Numbers

Seibel had his analytics dashboard up on screen at all times and could memorize key KPIs [1]. "I'm obsessed with the internal key KPIs for anything I'm working on. I'm like an addict to look at that stuff." Founders who know their stats well talk about them differently -- they don't round off numbers, and they know whether they're up or down 10% at any given time [1].

Caldwell contrasts this with founders who say "Yeah, I think it was an up week" but cannot say whether revenue went up or down. "What are you doing?" [1]

## Social Media and Information Diet

Social media is "the black hole for time" [1] [2]. Caldwell uses the metaphor of an information diet: just as what you eat affects your mood, the information you consume affects your state of mind [2]. Doomscrolling pandemic or war news, following investors who talk constantly about funding markets -- these degrade founder performance without providing useful information [2].

Successful founders explicitly block social media: grayscaling phones, turning off notifications, aggressively using screen time tracking [2]. Seibel unfollowed everyone on Twitter, installed a Chrome extension that disables most of Twitter's features, uninstalled Facebook years ago, and does not have the Twitter app on his phone [1].

"If you don't aggressively protect your time, the world is going to steal it from you" [1]. Effective people use both organizing tools and protection tools. The belief that "successful people just have stronger willpower" is false -- they reach for tools to manage their weaknesses [1].

## Things Great Founders Avoid

### Collecting Credentials Instead of Building

Accumulating mentors, advisors, accelerators, and idea labs creates a "bottomless pit of time suck" [1]. Caldwell compares it to going to Hollywood and taking acting lessons for years while never auditioning. "It feels like you're making progress, but you're not" [1].

The things that de-risk a startup: talking to customers, launching a product. "God forbid you're doing anything more than those things" [1].

### Hedging Bets

Keeping graduate school open, holding a Google job offer, exploring finance, and talking to three friends about a startup -- founders want to move all these pieces down the board simultaneously [1]. "If there's a team that's exactly as competent as you and they're not hedging, they're always going to win" [1].

## Lifestyle Infrastructure

### The Adulting Requirement

If you want to be a founder of a very successful company, you may have to "adult harder than the people around you" [2]. This includes sleep, diet, exercise, and living environment. Seibel frames the home as a potential source of strength or weakness: "At minimum, your home should be neutral. For the most successful people, the home is actually a plus" [2].

From a clinical perspective, Caldwell notes that when someone is struggling, the first things to examine are sleep disruption, diet, and exercise. "If you don't have your house in order on those fronts, you are not well-situated to deal with really hard things" [2].

### The Debt Metaphor

Founders can sacrifice healthy habits for five to seven years -- the human body allows this. But building a company is a multi-decade endeavor, and "the stuff you can get away with in your twenties, you can't get away with in your thirties" [2]. Caldwell frames it as going into "personal health debt" that eventually comes due as a breakdown or meltdown [2].

### Founder Salary

Seibel argues that small, incremental salary increases (5-10%) can make a huge lifestyle difference and are almost always worth it [2]. The philosophy: "salary is for living, equity is for upside" [2]. If startup salary allows you to save money, it is probably too high. If it creates a lifestyle that allows you to be all-in on the startup because the real world is not distracting you negatively, it is correct [2].

### Co-Founder Conflict Resolution

Seibel identifies the anxious-vs-avoidant personality dynamic as the root cause of most co-founder problems [2]. The actual thing they are debating is rarely the issue -- it is the style of dealing with frustration. If co-founders can figure out this dance, it is "so much more helpful when dealing with external challenges" [2].

### Social Circle Selection

When taking punches, the friends you choose to spend time with matters. "The best people, stuff goes wrong and they go take a hike. The worst people, stuff goes wrong and they drink and do drugs" [2]. Therapy and medication are destigmatized tools that many successful founders use, though it rarely appears in press releases [2].

## When to Take No Action

Caldwell's most counterintuitive advice: when something bad happens, the instinct is to immediately react -- call a meeting, fire off an email. But "sleeping on it is the minimum thing I would do" because the next day brings clarity [2].

The best founders use "no action" as a key play 20-40% of the time. Other founders do not even know the play exists [2]. The exception: when someone's health is on the line or legal consequences are imminent [2].
