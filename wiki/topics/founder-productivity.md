# Founder Productivity

YC Group Partners collectively argue that founder productivity is not about tools, systems, or optimization hacks. It is about choosing the right thing to work on and then doing it without distraction. The most productive founders they know are not deep in productivity systems -- they are simply working on their startup because they love working on it [1]. [[Dalton Caldwell]] and [[Michael Seibel]] extend this with a deep dive on the maker/manager schedule distinction and the psychological traps that consume founder time [2].

## Priority, Not Productivity

"When we say productivity, often what we're actually saying is priority" [1]. Aaron Epstein frames the foundational questions: What is my goal? What is the number one thing I am trying to do? Are the things I am doing actually aligned with that? These "stupid simple questions" yield 80% of the benefit [1].

Epstein's method: identify your main KPI (usually revenue), determine why it is not growing faster, figure out which activities would make it grow faster, then audit your calendar to ensure those activities occupy the bulk of your time [1].

[[Sam Altman]]'s 2018 *Productivity* essay states the same argument as a direction-vs-velocity claim and puts it first: "It doesn't matter how fast you move if it's in a worthless direction. Picking the right thing to work on is the most important element of productivity and usually almost ignored" [4]. The closing line of the essay is the same inverted: "Productivity in the wrong direction isn't worth anything at all. Think more about what to work on" [4]. Everything downstream of this -- lists, focus, sleep -- is secondary. And the timescale correction: "The right goal is to allocate your year optimally, not your day" [4].

## Maker Schedule vs. Manager Schedule

[[Paul Graham]]'s essay on the maker/manager schedule is one of the few genuinely valuable productivity concepts cited by multiple YC partners [1] [2]. The idea: there are two incompatible time systems.

**Manager schedule** fills a day with sequential meetings. Each meeting can be short. Context-switching between meetings is manageable because the tasks are social and communicative [2].

**Maker schedule** requires long, uninterrupted blocks. Caldwell, who was a developer at his startup, explains: "When you're programming, the more of the program you can keep in your head at any one time, the easier it is. It takes like an hour or two cold of looking at a program for it to get loaded into RAM. And so if you're interrupted, if you have to program in hour increments, man, are you gonna suck?" [2].

A great maker block is eight hours of uninterrupted work. The same principle applies to writing, music, design, and art [2].

Seibel, who was the business co-founder, describes realizing this only after his technical co-founder Emmett Shear explained: "Imagine that I'm doing ridiculously complicated word problems and you're interrupting me in the middle of them." After that, "it clicked" [2].

At Justin.tv, the team discovered that their real productive work began after 5 or 6 PM, when the building quieted and interruptions stopped. They restructured around this: nothing productive before lunch, and the post-lunch block was protected from meetings [2].

### Maker Schedule Applies Beyond Code

Caldwell extends the maker concept to sales and customer conversations. "Scheduling twenty-minute blocks to talk to customers is not the same as an eight-hour block." If a co-founder's primary job is sales, it should be scheduled in long, focused blocks [2].

A practical solution from a technical founder: mornings on a manager schedule with meetings, then late afternoon and evening on a maker schedule with zero meetings, yielding six hours of uninterrupted coding time [1].

## Fake Work

Fake work is one of the most dangerous traps for founders. Unlike watching Netflix, which is obviously unproductive, fake work "doesn't feel like fun. It feels like you're working, but actually you don't make any progress" [1].

Founders fall into fake work when they lose sight of the goal they are trying to achieve with the work they are doing. Common manifestations: building elaborate Notion architectures pre-[[Product-Market Fit]], attending conferences without a clear customer acquisition goal, spending hours on social media, and endlessly tweaking internal processes rather than talking to customers [1].

One Group Partner describes pre-PMF Notion documents as "the equivalent of cleaning your room because you don't want to do your homework" [1].

## Productivity Porn

Productivity porn is a subset of fake work: articles, podcasts, and systems about how to be more productive. "When you're reading them, you feel productive because you are reading these great tips about how to be productive" [1].

The reality: "Most of the successful founders that I know are not really into productivity porn. They're not trying to maximize their lifestyle in crazy extreme ways. The typical successful founder is just really focused on their startup and they just sort of work on the startup all the time because they love working on it" [1].

Altman names the same pattern in 2018 (predating YC's "productivity porn" framing by roughly four years): "Don't fall into the trap of productivity porn -- chasing productivity for its own sake isn't helpful. Many people spend too much time thinking about how to perfectly optimize their system, and not nearly enough asking if they're working on the right problems. It doesn't matter what system you use or if you squeeze out every second if you're working on the wrong thing" [4]. The structural warning is identical to the YC partners' version — the specific optimization system does not matter; the direction does.

## Time Traps

### Social Media

Caldwell identifies social media as the primary time sink: "It's the black hole for time" [2]. The trap is that checking Twitter or Discord feels like staying informed but produces no output [2].

Seibel describes his own intervention: he unfollowed everyone on Twitter and installed a Chrome extension that disabled most of Twitter's features. He uninstalled Facebook years ago and keeps no social media apps on his phone. "People that actually are super productive do abnormal things to turn all this crap off" [2].

The exception: when social media is where the customers are. For open source projects, trending on GitHub or Hacker News may be the right go-to-market [1].

### Collecting Mentors and Credentials

Caldwell raises the question: "Three different accelerators and an idea lab. An advisory board. Why do you think founders are attracted to that versus just building something and launching it?" Seibel answers: "It smells like you're doing successful startup stuff." But it is a bottomless pit. "You could be in that bottomless pit for years and be a startup founder that's never built a product and has never gotten a single customer" [2]. Caldwell adds an analogy: "It's like going to Hollywood and getting acting lessons. You're in acting lessons mode for years, and you're like, 'Yeah, I'm an actor,' but you're not" [2].

### Hedging

Many aspiring founders try to keep graduate school, a FAANG job offer, and a startup going simultaneously. Seibel's response: "I don't think you can be great at those four things at the same time." Caldwell adds the competitive argument: "If there's a team that's exactly as competent as you and they're not hedging, they're always going to win" [2].

## Stack Ranking

A Group Partner learned from an early board member to stack rank every project. When you have ten projects for the quarter, force yourself to rank them one through ten. "You probably will only do the top three" [1]. Steve Jobs expressed the same idea: "Focus is saying no" [1]. [[Sam Altman]]'s 2023 version is more operational: "Concentrate your resources on a small number of high-conviction bets... You can delete more stuff than you think" [3]. The "you can delete more than you think" clause is the key -- founders systematically underestimate how much roadmap, calendar, team structure, and product surface can be removed without cost, and the gap between what is actually deletable and what the founder believes is deletable is where [[Founder Productivity|fake work]] accumulates.

## Manager Mode Productivity

Caldwell and Seibel each contribute manager mode practices [2]:

1. **To-do list first.** Caldwell insists on starting with the to-do list, not the inbox. If work is inbox-driven, "other people are in control of your time."
2. **Write everything down.** Both emphasize this. "The worst thing is when you have to have another meeting because people didn't write the sh*t down from the first meeting."
3. **Know your metrics cold.** Seibel had his analytics dashboard visible at all times and memorized key internal stats. Caldwell observes: "People that know their stats well talk about their stats so differently. They don't round off numbers to the nearest zero" [2].

## Two Sets of Tools

Seibel identifies two categories of productivity tools [2]:

1. **Tools that organize your time** -- task managers, calendar systems, meeting agendas, analytics dashboards
2. **Tools that protect your time** -- app blockers, unfollowing, notification disabling, saying no to meetings

"Effective people use both constantly. Ineffective people sometimes think, 'If I were just stronger-willed, I wouldn't need tools.' That's not true" [2].

## One Goal

During the YC batch, founders work toward a single demo day goal -- one number. "Picking one helps you focus. You need that focus on one thing and only one thing" [1].

## Deciding What Not to Do

"The most important mental model for time management is not what you decide to do. It's what you decide to not do" [1]. The most productive people are aggressive about saying no. "Being very judicious about all of the things you say no to is actually the best hack" [1].

## YC as Productivity Design

Graham designed YC to maximize maker time [2]. There are few events -- no all-day classes. There is a hard deadline (Demo Day). A large portion of the program is simply asking founders what they will accomplish by Demo Day and then asking each week whether they did it. "It turns out that there's a lot of magic in that" [2]. [[Sam Altman]] formalizes this cadence as a general rule: "Plans should be measured in decades, execution should be measured in weeks" [3]. The order-of-magnitude gap between vision horizon and accountability horizon is what makes fast iteration actually protective rather than chaotic -- "fast iteration can make up for a lot; it's usually ok to be wrong if you iterate quickly" [3].

## Lists, Meeting Length, and Saying No (Sam Altman)

Altman's 2018 essay distills his prioritization system into three pillars: "Make sure to get the important shit done. Don't waste time on stupid shit. Make a lot of lists" [4]. The operational details he commits to:

- **Paper lists, re-transcribed.** Year, month, and day scopes. Re-transcription forces periodic re-evaluation of what stays on the list. "I don't bother with categorization or trying to size tasks or anything like that (the most I do is put a star next to really important items)" [4].
- **Start and end each day with progress.** Momentum-generating sequencing -- "the more I get done, the better I feel, and then the more I get done."
- **Ruthless no.** "I try to be ruthless about saying no to stuff, and doing non-critical things in the quickest way possible. I probably take this too far -- for example, I am almost sure I am terse to the point of rudeness when replying to emails" [4].
- **Keep 10% open for chance.** "Though probably 90% of the random meetings I take are a waste of time, the other 10% really make up for it" [4].

A specific heuristic with operational teeth: "Most meetings are best scheduled for 15-20 minutes, or 2 hours. The default of 1 hour is usually wrong, and leads to a lot of wasted time" [4]. The mornings are protected: "The first few hours of the morning are definitely my most productive time of the day, so I don't let anyone schedule anything then. I try to do meetings in the afternoon" [4].

A money-vs-time insight for the founder-who-still-pennywise-pound-foolishes: "I don't think most people value their time enough -- I am surprised by the number of people I know who make $100 an hour and yet will spend a couple of hours doing something they don't want to do to save $20" [4].

## Focus as Force Multiplier (How to Be Successful)

Altman's 2019 sixth principle: "Focus is a force multiplier on work ... Almost everyone I've ever met would be well-served by spending more time thinking about what to focus on. It is much more important to work on the right thing than it is to work many hours" [5]. Paired with an implicit speed requirement: "Once you have figured out what to do, be unstoppable about getting your small handful of priorities accomplished quickly. I have yet to meet a slow-moving person who is very successful" [5]. The pairing sharpens the direction-vs-velocity claim -- direction matters most, *then* speed-on-the-chosen-direction matters more than most founders admit.

The work-ethic complement: "You can get to about the 90th percentile in your field by working either smart or hard, which is still a great accomplishment. But getting to the 99th percentile requires both -- you will be competing with other very talented people who will have great ideas and be willing to work a lot" [5]. The sequencing advice for compounding: "Do it at the beginning of your career. Hard work compounds like interest, and the earlier you do it, the more time you have for the benefits to pay off" [5].

## Compounding Applied to Careers

Altman's 2018 productivity frame and 2019 success frame converge on compounding. From *Productivity*: "Compound growth gets discussed as a financial concept, but it works in careers as well, and it is magic. A small productivity gain, compounded over 50 years, is worth a lot. So it's worth figuring out how to optimize productivity. If you get 10% more done and 1% better every day compared to someone else, the compounded difference is massive" [4]. From *How to Be Successful*: "You don't want to be in a career where people who have been doing it for two years can be as effective as people who have been doing it for twenty -- your rate of learning should always be high. As your career progresses, each unit of work you do should generate more and more results. There are many ways to get this leverage, such as capital, technology, brand, network effects, and managing people" [5].

The career-level corollary to the startup-level compounding-with-scale framework in [[Sam Altman]]'s [[Key Frameworks]] — what scales the business is the same category of thing that scales a career, and founders systematically underuse both.

## Physical Factors (Sam Altman)

Altman's 2018 *Productivity* essay is unusual in the YC canon for giving physical habits equal billing with prioritization. "It probably took a little bit of my time every week for a few years to arrive at what works best for me, but my sense is if I do a good job at all the below I'm at least 1.5x more productive than if not" [4]. The order of importance he states:

1. **Sleep.** Top input. Cold, dark, quiet room; great mattress; no late eating; minimal alcohol. A [Chili Pad](https://www.chilitechnology.com) for temperature; an [eye mask](https://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B00GSO1D9O/) and [ear plugs](https://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B001J4HB1C) when traveling. Low-dose sleeping aids or cannabis when necessary (tradeoffs acknowledged). A full-spectrum LED light for 10-15 morning minutes: "If you try nothing else in here, this is the thing I'd try. It's a ridiculous gain for me" [4].
2. **Exercise.** Heavy weights 3x/week, occasional HIIT. "In addition to productivity gains, this is also the exercise program that makes me feel the best overall" [4].
3. **Nutrition.** ~15 hours of fasting most days (no breakfast, espresso on wake); avoid sugar and inflammation-provoking foods; espresso morning and after lunch (~200mg caffeine total); vegetarian with supplements calibrated by quarterly blood tests (services like [WellnessFX](https://www.wellnessfx.com)).

Meta-point: "Very likely what is optimal for me won't be optimal for you. You'll have to experiment to find out what works best for your body" [4]. The invitation is not to copy the stack; it is to take the feedback-loop question seriously.

## Don't Trade Relationships for Output

Altman's 2018 closing discipline: "Don't neglect your family and friends for the sake of productivity -- that's a very stupid tradeoff (and very likely a net productivity loss, because you'll be less happy). Don't neglect doing things you love or that clear your head either" [4]. The framing is deliberately instrumental — he is not making a work-life-balance argument but a productivity-math argument. Isolation and under-recovery compound badly.

On overcommitment: "I think it's good to overcommit a little bit. I find that I generally get done what I take on, and if I have a little bit too much to do it makes me more efficient at everything, which is a way to train to avoid distractions (a great habit to build!). However, overcommitting a lot is disastrous" [4].
