# Equity Split

Equity split refers to how ownership of a startup is divided among co-founders and early contributors. It is one of the most consequential early decisions a startup makes and one of the most common sources of [[Co-Founders|co-founder]] conflict.

## The Default: Equal Splits

Y Combinator's general guidance favors equal or near-equal splits among co-founders. [[Michael Seibel]] presents the case for this position in detail, drawing on his own experience across multiple startups [3].

### Why Founders Get It Wrong

Seibel identifies common reasons founders cite for unequal equity splits, and argues that all of them fail the same test [3]:

- "I came up with the idea for the company"
- "I started working n months before my co-founder"
- "My co-founder took a salary for n months and I didn't"
- "I am older/more experienced than my co-founder"
- "I brought on my co-founder after raising n thousands of dollars"
- "I brought on my co-founder after launching my MVP"
- "We need someone to tie-break in case of founder arguments"

All of these reasons over-weight early contributions relative to future work. Seibel's rebuttal rests on four points [3]:

1. **It takes 7 to 10 years to build a company of great value.** Small variations in year one do not justify massively different founder equity splits in years two through ten [3].

2. **More equity equals more motivation.** Almost all startups fail. The more motivated the founders, the higher the chance of success. Getting a larger piece of the equity pie is worth nothing if the lack of motivation on the founding team leads to failure [3].

3. **If you don't value your co-founders, neither will anyone else.** Investors look at founder equity split as a signal for how the CEO values the co-founders. Giving a co-founder 10% or 1% communicates that they are not important. "Why communicate to investors that you have a team that you don't highly value?" [3].

4. **Startups are about execution, not ideas.** Dramatically unequal splits often give undue preference to the co-founder who initially came up with the idea, as opposed to the team that got the product to market and generated initial traction [3].

### The Motivation Framework

Seibel frames equity split primarily as a tool for long-term motivation, not as compensation for past work [3]. As CEO, the first thought should not be "how do I negotiate the best deal?" but "how do I come up with an equity split that's going to maximize the motivation of my teammates?" [3].

The equity stake should be the thing that "gets them to wake up in the middle of the night, gets them to work on the weekends, gets them to work late, gets them to recruit their friends. It gets them to feel like they are true owners of the company and not just employees" [3].

Seibel notes that while he has historically recommended equal splits as a simple rule of thumb, the rule cannot be applied universally. The guiding question remains: "If you're not really interested in the future motivation of your co-founders, if you don't think you're gonna need them in the long term, why are you making them co-founders at all?" [3].

## Vesting as the Safety Mechanism

The primary risk mitigation tool for generous equity grants is vesting with a cliff [3]. The standard structure:

- **Four-year vesting**: The co-founder earns equity over four years
- **One-year cliff**: If a co-founder leaves or is fired within the first year, they receive nothing
- After the cliff, 25% of shares vest, then an additional 1/48th of total stock vests each month [3]

Seibel calls vesting and the cliff the CEO's "get-out-of-jail-free card": if the co-founder choice was incorrect, as long as it is corrected within one year, there is no long-term harm to the company [3]. Because this safety mechanism exists, it benefits founders to be more generous with equity, not less [3].

### Board Seat as Additional Protection

Seibel recommends that only the CEO hold a board seat before a significant equity fundraise. This prevents board disputes during tough decisions, including the unlikely event that the CEO has to fire a co-founder [3].

## The Co-Founder Conversation

The [[Co-Founders]] article covers the broader question of co-founder selection. Equity split should be one of the earliest conversations, addressed before the company is incorporated. Key considerations include:

- Relative contributions (past work on the idea vs. future contributions)
- Full-time vs. part-time commitment
- Vesting schedules (standard is 4-year vesting with 1-year cliff)
- What happens if a co-founder leaves

## University Spinouts

[[Jared Friedman]] notes that equity split is particularly fraught in [[University Spinouts]], where the question of how much equity the university, the professor, and the founding team each receive can become contentious. Universities typically demand significant equity (often 5-15% or more) plus licensing fees, which can create conflicts with venture investors who expect clean cap tables [2].

## Co-Founder Breakup Guidelines (Pre-PMF)

Seibel provides specific guidelines for what happens when a co-founder leaves or is fired before [[Product-Market Fit]] [4]:

- **Before the one-year cliff**: Departing founder retains a token amount (2-5%)
- **After the one-year cliff but pre-PMF**: Departing founder retains no more than 5%, which often means giving back some equity
- **If fired**: One to three months severance is reasonable
- **If they leave voluntarily**: Severance is not typical
- **All departures**: Resign from board, sign a release, grant proxy voting rights to remaining founders

The rationale for the 5% cap: "When a company's pre-product-market-fit, so much work is left to be done. You want to motivate the people who are remaining to distribute that equity to people who can actually help" [4].

## CEO Must Have Firing Authority

"The CEO should have the ability to fire founders who are not performing. There has to be a captain of the ship. If you're not willing to join a team under these circumstances, maybe you should go start a company and be the CEO" [4].

## Bad Advice to Avoid

**Performance-based equity**: Impossible to set meaningful goals at the start of a startup, and goals change with pivots. "This is probably not an area that you should be innovating" [4].

**Part-time founders**: "A part-time founder isn't a founder and shouldn't really be considered in this equation" [4].

**Dynamic equity agreements**: Complicated structures where equity shifts based on milestones. "If you're trying to motivate people, it's really nice for them to know what they have" [4].

## The Equity Equation for Non-Founders

[[Paul Graham]]'s [[Equity Equation]] provides a mathematical framework for evaluating any equity trade: if someone joining the company makes your stock worth more than 1/(1-n) where n is the fraction you are giving up, the trade is worth it. This applies to early employees, advisors, and anyone receiving equity.
