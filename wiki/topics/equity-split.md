# Equity Split

Equity split refers to how ownership of a startup is divided among co-founders and early contributors. It is one of the most consequential early decisions a startup makes and one of the most common sources of co-founder conflict. [[Michael Seibel]] and [[Harj Taggar]] both independently advocate for near-equal splits, reinforced by Y Combinator's broader guidance across multiple sources.

## The Case for Equal Splits

Both Seibel and Taggar converge on the same advice: default to equal equity splits [1] [5].

Taggar's reasoning: "If this startup is successful, you will end up working on it for the next 10 years or more. Even if someone's been thinking about the idea for a little bit longer, that will really be quite insignificant over the long term" [5].

Seibel makes a strong case, calling it "probably controversial, but it's what we have done for all of my startups, and what we almost always recommend at YC" [1]. He identifies four fundamental errors in the reasoning founders use to justify unequal splits [1]:

1. **It takes 7 to 10 years to build a company of great value.** Small variations in year one do not justify massively different splits in years 2-10.
2. **More equity = more motivation.** "Getting a larger piece of the equity pie is worth nothing if the lack of motivation on your founding team leads to failure" [1].
3. **If you don't value your co-founders, neither will anyone else.** Giving a co-founder 10% or 1% communicates to investors that they are not important [1].
4. **Startups are about execution, not about ideas.** Unequal splits often give undue preference to whoever initially came up with the idea [1].

Seibel uses the "Jedi founder vs. midwit" framing: both the smartest and most experienced founders are generous with equity. The midwit tries to do complicated calculations about skill sets, contributions, and time commitments [4].

## Bad Reasons for Unequal Splits

Seibel systematically dismantles common justifications [1] [4]:

- **"I came up with the idea."** YC receives 30,000 applications every six months. "Ideas are a dime a dozen and execution is the game" [4].
- **"I started working six months before."** If 99% of the work remains, a six-month head start on a 10-20 year journey is not significant [4].
- **"My co-founder needs a salary and I don't."** Salary and equity serve different purposes [4].
- **"I'm older and more experienced."** If essential enough to be a co-founder, they need enough equity to stay motivated [4].
- **"I hired my co-founder after raising money."** Fundraising does not massively change success probability [4].

Seibel summarizes: "All of these answers are a flavor of short-term thinking. The best founders are long-term thinkers" [4].

## Vesting as the Safety Net

Seibel emphasizes that vesting and a cliff, not unequal splits, are the correct mechanism for protecting against co-founder departures [1]. Standard terms:

- **Four-year vesting**: The co-founder must work for four years to earn their full equity stake.
- **One-year cliff**: If a co-founder leaves within the first year, they receive nothing.
- After the cliff, 25% vests, then 1/48th per month thereafter [1].

"This is your get-out-of-jail-free card. If you made a decision that was incorrect about choosing your co-founders, as long as you correct it within one year, there's no long-term harm to the company" [1].

Seibel is emphatic that vesting applies to all founders: "Giving away founder equity is not something that you should be innovating on" [4].

## Bad Advice to Avoid

**Performance-based equity.** At the start of a tech startup, goals are unclear. Measuring contributions precisely is a "really big fallacy" [4].

**Part-time founders.** A part-time founder is not a founder [4].

**Dynamic equity agreements.** Complicated arrangements are "too fancy and ill-advised" [4].

## Co-Founder Breakup Equity Guidelines

Seibel provides specific YC guidelines for pre-[[Product-Market Fit]] companies [4]:

- **Before the one-year cliff:** The departing founder receives a token amount -- 2-5% of the company.
- **After the cliff but pre-PMF:** The departing founder should retain no more than 5%, which may mean giving back some vested equity.

Additional guidelines [4]:
- A fired founder may receive 1-3 months severance. A founder who leaves voluntarily typically does not.
- Every departing founder should resign from the board, sign a release, and give proxy voting rights to remaining founders.
- The CEO should have the ability to fire non-performing founders regardless of equity split.

## The Activation Energy Argument

Seibel addresses the objection that long-term CEOs like Bezos or Zuckerberg prove one founder deserves vastly more equity [4]:

"The beginning is extremely important. Those first four to six years is where a lot of value is being created, and those first four to six years is when most companies die." Early co-founders provide the activation energy the company needs. "Without them, maybe you're not in the game at all" [4].

## The Co-Founder Conversation

Equity split should be one of the earliest conversations, addressed before the company is incorporated. Key considerations include:
- Relative contributions (past work vs. future contributions)
- Full-time vs. part-time commitment
- Vesting schedules
- What happens if a co-founder leaves

## University Spinouts: A Special Case

[[Jared Friedman]] notes that equity split is particularly fraught in [[University Spinouts]], where universities typically demand 5-15% or more plus licensing fees, creating conflicts with venture investors who expect clean cap tables [2].

## The Equity Equation for Non-Founders

[[Paul Graham]]'s [[Equity Equation]] provides a mathematical framework: if someone joining makes your stock worth more than 1/(1-n) where n is the fraction you are giving up, the trade is worth it.
