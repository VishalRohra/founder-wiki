# Series A Term Sheets

Jason Kwon and [[Aaron Harris]] of YC describe a common problem: founders seeing their first term sheet do not know what "good" looks like [1]. This puts them at a disadvantage because VCs see term sheets constantly. YC, having seen hundreds of Series A term sheets, works with founders to identify where terms diverge from standard, what can be done about divergences, and when negotiation makes sense.

YC drafted a standard, clean Series A term sheet representing what they see most often from good Silicon Valley VCs. The term sheet fits on a single page [1]. This brevity was not always the norm but became common over the last decade as some investors shortened their legalese as a signal: "We aren't going to get bogged down in the minutiae. We're going to make this easy, friendly, standard and fast" [1].

A [downloadable Word version](https://www.ycombinator.com/series_a_term_sheet/) of the standard term sheet is available from YC.

## Pricing

The lead investor in a Series A round generally wants 20% of the company, but pricing can flex depending on leverage held by each side. YC considers price important but too specific to each raise to standardize. Their focus is on control and structure terms that are less familiar to founders and more prone to cause confusion [1].

## Control Terms: What the Term Sheet Tells You

The term sheet is a signal about the investor. A contract allocates risks between parties, so the terms an investor insists on reveal their perceived risks [1].

### Board Composition

The standard term sheet is founder-friendly because founders retain board control at 2-1. The most common way founders lose control at Series A is through a 2-2-1 board structure: two founders, two investors, and one independent board member. Losing board control is significant because it means founders can be fired from their own company [1].

Another control risk is a separate provision requiring the investor director's approval for operational decisions such as setting annual budgets, hiring/firing executives, pivoting the business, or adding new lines of business. When investors structure boards to take power away from founders, the outward justification is governance or accountability. Kwon and Harris state plainly: "when an investor says that they're committed to partnering with you for the long-term -- or that they're betting everything on you -- but then tells you something else with the terms that they insist on, believe the terms" [1].

### Standard Investor Vetoes

The standard voting rights section includes vetoes on financing (clauses covering issuance of new equity) and sale of the company. Kwon and Harris note these are standard across most term sheets and use similar technical jargon, but their concrete implications are not facially obvious. Founders should have a lawyer walk through the scenarios [1].

Founders should ensure their lawyer and the investor's lawyer both specialize in startups and venture deals, where "standard" terms have accepted practice [1].

## Dirty Economic Terms to Watch For

The standard term sheet is instructive for what it does not contain. Non-standard or "dirty" economic terms include [1]:

- **Liquidation preference greater than 1x**: The investor gets back more than its invested capital first.
- **Participating preferred**: The investor double-dips by getting money back plus pro rata portion of exit proceeds, rather than choosing between the two.
- **Cumulative dividends**: The investor compounds its liquidation preference every year by X%, increasing the economic hurdle before founders and employees see value.
- **Warrant coverage**: The investor gets extra fully-diluted ownership without paying for it at the agreed valuation.

These are all ways of adding structure to reduce typical venture risk. The investor is "essentially saying, 'I'm sort of afraid of losing my money.'" It can foreshadow behavior when things go badly, such as pushing founders to sell when they do not want to, or pulling back on risk when risk is needed [1].

Good investors address economic risks by negotiating on valuation and are otherwise happy to give standard terms because "the real money in venture is not made with structure, but by building long-term value" [1].

## Series A Terms as Foundation

The Series A documents set a foundation and precedent for future rounds. Good foundations make the next term sheet and financing round fast and simple, as future investors step into the same straightforward terms. Bad foundations complicate everything: future investors ask for the same heavy terms, existing investors refuse to drop terms that new investors want removed. "Unwinding bad terms is difficult, and oftentimes impossible" [1].

## Don't Over-Optimize

The goal is a clean deal, not a perfect deal. "No one ever built an enduring company just by winning their Series A negotiation." Even if terms are not ideal, execution can outrun suboptimal terms or establish leverage to renegotiate later. The ultimate goal is closing fast and getting back to work [1].
