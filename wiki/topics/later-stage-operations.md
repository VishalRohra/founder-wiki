# Later-Stage Operations

[[Sam Altman]] addresses the challenges that startups face 12-24 months in, after finding [[Product-Market Fit]]. The main job shifts from building a great product to building a great company.

## Management at Scale

Before PMF, everyone can report to the founder. This works well up to roughly 20 employees. "But what works well at 20 employees can be disastrous at 40."

The initial structure need not be complex: "all you need is for every employee to know who their manager is and for everyone to have exactly one manager." Managers should know their direct reports. Cluster people into teams that make sense. "Clarity and simplicity are important -- I wouldn't try to innovate on management structure."

### Common Management Failures

**Senior hires:** In the early days, hiring senior people is usually a mistake. As the company scales, "it's actually valuable to add senior people/executives to the team that have built companies before."

**Not delegating:** Founders take on too much personally. "As your company keeps growing, you become stretched thin and things get done poorly."

**Delegating poorly:** Having employees do grunt work while making all decisions yourself does not work for scalability or employee effectiveness. "Empower employees to make their own decisions. Let them know how you think about a problem, but let them own the decision process."

Altman advises: delegate most things, especially what you do not want to do. Keep one thing you enjoy or where your involvement adds disproportionate value.

**Personal organization:** Developing a system to track tasks and follow-ups becomes crucial as you become a manager.

**Communication at scale:** Beyond a certain size, one-on-one conversations with everyone are impossible. "Learn to communicate in clear, succinct writing, and share it with everyone. It's one of the highest leverage things you can do, and it scales to thousands of people." Hold all-hands meetings at least monthly and maintain one-on-ones with direct reports.

## HR and People Operations

Most founders ignore HR initially. Continuing to ignore it at later stages is "a huge mistake." Good HR means three things: clear management structure, a way for people to raise workplace concerns, and pathways for career evolution.

### Performance and Compensation

Performance feedback "can be light, but it should happen frequently." It should be clear how performance ties to compensation. "People talk, and they will eventually find out co-workers' compensation levels. If it's all over the place, it can be a complete disaster." **Compensation bands** solve this: a mid-level engineer knows their range, a senior engineer knows theirs.

### Equity

"YC company data suggests that most successful companies give out a lot of equity." Altman recommends planning to give away **3-5% of the company each year for ten years** from founding. This is a lot of stock but keeps people motivated and aligned.

Critical: **refresher grants** for employees approaching vesting cliffs. "You never want an employee in a place where they start thinking about leaving because they've vested three out of their four years. Always stay in front of people's vesting schedules." Altman links to Wealthfront's post on [refresher grants](https://blog.wealthfront.com/how-long-should-you-stay-at-your-job/).

### Other HR Practices

- **Build a diverse team.** "You want unity of vision, but diversity of backgrounds is good. Myopic culture is typically less effective."
- **Announce offers internally.** Up to a few hundred employees, announce potential offers on an internal mailing list. "Very often someone in the company will know something good or bad about the prospective employee."
- **Structure onboarding.** "Have a program in place to ramp up new employees."
- **Build paths for early employees.** The original 10-15 may not fit executive roles, but "they're probably loved by their peers, and very productive." Talk to them directly about career progression.
- **Monitor burnout.** "Building a company really is a marathon and you cannot sustain people working 100 hour weeks in perpetuity."
- **Hire a full-time recruiter** once you have PMF. "Getting the best people is arguably the most important thing to do."

## Company Productivity

"Building a company that is able to innovate repeatedly is the hardest thing in business." Companies become unproductive because people are not on the same page or do not understand priorities.

### The Alignment Test

Altman describes a test he gives struggling companies: "If I asked 10 employees what the top three goals are for the company, would they say the same thing?" 100% of founders say yes. "I'll then go and do it, and 100 percent of the time no two employees say the same thing."

The fix: reiterate the roadmap and goals frequently. Have management meetings with direct reports every week. Hold company all-hands at least monthly covering the roadmap, three-month trajectory, and how it connects to longer-term goals.

### Operational Tips

- **Do offsites.** Take best people outside the workspace where "everyone has time to just talk and think through the bigger picture."
- **Get legal docs in order.** Assign someone to collect every agreement the company has ever signed.
- **Start FP&A early.** "If you have someone build a really great model of the business, you can optimize and understand how things are working at a level that most people totally miss." Worth doing well before hundreds of employees.
- **Hire a fundraiser.** A full-time fundraiser after the B round, preparing for the C round, "you'll almost certainly get better results than if you hire an investment banker later on" and pay less in fees and dilution.
- **Pay attention to unit economics.** "Sooner rather than later, you need to figure out how to make more money from each user than you spend." Most great companies historically have had good unit economics soon after beginning to monetize.
- **Watch runway.** "Don't ever get down to just a couple months of cash in the bank."

## Founder Psychology at Later Stages

"The highs are higher but the lows keep getting lower." As success grows, "people start rooting against you. Journalists and people on the internet will find ways to pick apart what you're doing." Altman advises learning to ignore the haters early.

Long-term commitment is a massive advantage: "Have a strategy that assumes you will work on your company for the next 10 years." Very few founders make this commitment.

Take vacation. "We often see founders that go three or four years without ever taking a real vacation." Burnout is inevitable without rest.

Do not lose focus. "Focus is what made you successful in the first place." Talking to potential acquirers is "another very dangerous" distraction.

## Navigating Success: The Airplane vs. Spaceship Framework

Altman uses a metaphor (from David Weiden): starting a startup is like being pushed off a cliff with a bag of aerospace parts. You hope they are spaceship parts. Sometimes they are, sometimes they are nothing, and a reasonable amount of the time "it turns out you have the parts for an airplane."

An "airplane" is defined as a company with profitability within reach, on track to be worth $100 million with several more years of hard work. "This is nothing to be ashamed of -- quite the opposite."

### The Investor Misalignment Problem

Founders and investors often diverge at this point. "Many startup investors are looking for spaceships -- airplanes don't matter much to them, though they matter quite a bit to the founders." Existing investors will usually take almost any risk to see if an airplane can become a spaceship.

Altman advises: "Be frank and realistic with your existing investors; you will eventually persuade them that a small multiple is better than a loss." The best investors understand and may even "treat you extra-generously in the hopes of funding your next company."

### The Valuation Trap

"Raising too much money or raising money at too high a valuation can severely limit your optionality." Founders who know they have an airplane but convince investors it might be a spaceship cause enormous heartache and "often precludes your opportunity for a good acquisition later."

The answer: "Don't raise a lot of money at a high price when you think the company is probably not going to increase another 10x in value and you'd be pretty happy selling the company soon." This may require finding a path to profitability with current cash.

### Positioning for Acquisition

"Companies get bought, not sold." Forcing an acquisition is very difficult and "nearly impossible if you haven't built something legitimately valuable." Begin developing relationships with potential acquirers early -- "it usually takes a long time, and personal relationships usually help."

"Silicon Valley is filled with people who had one or several mid-success companies before eventually hitting it out of the park."

## Dealmaking

12-24 months in, business development starts to matter (assuming a great product exists). Key principles:
- Develop personal connections. "No one wants to feel like they're being used transactionally."
- Set up competitive situations. "You get good terms and deals done when you have a competitive situation and you stay persistent."
- Ask for what you want. "Most of the time you won't get laughed out of the room, and you might actually get it."
