# Three Traits of Great Leaders

[[Ali Rowghani]] proposes a framework for measuring leadership based on observations from working closely with four leaders he considers extraordinary: [[Ed Catmull]] (Pixar founder), [[Steve Jobs]] (Pixar CEO), John Lasseter (Pixar Chief Creative Officer), and Bob Iger (Disney CEO).

Despite radical differences in style and temperament ("one was an introverted scientist while another was an extroverted artist. One was a college dropout who had founded a company and was infamous for brash behavior while another was a career executive who was exceptionally genteel and diplomatic"), all four built extraordinary trust by excelling in the same three areas.

## The Three Traits

### 1. Clarity of Thought and Communication

Great leaders think and communicate clearly. They describe a vision that people find compelling enough to work hard to achieve. "If your employees are confused about your mission and strategy, or do not find it motivating or credible, they will not follow you with the focus and determination necessary to succeed."

**How to improve**: Spend more time thinking about what is truly important for the business. Once crystallized, practice expressing it in simple terms. "Simplicity is vital."

**Bezos example**: Amazon's strategy built on three enduring customer preferences: lower prices, bigger selection, and faster delivery. As [[Jeff Bezos]] said, "You can build a business strategy around the things that are stable in time....when you have something that you know is true, even over the long-term, you can afford to put a lot of energy into it."

**Lutke example**: [[Tobi Lutke]] and his senior team at Shopify spend hundreds of hours preparing for their annual employee Summit. "We want to be a loosely coupled, highly aligned company. The Summit is the main enabler of this because it is a grand sync."

Communication preparation becomes increasingly important as the company grows: "your employee base grows more diverse, and fewer of your employees have a personal relationship with you. Hence, they are much less likely to just 'know what you mean.'"

### 2. Judgment About People

Great leaders have strong intuition about people, particularly in selecting those they give power and responsibility. "Nothing does more damage to an organization or to the standing of a leader than picking the wrong leaders or failing to correct these mistakes when they happen."

The judgment around the initial hiring or promotion decision matters most: "leaders who fire too many of their own also lose a lot of credibility and trust."

**How to improve**: Gather more data. Meet as many of the best people in a field as possible to sharpen recognition skills. Spend extensive time with executive candidates. Do extensive reference checks asking for examples of behavior showing good judgment and high integrity, "because these traits are hard to test in an interview."

**Uber CTO example**: Thuan Pham was interviewed by CEO Travis Kalanick "for 30 hours straight, one-on-one, over two weeks," including over Skype while Kalanick was traveling. Pham said: "Throughout those 30 hours, I actually forgot it was an interview. It was just like a discussion between two colleagues."

### 3. Personal Integrity and Commitment

Great leaders have exceptional personal integrity and commitment to their mission. Integrity means:
- Standing for something meaningful beyond oneself rather than being motivated by narrow personal interests
- Admitting mistakes rather than acting as though always right
- Receiving critical feedback openly and working to improve
- Avoiding favoritism, conflicts of interest, inappropriate language, and inappropriate work relationships

**The transparency test**: "if your team had full transparency into your private communications and behavior towards employees, would you be embarrassed by anything you have done or said? This is a high bar, but one that great leaders strive to meet."

Beyond integrity, great leaders derive "deep personal meaning and fulfillment from leading people to achieve a mission." Their commitment translates into high productivity, which becomes the foundation for pushing the organization to do the same.

## It All Adds Up to Trust

Building trust is "both a science and an art."

**The science**: Trust grows when leaders' predictions about the future (products to build, investments to make, competitive landscape changes) prove accurate, and when the people they choose to lead prove to be the right ones.

**The art**: Trust grows when leaders "say the right thing at the right time, and show empathy and good judgment." It grows when they stand for ideals bigger than themselves and are honest about what they don't know. "This is why you can't try to copy Steve Jobs or Ed Catmull in your quest to be a great leader. You can only be yourself."

Great leaders treat hard decisions (firing people, saying no, taking responsibility for mistakes) as "opportunities to build trust." When faced with a difficult challenge, they optimize for trust.

"In difficult times, as you evaluate one course of action versus another, ask yourself which path will generate more trust in you as a person and as a leader. Always try to choose that path."

## Measuring Leadership: Survey Questions

Rowghani recommends gathering employee data systematically once a company reaches about 50 people. He provides detailed survey questions organized by trait:

**Clarity of Thought / Communication**:
- CEO: Write down mission, strategy, and key metrics in under 2 minutes. Write down 2-3 themes you consistently emphasize.
- Employees: What is the mission and strategy? What are the most important metrics? How does your work contribute? How often has this changed? What does the CEO consistently emphasize?

**Judgment About People**:
- CEO: Rate each leader you have hired/promoted. Name any you think are wrong for the role.
- Employees: Has the CEO chosen good leaders? Which do you respect? Are there weak ones? Have high performers left, and why?

**Personal Integrity / Commitment**:
- CEO: Have any actions diminished employee confidence in your integrity? Do you ask for feedback and respond to it?
- Employees: Rate the CEO's integrity/moral compass. Does the CEO listen and respond to feedback? Have you seen favoritism or unethical behavior? What motivates the CEO?
