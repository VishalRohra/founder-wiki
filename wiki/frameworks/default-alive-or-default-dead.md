# Default Alive or Default Dead

A diagnostic framework introduced by [[Paul Graham]] in his essay "[Default Alive or Default Dead?](http://www.paulgraham.com/aord.html)" [1]. The question: assuming your expenses remain constant and your revenue growth continues on its recent trend, will you make it to profitability on the money you have left?

## The Question

For any startup operating more than 8-9 months, this is Graham's first question [1]. The answer determines the entire conversation. Default alive companies can discuss ambitious plans. Default dead companies need to talk about survival [1].

"The startling thing is how often the founders themselves don't know. Half the founders I talk to don't know whether they're default alive or default dead" [1].

Trevor Blackwell built a [calculator](http://growth.tlb.org/#) to help [1].

## Why Founders Don't Know

Two reasons [1]:

1. **The question changes polarity.** Early on, asking about profitability is meaningless (like asking a 3-year-old about self-support). But as the company matures, it becomes critical. "That kind of switch often takes people by surprise."

2. **They assume they'll raise more money.** "But that assumption is often false, and worse still, the more you depend on it, the falser it becomes."

Graham's recommendation: start asking too early rather than too late. "It's probably not that dangerous to start worrying too early that you're default dead, whereas it's very dangerous to start worrying too late" [1].

## The Fatal Pinch

The terminal condition: **default dead + slow growth + not enough time to fix it** [1]. Founders arrive here by not realizing it's where they're headed.

Graham recommends making hopes explicit: "Say 'We're default dead, but we're counting on investors to save us.' Maybe as you say that, it will set off the same alarms in your head that it does in mine" [1].

## Plan A and Plan B

Even with steep growth (5x/year or more), investors are unreliable [1]. "You can never safely treat fundraising as more than a plan A." Every startup should have a written Plan B:

- Precisely what to do to survive without more funding
- Precisely when to switch to Plan B if Plan A fails

See also [[Financial Survival]].

## The Growth-Spending Disconnect

Graham makes a counterintuitive empirical observation: "In practice there is surprisingly little connection between how much a startup spends and how fast it grows" [1]. Fast growth usually means the product hits a nerve. High spending usually means expensive development, expensive sales, or waste. These are independent [1].

This directly challenges the conventional wisdom that you need to spend to grow. In many cases, the path to growth is [[Do Things That Don't Scale]] or redesigning the product "in the way only founders can" [1].

## Overhiring: The Mechanism

"Hiring too fast is by far the biggest killer of startups that raise money" [1].

Why founders overhire [1]:
1. So much work to do -- naive founders think headcount solves this
2. Successful startups have big teams -- confusing effect for cause
3. Slow growth is painful to face -- hiring distracts from the real problem (unappealing product)

VCs encourage overhiring because "kill-or-cure strategies are optimal for VCs" due to portfolio diversification. "But as a founder your incentives are different. You want above all to survive" [1].

### The Common Death Pattern

1. Make something moderately appealing with decent initial growth [1]
2. Raise a round easily (founders seem smart, idea sounds plausible)
3. Growth is ok but not great (because product is only moderately appealing)
4. Hire aggressively to boost growth
5. Growth never comes (because the problem is the product, not headcount)
6. Rapidly burning runway with high expenses and slow growth
7. Now unappealing to investors
8. Company dies

The fix: address the product problem. "Hiring people is rarely the way to fix that. More often than not it makes it harder" [1].

[[Sam Altman]]: "I think it's roughly correct to say that YC's most successful companies have never been the fastest to hire, and one of the marks of a great founder is being able to resist this urge" [1].

[[Paul Buchheit]]: "A related problem that I see a lot is premature scaling -- founders take a small business that isn't really working (bad unit economics, typically) and then scale it up because they want impressive growth numbers" [1].

## The Airbnb Counter-Example

Airbnb waited 4 months after raising money at the end of YC before hiring their first employee [1]. The founders were terribly overworked, "but they were overworked evolving Airbnb into the astonishingly successful organism it is now" [1].
