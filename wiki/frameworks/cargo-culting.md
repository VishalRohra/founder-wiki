# Cargo Culting

Cargo culting in startups is the practice of superficially copying another company's tactics without understanding why those tactics worked [1]. The term originates from Pacific Islander communities that built fake landing strips hoping airplanes would return -- mimicking the visible artifact without understanding the underlying system [1]. [[Dalton Caldwell]] and [[Michael Seibel]] identify three levels of cargo culting, each progressively more dangerous.

## Classic Cargo Culting: Copying Successful Companies

The first generation of cargo culting involved copying demonstrably successful companies -- Google, Facebook, and Uber -- without understanding the context that made their strategies work [1].

### Google Cargo Culting

In the 2000s, Google was the archetypal startup. The cargo cult copies [1]:
- Open offices with bright colors and free snacks (the conclusion: this is what made Google great)
- Flat organizational structures with no managers
- "Hire as many smart engineers as possible" regardless of need
- Cute company names with dropped vowels (started by Flickr)
- Logos with primary colors and lens flares

The reality: Google's hiring strategy worked because they were solving a genuinely hard technical problem (search was not a commodity despite conventional wisdom). They hired during the post-dot-com crash when great talent was available cheaply. These contextual factors did not transfer to the average startup copying Google's office layout [1].

### Facebook Cargo Culting

Facebook's influence produced [1]:
- "Going viral" as a strategy for every product, including ones people use five minutes per month
- Never charging users; building an ads business
- Share buttons on everything
- Ignoring privacy concerns

The reality: going viral is a viable strategy when users spend two hours a day on your product. Facebook's ad business worked because of unprecedented scale and engagement. "Not clear that every business should be an ads business" [1]. Seibel notes: "The second Facebook-style profile you had was delivering infinitely less value than the first one" [1].

### Uber Cargo Culting

The Uber cargo cult was distinctive because many of the beliefs about Uber were not even accurate [1]:
- "Spend as much money as possible"
- Expand to as many cities as possible before the product works in one
- Ignore unit economics
- Ignore laws
- "Blitzscale" (a book was written about it)

The reality: investors funding Uber were looking at markets where Uber had excellent unit economics, then funding rapid expansion into those proven markets. "It wasn't people looking at Uber serving no markets well, burning money left and right" [1]. Uber was immediately and obviously valuable when it launched in San Francisco. "Superficially copying Uber and pretending that you're Travis doesn't work. It's just like wearing a black turtleneck does not make you Steve Jobs" [1].

## Modern Cargo Culting: Copying Unproven Companies

The second level is more dangerous because the company being copied has not succeeded. A founder bases their strategy on a fundraising announcement and a podcast interview, with no knowledge of the target company's revenue [1].

Caldwell describes the pattern: "You're the CEO of a company. The strategy you're betting the farm on is based on you read a fundraising announcement and you have no other context?" [1].

WeWork and Zume Pizza (robotic pizza making) are examples of companies that attracted cargo cult followers based on high valuations, not business results. Both eventually flamed out, taking their imitators with them [1].

## Super-Modern Cargo Culting: Copying Struggling Startups

The third and most dangerous level: copying another startup that is itself struggling. Caldwell: "I love when someone references a startup that we know and we're like, no" [1]. Founders copy strategy from companies they believe are succeeding based on superficial signals (GitHub stars, social media presence) without any evidence of actual business performance.

## The Startup Facade

Modern cargo culting also manifests as imitating what a startup "should look like" rather than building something that works [1]:
- Raising money (regardless of need)
- Having impressive advisors
- Filing patents
- Creating a beautiful pitch deck
- Attending conferences
- Getting press

"These are things that founders do to impress themselves, to impress other founders. The user is not essential" [1].

## Influence vs. Plagiarism

Caldwell draws an analogy to academic writing: plagiarizing a paper by copy-pasting from three sources without understanding them is not original work. Reading many sources, digesting the information, and synthesizing it into your own original work with cited sources is [1].

The same applies to music: ripping off someone's song is plagiarism; being influenced by sounds and integrating them into new music is how the industry works. "Everyone's being influenced by everyone else" [1].

The key distinction: copying completely without thought versus being influenced and borrowing good ideas to integrate into your own thing [1].

## The Right Starting Point

Seibel argues that cargo culting is avoidable when founders start from the user's perspective. "What does the user want?" leads to understanding which company tactics are essential versus superficial [1]. Do people care about Google's logo or about getting good search results? Do they care about which city Uber expanded to, or that Uber shows up in five minutes? Starting with the user makes the essential visible and the cargo cult artifacts obviously irrelevant [1].

Starting from a fundraising announcement makes cargo culting almost inevitable [1].
