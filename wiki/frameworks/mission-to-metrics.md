# Mission-to-Metrics Framework

The Mission-to-Metrics framework is a three-level alignment tool described by [[Ali Rowghani]] as one of the three tasks that only a startup CEO can perform [1]. It connects the company's purpose (Mission) to its direction (Strategy) to its performance measurement (Metrics), creating a chain that allows every employee to understand how their work contributes to the company's goals.

## The Three Levels

[![Mission-to-Metrics Diagram](images/3k-the-second-job-of-a-startup-ceo/cb2bc3e4792d.png)](https://blog.ycombinator.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/11/Artboard-2white_wborder.png)

### Mission (Purpose)

The mission should feel "ambitious and permanent" and find its roots in the reasons the company was founded [1]. It should not change often.

### Strategy (Direction)

Product strategy and go-to-market strategy should be revisited at least twice per year to ensure relevance [1]. Rowghani strongly recommends writing strategy in long form rather than relying on slide decks: "the CEOs who are most effective in developing and communicating strategy take the time to write their strategy out, in long form" [1]. He cites Jeff Bezos's practice at Amazon of requiring 6-page memos for strategic meetings, though he notes founders need not go that far.

### Metrics (Pace and Performance)

A common mistake is equating key internal metrics with top-line results like revenue or user growth [1]. This fails because top-line results are usually not directly actionable. Instead, CEOs must dig deeper to find the drivers. Rowghani cites Facebook's discovery that connecting a new user to 10 friends within 14 days correlated with retained usage. Facebook then set "number of new users with 10 friend connections" as the key product metric [1].

"If you don't know what drives revenue, customer acquisition, or user growth, you aren't likely to be successful anyway" [1].

## The SpaceX Example

Rowghani's most vivid illustration comes from a friend who visited the SpaceX manufacturing floor [1]. He stopped to ask an employee assembling a large part, "What is your job at SpaceX?" The employee answered:

> "The mission of SpaceX is to colonize Mars. In order to colonize Mars, we need to build reusable rockets because it will otherwise be unaffordable for humans to travel to Mars and back. My job is to help design the steering system that enables our rockets to land back on earth. You'll know if I've succeeded if our rockets land on our platform in the Atlantic after launch."

The employee could have simply said he built a steering system. Instead, he traced his specific task through the strategy to the mission. "That is alignment" [1].

## Communication Is Constant

The framework only works if it is communicated relentlessly. Rowghani warns that founders must "reiterate the Mission-to-Metrics much more than what feels reasonable, which may run counter to your instinct to be efficient" [1].

The test is not whether employees can repeat the framework, but "whether they can make good decisions in your absence based on the context you have provided" [1].

This aligns with Aaron Epstein's advice on management communication: "You should feel like you sound like a broken record about the things that are important to your company" (see [[Startup Management]]).

## How to Build It

Rowghani's practical process [1]:

1. Write your Mission, Strategy, and Metrics in long form
2. Get feedback from your leadership team and key employees
3. Communicate it to everyone regularly
4. Test alignment by checking whether employees can make good decisions without you
