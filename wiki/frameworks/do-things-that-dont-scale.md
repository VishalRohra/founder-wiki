# Do Things That Don't Scale

A framework from [[Paul Graham]]'s essay "[Do Things That Don't Scale](http://www.paulgraham.com/ds.html)" arguing that nearly every successful startup required deliberate, laborious, unscalable effort to get started [1]. The metaphor: car engines once needed hand cranks before electric starters. "Once the engine was going, it would keep going, but there was a separate and laborious process to get it going" [1].

The core insight: "Actually startups take off because the founders make them take off" [1].

## The Techniques

### 1. Recruit Users Manually

"Nearly all startups have to. You can't wait for users to come to you" [1].

**The Collison Installation.** Named after Stripe founders Patrick and John Collison. At YC, when anyone agreed to try Stripe, they wouldn't say "we'll send you a link." They'd say "Right then, give me your laptop" and set them up on the spot [1]. Patrick Collison later described the inflection point: "At some point, there was a very noticeable change in how Stripe felt. It tipped from being this boulder we had to push to being a train car that in fact had its own momentum" [1].

**Airbnb's door-to-door hustle.** The founders went door to door in New York, recruiting hosts and helping them improve listings [1]. They arrived at YC Tuesday dinners with rolly bags, having just flown back from somewhere. "Early on it was so fragile that about 30 days of going out and engaging in person with users made the difference between success and failure" [1].

**Finding the right initial vein.** If you built something for yourself, find your peers. Otherwise, do an untargeted launch, observe who's most enthusiastic, and seek out more like them [1]. Ben Silbermann noticed early Pinterest users were designers, so he recruited at a design blogger conference [1].

### 2. Delight Users Obsessively

Wufoo sent each new user a hand-written thank you note [1]. Graham has "never once seen a startup lured down a blind alley by trying too hard to make their initial users happy" [1].

Three reasons founders don't do this: (1) engineering training doesn't include customer service; (2) they worry it won't scale; (3) they've never experienced such attention from companies themselves [1]. But "that's one advantage of being small: you can provide a level of service no big company can" [1].

Garry Tan warns against the opposite trap: founders who imitate big-company flaws (like indifference to users) to seem "professional" [1].

### 3. The Contained Fire

Start with a deliberately narrow market [1]. "It's like keeping a fire contained at first to get it really hot before adding more logs" [1].

Facebook started Harvard-only. Mark Zuckerberg said creating course lists for each school "made students feel the site was their natural home" [1]. Other startups serve as excellent early adopter companies because they're open to new things and grow fast [1].

### 4. Pulling a Meraki (Hardware)

Assemble your own hardware [1]. Meraki's founders assembled their own routers. Pebble assembled the first several hundred watches by hand. Building yourself means you "can tweak the design faster when you're the factory" [1]. Eric Migicovsky of Pebble: "how valuable it was to source good screws" [1].

### 5. The Consulting Model

For B2B: "pick a single user and act as if they were consultants building something just for that one user" [1]. Safe as long as you're not paid hourly. When you are a product company being extra attentive, customers are grateful. When they pay by the hour, they expect everything [1].

At Viaweb, Graham's team used their own software on behalf of merchants: "we were trying to sell luggage and pens and men's shirts. But in retrospect it was exactly the right thing to do" [1].

### 6. Be Your Software

When user count is small, do by hand what you'll later automate [1]. Stripe delivered "instant" merchant accounts by manually signing users up behind the scenes. "Less frightening than the far more common case of having something automatic that doesn't yet solve anyone's problems" [1].

## What Doesn't Work

**Big launches.** "There may even be an inverse correlation between launch magnitude and success. The only launches I remember are famous flops like the Segway and Google Wave" [1].

**Partnerships.** Six months after a big-company partnership, founders always say: "That was way more work than we expected, and we ended up getting practically nothing out of it" [1].

**Any strategy that omits effort** -- expecting a big launch or a big partner to deliver users -- is "ipso facto suspect" [1].

## Startup Ideas as Vectors

Graham proposes viewing startup ideas as pairs: what you're building + the unscalable thing you'll do to get started [1]. In the best case, both become company DNA: aggressive acquisition stays aggressive, hand-building teaches irreplaceable lessons, and delighting a handful of users creates a culture of delight [1]. See also [[Startup Ideas]].

An idea where "the second component is empty -- an idea where there is nothing you can do to get going, e.g. because you have no way to find users to recruit manually -- is probably a bad idea, at least for those founders" [1].
