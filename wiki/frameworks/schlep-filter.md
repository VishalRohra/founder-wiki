# Schlep Filter

The Schlep Filter is a concept introduced by [[Paul Graham]] to describe an unconscious mental filter that prevents founders from seeing good startup ideas because those ideas involve tedious, unpleasant, or frightening work [1]. Graham wrote a [separate essay](http://www.paulgraham.com/schlep.html) on the condition it induces, which he calls "schlep blindness."

## The Mechanism

Most programmers wish they could start a startup by just writing brilliant code, pushing it to a server, and having users pay them lots of money [1]. They would prefer not to deal with tedious problems or get involved in messy ways with the real world. This is a reasonable preference, because such things slow you down. But this preference is so widespread that the space of convenient startup ideas has been stripped pretty clean [1].

"If you let your mind wander a few blocks down the street to the messy, tedious ideas, you'll find valuable ones just sitting there waiting to be implemented" [1].

## Stripe as the Canonical Example

Stripe is the most striking example of what happens when you turn off the schlep filter [1]. Thousands of programmers were in a position to see the payments opportunity. Thousands knew how painful it was to process payments before Stripe. But when they looked for startup ideas, they did not see this one, because unconsciously they shrank from having to deal with payments [1].

Dealing with payments is a schlep for Stripe, but not an intolerable one. The result of most people avoiding the idea: Stripe had comparatively smooth sailing in areas that are sometimes painful, like user acquisition. "They didn't have to try very hard to make themselves heard by users, because users were desperately waiting for what they were building" [1].

## The Unsexy Filter

The unsexy filter is a related but distinct concept [1]. While the schlep filter prevents you from working on problems you fear, the unsexy filter prevents you from working on problems you despise or find boring.

Graham and his co-founders overcame the unsexy filter to work on Viaweb. There were interesting things about the architecture of their software, but they were not interested in ecommerce per se. They could see the problem was one that needed to be solved [1].

Turning off the schlep filter is more important than turning off the unsexy filter for two reasons [1]:
1. The schlep filter is more likely to be an illusion -- the feared schleps may not be as bad as imagined
2. It is a worse form of self-indulgence -- "Starting a successful startup is going to be fairly laborious no matter what. Even if the product doesn't entail a lot of schleps, you'll still have plenty dealing with investors, hiring and firing people, and so on"

The unsexy filter, while still a source of error, is not entirely useless. If you are at the leading edge of a rapidly changing field, your ideas about what is sexy will be somewhat correlated with what is valuable. And if you find an idea sexy, you will work on it more enthusiastically [1].

## How to Overcome the Filters

Graham recommends several specific tactics [1]:

1. **Actively seek schlepy ideas.** Instead of merely turning off the filter, seek out ideas that are unsexy or involve schleps. "Don't try to start Twitter. Those ideas are so rare that you can't find them by looking for them. Make something unsexy that people will pay you for" [1].

2. **Ask what you wish someone else would build.** "What would you pay for right now?" This bypasses both the schlep and unsexy filters because you are imagining yourself as the customer [1].

3. **Look for dying industries.** "Since startups often garbage-collect broken companies and industries, it can be a good trick to look for those that are dying, or deserve to, and try to imagine what kind of company would profit from their demise" [1].

4. **Find disdained markets.** When big players ignore or look down on a market, that disdain often misleads them. HP turned down the Apple I because it used a TV as a monitor, which seemed intolerably declasse [1].

## The Connection to Starting a Startup Generally

Graham argues in his 2014 Stanford lecture that startups involve unglamorous but necessary schleps [2]. This is distinct from the bogus work that plagues big companies and school. "In a startup you have to spend a lot of time on schleps, but this sort of work is merely unglamorous, not bogus" [2].

The schlep filter is one of several reasons that the method of deliberately trying to think of startup ideas produces worse results than organic discovery. When you try to think of ideas, the schlep filter is active. When you naturally notice problems in your own life, you have already accepted the schlep because you are living with the problem [1].
