# Default Alive or Default Dead

"Default Alive or Default Dead" is a diagnostic framework introduced by [[Paul Graham]] in October 2015 [1]. It poses a single question to startups past their first 8-9 months: assuming expenses remain constant and revenue growth continues on its current trend, does the company make it to profitability on the money it has left?

"Or to put it more dramatically, by default do they live or die?" [1]

## The Diagnostic

The question is binary. If default alive, you can discuss ambitious new things. If default dead, you need to talk about how to save the company [1].

Half the founders Graham talks to do not know the answer [1]. Trevor Blackwell built a [calculator](http://growth.tlb.org/#) to help founders determine their status.

The reason founders don't know: it is not a question that makes sense to ask early on, "any more than it makes sense to ask a 3-year-old how he plans to support himself" [1]. But as the company grows older, the question switches from meaningless to critical, and that switch often takes people by surprise [1].

Graham proposes starting to ask too early rather than too late. "It's probably not that dangerous to start worrying too early that you're default dead, whereas it's very dangerous to start worrying too late" [1].

## The Fatal Pinch

The most dangerous state is what Graham calls the "fatal pinch": default dead + slow growth + not enough time to fix it [1]. Founders end up there by not realizing that is where they are headed.

A secondary cause: founders assume it will be easy to raise more money. "But that assumption is often false, and worse still, the more you depend on it, the falser it becomes" [1].

Graham suggests making the assumption explicit: "Say 'We're default dead, but we're counting on investors to save us.' Maybe as you say that, it will set off the same alarms in your head that it does in mine" [1].

## The Investor Dependency Problem

It would be safe to be default dead if you could count on investors. But you cannot. Investor interest is a function of growth, and sometimes something about your business will spook investors even if growth is great [1].

The rule: fundraising should never be more than a plan A. "You should always have a plan B as well: you should know (as in write down) precisely what you'll need to do to survive if you can't raise more money, and precisely when you'll have to switch to plan B if plan A isn't working" [1].

Even with steep revenue growth (over 5x a year), you can only start to count on investor interest, never rely on it [1].

## Growth vs. Spending: The False Dichotomy

"In practice there is surprisingly little connection between how much a startup spends and how fast it grows" [1]. When growth is fast, it is because the product hits a nerve. When spending is high, it is because the product is expensive to build or sell, or the company is wasteful [1].

These are independent variables, not a tradeoff. The implication is that cutting spending rarely hurts growth and often does not affect it at all.

## Overhiring: The #1 Killer

"Don't hire too fast. Hiring too fast is by far the biggest killer of startups that raise money" [1].

Three reasons founders overhire:
1. There is so much work to do that they assume hiring will get it done
2. Successful startups have lots of employees, so it seems necessary
3. They do not want to face the real reason for slow growth: the product is not appealing enough [1]

VCs encourage overhiring because kill-or-cure strategies are optimal for portfolios. "VCs want to blow you up, in one sense of the phrase or the other. But as a founder your incentives are different. You want above all to survive" [1].

Sam Altman added: "I think it's roughly correct to say that YC's most successful companies have never been the fastest to hire, and one of the marks of a great founder is being able to resist this urge" [1].

## The Common Death Spiral

The typical pattern [1]:

1. Startup makes something moderately appealing
2. Decent initial growth
3. First round raised easily (founders seem smart, idea sounds plausible)
4. Growth is ok but not great
5. Founders convince themselves hiring will boost growth; investors agree
6. Growth still doesn't come (product is only moderately appealing)
7. Running out of runway with high expenses and slow growth
8. Unappealing to investors for next round
9. Company dies

What they should have done: address the fundamental problem with the product. Hire slowly. Seek growth by [[Doing Things That Don't Scale]], or by redesigning the product in the way only founders can [1].

## The Airbnb Counter-Example

Airbnb waited 4 months after raising money at the end of YC before hiring their first employee [1]. The founders were terribly overworked, but they were overworked evolving Airbnb into the organism it became. This is the model Graham holds up: founders doing the work themselves, staying lean, letting the product evolve before adding headcount [1].

## Premature Scaling

Paul Buchheit identifies a related pattern: "founders take a small business that isn't really working (bad unit economics, typically) and then scale it up because they want impressive growth numbers. This is similar to over-hiring in that it makes the business much harder to fix once it's big, plus they are bleeding cash really fast" [1].

## The Alternative Path

If you are default dead, instead of hiring to boost growth, Graham recommends [1]:
- Doing things that don't scale (see [[Doing Things That Don't Scale]])
- Redesigning the product -- something only founders can do effectively at the early stage
- Operating cheaply to extend runway
- Having an explicit written plan B for survival without more funding

The alarm bells from asking "am I default alive or dead?" can counteract the forces pushing you to overhire and instead compel you toward the paths to growth that actually work for most startups [1].
