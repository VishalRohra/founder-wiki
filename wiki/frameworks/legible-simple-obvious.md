# Legible, Simple, Obvious

A framework from [[Kevin Hale]] for designing pitch deck slides that investors can understand and remember [1]. The framework is built on a single premise: before anyone can remember your ideas, they have to understand them. If a founder just communicates clearly, they will do better than 99% of startups.

## The Three Principles

![Legible, Simple, Obvious](../raw/posts/images/4T-how-to-design-a-better-pitch-deck/35131887eed3.png)

**Legible**: The audience can physically read the slide. Use large type, bold text, a simple font, and good contrast from the background. Text at the top is easier to read from the back of a large room. At Demo Day, the room holds over 500 people, many of whom are older with imperfect eyesight [1].

**Simple**: Each slide expresses one idea. The word "simple" shares a root with "complex" -- the Latin "plex" meaning braid, twist, or fold. Simple ideas are "one fold." A simple slide does not intertwine multiple ideas. Since there are only five to seven key points, the deck should have only about five to seven slides [1].

**Obvious**: The slide can be understood at a glance. Test: show it to a stranger and ask what it means. If they do not immediately say your idea, you lose. Obviousness matters because Demo Day presentations are 2 minutes and 30 seconds, and investors are easily distracted. "If they don't get your point right away, they will check their email" [1].

## How to Apply

1. Start with legibility and simplicity.
2. Make the idea explicit. Add a direct caption or label that states the takeaway. Hale calls this "CliffsNotes right on the slide." A growth graph without labels requires study; a growth graph captioned "10x growth in 6 months" is instant [1].
3. Remove information distractions. Each additional piece of information increases comprehension time logarithmically ([Hick's Law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hick%27s_law)). Diagrams are "little mazes for ideas" [1].
4. Avoid screenshots, screencasts, and videos. They are almost always illegible, complex, and non-obvious. Replace with simplified bulleted steps describing the user flow [1].

## The One Exception

Deliberate complexity is acceptable when the point is to overwhelm the audience to illustrate how hard a problem is. Hale cites Magic (YC company), which showed a grid of every on-demand service logo across every industry to illustrate the scope of the problem their software solves [1].

## Anti-Patterns

- Too much text
- Excessive explanations and caveats
- Excessive branding per slide
- Photos without titles or captions
- Animations and transitions
- Memes, subtle humor, accidental humor

"Investors invest in teams not slides. Your slides should make your ideas more clear. Don't let your slides distract investors from what you're saying out loud" [1].
