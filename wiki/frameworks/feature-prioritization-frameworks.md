# Feature Prioritization Frameworks

[[Emmett Shear]] describes three mental frameworks for deciding what to build next, drawn from his experience building [[Justin.tv]] and [[Twitch]] [1]. Startups typically progress through them in order: Built For Me, then Switch To Us, then Three Numbers Matter, as the type of challenge shifts [1].

## The Three Frameworks

### Built For Me

You are the primary exemplar user. Build what you wish the product did [1]. Justin.tv originated this way -- the founders wanted to make a live reality TV show and built the technology for themselves. Twitch started similarly: Shear personally enjoyed the video game streaming part of Justin.tv [1].

**Best when:** You truly love your product, the team is small (hard to scale intuition), and you have genuine access to your own desires [1]. Warning: "'I think I want this because it's what I believe I'm supposed to want' feels subjectively very much like 'I want this'" [1].

**Why it matters strategically:** Many of the biggest startups started this way because "it gives you an automatic advantage against big incumbents who can't use the same method" [1]. Big companies can only replicate this with "hack weeks" that unleash the "Built For Me capacity latent inside any organization" [1].

The one exception at scale: when the CEO is the lead product designer (Steve Jobs at Apple), a big company can escape the dilemma and do "ground-breaking category creation for a product that's great for difficult-to-measure reasons" [1].

### Switch To Us

People are already doing the behavior you want. You want them to do it with your product instead [1].

**Process** [1]:
1. Interview 5-6 dedicated users each from your service, each competing service, and closely-adjacent services (yields ~50 interviews).
2. Categorize responses and score them in a spreadsheet.
3. Look for patterns. Double down on strengths, shore up weaknesses. Target one competitor at a time.
4. Prefer things you can build fast -- "customers don't care how hard something is to build, just how effective it is for their problem."

**The anti-goal: validation.** "Validating an idea you already have is an anti-goal. You can't improve an idea through any amount of validation. The goal of this process is to have better ideas as much as it is to prioritize them" [1].

**Best when:** Your product is important enough that customers have articulate opinions (prosumers, SMBs -- not casual consumers who "may have no idea what it is about SnapChat that they like"), and you are selling "a better mousetrap, rather than inventing the mousetrap category" [1].

**Suggested Interview Questions** [1]:
- What do you like about your current solution?
- What don't you like about it?
- How did you wind up choosing your current solution?
- What were you doing before?
- What would you do if you were the CEO of the current company's product you're using?
- What's the single most annoying thing about your current solution?
- What's the single coolest new thing about your current solution?
- Is there anything that would convince you to switch to using our product immediately if we built it?
- If you had a magic wand and could create any experience you wanted, how would it work?
- What's one thing about your current solution that's surprisingly hard?
- Follow up with "interesting, tell me more" or "why is that?" -- "People know more than they think they do, they just need a good interviewer to draw it out of them."

### Three Numbers Matter

Identify the three most important qualities of your product for your customer, then relentlessly move those metrics [1].

**Examples** [1]:
- **Amazon**: Lower prices, more selection, faster/more reliable shipping
- **Twitch**: Audience size, positive interactions, money (for streamers)

**Why three:** "Empirically, three seems to be the maximum number of focus areas that people can keep in mind consistently. Think about how lists like 'X, Y, and Z' feels natural, but 'W, X, Y, and Z' does not" [1].

A bonus fourth area -- "Stuff You Have To Do" -- covers necessities like PCI compliance and uptime that "should be done, but should not be included in the top level three metrics" [1].

The approach is recursive: each metric can be decomposed into three sub-drivers. Allocate investment by intuition: "maybe it's 33/33/33, maybe it's 50/25/25" [1].

**Best when:** People choose your product for measurable reasons [1]. "Good for making utilitarian things, less good for making games and toys and beautiful UX" [1].

**On operational excellence:** For startups, "it's rare that you can win through pure operational excellence. Usually you need to do just enough operational excellence that it's acceptable to your customers, and then focus on delivering on what your customers really care about" [1].

## Transitions Between Frameworks

Twitch has been running primarily on Three Numbers Matter, but returns to Switch To Us for new customer segments [1]. When launching a new music category, the team talked to artists livestreaming elsewhere to understand their needs. "As the category grows, we have begun to identify the set of metrics that matter most for us and we will begin to transition towards Three Numbers Matter instead" [1].

## The Hard Case

Some products defy all three frameworks: you are building a brand new mousetrap, people choose it for hard-to-measure reasons, and you are not the end user [1]. "It's really really hard!" This constrains big companies more than small ones, since Built For Me does not scale. The result: big companies tend to work on products where drivers are measurable or on incremental innovation [1].
