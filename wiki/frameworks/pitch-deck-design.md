# Pitch Deck Design

[[Kevin Hale]] presents a framework for designing pitch decks based on three principles: **legible**, **simple**, and **obvious** [1]. The framework is adapted from a presentation he gives to YC startups before Demo Day. The core insight: "Investors invest in teams not slides. Your slides should make your ideas more clear" [1].

There are probably 100 reasons why a company is great, but people can only remember a few after a short pitch [1]. With each startup, YC identifies the 5-7 most important ideas investors should know. From Prototype Day experience, "you'll be lucky if they can remember even 1 or 2 of those points in the context of 100 other companies also presenting alongside you" [1].

"If you just communicate your points clearly, you'll do better than 99% of startups. Because before anyone can remember, they have to understand" [1].

## The Three Principles

### 1. Legible

"If they can't read it, they won't be able to understand it" [1]. At Demo Day, 500+ people fill the room. Many are older with imperfect eyesight. Legible slides work for everyone [1]:

- Use large type
- Bold text
- A simple font
- Good contrast from the background
- Text at the top (easier to read from the back of the room)

**Shred Video example**: Their Prototype Day slide explaining what the company builds had text too small and too skinny [1]. The text explaining their technology was in "light gray at the bottom of the slide." Their technology (software that automatically edits videos from hours of raw footage into professional-looking output in seconds) was hidden. By Demo Day, they had simplified the description and made the text "large and in charge" [1].

### 2. Simple

Simple and complex share the same word origin: "plex" means braid, twist, or fold [1]. A simple idea is one fold, one idea. A simple slide expresses one idea. "Do not crowd your slides with multiple ideas" [1].

Since there are only 5-7 ideas to communicate, there should be about 5-7 slides [1].

**Afrostream example**: An early slide tried to convey both what they built and who it was for, with too much text including "all the little nuances of their business" [1]. The Demo Day version was "short, sweet, and easy to recollect" [1].

### 3. Obvious

Obvious slides can be understood at a glance [1]. Test: "Show it to a stranger and ask them to tell you what it means. If they don't immediately say your idea, you lose" [1].

Speed of understanding matters because [1]:
1. Demo Day presentations are 2 minutes and 30 seconds
2. "People are easily distracted" -- investors especially so: "If they don't get your point right away, they will check their email."

**How to make things obvious** [1]:

**Make it explicit.** A growth graph alone is not explicit. A growth graph with a bold caption stating "10x growth in 6 months" is explicit. "It's like I put CliffsNotes right on the slide" [1].

**Avoid information distractions.** [Hick's Law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hick%27s_law): each piece of information added increases comprehension time logarithmically [1]. Diagrams are "like little mazes for ideas" that make the path to the idea too long [1].

Distractions to avoid [1]:
- Too much text
- Excessive explanations and caveats
- Excessive branding per slide
- Photos with no titles or captions
- Animations, transitions
- Memes
- Subtle humor
- Accidental humor

"Don't try to make the slides the thing they'll remember" [1].

### The Exception

Hale allows one exception: when the point is to show complexity or overwhelm the audience [1]. **Magic example**: A slide showing every on-demand service across industries, markets, and verticals on a grid. Normally showing all potential competitors is not ideal, but the logos represent the problem (users need separate accounts for each service) and the opportunity (every service Magic can charge a premium on top of). "It's overwhelming for a reason" [1].

## On Screenshots

Hale describes screenshots as the worst offenders: "Screenshots are almost always illegible, complex, and non-obvious. They break all 3 rules!" [1]. The text in most interfaces is too small (not legible), most interfaces do multiple things (not simple), and most screenshots take longer than a glance to understand (not obvious) [1].

Instead of showing product screenshots, show a simplified version of what the product does [1]. A bulleted list of steps is Hale's preferred technique. **SparkGift example**: A bulleted list explaining product steps was "far better at explaining the ease of use" than any actual UI workflow [1]. **Meadow example**: Explained their product in just two points, which was "far better than any combination of buttons, links and chrome" [1].

This guidance applies equally to screencasts and videos: "Think twice before trying to use either one" [1].
