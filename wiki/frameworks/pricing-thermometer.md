# Pricing Thermometer

The Pricing Thermometer is a framework introduced by [[Kevin Hale]] that makes the forces behind pricing visible and actionable [1]. Three variables sit on a vertical axis: cost at the bottom, price in the middle, and value at the top.

## The Two Gaps

**Gap between cost and price = Incentive to Sell.** The larger this margin, the more the company is motivated to push the product -- invest in salespeople, marketing, and distribution [1].

**Gap between price and value = Incentive to Buy.** The larger this gap, the easier it is for the customer to say yes. The customer perceives they are getting a good deal [1].

Most startups fail by placing their price too close to cost (leaving little incentive to sell) or too close to value (leaving little incentive to buy) -- or, most commonly, they place it too close to cost and too far below value, undercharging significantly [1].

## Two Approaches to Setting Price

**Cost-plus pricing**: Start with cost, add a margin. Simple but leaves money on the table [1].

**Value-based pricing**: Start with the value the customer perceives, work backward. Allows charging more and manipulating the customer's incentive to buy. Hale states this is the approach "almost consistently across all businesses, everyone will tell you, you should strive for" [1].

The challenge: startups often do not understand their costs, their value, or both -- so the price ends up in an arbitrary location [1].

## The 10/5/20 Rule

Hale provides a concrete heuristic for founders who do not know where to begin [1]:

1. **10X**: Set a price that is one-tenth of the perceived value. A $10 product should be easily perceived as worth $100.
2. **5%**: Raise prices by 5% at a time.
3. **20%**: Keep raising until 20% of deals are lost. That is the approximate sweet spot.

## Application

The thermometer explains why charging less than competitors rarely works for startups. Early adopters are not price-sensitive -- they care about benefits, not bargains. A low price creates reputation risk ("why is it too good to be true?") and shrinks the incentive to sell, limiting what the startup can invest in distribution [1].
