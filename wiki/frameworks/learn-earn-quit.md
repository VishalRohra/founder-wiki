# Learn, Earn, or Quit

[[Garry Tan]] proposes a two-axis framework for evaluating any job or career position: are you learning, and are you earning? [1]. If both, you are in a great place. If one, that can be acceptable. If neither, "it's time to move on. You're wasting your life" [1].

## Learning

Tan recommends prioritizing learning first, especially early in a career. His friend Steven Sinofsky calls this "the protein before the carbs" [1].

Learning starts with self-knowledge: "What do you like? What do you enjoy? What allows you to enter a state of flow?" Then acquiring skills: investing the effort to become genuinely good at something [1].

Tan divides skill sets into two categories [1]:

- **Makers**: design, product, and engineering
- **Hustlers**: management, sales, and marketing

### How to Start Learning

For hustlers: learn to market or sell. Marketing means "helping other people communicate their problem and how their solution is the best." For sales: "go get those customers. Without even asking, send those customers to a founder, and they'll always take your email" [1].

For builders: find a problem, build a first version that solves it. "When you're starting out, it sort of maybe doesn't even matter what that problem exactly is." Tan notes that several successful founders started by making gradebook software because they were in school and had access to teachers frustrated with their grade books. They did not stay in education -- "they ended up making billion dollar companies in lots of other spaces, but they learned to create in gradebook software" [1].

### Building a Portfolio Over Credentials

Tan argues that a demonstrated ability to create is more powerful than any credential. "If you can point to a beautiful app or website or GitHub repo with elegant code that you created, you have done a thing that will almost always set you up to be a top ten percent player off the bat with no credentials" [1].

Tan's personal example: at fourteen, he got his first job cold-calling the Yellow Pages internet section. His credential was a website for his junior high school underground newspaper that had won design awards [1].

## Earning

Earning means putting money in your pocket [1]. Tan identifies equity as the highest-leverage form of earning: "To make the most amount of money, you need to create true wealth by owning the stock of a company that became worth a lot of money" [1].

### For Founders

Starting a company means starting with 100% of a pie that may be worth zero. "If you do manage to get to [[Product-Market Fit|product-market fit]], you could have a company with an exit in the tens of millions, hundreds of millions, or billions of dollars. If you own ten to thirty percent of that company by then, you could be a centimillionaire to billionaire" [1].

### For Employees

Early employees may hold 0.25% to 2% or more. "It can still be an outrageously good outcome, and you might not even have to take as big a risk." Being early at a fast-growing company means the equity may already be worth something meaningful even if the pie was not started from zero [1].

### The Hedonic Treadmill Warning

Tan warns about the earning trap: earning money to spend it on bigger houses, nicer cars, and lifestyle inflation. "Before you know it, you're running on a hedonic treadmill." For founders-in-waiting, the advice is: save the money. "If you're young and still thrifty, don't change your lifestyle. That money you spend needs to be saved so that you can invest your time in creating your own startup" [1].

## Learn and Earn Together

The best position is learning and earning simultaneously [1]. Tan uses his own career as an illustration:

- At Palantir: learning to run teams and build product while earning equity in a $40 billion company [1]
- At Y Combinator: learning to invest and work with earliest-stage startups while earning carry [1]
- At Initialized Capital: learning from founders daily while building his own venture firm [1]

## Neither Learning Nor Earning

The worst position: not learning, not earning, and not enjoying the work. "Sometimes these high-paying jobs that feel meaningless are jobs." Tan references David Graeber's concept of "bullshit jobs" -- work that exists for political or structural reasons rather than productive ones [1].

Tan recalls interning at a bad startup during college: an enterprise software company that raised money but never closed customers. "I didn't earn much because interns don't get paid a lot, but I didn't learn very much either. It was basically a wash" [1].

The prescription: "If you're not learning, you don't have a way to earn equity or even a good salary, and especially if it's not fun or meaningful for you, you gotta quit" [1].

## The Recommended Sequence

Tan recommends learning first, then earning [1]. Reversing the order -- earning first -- means slower learning and risk of lifestyle lock-in. "If you try and do it in reverse and you try to earn first, you'll probably still learn but maybe slowly, and you don't know what you're going to give up" [1].
