# The Midwit Meme

The midwit meme, as applied to startups by [[Dalton Caldwell]] and [[Michael Seibel]], captures a recurring pattern: the naive beginner and the experienced expert arrive at the same simple conclusion, while the person with intermediate knowledge overthinks themselves into paralysis [1]. The framework is a diagnostic for recognizing when founders are overcomplicating decisions that should be straightforward.

## The Structure

In the meme format, three levels of intelligence are depicted. The "idiot" and the "genius" reach the same simple answer. The "midwit" in the middle constructs an elaborate, multi-variable analysis that leads to worse outcomes [1].

Caldwell explains why this maps to startup life: founders feel lost, have no boss, have no constraints, and "tend to overthink everything. In every decision, you kind of think in circles over and over again" [1].

## Elon Musk as the Canonical Example

Seibel and Caldwell use Elon Musk to illustrate that the "idiot" approach and the "genius" approach are often indistinguishable [1]:

- **SpaceX**: Musk went to Russia to buy rockets. The Russian government could not tell if he was brilliant or insane. "Is that genius or stupid? I can't tell" [1].
- **Tesla**: The simple version: "Electric cars are good." The midwit version: no one has successfully started a car company since the 1950s, the infrastructure does not exist, every prior attempt failed. The genius version: "Electric cars are good" [1].
- **The Boring Company**: "Traffic is bad. What if we dug a hole under the ground?" The midwit response involves bus system optimization and transportation theory. "Drill tunnel to avoid traffic is certainly at the extremes" [1].

The key insight: "If he was an overthinker, he would rule all this stuff out. All this stuff is obviously a bad idea. And anyone in the midwit territory would rule out all these ideas" [1]. The hard work is in the implementation, but the initial idea is simple.

## Applied to Startup Decisions

### Fundraising

Caldwell created an actual midwit meme for YC founders nervous about fundraising. The naive founder says: "I need $800K, so I'll raise $800K." The experienced founder says: "I need $800K, so I'll raise $800K." The midwit wants to "construct the perfect round," worries about signaling risk, allocation for value-added investors, and spends 15 levels deep in a hypothetical for an offer they do not have [1].

### MVPs and Launching

Extremes: build something fast and ship it. The midwit worries about bad reputation from bugs, believes they only have one shot to launch, fears competitors will copy the idea, debates between TechCrunch, New York Times, and Hacker News coverage, and considers rebranding because a similar name exists [1].

### Co-Founders

Extremes: "It's fun to work with my friends, so I'll work with my friend." The midwit wants a co-founder with five to seven years at Google who reached at least level seven. The founders of Twitch chose their co-founder because "this guy helped us find an apartment" [1].

### Product-Market Fit

Extremes: "Did I sleep last night?" (If you have time to wonder whether you have PMF, you probably do not.) The midwit creates 15 different metrics and a complicated framework to determine if they might have "early product-market fit" [1].

### Applying to YC

Extremes: fill out the application and submit it. The midwit spends months on application strategy, seeks introductions to alumni, gets recommendations from strangers, and sends drafts to 15 people for feedback. "Not correlated with success" [1].

## Beginner's Mind

Seibel connects the midwit meme to the Zen concept of beginner's mind: "What would an idiot do?" is actually how you divine the genius approach. "Everyone can reach for the idiot approach. Everyone has the intellectual capacity to reach for it" [1].

The advantage of youth maps to this: young founders "think everything is easy and none of the innumerable reasons why something might be hard even occurs to them" [1]. The worst position is knowing just enough to be afraid: "You know just enough to know why the things you're trying are hard and the odds are stacked against you. And that freaks you out" [1].

## Trap Door Decisions: When to Overthink

Not everything should be simplified. Caldwell identifies categories where careful thinking is warranted [1]:

- **Legal issues**: "Don't break any law. Saying you didn't know there was a law is not a great legal defense"
- **Regulatory issues**: Especially in healthcare, finance, and regulated industries
- **People's health and well-being**: "Let's measure twice, cut once" for online pharmacies and similar products
- **Distinguishing high-stakes from low-stakes**: Good founders are skilled at determining which decisions fall into the overthink-worthy category and which do not

The framework is not "never think." It is: most of the work is not in optimizing decisions. "Most of the work is launching, getting the first customer, selling. It's doing these things yourself" [1].

## Why Brains Reject Simplicity

Caldwell argues that unless you have personally witnessed a successful startup from the inside, "your brain rejects the idea" that the beginning was silly and simple. "It had to be lots of strategy meetings and whiteboarding, flow charts, Gartner Magic Quadrants" [1]. The reality of how big companies actually got built is hard to believe for people who have not seen it firsthand.
