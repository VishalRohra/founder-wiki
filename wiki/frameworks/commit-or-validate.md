# Commit or Validate

[[Dalton Caldwell]] and [[Michael Seibel]] argue that general startup advice fails when applied uniformly to two fundamentally different types of founders: those who bring deep domain expertise and those who are building expertise from scratch [1]. The framework determines how much a founder should trust their gut versus how much they should validate through customer research.

## The Two Archetypes

**Founder with expertise**: Someone who worked at a company, built something valuable there, saw it deployed and used, and left to productize that tool for a broader market. Or: someone who has built and launched consumer products before and has developed taste for what works [1].

**Founder without expertise**: Two people fresh out of college, technically capable and motivated, but without deep knowledge of any specific problem domain. They are smart but "don't have a lot of preferences. Or to the extent you have preferences, they're the same as everyone else" [1].

Seibel observes these two patterns constantly at YC, and the advice for each is "precisely different" even when the startup idea is identical [1].

## When to Trust Your Gut

Founders with expertise should lean more heavily on their intuition [1]. Seibel's guidance: "If you were inside of a company, you built a tool, everyone at the company uses the tool, it's still being used even though you don't work there anymore, you probably know more than you think" [1].

The advice for such founders is: "Make something that would impress you. Make something that you would be proud of." When you can tell whether the product is good because it solves a problem you personally have or have solved before, that self-evaluation is a reliable signal [1].

Caldwell extends this to arcane technical domains: "If you wanted to build a new compiler, if you wanted to build something that's really arcane, but that you know a lot about and you have a lot of taste, again a lot of opinions about, a lot of expertise on, yes, often you should listen to that expertise" [1].

### The Concrete Example

Caldwell illustrates with a real estate software startup [1]. Category A: a founder who spent ten years at a mortgage underwriting company building software. "Your judgment about knowing what mortgage underwriters want, if you've been building software inside of a mortgage underwriting company for ten years, it's probably pretty good." Such founders should trust their gut [1].

Category B: founders with no industry experience beyond having bought a house. "These folks can still be successful, but those folks should probably trust their gut a little bit less that they know precisely what to build" [1].

## When to Validate

Founders without deep expertise should focus on building expertise through rapid learning [1]. Seibel advises: "If you can start in a good general idea area, a good place where building expertise here will be valuable, and if you don't over hamper yourself with a complicated plan, you give yourself opportunity to build, to figure something out" [1].

The failure mode for founders without expertise is overconfidence: planning out their hiring, reading Goldman Sachs reports about market sizes, and budgeting two years to build a "comprehensive solution." The better approach: "Build the smallest thing possible and learn some more" [1].

Seibel also recommends choosing a domain you are excited to learn about, because "you have a lot of learning to do." Choosing an area you hate because you think it will raise money is "not the best play" [1].

## The Crossed-Wires Problem

The most common mistake is when the two archetypes behave like each other [1]. Caldwell identifies both failure modes:

**The expert who acts like a novice**: An experienced founder with deep domain knowledge hesitates and over-validates. Common rationalizations include: "Maybe the thing I built was only useful at my company and no one else will want it," or "I know what to build and they want it, but I don't think VCs will fund it" [1]. Seibel adds a specific version: founders who abandon their actual expertise because it does not involve AI, building something AI-related instead "because we think we need to say it's AI related to raise money" [1].

**The novice who acts like an expert**: A founder with no domain knowledge builds an elaborate plan as if they already know the answer. They are "planning out their hiring and talking about how much money they need and reading Goldman Sachs reports about market sizes" instead of learning the basics of their chosen domain [1].

## How to Self-Diagnose

Seibel offers a self-diagnostic: if you find you "don't have a lot of opinions about what to work on" or your opinions are "very similar to the fiftieth percentile of other people," you are probably in the validate-first category [1]. The question is whether you are bringing expertise or building expertise, and then implementing a plan based on that honest assessment [1].

For founders who do have expertise, Seibel's test: "Would you have liked this? Would you have bought it? Would you have talked to yourself? Would you have read this email that you just sent about this thing?" These tests all work when you have pre-existing experience [1].
