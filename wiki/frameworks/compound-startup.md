# The Compound Startup

The compound startup is a concept articulated by [[Parker Conrad]] to describe a software company that builds multiple interoperable applications on a shared platform layer, rather than focusing on a single point solution [1]. Rippling is the canonical example: it is not just payroll, not just benefits, not just IT provisioning, but all of these working together through a unified employee data layer [1].

## The Thesis

The core argument: many of the deeper problems within organizations -- business process management, decision-making, coordination -- cannot be solved by narrow software that addresses only one function. A suite of seamlessly interoperable applications can solve problems that individual tools cannot [1].

Conrad traces this approach to older enterprise software companies -- SAP, Oracle, Microsoft, and to some extent Salesforce -- which were compound businesses before the term existed. The SaaS era created a temporary window where "you could go out and do this really narrow thing and turn it into a SaaS company," with most such companies reaching low-single-digit billions in value [1].

That window is closing. The landscape is overcrowded with point solutions. Sales and marketing has gotten harder and more expensive: "Public companies are spending 50% more on sales and marketing but they're adding 10% less in new ARR with that increase in spend" [1].

## Advantages Over Point Solutions

1. **Deeper problem-solving**: Compound software can automate business processes that cross functional boundaries, something no point solution can do alone [1].
2. **Platform investment**: Shared capabilities like analytics, approvals, permissions, and workflow automations benefit all applications. "Most of our competitors just can't go as deep on that stuff" [1].
3. **Higher wallet share**: When all functions are in one system, revenue per customer is much higher. One of Conrad's early Rippling memos calculated that combining multiple functions raised per-user-per-month pricing from $115 to hundreds of dollars [1].
4. **Stronger moat**: "There's this island of product-market fit that's kind of over the edge of the horizon line that's sort of harder to get to. But if you can get there... it's much harder to displace" [1].

## Limitations and Risks

Conrad is candid about the narrowness of the opportunity: "It's actually a deeply cynical view of software markets because I think it's possible that compound software businesses are the wave of the future. And also, there will be three of them" [1].

The approach requires enormous R&D investment, years of building before launch (Rippling spent roughly two years with mostly engineers before going to market), and founders with deep domain conviction. Conrad advises caution: "I'm not sure that it's like good advice" for most companies [1].

## Relationship to Asian Tech Companies

[[Garry Tan]] observes that compound software resembles Asian super-app strategies more than typical American startup approaches. Conrad agrees it has parallels but locates the closer analogy in pre-SaaS enterprise software: companies built 20+ years ago that were compound from the start [1].
