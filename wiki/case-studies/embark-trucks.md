# Embark Trucks: From Campus Shuttles to Self-Driving Semis

Embark Trucks (YC W16) went public, completing a journey from a YC batch team of 19-20 year olds to a publicly traded self-driving truck company. [[Geoff Ralston]], who interviewed the founders in late 2015, describes the trajectory [1].

## YC Interview: Age as a Red Flag, Then Irrelevant

When Ralston saw Alex Rodrigues, Brandon Moak, and former co-founder Michael apply to YC, the immediate concern was whether these teenagers were serious or "merely playing with golf carts and having a good time while never planning to leave school" [1].

The interview dispelled that concern. "Their thinking was startlingly clear and robust. Their answers were direct and to the point. They had a plan and were serious and, by the way, they'd already built the first self-driving vehicle in Canada" [1]. Ralston's conclusion: "Those qualities make age irrelevant" [1].

## The Original Idea: Constrained Environments

The team entered YC as Varden Labs, building self-driving shuttles for campus environments where roads were controlled and speeds low. This was a deliberate constraint: "the first self-driving vehicles would be delivered in a highly constrained environment because the unconstrained problem was, frankly, too difficult" [1].

Two problems emerged during the batch. Campus environments were less constrained than expected, and each campus presented unique challenges that made generalization difficult [1].

## Resisting a Premature Pivot

An Australian mining company approached the team about building self-driving mining trucks in another constrained environment. The team was tempted but chose to stay the course, continue signing up campuses, and deliver a strong Demo Day. Ralston calls this "one of the early, impressively focused decisions" [1].

## The Highway Pivot

Despite initial success, Alex and Brandon eventually concluded the campus market was too small. They pivoted to self-driving trucks constrained to highways -- "an enormous market and although still fabulously difficult, a more tractable problem" [1]. They persuaded their investors to stay on, built their first self-driving truck quickly, and continued to demonstrate the execution speed that had impressed Ralston from the start [1].

## IPO

Ralston describes the founding team's defining traits as "energy, optimism, and above all, the clarity of their thinking" [1]. The IPO was "but one step" in the company's trajectory [1].
