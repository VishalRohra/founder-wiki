# PlanGrid: Building Software for an Industry That Had Never Used It

PlanGrid (YC W12) built mobile construction productivity software. The company was acquired by Autodesk for $875 million in 2018, making it one of the largest acquisitions of a YC company at the time. Co-founded by [[Tracy Young]] and four others, PlanGrid grew to 450 employees and solved a problem the founders experienced firsthand as construction engineers [1].

## Origin: The Saddest Story in Silicon Valley

Two of PlanGrid's five co-founders were construction engineers; three were software developers. Young wanted to be an architect but was rejected from architectural programs, leading her to construction engineering. On job sites, she performed QA/QC -- taking specifications and blueprints into the field and checking them against the actual construction [1].

The idea came when co-founder Ryan Sutton-Gee tried to open construction blueprints on a first-generation iPad. The screen displayed "out of memory" because blueprints are 10,000 by 10,000 pixels, far too large for the device to handle. Sutton-Gee's reaction was immediate: "We should build this" [1].

What followed was what Sutton-Gee called "the saddest story in Silicon Valley": having a great idea but no technical co-founders. Young and Sutton-Gee pitched the idea to programmer friends. All agreed it was good. None would quit their jobs [1]. Co-founder Antoine, a high-frequency trading engineer in Chicago, told them: "This is a fantastic idea. You guys should learn how to program" [1].

The breakthrough came accidentally. Young's boyfriend Ralph (now her husband) saw a recurring Thursday night calendar event labeled "peanuts" on Young's shared Google Calendar. When she explained the idea, he was offended: "Do you even know what I do? I'm a full-stack engineer. I'm the best engineer I know. I write iOS and Android apps on the side. I'm a rendering engineer at Pixar Animation. Why have you not told me about this before?" [1]. Ralph became a co-founder and eventually CTO.

## First Users: Lending iPads and Walking Through the App Store

PlanGrid started as a "fun project" with no expectation it could become a business. Young realized it was a real business when they sold their first million dollars without a sales team, roughly two years after starting [1].

The first 20 users were people the founders had worked with on construction projects or gone to university with. Young called them every week: "Hey it's Tracy, just checking in. How's the new update going? Did you load it?" She could see whether they had downloaded updates but called anyway [1]. The signal that the product was working: "At some point people would start using the word, like love" [1].

The product's simplicity was deliberate and essential. PlanGrid's target users -- superintendents, foremen, electricians, carpenters -- had never used software to do their jobs. "How do you design and build software for people who don't even know how to use a computer?" [1]. The team enforced a rule: "If we could build this feature in two buttons, let's make sure we don't do it in five" [1].

The biggest early barrier was hardware, not software. In 2012, construction workers did not have iPads. PlanGrid loaned out iPads for free, then later charged enough to cover hardware costs. Founders sat down with individual users, walked them through the App Store, typed in passwords, and loaded projects. "We knew that it wasn't going to happen because there's so many non-literal fires to put out on a job site every single day" [1].

This was a textbook [[Doing Things That Don't Scale]] approach: educating an entire industry on mobile devices, then educating them on the software, one user at a time.

## Product Breakthroughs

PlanGrid consciously designed one product that worked for everyone in construction -- from project executives to electricians -- across all project types (residential, commercial, roads, bridges). This maximized TAM while avoiding the trap of custom versions for different profiles [1].

Key features that drove adoption:

1. **Sheet overlay and version comparison.** Users could overlay different drawing types (equipment, electrical, plumbing) to see the same room from multiple perspectives, then compare old and new versions with changes highlighted in red [1].
2. **Full sheet search.** PlanGrid was the only construction system that allowed searching for any word across all sheets. A user looking for "magnetic door hold opener" across 5,000 sheets could find the five relevant ones instantly [1].
3. **Automatic version control.** Using machine learning (before it was trendy), PlanGrid could read sheet identifiers like "A101, first floor floor plan" and automatically detect when a new version was uploaded, prompting the user rather than requiring manual version management. The US construction industry loses $20 billion annually to waste caused by building from outdated plans [1].

## Bottoms-Up Sales in a Top-Down Industry

Traditional construction software had existed for 30 years but was written for office workers -- CIOs, VPs of operations -- because the hardware to bring software into the field did not exist. PlanGrid went bottoms-up, selling directly to field workers. "We went directly into the field, which is normally not where software is sold" [1].

## Hiring: Pain Tolerance and Positive Attitude

Young describes two eras of hiring at PlanGrid:

**Sub-50 employees**: Hire generalists with high pain tolerance. "Those five people have to wear multiple hats. They had to not complain about it. There's work like, taking out the garbage because you probably don't have an office manager, and then also code" [1].

She assessed pain tolerance through background signals. One early hire, Taylor, had been canvassing door-to-door, getting cursed at and doors shut on her. Another, Saman, was "literally a door-to-door pencil salesman" for Staples in 2014-2015. He now leads PlanGrid's BD team [1].

**Post-50 employees**: Specialization becomes necessary. For leadership hires, Young looks for authenticity: "If there's ever a moment where you're in an interview and it feels like someone's bullshitting you, they're probably bullshitting you" [1]. She emphasizes reference checking and looks for whether the candidate has done the specific job before.

The cross-country train test: co-founder Ryan's hiring filter was "who would I rather be stuck on a cross-country train with?" [1].

## Firing: The Most Common YC Advice

Young reports that at YC, "almost every single speaker talked about" not firing people fast enough [1]. The blast radius of keeping the wrong person in a leadership role is enormous because "everyone else knows" when someone is not working out.

Her framework: Make it not a surprise. Time-box it. Communicate explicitly: "This is the expectations for this role, you're not meeting the expectations. I think this is what you need to do to fix it. If you don't do this, I will have to ask you to leave in three months from now. That is what you owe your team member" [1].

Young describes her own failure to fire fast enough as one of her deepest regrets: "I still to this day feel this like, it still hurts me at a deep level that I fired someone and it was a complete surprise to them" [1].

## Scaling: Everything Breaks at 150

PlanGrid grew from 5 co-founders to 450 employees. Young identifies 150 employees as the breaking point, consistent with Dunbar's number: "150 was when everything broke" [1].

When hiring executives, the most common mistake was scale mismatch. Executives from very large companies "had never seen a startup of this size. 'Cause it's painful at this size. You don't have 50 recruiters helping you recruit" [1].

## Balancing Parenthood and CEO Responsibilities

Young was pregnant, gave birth, and nursed while leading a 400+ person team, hitting revenue targets, managing a Series C, and navigating an Autodesk acquisition. "You can do all of that. I'm living proof that you can do all of it" [1].

## Negative Ego and Authenticity

Young's executive coach, Stratt, told her: "For someone who doesn't have a lot of ego, you have a lot of negative ego" [1]. She was constantly putting herself down and feeling guilty about mistakes rather than acknowledging them and moving forward. Before every board meeting, she was convinced the board would fire her. When she accidentally mentioned this to board member Carole, "she laughs. This loud cackle almost, like, 'Are you fucking out of your mind?'" The company was doing well; the fear was entirely internal [1].

Her advice to her earlier self: "Learn to be more authentic earlier." In her construction career, she smoked cigarettes to fit in with the crew and even tried chewing tobacco. As CEO, she tried to match what she thought a CEO should look like. "At some point, I don't even know when it happened, but it happened slowly... I just became more and more and more of myself. And the last few years have been the happiest for me" [1].
