# Astranis: Building Satellites Without Being a Billionaire

Astranis (YC W16) builds small satellites for geostationary orbit -- a new category of spacecraft designed to provide telecommunications services at lower cost than traditional large satellites. The company was started by two founders in a San Francisco apartment, went through Y Combinator with a $500,000 investment, and is now worth over a billion dollars with more than 400 employees [1].

## Origin

John Gedmark, an aerospace engineer by background, had spent time on large satellite programs and "knew that that was not for me" [1]. He reconnected with Ryan McKlinko, a co-founder he had known for over twenty years through Students for the Exploration and Development of Space. McKlinko had moved to San Francisco to work at Planet, one of the pioneering space startups [1].

The insight: nobody was taking a new approach to satellites in geostationary orbit, a "super valuable orbit" for telecommunications. They decided to take a crack at it [1].

## The MVP Problem in Hard Tech

As Gedmark puts it: "Turns out minimum viable product for a geostationary telecommunication spacecraft is not very minimal" [1].

The approach was to find the smallest possible proof point. From the earliest days, they drew a line in the sand: "We as a company are going to put something in space and show that we can build a satellite and that it will work in space. Let's just figure out how to do that and do it at the lowest possible cost" [1].

## The YC Batch: Three Months to Build a Satellite

When Astranis joined YC in Winter 2016, their partner told them: "You have three months until you can show up on demo day, and whatever you have on demo day, it better be as impressive as possible. Anything that doesn't get done until after demo day, forget about it. It won't matter" [1].

The team committed to building a first prototype satellite in three months -- work that could take other companies years. Because it was a demo rather than a production satellite, they could be flexible on requirements. The core elements were "fundamentally kind of simple" when building for demonstration rather than deployment [1].

They built the prototype and brought it to Demo Day.

## Scrappy Infrastructure

The seed round raised at Demo Day was one of the largest at YC at the time, but space is expensive, so scrappiness was essential [1]:

- They worked out of an 1,800 square foot apartment
- Built satellites on Ikea desks fitted with ESD mats
- Built a "clean room" from PVC piping and shower curtains, sprayed with a conductive substance called staticide to prevent static discharge
- Proved you could build spacecraft-grade hardware without expensive infrastructure [1]

## Demo Day as Proof

Gedmark's Demo Day pitch became one of the videos YC continues to show to new batches as an example of what a startup can accomplish in three months. The pitch addressed the two main investor objections [1]:

1. **It seems impossible** -- answered by showing they had already built a working prototype
2. **It could be a science project, not a business** -- answered by demonstrating the $122 billion addressable market

Gedmark had practiced the pitch fifty times over three days. It paid off: Astranis raised enough to eventually launch a satellite into space [1].

## Scaling

Eight years after founding, Astranis has [1]:
- More than 400 employees working out of Pier 70 in San Francisco
- In-house testing facilities including vibration tables and thermal vacuum chambers
- A dedicated SpaceX Falcon 9 launch for four new satellites (believed to be the first new company in history to purchase an entire dedicated Falcon 9)
- An operational satellite already in space from 2023
- Next-generation satellite designs in development

The company brought testing in-house as it scaled, including discovering they could perform ion thruster tests inside their existing thermal vacuum chambers -- a discovery that saved the cost and time of sending thrusters elsewhere for testing [1].

## Lessons for Hard Tech Founders

[[Jared Friedman]], who worked with Astranis at YC, frames the broader lesson: "You don't need to be Elon Musk to start a hard tech company." The requirements are domain experience, a great co-founder, a scrappy mindset, and persistence [1].

The 2016 YC batch was a pivotal moment for aerospace at YC: three companies from that era -- Astranis, [[Boom Technology]], and Relativity Space -- are now all billion-dollar companies [1].
