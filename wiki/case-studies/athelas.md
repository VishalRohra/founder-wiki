# Athelas

Athelas is a healthcare technology company co-founded by Tanay Tandon and Deepa Kulkarni that evolved from a single blood diagnostic device into what [[Garry Tan]] describes as "Rippling for healthcare" -- a compound startup providing the operating system for running a healthcare practice [2]. In 2024, Athelas merged with Commure to form an entity valued at over $6 billion, with Tandon serving as CEO [2].

## Origin: Hackathon to Blood Diagnostics

Tandon and Kulkarni met Garry Tan when they won the YC hackathon as high school students [2]. They were working on blood test diagnostics: a device that analyzes a small volume of blood using computer vision and microfluidics to generate a complete blood count (CBC) [1] [2].

After dropping out of Stanford, they commercialized the device -- called the Athelas One, later the Athelas Home -- at a time when one-drop blood testing was "relatively out of favor" due to the Theranos scandal [2]. Tan notes this as a lesson for founders: "Even though the headlines might be saying one thing, the real builders know they should pursue that which is true and that is in front of them" [2].

The first years focused entirely on manufacturing, clinical trials, FDA clearance, and bringing the device into patients' homes. The device generates revenue primarily through pharmaceutical company partnerships, earning "tens of millions of dollars per year in real gross margin" [2].

## Expansion: The Compound Startup

The diagnostic device served as a Trojan horse into healthcare practices. As engineers spent time in clinics, they discovered the back-office problems providers face: billing, credentialing, eligibility verification, insurance portals -- all powered by decades-old software, much of it acquired by private equity and no longer actively maintained [2].

Tandon describes the landscape: "The future is already here but not evenly distributed. We spend a crazy percentage of our GDP on healthcare and yet we're stuck with some of the worst tools in the world" [2]. Providers must learn to use disparate tools across decades of software -- Availity for portals, Waystar for eligibility, various systems for credentialing -- before they can even bill for a claim [2].

Athelas built an integrated suite of software and hardware tools that became "the operating system for running a healthcare business" [2]. This follows the compound startup model pioneered by Rippling: instead of solving one problem well, solve thousands of specific problems in a single industry.

## Work Tax and AI

Tandon coined the term "work tax" to describe the administrative overhead in healthcare: "We walked into an old health system and they were giving out blood pressure devices. Six people needed to sign off on a clipboard before one blood pressure device got to a patient. That's all work tax" [2].

AI -- particularly large language models -- enables Athelas to compress and automate these workflows [2]. Their product Scribe runs during appointments: a microphone records the conversation between doctor and patient, automatically generates transcription, clinical notes, CPT codes, and the insurance claim. "For the private practices that use Athelas Scribe today, they've eliminated hours of work every day" [2].

Administrative spending accounts for 15-30% of all healthcare spending in the US [2]. Tandon frames the mission: if healthcare spending can be reduced from $4 trillion to $2 trillion over 20 years while improving outcomes, that creates "$2 trillion of non-inflationary stimulus in the American economy every year" [2].

## Financial Position

As of 2024, Athelas is near cash-flow positive with hundreds of millions in revenue, having raised capital but spent relatively little of it. The company describes itself as in "full-on aggressive wartime mode" and is hiring aggressively across engineering, design, and product [2]. Tandon notes that domain expertise is not required: "The best engineers at Athelas are those who are able to take concepts around abstraction, concepts around how to automate workflows, and apply them to healthcare. We'll teach you the healthcare piece" [2].
