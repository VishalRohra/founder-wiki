# Lever: Building Recruiting Software from the Inside Out

Lever (YC S12) builds recruiting software for teams to source, interview, and hire talent. Co-founded by Sarah Nahm, Nate Smith, and others, the company raised its Series A in October 2014 after spending two years (2012-2014) finding [[Product-Market Fit]] [1]. Nahm became CEO on the eve of the Series A, transitioning from individual contributor designer to company leader through a collective team decision [1].

## The Founding Path: Non-Linear and People-First

Nahm grew up in Birmingham, Alabama, "as far from Silicon Valley" as possible [1]. She studied design at Stanford, where her freshman class was the first to start with Facebook. She planned to go into design consulting at IDEO. Instead, she ended up at Google through a chance encounter at Stanford's Career Development Center, where someone asked if she wanted to sign up for Google interviews [1].

At Google, her first role was speech writer for Marissa Mayer -- "a job that nobody had had before" [1]. She later worked on the Chrome team under Sundar Pichai. Nahm describes her Google experience as a "crash course in a lot of what it is to be a founder, because I had to kind of create something out of nothing and figure out what I was doing and be really bad at it for a while" [1].

Leaving Google was easy. Starting Lever was harder. Nahm did not leave to start a company; she left to grow and experience more things [1]. The co-founders "were almost trying out working with each other for a while before we actually officially went for it." Nahm credits co-founder fit for much of Lever's success: "Making sure that you're finding the right people is like probably the hardest to predict but most important part of that super, super early stage" [1].

## The Thesis: Knowledge Work Eating the World

Lever's founding thesis, crystallized around 2012, was that "just like software was eating the world, knowledge work was eating the world" [1]. As technology and digital transformation hit every industry -- even manufacturing -- organizations needed people who could "program a robotic arm or rethink manufacturing process," not undifferentiated assembly line workers [1].

This transformed how companies needed to think about hiring. Career tenure was shrinking (three to five years, 18 months in Silicon Valley). The companies winning were the ones with 10x talent driving leapfrog innovation [1]. Recruiting, as a function, had not kept up with these shifts.

## Building and Throwing Away

Lever spent two and a half years on the "blurry period" of product development [1]. They built an entire first product, gathered customer feedback, and realized they had built "something incremental... just a better version of what was there before" [1]. They threw it out.

Nahm frames the discipline that saved them: "What we did know was how we were going to hold ourselves accountable, and that was through listening to our customers" [1]. The founding team did not know the exact product they would build, but they knew the standard they would hold themselves to -- customer honesty over internal conviction.

## Proactive Recruiting: Lever's Own Playbook

As a recruiting software company, Lever applied its own principles to its hiring. Nahm's co-founder Nate Smith built the engineering organization with 81% of the team proactively sourced (outbound, not inbound applicants) [1].

### The Nurture Pipeline

Lever's most distinctive hiring strategy was relationship nurturing. Many hires were people the team had met two to three years before their eventual start date [1]. Nahm frames this not as building a "database of talent" but as "investing in relationships" that pay returns when the candidate is ready for their next move. These nurtured candidates were hired 35% faster than people meeting Lever for the first time [1].

### Motivation Fit

Lever's first stage of the hiring process is not a phone screen. It is called "motivation fit" -- explicitly named as such in their process [1]. Questions include:
- Where are you taking your career?
- What past career decisions have been most meaningful to you?
- What are you doing at your current job that you'd like to do more of?
- What are you not getting to do that you hope to do next?

Nahm listens for whether candidates thrive with ambiguity, whether they have specific self-awareness about what "impact" means for them personally, and whether they are at the right stage for early-stage startup life [1].

### Behavioral Interviewing and Career Trajectory

Nahm advocates behavioral interviewing for early-stage startups because founders are "hiring for a lot of roles you have never actually had personal experience doing" [1]. Lever uses a "career trajectory" interview that walks through a candidate's career from college onward, step by step, looking for patterns of success and failure [1]. This is a modified version of "top grading," a well-documented methodology [1].

### Impact Descriptions, Not Job Descriptions

After raising its Series A, Lever took a company off-site (the entire company fit in a van) to design its hiring process [1]. They created an internal exercise: define what someone in each role needs to achieve in 1, 3, 6, and 12 months -- not tasks, but outcomes and impact [1].

Candidates who saw these documents "would just light up" [1]. It took Lever six months to realize they should replace traditional job descriptions with these impact descriptions externally. The result: more top-percentile candidates applied, and the descriptions attracted more diverse applicants because they did not broadcast assumptions about what kind of person should apply [1]. "By having this lengthy list of skills and requirements, you're basically broadcasting to the world assumptions about what kind of people should apply" [1].

## Diversity and Inclusion: Culture Before Demographics

Nahm was the only woman at Lever for two to two-and-a-half years [1]. She argues that the biggest roadblock to inclusive hiring is "thinking that it's about demographics and thinking that they have to reflect the demographics of diversity before they're allowed to make their culture more diverse or inclusive" [1].

Her advice: start by making the culture inclusive, then diversity in hiring follows. "What's the point of hiring all this quote diverse talent if when they get there they're not ready to succeed?" [1].

### Concrete Inclusion Practices

**Dish duty and cultural surface area.** Early at Lever, "a very obvious group of people did the dishes and a very obvious group of people did not." After someone shared research about women disproportionately doing office chores, the team built a Slackbot that assigned rotational dish duty. It became part of the culture and a talking point on candidate tours [1].

**Holiday schedule as culture.** Lever shipped a holiday calendar that did not include Martin Luther King Day (it had included Presidents' Day in the same month). Employees pushed back. The team realized "our holiday schedule was surface area to our culture" and revised it [1].

**Technical/non-technical divide.** Lever deliberately rejected the Silicon Valley stereotype of "technical" versus "non-technical" people. They ran an internal training called "I'm Technical and So Are You." Nahm, who has a BS in Mechanical Engineering from Stanford, had herself internalized being the "non-technical founder" [1]. Engineering teams needed to understand customers; non-engineering teams needed to design scalable systems. The divide was holding both sides back [1].

**Employee resource groups with policy input.** Sixty-five percent of Lever employees belonged to an employee resource group. Lever's parents ERG was asked to help revise the parental leave policy. "Using these groups to inform real decisions is kind of the next level" [1].

**Colors at onboarding.** During "Ramp Camp" (Lever's week-long onboarding), all new hires take a personality assessment that outputs a color. This creates shared language for discussing working-style differences without resorting to stereotypes. At the Ramp Camp graduation happy hour, new hires wear leis matching their colors [1].

Holly Liu, interviewing Nahm, observes that this creates both common language and permission to discuss differences. Nahm notes that most of these practices emerged bottom-up from the team, not top-down from leadership [1].

## Advice for YC Batch Companies

Nahm's summary advice: "Your culture is your people. It's who you hire, it's who you fire, it's also who you're recognizing, who you're promoting, who you're rewarding and how you do all those things." She argues that building a great team "is the shortest path to building a strong culture" and "the shortest path to building your results." Her framing: "A founder's number one job is recruiting" [1].
