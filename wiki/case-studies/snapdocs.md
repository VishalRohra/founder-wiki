# Snapdocs: Navigating Market Cycles

[Snapdocs](https://www.snapdocs.com/) is the leading digital closing platform for the mortgage industry. As of the interview, the company touches 25% of all US real estate transactions and is valued at $1.5B. Founder and CEO [[Aaron King]] (YC W14) built the company with minimal outside funding through volatile market conditions [1].

## Capital Efficiency as a Discipline

Snapdocs started as a side project years before [[Aaron King]] applied to YC. By the time he applied, the company had a live product, customers, and revenue. Even after YC, the company did not raise much immediately [1].

Three years after YC, Snapdocs raised its Series A. At that point, the company had spent about $1M of its seed round and was at a $5M revenue run rate. The lead investor later told King how capital efficient Snapdocs had been compared to peers [1].

King frames capital discipline not as a constraint but as a virtue: "I've learned that you should take the same approach even when you do have the capital to be less disciplined" [1]. Capital constraints kept the team focused on going deep with paying customers, avoiding the common trap of building products no one wanted [1].

The approach also forced the team to think about the smallest version of every product in order to ship quickly, maintaining a short feedback loop with users [1]. King acknowledges the cost: "We built many things that weren't engineered for scale or flexibility. However, now we can afford to reengineer those unscalable solutions because we built something people want" [1].

## Product Development

Before raising the Series A, Snapdocs was heavy on customer involvement. The team spent time in customers' offices watching them use the product and understanding their work [1]. They also kept prospects in the loop as they built new features:

> "Some of the best feedback came from people who had chosen to not yet work with us. Responding to that feedback with a killer feature was a great way to ultimately get them on board." [1]

The in-person interactions built trust and rapport. Customers would call the team the moment they spotted a problem or saw a competitor doing something compelling. Customer churn has always been low as a result [1].

Despite small size, the team maintained a disciplined product release process while shipping code daily, sometimes multiple times per day [1].

### The Value of Low Investor Pressure

Not having heavy investor pressure early on allowed Snapdocs to experiment in an underappreciated part of the market. The Serviceable Available Market (SAM) of the initial product was roughly only $20M, but King believed it would allow expansion into more critical parts of the mortgage ecosystem [1]. He notes: "It was the type of opportunity that would be hard to discover through market analysis or spreadsheet exercises. You had to get deep into the problem set to see the opportunity" [1].

## Hiring Under Capital Constraints

Before the company could afford full-time employees, King worked with contractors and leaned on friends. Friends would come over to whiteboard or work through code problems [1].

When King could afford to hire, he treated early employees like founding team members — generous with equity and transparent about the business's potential and challenges. "Getting a few really good people into the company early on was foundational to the company's success" [1].

The first full-time hire was an engineer from a previous role (and one of the friends who helped in the early days). The second and third hires came from Hacker News job postings. All three turned out to be excellent [1].

### The Recruiting Regret

King's biggest early hiring mistake was being too timid to approach people he respected:

> "I should have tried to convince them to quit their successful jobs and join our small (yet risky at the time) startup. I'm fearless on this approach now, but back then I was intimidated to try to convince a friend to join a company that might fail. In hindsight I did them a disservice by not trying to recruit them." [1]

His lesson: smart people can evaluate the opportunity for themselves. Be honest about inherent challenges, but give them the opportunity to take a risk on you [1].

The team only hired when there was a clear and painful need. This made running the company harder but kept everyone working on the most important things [1].

## Navigating Market Cycles

A core lesson from Snapdocs is how to selectively respond to market changes. King describes the approach as "selectively ignoring some market changes, while reacting quickly to others" [1]. The filter is how a change aligns with resources, vision, and the north star metric of market share growth.

### Fluctuations in Mortgage Volume

The biggest dynamic change Snapdocs faces is fluctuations in monthly/annual mortgage volume, driven by economic factors. When well-resourced with momentum, the team ignores market downturns and stays focused on market share growth. Other times they scale up or back based on the fluctuation's size [1].

### Resisting Trendy Technology

Industry appetite for new technologies changes quickly. There were times when the market demanded a technology but King believed underlying industry factors would prevent it from scaling. Building it would pull resources from long-term priorities [1]:

> "Sometimes to the protests of our sales team, we ignored it or invested minimally in these trendy areas. By doing so, we were able to stay focused on the things that were truly going to transform the industry." [1]

### The Cost of Change Scales with Company Size

King notes that navigating change was relatively easy in the first few years when the team could all fit in one room, product cycles were short, and the cost of making a change was low. As the company grew, changes to speed or direction became more expensive: investments take longer to play out, headcount changes take longer to scale, and there are more layers to communicate through [1].

## The COVID Pivot (March 2020)

COVID accelerated demand for Snapdocs' product but shifted what customers wanted. The team had to expand quickly and pivot the roadmap immediately [1].

Decision-making worked in concentric circles [1]:
1. **Inner circle (3-4 people)**: Where the hardest and messiest conversations took place. Quickly defined problems, opportunities, and direction.
2. **Senior leadership team**: Further discussion and arming leaders with everything needed to communicate changes.
3. **Company-wide meeting**: Shared new direction and answered questions.

This entire process took about two weeks. For ongoing rapid changes, the company used temporary pods of 4-5 team members focused on specific challenges that would spin up for a few weeks and dissolve. Company-wide all-hands went from monthly to weekly [1].

### Intellectual Honesty About Growth

The team worked hard to be intellectually honest about COVID-driven growth:

> "This industry works in cycles. We're in an up cycle now and that's great. There's going to be a down cycle. We don't know when or how strong it's going to be. But we should not overly congratulate ourselves for the current situation, just as we shouldn't be too hard on ourselves when we're fighting through an inevitable downturn in the future." [1]

## Shifting Gears: From Efficient to Effective and Back

When Snapdocs raised its Series D in 2021, transactions had grown to millions of closings per year with thousands of lenders and title companies using the platform monthly. The company shifted from frugal to aggressive, raising larger rounds and hiring seasoned executives [1].

King describes the key question: "Is what I did yesterday the thing that's going to get me to where I need to be tomorrow?" The answer required changing parts of the culture and capital investment strategy [1].

But when mortgage demand cooled later that year, the company returned to its roots: ditched the motto of being effective over efficient, returned to ruthless prioritization, and shifted away from investing in future scale that would not be needed for years [1].

King's summary: "Market fluctuations are normal and unavoidable. Startups should scale up at times and scale back at others. It's hard and painful... But these ups and downs are natural and a necessary part of building an enduring company" [1].
