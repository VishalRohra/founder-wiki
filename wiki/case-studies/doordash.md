# DoorDash: Creating a Market from Scratch

DoorDash (YC S13) is the largest food delivery platform in the United States, commanding mid-60% market share as of 2025, with over seven million drivers on the platform. Co-founder and CEO [[Tony Xu]] built DoorDash from a static HTML page with eight PDF menus into a public company. The story illustrates how doing every delivery yourself for years produces irreplaceable customer insight, why contrarian geographic strategy can beat better-funded competitors, and how crisis moments clarify mission.

## The Macaroon Store Origin

Before DoorDash, Xu and his co-founders (Andy Fang, Stanley Tang, and Evan Moore) worked on several projects together at Stanford GSB. One project -- a tablet-based survey app that sat at the point of sale to help merchants measure marketing effectiveness -- met one criterion (the founders enjoyed working together) but not the other (they did not love the idea). They cancelled it [1].

The founding insight came from shadowing a macaroon store owner. The founders' approach was not surveys but immersion: "Can we follow you around for a day? We'll go and pack boxes with you, do your accounting with you, make salads with you" [1]. Xu explains the rationale: "Sometimes it's very, very hard for any customer to tell you exactly what is inside their brain when you ask them what problems they have. And so we wanted to feel and maybe trying to figure it out ourselves" [1].

At the end of the visit, the store owner showed them "a booklet of orders she had turned down. All of them were delivery orders." It was a thick booklet, representing significant lost revenue for a one-person shop. The founders asked why she was not pursuing them, and began "unraveling that thread" [1].

As they studied the problem further, the thread expanded: not just this macaroon store, but all bakeries, all restaurants, all retailers. There were roughly one million restaurants in the United States, but only about 20,000 offered delivery -- the ones that incumbents like GrubHub and Seamless worked with, all of which had their own delivery fleets. DoorDash's question: "What would happen if you could enable the other nine hundred eighty thousand to do it?" [1].

## Palo Alto Delivery: The MVP

The founders shipped paloaltodelivery.com in under an hour -- "maybe forty-five, fifty minutes." It was a static HTML page with eight PDF menus from restaurants in Palo Alto that they frequented as students and a Google Voice number. When a customer called, all four founders' phones would ring simultaneously. Whoever picked up first took the order [1].

All four co-founders did every delivery themselves. The total investment: less than ten dollars for the domain name [1].

## YC Summer 2013: Answering Three Questions

The founders entered YC with three open questions [1]:

1. **Would consumers pay for delivery?** Delivery is not a new idea -- "it's been around since horses." In a highly capitalistic market like the US, if something does not exist, there may be a good reason.

2. **Would merchants (restaurants) pay?** The founders knew restaurants had the need, but not whether they would pay for a delivery service.

3. **Would a driver workforce exist?** Would people want to work as delivery drivers?

### Consumer Validation

The earliest customers were families with young children, typically mothers making meal decisions. They told the founders what they wanted, which restaurants they preferred, and what was important. The critical signal: "They kept coming back again without our throwing advertising at them, coupons, or discounts at them" [1].

### The Driver Experiment

Because the founders had done every delivery themselves, they could speak credibly when recruiting drivers through Craigslist ads. But Xu was uncertain whether a driver workforce would form, since transporting people (Uber) presumably paid better than transporting food. He ran a test [1]:

He recruited 20 DoorDash drivers and 20 Uber X drivers, both earning $20/hour. He offered each group $25/hour guaranteed to switch platforms. Out of 40 drivers, exactly one said yes [1].

The insight: these were fundamentally different populations. DoorDash drivers skewed younger and more female. Today, almost 60% of DoorDash's 7 million Dashers are women. They come from hundreds of industries and often deliver by scooter, bicycle, or motorcycle rather than car -- very different from ride-hailing [1].

## Demo Day Failure and Near-Death

Internally, the team felt they had answered their three questions with positive evidence. Demo day, however, "was not successful." DoorDash was not among the favorites [1].

The company almost went to zero. The initial YC grant was small, and Xu could not raise seed financing. People were not convinced a 10-week dataset proved a business. For investors, DoorDash "would be a conviction bet on the team and on the idea that there would be a potential market" at a time when the market did not yet exist [1].

### The Stanford Football Incident

In the fall of 2013, the first home game for Stanford football ended at an awkward time, and everyone in Palo Alto ordered DoorDash simultaneously. Every delivery was at least an hour late, probably closer to 90 minutes. The founders could not shut off the website and had no way to fulfill the demand [1].

That night at 9 or 10 PM, the founders calculated that refunding everyone would cost 40% of their nearly depleted bank account. They made the decision in 10 seconds: refund everyone. Then they stayed up all night baking cookies and delivered them at 5 AM before customers woke up [1].

Xu describes this as the origin story of DoorDash's core company value: "customer obsessed, not competitor focused." He attributes it partly to survival bias, but says the founding team "always had this desire to at least do things the right way, even if we wouldn't have made it" [1].

A few weeks after the incident, they raised their seed round. "Luckily, all you need is one investor to say yes" [1].

## The Suburban Contrarian Bet

As the market grew, competitors focused on expected geographies: San Francisco, New York, dense urban cores where order density seemed necessary for economics. DoorDash took a different path [1].

Because the founders had done every delivery themselves for two years (and continue to do deliveries annually to this day), they kept hearing that need was strongest outside city centers. The logic was simple when viewed from the customer's perspective: "If you and I lived in New York City and we walked outside of the elevator of the building, we probably could walk into hundreds of restaurants. In a place outside of New York City -- Long Island, for example -- you would be walking for miles probably before you would see the first restaurant" [1].

The unit economics confirmed the intuition, line by line [1]:

- **Higher baskets**: Suburban areas had more families, more mouths to feed, larger orders, larger revenue per delivery.
- **Easier parking**: Drivers spent less time finding parking.
- **Simpler deliveries**: Single-family homes versus high-rise buildings in Manhattan.

"The first instinct was just listening to what the customers had told us -- where the need was. But it was very easy to prove out very, very quickly thereafter" [1]. Over the next decade, the majority of growth in the food delivery industry came from places outside of city centers [1].

## Three Years of Fundraising Rejection (2016-2018)

Even as internal metrics moved in the right direction across every dimension, Xu received hundreds of investor rejections across 2016, 2017, and 2018, including a down round for the Series C [1].

Xu's analysis of why investors passed: "There are moments where it's just before that moment" -- the point right before repeatable profitable growth becomes undeniable. In a market without an obvious winner, with better-capitalized competitors, investors were "gunshy" [1].

The turning point came with the Series D in March 2018. The internal team could see what was coming: DoorDash had consistently superior retention and frequency. "One of the hardest things to really fool is the retention and the engagement of a product." Every marketing dollar went further for DoorDash than for competitors. Once they received the capital to launch across geographies, "it was just a matter of time" [1]. By 2019, DoorDash surpassed Uber Eats and GrubHub to become the category leader.

## COVID-19: Crisis as Clarity

COVID-19 shelved DoorDash's 2019 plans to go public. The period felt like "DoorDash in YC" again: seven days a week, 10 AM to 2 AM, multiple all-company meetings per day [1].

Xu found that crisis simplified decision-making. The priorities were immediate and obvious [1]:

1. **Keep everyone safe.** Acquire tens of millions of units of PPE. Ship contactless delivery in four to five days.
2. **Get everyone liquid.** The average merchant has 17 days of cash on hand; every hour matters. Dashers, many furloughed or laid off, needed instant liquidity.
3. **Take care of the community.** Partner with dozens of large hospital networks (UCSF, Stanford, Mount Sinai) to provide free delivery to hospital workers.

Two additional decisions were controversial internally [1]:

- **National TV campaign advertising for competitors.** DoorDash spent millions on a campaign saying "whether you order on us or any of our peers, just order. The dining rooms may be closed, but the kitchens are open."
- **Cutting commissions in half.** DoorDash was the only platform to do this, at a cost of over $100 million, while the company was still unprofitable and considering an IPO.

Xu framed the decision through the lens of mission duration: "Are we just here to build a company that ends in 2020 or ends in an IPO? No. We're here to build a company that will grow and empower every physical business and grow the GDP of every city. That's not going to happen in one year, two years, five years, ten years." From that perspective, the costs were "a drop in the bucket in the grand scheme of the journey" [1].

## Doing the Work as Expertise

Xu's advice to his 18-year-old self: "Especially when you're on the cusp of something new -- take AI -- there's no better way to be the expert than just to do the work. And you might be surprised at how quickly you get to become the expert" [1]. He had no background in logistics or delivery, but by doing deliveries for years, he developed a strong point of view on how the physical world works. The principle applies regardless of field -- AI, biotechnology, or any emerging domain [1].

## The Physical World Opportunity

Xu argues the physical world remains an untapped frontier: "Where is the last parking spot in a rainstorm here in San Francisco? How many apples does the local grocery store have in aisle six? These are questions that actually nobody has the answer to. No LLM has the answer to" [1]. Physical businesses -- small, medium, and large -- produce the vast majority of jobs and GDP in any society. DoorDash's long-term mission is to grow the GDP of every city by empowering these businesses [1].
