# Rippling: Building the Same Company Twice

Rippling is an all-in-one HR, finance, and IT software company valued at $13.5 billion [1]. Co-founded by Parker Conrad and Pano, the company is a direct continuation of Conrad's earlier company Zenefits, rebuilt from scratch with deeper conviction and a different operational philosophy. Conrad's journey spans three companies over roughly 15 years and includes one of the more dramatic founder narratives in recent YC history.

## SigFig: Seven Years of Grinding Failure

Conrad's entrepreneurial career began at Amgen in Los Angeles, where career progression was slow. His college roommate from Harvard (who worked at Amazon) proposed starting a company together. They made "exactly the wrong" move: deciding to start a company first, then trying to figure out what to build [1].

Their first idea was a Wikipedia-style platform for stock research, launched around the time Wikipedia took off. "It wasn't super clear why people would do that, but they were doing it for Wikipedia, so why not do it here?" [1]. It never worked. Conrad identifies a broader lesson: "A lot of consumer businesses end up being kind of random. Whether things work or not feels like very much outside the control of the company. It's one of the reasons I hate consumer businesses" [1]. Many entrepreneurs who start in consumer end up pivoting their careers to B2B for this reason [1].

They raised a $2.5 million seed round at a $7.5 million valuation off a pitch deck. "I was like, 'Oh my God, we are rich!'... It was very exciting and heady, and it was all downhill from there" [1].

The company (later renamed SigFig) went through seven years of pivots. Conrad describes the cycle: "We would always have five ideas that we thought would work... four of them would just completely fail, and one of them would mostly fail, but maybe there was some glimmer of hope" [1]. They pitched approximately 70 investors during the 2009 downturn with no success.

Conrad emerged from this experience "deeply cynical about fundraising." He observed that investor enthusiasm shifts every six to nine months -- "Facebook apps," "social local mobile," Web3, AI -- while building a company takes years. "There's no way the attention span that investors have on this stuff is always so much shorter than the time that's required to build a business" [1].

## Fundraising Philosophy: Don't Optimize for It

Conrad's hard-earned fundraising principle: "You should just basically wait until you're getting investors that are emailing you term sheets that are signed with a blank for the valuation, and they say, 'Just fill in the valuation and send it back to us.' And when they do that, then it's time to fundraise. And until then, you should probably just focus on making your business better" [1].

He argues that most advice-seekers should "not spend any time trying to optimize for a fundraising outcome" [1]. This directly shaped his decision to do YC with Zenefits: if he could get a few hundred customers making insurance commissions, he would never need to talk to VCs again [1].

## Zenefits: Meteoric Rise

The core insight behind Zenefits was that many separate administrative tasks for running a business -- insurance, benefits, payroll, offer letters -- all needed to work together but did not. Doing them all in one system eliminated enormous manual work: "This kind of magic button to hire an employee, and then they sort of got set up everywhere" [1].

Conrad describes [[Product-Market Fit]] by contrast with his SigFig experience. At SigFig, one idea out of five would mostly fail. At Zenefits, "they all worked unbelievably well. Just unbelievably well. Wildly exceeded every expectation." Even things they thought probably would not work "would work unbelievably well as well." The market was "pulling the product into existence because it was so clearly what everyone wanted" [1].

Conrad's advice on PMF is stark: "If it's not working, give up immediately. It very rarely happens that people with something that's not working end up pivoting the business in a way that eventually it starts working. There's always that Airbnb story, but most of the time that's not what happens" [1].

Growth numbers: $0 to $200K revenue exiting YC (about six weeks). $0 to $1M in the first year. $1M to $20M in year two. $20M to $65M in year three (plan had been $100M) [1].

### PG and Launch Urgency

Conrad joined YC as a solo founder with a prototype and little built. PG told him he needed to launch the first week of February. Conrad protested that it was three weeks away. PG's response: "What I'm telling you is you're screwed then. I don't see how -- you might as well give up now and go home" [1]. Coming off seven years of failure, this was devastating.

They launched in the second week of February, a few weeks ahead of a competitor. The TechCrunch narrative cemented: Zenefits was the innovative company; the competitor was "trying to copy what Zenefits is doing" [1].

Conrad credits YC's value primarily in creating urgency: "It is the right level of urgency and pressure into the system to get you going early on" [1]. Starting a company "feels a lot like being unemployed... If you don't get anything done, nobody really yells at you" [1].

## The Fall: Zenefits to Zero

At $65M ARR, Conrad was fired within a week of the fiscal year-end. "Everything that was working so well for us top of funnel stopped working" coincided with investors who had invested at high multiples "underwriting an enormous amount of future growth that suddenly was not materializing" [1]. Compliance issues surfaced in this context.

David Sacks became CEO but, according to Conrad, "hired a comms person... and they just focused on attacking me over and over again for like six months" [1]. Conrad was under legal restrictions and could not respond publicly. The press narrative became one-sided [1].

[[Garry Tan]] describes the context from YC's perspective: "I saw that you had created Zenefits -- Zenefits would not have existed or created any of that enterprise value without you. And to see what they did to you, it was deeply unfair" [1].

## Rippling: The Rebuild

Conrad had "either the advantage of or the misfortune to build the same company twice" [1]. He and co-founder Pano (who had worked with him at Zenefits) shared a conviction: "There's a hundred billion dollar opportunity just sitting right there on the floor, and nobody can see it except for us. Everyone's just walking by oblivious to it" [1].

Key differences in the rebuild:

**No operations, no customer support initially.** Conrad believed the "critical flaw at Zenefits" was scaling too much on manual operations and support rather than building true end-to-end software. Rippling spent roughly two years building with mostly an engineering team before launching [1].

**Expanded scope.** Rippling extended the Zenefits insight beyond HR: "For the same reason you want this one place to manage payroll and benefits... you actually also want to be able to ship someone their computer or get them set up with access to email and Salesforce and GitHub" [1]. Employee data became the center of a broader business software thesis, not just an HR product [1].

**Deep conviction over exploration.** "I felt like I had a lot of insights about the market and what was needed and very little self-doubt this time around versus the first time" [1].

## The Compound Startup Thesis

Conrad coined the term "compound software business" to describe Rippling's approach. The thesis: many deeper organizational problems cannot be solved by narrow point-solution software. A suite of seamlessly interoperable applications can solve problems that individual tools cannot [1].

He traces this to older software companies -- SAP, Oracle, Microsoft, and to some extent Salesforce -- which were compound businesses. The SaaS era created a moment where narrow point solutions could each become low-single-digit-billion-dollar companies. But that era created an overcrowded, fragmented landscape where "the types of problems that you can solve for customers tend to be much more limited" [1].

Rippling invests heavily in platform capabilities that cut across applications: analytics, approvals, permissions, and workflow automations. "Most of our competitors just can't go as deep on that stuff" [1].

Conrad is candid about the thesis's limitations: "It's actually a deeply cynical view of software markets because I think it's possible that compound software businesses are the wave of the future. And also, there will be three of them" [1].

## AI in Enterprise Software

Conrad argues that AI's biggest impact on enterprise software is not generative -- it is the ability to read and ingest information at scale. "The fact that these systems can read and their context windows are so large is actually much more powerful for B2B software than the fact that they can write" [1].

Rippling launched an AI performance management feature that analyzes an employee's first 90 days -- pull requests, sales calls, support tickets -- to predict whether they are on track for success or need intervention [1].

On CRM and AI: Conrad argues AI should not replace the rep's forecast. Instead, AI should provide a second opinion (based on listening to all sales calls) and flag disagreements between the rep's assessment and the AI's. "If you only have time to look at five deals or five new hires, these are the ones that you should look at" [1].

On AI SDRs: "They don't work... But the second that it flips and they can, it won't have the impact that people are hoping. What'll happen is it'll just destroy outbound as a channel" [1].

## Founder Mode

Conrad agrees with the concept that founders need to "go all the way to ground" when something is broken -- "review the support tickets, listen to the sales calls, do the job on the factory floor" [1]. But he warns against misinterpretation: "You need really good executives, and you don't want to do the founder mode thing unless something's broken... You can't do it everywhere" [1].

AI extends this capability. An LLM can read across the entire organization, effectively expanding the CEO's context window. "The powerful thing about LLMs inside of businesses is that it can flatten organizations and give executives and managers just a larger sort of window into what's going on" [1].
