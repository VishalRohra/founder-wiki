# Boom Supersonic

Boom Technology (YC W16) is building supersonic passenger airplanes. Founded by [[Blake Scholl]], it represents a case study in starting a hard tech company, selling before having a product, and using YC's structure to accelerate an aerospace startup [1].

## The Opportunity

Fifty years after the Concorde was designed, supersonic travel had not been made mainstream [1]. Scholl frames this as a unique story in technology: "Our phones are better, our computers are better, we're about to have cars that drive themselves and genetically-engineered medicine, and yet we're still flying at 1960 speeds" [1]. The industry is run by optimizers, not founders. Boeing's CEO publicly declared "no more moonshots" [1].

The insight: aviation breakthroughs have always come from founder-led companies [1]. The first airplane (Wright Brothers), the first practical airliner (DC-3), and the first jetliner (De Havilland Comet) were all created by companies run by their founders. The last new commercial aircraft company was founded in 1921. The last founder retired from the industry in 1958 [1].

## Why YC

Scholl was initially skeptical: "Isn't YC for mobile social apps?" [1]. [[Sam Altman]] connected him with hard tech founders who had done YC. They raved about how "YC can help normalize and catalyze ideas that are off the beaten path" [1]. Scholl concluded YC would not help with airplane building but would help with "getting and demonstrating traction and raising capital" [1].

Capital was the biggest risk: "there was a very real risk that we'd never get the money to build anything, and that the company would come and go without ever really starting" [1].

YC gave two pieces of advice: (1) "Get your ass out of stealth mode," and (2) "Go sell some airplanes" [1].

## The Virgin Deal

YC group partners told Scholl: "Show up on demo day with sales or your goose is pretty cooked" [1]. His pipeline included United, Delta, Lufthansa, and Air China -- all impossible to close in 8-9 weeks. "There's no way I'm closing Lufthansa by demo day. Just not going to happen. I'll be lucky if I close Lufthansa in nine years, let alone nine weeks" [1].

Strategy pivot: focus on startup airlines and Virgin [1]. They secured an LOI from a startup doing all-business-class service.

Then, 24 hours before Demo Day Two, Virgin came through: "You can announce the following... We'll take the first 10 airplanes, and we've got options on them. And through Virgin Galactic, we're gonna help you build it" [1]. Scholl "fell off my chair and almost screamed. I had to read the thing three times before I would tell the team" [1].

The result: "$5 billion in LOIs, a record that probably won't be broken soon" [1].

### How They Closed Virgin

1. **Persistence.** YC's biggest sales advice: "Be so persistent that you're sure you're being annoying. And you'll feel like you must be annoying, but when you have that feeling that means you're probably doing it right" [1].

2. **Multiple avenues to the decision-maker.** They had friends in Virgin Galactic supporting them AND astronaut Mark Kelly on their advisory board working Richard Branson directly [1]. "We made sure we had two different avenues to him so that one of them couldn't block us" [1].

3. **Crashed the spaceship rollout.** When Virgin Galactic unveiled their new spaceship in Mojave, Boom engineered a meeting with Branson by telling each side they already had access to the other [1]. "I had to fight my way past the 18-year-olds that were, like, blocking and checking the invite list" [1].

4. **Asked for the right thing.** During the 15-minute breakfast meeting (with Branson and his mom): "We're not asking for your money, we're asking, 'When this flies, do you want a Virgin logo on it?'" [1]. This was key -- making the deal as easy as possible for the other side. See also [[Do Things That Don't Scale]].

5. **Credible deadline.** Demo Day created urgency [1]. Virgin cared about publicity and brand, and "they really wanted to be part of it." Without that deadline, the deal would not have happened [1].

Scholl's summary: "Be fricking persistent, have a deadline, and if you're in a hurry, make the deal as sweet as you possibly can for the other side" [1].

## Hardware at YC: What to Demo

For hardware companies in YC, the challenge is demonstrating progress when the product cannot be finished in three months [1]. Scholl's framework: "Find the thing that you're gonna get done in three months that is progress and looks like progress" [1].

They considered technical milestones ("in our simulations we get a lift-drag ratio that's hitting a critical threshold") but rejected them -- "super meaningful, but no one's gonna understand it or believe it or get excited about it" [1]. They also rejected purely cosmetic demos (a flight simulator) [1].

What they actually did: completed the aerodynamics, built a physical model that showed the innovations, did the high-level jet engine design with a 3D-printed model, and physically brought to Demo Day one of the three actual engines that would power the first airplane [1]. "People, of course, were like, 'OMG, jet engine'" [1]. The engine was real -- "it wasn't bullshit" [1].
