# Boom Technology

Boom Technology (YC W16) is building supersonic passenger airplanes. Founded by [[Blake Scholl]], the company aims to make high-speed travel economical for airlines and affordable for passengers -- fifty years after the Concorde's design [1].

## Origin

Scholl had been talking about supersonics for 10 years before starting Boom. After his first startup was acquired by Groupon, he made a list of startup ideas in descending order of how awesome it would be if they worked, ignoring feasibility. "I figured, 'I'll cross things off the list and I'll end up working on idea number five.' And, as luck had it, the more I got into supersonics, the more I learned that it's not crazy, and actually the time is now" [1].

His previous work on internet coupons at Groupon was a catalyst: "There's nothing like working on Internet coupons to make you yearn to work on something you really love" [1].

## The Case for Founder-Led Aviation

Scholl observes that supersonic travel represents a unique case in technology where capability went backward. "Our phones are better, our computers are better, we're about to have cars that drive themselves and genetically-engineered medicine, and yet we're still flying at 1960 speeds" [1].

He traces every major breakthrough in aviation to founder-led companies: the Wright brothers (bicycle entrepreneurs), the DC-3 (first practical airliner), and the De Havilland Comet (first jetliner). The last new commercial aircraft company was founded in 1921. The last founder retired from the industry in 1958. Since then, Boeing and Airbus have optimized existing designs but have not advanced human capability [1].

## YC Experience

Scholl was initially skeptical: "Isn't YC for mobile social apps?" But after connecting with other hard tech founders who had done YC, he concluded it would help with traction and raising capital -- the biggest risk for Boom in the early days [1].

YC gave two pieces of advice: get out of stealth mode, and go sell airplanes. Both proved critical [1].

## The Virgin Deal

For demo day, Scholl assessed his pipeline (United, Delta, Lufthansa, Air China) and concluded none would close in eight weeks. He focused on startup airlines and Virgin [1].

The team pursued Richard Branson through two channels: friends inside Virgin Galactic and astronaut Mark Kelly on Boom's advisory board. They secured a 15-minute breakfast meeting by coordinating requests from both avenues and showing up at a Virgin Galactic spaceship rollout [1].

The ask was crafted to be easy: "We're not asking for your money, we're asking, 'When this flies, do you want a Virgin logo on it?'" Using demo day as a deadline, Boom told Virgin, "We are launching the company on demo day, and either you can be part of it or not" [1].

The result: 24 hours before Boom's demo day slot, Virgin committed to the first 10 airplanes with options, plus Virgin Galactic support for building. Boom presented $5 billion in LOIs -- a demo day record [1].

## Lessons

Three sales principles distilled from the experience [1]:
1. Be persistent beyond the point of discomfort
2. Have a believable external deadline
3. Make the deal maximally attractive to the other side

For hardware companies at YC, find progress that "both is and looks like actual, substantive progress." Boom built a physical model of the airplane, 3D-printed the jet engine design, and brought to demo day one of the actual engines that would power the first aircraft [1].
