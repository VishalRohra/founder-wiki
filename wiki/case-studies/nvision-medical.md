# nVision Medical: From Ovarian Cyst to $275M Acquisition

nVision Medical (also referred to as Envision Medical) was a medical device startup founded by Surbhi Sarna that developed the first and only FDA-cleared device for collecting cells from the fallopian tube for cancer evaluation. Boston Scientific acquired the company for $275 million [1]. Sarna is now a Group Partner at Y Combinator, where she mentors healthcare and biotech founders.

## Origin: A Personal Health Crisis

At thirteen, Sarna experienced sudden, severe abdominal pain. She was nearly misdiagnosed with appendicitis before doctors determined she had repeat complex ovarian cysts [1].

The diagnostic limitations were stark. Ultrasound could identify that a mass existed but not whether it was cancerous. The available blood test was "no better than a coin toss" for accuracy. Biopsy was not an option because "if it is cancerous, you risk spreading cancer cells all over the body." Surgery to remove the cyst often meant removing the entire ovary, with impacts on fertility and ovarian function [1].

Her family faced a binary choice: invasive surgery with lasting consequences, or wait and see if she had "one of the most lethal cancers impacting women" [1]. They chose to wait. After months, the cyst turned out to be benign. But the experience revealed how "antiquated everything to do with women's health was" [1].

As a high school senior, Sarna wrote in her college personal statement that she wanted to start a company in women's health. That statement became her career [1].

## Founding at 24

Sarna worked as an engineer at Abbott Vascular and then at a smaller medical device startup. At 24, even though her plan was to get more industry experience, "there was just something in me that did not feel satisfied. There was this energy that I couldn't contain" [1]. She left to start the company.

## Talking to Users: The Gynecologist Visit

Sarna needed to validate her device concept with gynecologists but did not know any personally. Her solution: she called her own gynecologist's office, "pretended to have a stomach ache, asked to see her as soon as possible, and in the middle of a pelvic exam said, 'Hey, I have this new catheter concept I wanted to talk to you about'" [1].

Dr. Cook was startled but agreed to meet at a cafe in Saratoga. She liked the concept and introduced Sarna to physicians at UC Davis and Stanford. "It really blossomed from there" [1].

Sarna's advice on early networking: "Right when you graduate from college or even a few years out, no one's expecting you to have a huge Rolodex. Instead, focus on the personal network that you've already built and just ask for introductions to people who are in the industry that you're trying to do something in. If you try hard enough and you do it for long enough, you will get to the right people" [1].

## Fundraising: 50 Rejections and Bias

Sarna was rejected at least 50 times by investors [1]. She identifies multiple barriers: "I'm a brown woman, I'm a woman of color, and I didn't have any one of the many higher degrees that they like -- MBA, PhD, MD. So there I am with my little bachelor's degree trying to go out and start a company" [1].

Investor reactions to women's health specifically included statements like "women's health isn't a big enough market" and referring to it as "bikini medicine" [1]. Sarna's internal response: "I guess 50 percent of the population isn't big enough for you" [1].

Two women investors finally committed, each conditional on finding one other investor. The seed round closed and enabled the first phase of development [1].

Sarna's framing for underrepresented founders: "It's hard to be a founder no matter who you are, but I would be lying if I said it's equally challenging for a woman." Her advice: "These days I sincerely believe that there are enough people who believe in your potential that you can just ignore the folks who are being biased for whatever reason. Don't let it impact your confidence and find the right people to work with" [1].

## From Prototype to FDA Clearance

After the seed round, Sarna attracted a "world-class team" and raised approximately $20 million more in venture capital. The device went through prototype development, multiple clinical studies, and FDA clearance -- becoming "the first and only device cleared to collect cells from the fallopian tube, which you can then evaluate for malignant features" [1].

## The $275M Acquisition

Sarna had no interest in an acquisition. Her goal was getting the product to patients as quickly as possible. But several companies, including Boston Scientific, read about the clinical data and initiated conversations [1].

The offer came via email on an ordinary workday. "I cannot describe just the pure disbelief that I felt in that moment. Can you imagine just being rejected over and over again, struggling to raise any amount of money to push this life-saving device forward, and then all of a sudden having that much validation from a company that knows the industry so well?" [1].

She signed the acquisition papers holding her son on his first birthday, with her mother on one side and her husband on the other [1].

## Transition to YC

After the sale, Sarna was introduced to [[Jared Friedman]] at YC. He was looking for YC's first healthcare and biotech partner. Sarna spoke at a YC event in front of 60-100 founders: "It was the first time that I had relayed my story, and the energy in the room was electrifying" [1].

She realized that "everything I went through, all of the struggle, all the failures, all the mistakes that I made, could actually be put in a productive place" [1]. She joined YC as a visiting partner and later became a full Group Partner.

Her evaluation framework for healthcare founders: "As long as you really know your stuff, I am not going to look at whether or not you have an MD or PhD and make my decision exclusively on that. I want to know that there's a big problem that needs solving and you are the founder to do it, because no one believes in the solution as much as you do" [1].
