# Gobble: Five Years in the Trough of Sorrow

Gobble (YC W14) is a dinner kit delivery service that helps anyone cook dinner in 10 minutes. Founded by [[Ooshma Garg]], the company spent five and a half years iterating through four different product versions before finding [[Product-Market Fit]] and raising a Series A from Trinity Ventures and Andreessen Horowitz. [[Paul Graham]] [tweeted](https://twitter.com/paulg/status/659749829040410624) about Garg: "Ooshma Garg does not give up" [1].

## Origin: Solving Her Own Problem

Garg was running a previous startup at Stanford, working 16-18 hours a day and eating nothing but takeout and Starbucks. She grew up with home-cooked North Indian food; her father would sing while cooking traditional meals. As an adult, recreating those meals took two hours after a full workday [1].

She posted an ad on Craigslist: would any chef make a home-cooked meal for $8 a plate? She received 70 responses in 24 hours. "There are so many people in the Bay Area that are proud of their food and want to earn a living from it" [1]. She chose favorite chefs, created a calendar of what they would cook each day, emailed friends asking if they wanted to order, and personally drove deliveries. The entire business was prototyped with email, PayPal, and her car [1].

## Searching for a Technical Co-Founder

Garg is a mechanical engineer with no software experience. She searched for a technical co-founder through coffee dates and friend introductions but "no one was as passionate about the idea as I was, they weren't willing to quit their job, and we just didn't connect in a deep way" [1].

She decided to proceed without one: "Screw it. I'm just gonna do it anyway" [1]. The investors agreed. She had the passion, a proof of concept, and previous startup experience. The co-founder fear did not need to be a blocker. Gobble raised a [seed round](http://techcrunch.com/2011/05/11/marketplace-for-home-cooked-food-gobble-raises-1-2m-from-founder-collective-reid-hoffman-and-other-angels/) from Founder Collective, Reid Hoffman, and other angels [1].

## The Multi-Year Trough

### Snake 1: First Engineer Quits Before Launch

Weeks before launch, Garg's first engineer quit. "He walked in and said, 'This is too much pressure. I can't handle it anymore. I quit'" [1]. She found Ruby on Rails consultants through investor introductions, begged one to learn the codebase, and eventually hired replacement engineers -- but the launch was delayed [1].

### The Marketplace Phase

Gobble launched as a chef marketplace and was covered by TechCrunch. But after the honeymoon period, Garg had to fight for every customer: "I put coupons in Palo Alto coffee shops and went to mom's events and pitched our company, and every time somebody signed up, I personally emailed them, followed up, and gave them a promo code to order again" [1].

### Snake 2: Failed Series A (First Attempt)

An investor offered to lead a Series A. Garg structured her entire fundraising process around this one firm, meeting only five investors. After the partner meeting, the deal fell apart. Lessons learned: "I didn't meet many different investors. I met five, not forty. I thought that a deal was done before it was done. Next time, I would know that the deal isn't done until the money is in the bank" [1].

### The On-Demand Experiment

Customer feedback said they wanted to order on demand rather than planning in the morning. In 2012, before most on-demand companies existed, Gobble launched "Gobble Instant." It failed: the target market was suburban families, delivery took 30+ minutes, food wilted in transit, and the economics were negative [1].

### Snake 3: Investor Pressure to Sell

Some investors were losing confidence. One suggested selling the company to a clothing startup for an acqui-hire. Garg refused because "our mission was still alive and well. Busy people still were not eating home-cooked food" [1].

### The Couch Revelation

Garg laid on the couch in her office, surrounded herself with blank paper, and "stopped listening to our investors and other people, and started listening to myself" [1]. She reflected on accumulated insights: dinner is daily, tastes are stable, timing is predictable. Dinner should be a subscription, personalized like Netflix or Pandora but for food [1].

### The Subscription Model

The personalized dinner subscription was Gobble's third product version. Revenue was five times higher than any previous version. But when Garg tried to raise Series A again, the food startup space had become competitive. Investors had already placed bets or wanted to wait. The second Series A attempt failed [1].

### Near Death

With no money left and existing investors unwilling to provide more (too old a company, no space in the fund, Thanksgiving and Christmas tanking numbers), Garg's bank account screenshot showed she had made her last full payroll. "Nobody knew this but me" [1].

## YC as Lifeline

A friend suggested Y Combinator. Garg's initial reaction: "What?! We've already raised seed money, and we've already launched a product. Why would I join a startup incubator?" [1]. But the alternative was death. "When you look at it that way, you put your pride aside and you keep the company alive" [1].

YC helped Gobble get back to basics. The team visited customers' homes, watching them cook and eat. The key observation: when people microwaved the prepared meals, "they would cringe" [1]. Microwaving was not a dignified way to feed a family.

## The Dinner Kit: Mise en Place for Families

Executive Chef Thomas Ricci taught Garg about *mise en place* -- the restaurant concept where prep cooks handle chopping, saucing, and marinating so executive chefs can finish and serve. Gobble applied this to home cooking: the company does the prep work, and the customer finishes the meal in one pan in 10 minutes [1].

The team tested the concept by giving TaskRabbit workers dinner kits to deliver to friends in Palo Alto. "Our social media blew up, the word of mouth blew up, the pride and the smiles blew up, and the growth blew up. We had hit the promised land" [1].

At the time of this talk, Gobble had zero marketing hires. All growth was organic. "That's what product market fit looks like" [1].

## Series A: The Same Firms That Said No

Gobble raised its Series A from Trinity Ventures and Andreessen Horowitz -- both of which had rejected Garg in her two previous fundraising attempts. "The lesson there is that it's important to accept rejection with grace. Understand that startups are a marathon and a long game, and the same people you may not work with right now, you may work with in the future" [1].

## Negative Press as Motivation

During the trough, a journalist wrote: "Gobble was puttering about on the peninsula with its paltry funding and ever-evolving business model." Garg's response to her team: "Let's put it in a box. We are the underdog. No one knows what we're up to, and we are about to come out like a storm" [1].

## Three Takeaways: Be a GEM

Garg's framework for founder resilience:

1. **Grit**: "The thing that comes from your core. It's this place inside of you when no one believes in you that tells you whether you should keep going." Compete with yourself, not others [1].
2. **Experiment**: "A company is not just a product, a company is solving a problem. It's a mission. And if the mission is alive, you should keep working on it." Gobble launched four different versions to find what worked [1].
3. **Mission**: Gobble's mission is to "create love through food." The mission outlasted every product iteration [1].
