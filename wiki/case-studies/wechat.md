# WeChat: Growth and Platform Strategy

WeChat is a mobile messaging platform created by Tencent that grew from 0 to 800M+ monthly active users in less than 6 years [1]. As of the analysis by [[Anu Hariharan]] (partnering with China Tech Insights, a research group within Tencent), WeChat had 889 million MAUs who used the app an average of 50+ minutes and 9-11 separate times per day [1]. That daily engagement matches "the combined time users spend across a portfolio of Facebook apps, including Instagram, Facebook and Facebook messenger" [1].

WeChat evolved from a messaging app into a platform that changed how Chinese people communicate, socialize, pay each other, and pay for goods [1]. Connie Chan of a16z described WeChat as "the one app to rule them all." Its influence extends globally: iMessage and Facebook Messenger both show "hints of WeChat" [1].

## Lesson 1: Build Your Own Competition

Tencent was already China's biggest social company before WeChat launched, with ~650M monthly active instant messenger accounts via QQ (a desktop messaging app similar to ICQ/AOL) [1]. A mobile version of QQ launched in 2008 but was "weighted down by desktop-centric features" like multiple user statuses, large file transfers, and embedded music streaming [1].

Tencent made a bold decision: a small skunkworks team from the QQ Mail group (the company's core product) was assigned to build a new mobile app that could potentially compete with other Tencent divisions [1]. **This team of only seven engineers created the first version of WeChat in just three months** [1]. WeChat launched in January 2011 as a simple messaging and photo sharing app.

WeChat was not first to market. Xiaomi's MiTalk led with 5M registered users [1]. WeChat saw little growth initially and realized it needed more than just chat. In May 2011, three months after launch, WeChat added voice messaging, allowing short audio notes [1]. This was strategic: "the native mobile keyboard to type Chinese characters at the time was not easy to use and consumers found sending voice notes intuitive, personal and convenient" [1]. Voice messaging spiked daily downloads to 50-60K consistently. The inspiration came from TalkBox and Kakao Talk [1].

Today Mobile QQ continues with 652M MAUs (as of Dec 2016), differentiated toward entertainment for younger users (animated video stickers, facial beautifying tools) [1]. Tencent proactively differentiated the two platforms.

The lesson parallels Amazon cannibalizing its book sales with Kindle and Uber moving from town cars to UberX: "If you don't create a culture of disrupting yourself, then a competitor is likely to disrupt you" [1].

## Lesson 2: Design for Groups ("Group Effect")

Allen Zhang (founder of WeChat) developed the product around "Group Effect": an individual's behavior alone can be quite distinct from how they behave in a group [1]. The team observed how features would be adopted by groups of users vs. individuals.

WeChat had to solve the zero-friends-on-day-one problem [1]. Facebook was famously relentless about getting users to 10 friends within 14 days. WeChat took a different approach:

**People Nearby**: Users could see other WeChat users (not yet contacts) in their vicinity [1]. The location-enabled feature "satisfied users' curiosity about knowing what's going on around them" and boosted user acquisition to over 100K per day [1].

**QQ Account Linking**: WeChat enabled importing existing QQ social graphs, but only for users already on both platforms, preserving differentiation [1]. This pushed registered users to 50M by November 2011, overtaking MiTalk [1].

**Shake**: Users shook their phones to find a random person to chat with, using GPS and accelerometer [1]. Two purposes: engagement for new users and offline visibility (like Pokemon Go). Used over 100M times in its first month (October 2011) [1].

**Message in a Bottle**: Users exchanged random messages by throwing a virtual bottle in the sea [1].

By November 2011, after launching these group interaction features, new user acquisition hit a record of 200K per day [1].

## Lesson 3: Extend Features from Users' Inner Desires

Allen Zhang was passionate about understanding the motivation behind communication nuances rather than relying on surveys or competition analysis [1].

**Moments** (launched in WeChat's second year, Chinese translation means "friend circles"): A photo-sharing feature where comments and likes are visible only to mutual friends, not friends-of-friends [1]. This was deliberate, inspired by Path (a US photo sharing app that limited network size) and "an old Chinese philosophy called 'circle cultures,' meaning smaller circles are much stronger at the core and as the circles get bigger, the ties get weaker" [1].

A team of only 10 people spent 4 months making over 30 versions of Moments before deciding on one to launch [1]. WeChat kept product teams lean for "relentless focus" [1].

**Official Accounts (OAs)** (released 2012): Allowed "one-way follow" similar to Twitter/Weibo [1]. Unlike Twitter, celebrities could send text, voice, and video updates that looked like regular WeChat messages, creating "a feeling of a personal and private conversation" [1]. Many celebrities set up WeChat OAs even though they had millions of followers on Weibo. OAs expanded to brands and businesses, enabling direct communication for booking, customer care, and Q&A [1]. "Where Twitter/Weibo were tools for businesses to broadcast and brand, WeChat via OAs became a channel for direct communication with customers" [1].

**Sticker Store**: WeChat was the first to create a marketplace for illustrators who could earn tip money via "rewards" from users (up to RMB 200 / $25 USD) [1]. The marketplace drove healthy competition among illustrators [1].

## Lesson 4: Big Ideas Come from Solving Your Own Problems

**WeChat Red Packets** originated from a Cantonese tradition: every manager at Tencent gives employees a red envelope with cash after Chinese New Year [1]. As the company expanded, this became exhausting, so managers asked for a technical solution [1]. See also [[Do Things That Don't Scale]].

A team of 20 built the first version in 3 weeks [1]. It was tested on WeChat OA users and immediately well-received. The team was "an avid user of its own products. Often they use the product in the middle of the night as a 'single user' to pay attention to details and identify bugs early" [1].

From New Year's Eve 2014 through the next afternoon, over 5M users used WeChat Red Packets [1]. In 2015, over 1B Red Packets were sent during Chinese New Year [1].

The random Red Packet version (each recipient gets a random fraction of the total, no more than RMB 200 each) created a lottery-like experience: "you have no idea how much money you will receive before you open the packet, and getting more than others can be a sign of good luck" [1]. This is another example of Group Effect: adoption increased as more users received packets within group chats [1].

Red Packets drove WeChat Pay adoption [1]. Growth was initially through tight social circles (friends and family willing to connect bank accounts). This trust-building was necessary before extending to merchant payments. A partnership with Didi (China's Uber) for in-app payments opened broader payment partnerships [1].

By 2016, Tencent had 600M mobile payment MAUs and over 600M average daily payment transactions [1].

## Lesson 5: Monetize Subtly

**Game Center**: Bridged WeChat with Tencent's game expertise [1]. A game called Rhythm Master had 700K DAUs but exploded to 17M DAUs (>20x) after launching in Game Center [1].

**Moments Native Ads** (January 2015): Limited to one ad per day per user (vs. Facebook's one per ~10 posts) [1]. Despite restrictions on ad load and limiting conversation to mutual friends, an Airbnb ad in Moments received 1.8M+ views after sharing, saw 5x increase in click-through rate, and drove 600% increase in new sign-ups [1].

**Social Coupons**: When a user redeems a coupon during an offline purchase, they can opt-in to share with friends [1]. Vendors can reward sharing users with additional coupons, creating a viral credit system [1].

## Lesson 6: Measure What You Value, Not What You Are Supposed To

WeChat does not only measure growth by users or messages [1]. They also measure "how deeply is the product engaged in every aspect of daily life (e.g., the number of tasks WeChat can help with in a day)" [1]. KPIs are shared only among department heads. Engineers receive the daily message: "are you creating more value for the user?" [1]

WeChat limits friends count, promotional messages, and social engagement display, treating excess as "noise that may distract from the utility of WeChat" [1].

**QR Codes**: WeChat elevated QR codes as a link from offline to online, becoming "the lynchpin for China's online-to-offline boom in 2015" [1]. Tencent's Pony Ma described QR codes as "a label of abundant online information attached to the offline world" [1]. QR codes failed in the US for three reasons: (1) the top phone and top social app did not support scanning, (2) users needed dedicated scanner apps leading to mobile websites (more cumbersome than typing URLs), (3) early use cases were low-value marketing spam [1].

**Mini Programs**: An extension of Official Accounts enabling users to access services without downloading apps, similar to how web browsers eliminated the need to install desktop programs [1]. Local restaurants and stores can be part of WeChat Mini Programs. Discovered via social connections or offline QR code scanning [1].

## Lesson 7: Don't Play Favorites with Features

One of WeChat's core product tenets is that it is a "tool" with utility-oriented qualities (highly functional, quick, easy to use) [1]. Allen Zhang "actively worries about users spending too much time in any one feature since this could prevent users from doing other valuable things" [1].

Zhang compares WeChat to Google: "once the user finds the right link, Google sends the user directly to the desired site" [1]. The team gets worried if too much time is spent on marketing content in Moments because "low quality content can distract users and reduce value long term" [1].

Despite the deep feature set, WeChat maintains a 4-tab UI: chat, contacts, discover, me [1]. "Whenever they experiment with adding one, the left-most tab sees conversion rates drop by 20-30%" [1].
