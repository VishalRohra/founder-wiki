# WeChat

WeChat is a Chinese super app with 889 million monthly active users (as of 2016) who use the app an average of 50+ minutes and 9-11 separate times per day [1]. This usage matches the combined time users spend across the entire portfolio of Facebook apps (Instagram, Facebook, and Messenger) [1]. [[Anu Hariharan]], partnering with China Tech Insights (a Tencent research group), documented the growth strategy that took WeChat from 0 to 800M+ MAUs in less than six years.

WeChat has inspired a new category of "messaging as a platform" globally. Hints of WeChat's influence appear in Apple's iMessage and Facebook's Messenger platform [1]. As Hariharan puts it: "At this point, WeChat is not only an app -- it is simply the internet" [1].

## Lesson 1: Build Your Own Competition

Tencent was already one of China's top internet companies in 2010, with ~650 million monthly active instant messenger accounts through QQ, its desktop messaging app [1]. The company had created a mobile version of QQ in 2008, but it was "weighted down by desktop-centric features" like multiple user statuses, large file transfers, and embedded services [1].

Tencent made a decisive move: it assembled a small skunkworks team from the QQ Mail team (its core product at the time) to build a new mobile app that could potentially compete with other Tencent products [1]. This team of only seven engineers created the first version of WeChat in three months [1]. WeChat launched in January 2011 as a simple messaging and photo sharing app.

WeChat was not the first mobile messenger. Xiaomi's MiTalk led with 5 million registered users [1]. Initial growth was slow. The breakthrough came in May 2011 (three months after launch) when WeChat added voice messaging, allowing users to send short audio notes [1]. This was inspired by TalkBox and Kakao Talk, and was strategic because "the native mobile keyboard to type Chinese characters at the time was not easy to use and consumers found sending voice notes intuitive, personal and convenient" [1]. As one of the first messengers to offer voice, text, and photos in a combined solution, daily downloads spiked to 50,000-60,000 consistently [1].

Mobile QQ continues to operate with 652 million MAUs (as of December 2016), differentiated by entertainment features (animated video stickers, facial beautifying tools), while WeChat focuses on utility services for daily life [1].

The lesson echoes Amazon's cannibalization of book sales with Kindle and Uber's move from town cars to UberX: "If you don't create a culture of disrupting yourself, then a competitor is likely to disrupt you" [1].

## Lesson 2: Design for Groups ("Group Effect")

Allen Zhang, founder of WeChat, was "adamant about developing the product around 'Group Effect'" -- the observation that individual user behavior differs from behavior in groups [1]. The WeChat team focused on how features would be adopted by groups of users, not just individuals. This accelerated adoption rates through bandwagon effects [1].

### Solving the Cold Start Problem

Like all social networks, WeChat faced the zero-friends problem. While Facebook was famous for getting users to 10 friends within 14 days, WeChat took a different approach [1].

**People Nearby** was a location-enabled feature that showed users others nearby who were also using the feature [1]. It satisfied curiosity about what was happening around them and boosted community feeling. User acquisition jumped to over 100,000 per day [1].

WeChat then enabled account linking between WeChat and QQ, allowing users to import their existing social graph. Importantly, the importing feature was only exposed to users already on both platforms, preserving differentiation [1]. This pushed registered users to 50 million by November 2011, overtaking MiTalk [1].

**Shake** used GPS and accelerometer data to let users shake their phones to find random chat partners [1]. It served dual purposes: giving new users engagement and creating an offline-visible phenomenon (similar to Pokemon Go). Shake was used over 100 million times in its first month (October 2011) [1].

**Message in a Bottle** allowed users to exchange random messages by throwing a virtual bottle into a virtual sea [1].

After launching these group interaction features, new user acquisition hit 200,000 per day in November 2011 [1].

## Lesson 3: Extend Features from Users' Inner Desires

Allen Zhang was "passionate about keying into the inner desires behind an exhibited behavior, which often led to features that fit naturally into users' worlds" [1]. WeChat's research approach was observational rather than traditional: the team closely observed how users behaved among groups of friends and strangers in everyday life, rather than relying on surveys, interviews, or competitive analysis [1].

### Moments

Launched in WeChat's second year, Moments (Chinese translation: "friend circles") was a photo-sharing feature for sharing with a private group of friends [1]. Unlike Facebook's News Feed, comments and likes on a post can only be seen by mutual friends, not friends of friends. This was inspired by Path (a US photo-sharing app limited to close connections) but grounded in an old Chinese philosophy called "circle cultures" -- "smaller circles are much stronger at the core and as the circles get bigger, the ties get weaker" [1].

A team of only 10 people spent 4 months making over 30 versions of Moments before deciding on a version to launch [1]. The core product functions remain largely unchanged from the original version.

### Official Accounts (OA)

Released in 2012, Official Accounts allowed a one-way follow (similar to Twitter, competing with Weibo) where fans could subscribe to celebrity content [1]. The differentiator: celebrities could send text, voice, and video updates that looked like regular WeChat messages, "creating a feeling of a personal and private conversation" [1]. This prompted many celebrities to set up WeChat OAs even with millions of Weibo followers.

OAs expanded to brands and businesses, becoming a channel for direct customer communication (booking, customer care, feedback, Q&A) rather than just broadcasting [1]. "Where Twitter/Weibo were tools for businesses to broadcast and brand, WeChat (via OAs) became a channel for direct communication with customers" [1].

### Sticker Store

WeChat created a marketplace for illustrators to sell stickers, with users able to "reward" any amount up to RMB 200 ($25 USD) to creators they liked [1]. This marketplace created competition among illustrators for the top spot, enriching the user experience.

## Lesson 4: Big Ideas Come from Solving Your Own Problems

**WeChat Red Packets** originated from a Tencent company custom: every manager gives employees a red envelope with cash on the first workday after Chinese New Year [1]. As the company expanded, giving out physical red packets became exhausting, so managers asked for a technical solution. The result became WeChat's most transformative feature.

The first version was developed by a team of 20 in 3 weeks [1]. From New Year's Eve 2014 through 4pm the next day, over 5 million users sent Red Packets [1]. Growth exploded in 2015 with over 1 billion Red Packets sent during Chinese New Year [1].

The random Red Packet variant makes receiving money in a group chat like "a lottery combined with a fortune cookie -- you have no idea how much money you will receive before you open the packet, and getting more than others can be a sign of good luck" [1]. This is another example of the Group Effect: since Red Packets are sent in group chats, adoption increased as more users received them.

Red Packets drove WeChat Pay adoption by building trust through close social circles first (friends and family sending money), then extending to merchants, online and offline stores [1]. A partnership with Didi (China's Uber equivalent) expanded the payment network further [1].

By 2016, Tencent had 600 million mobile payment MAUs and over 600 million average daily payment transactions [1].

## Lesson 5: Monetize Subtly

WeChat demonstrates that monetization and user growth are not mutually exclusive [1].

**Game Center** connected WeChat with Tencent's core gaming expertise. A Tencent game called Rhythm Master had 700,000 DAUs after a year, but exploded to 17 million DAUs (>20x growth) after launching in Game Center [1].

**Moments Native Ads** (launched January 2015) limited native ads to one per day in a user's feed, compared to Facebook's roughly one ad per 10 posts [1]. Users interact with and share ads like friend posts. An Airbnb ad on Moments received more than 1.8 million views after sharing, saw a 5x increase in click-through rate, and drove a 600% increase in new sign-ups [1].

[![WeChat Moments Ad Example](images/3t-how-wechat-grew-to-be-the-1-app-in-the-world/683b74fa5f3d.png)](https://blog.ycombinator.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/04/WeChat-Moment-Ad.png)

**Social Coupons** adapted the classic coupon model to friend-based endorsement. When a user redeems a coupon, they can opt to share the offer with friends, extending vendor reach through trusted recommendations [1].

## Lesson 6: Measure What You Value, Not What You're Supposed To

WeChat does not measure growth solely by users or messages. Instead, the team also measures "how deeply is the product engaged in every aspect of daily life (e.g., the number of tasks WeChat can help with in a day)" [1]. The daily engineering message is: "are you creating more value for the user?" [1].

WeChat deliberately limits friend counts, promotional messages, and social circle engagement visibility, viewing excess as "noise that may distract from the utility of WeChat" [1].

**QR Codes** became WeChat's bridge between online and offline worlds. Tencent's Pony Ma described QR codes as "a label of abundant online information attached to the offline world" [1]. QR codes failed in the US for three reasons: the dominant phone and social app did not support scanning, dedicated scanner apps added friction, and early use cases focused on low-value marketing content [1].

**Mini Programs** extended Official Accounts to enable in-app service access without downloading or installing apps [1]. A local restaurant or store could operate within WeChat Mini Programs, discovered via social connections or QR code scanning. WeChat claims this drives higher purchasing conversion rates [1].

## Lesson 7: Don't Play Favorites with Features

Allen Zhang actively worried about users spending too much time in any single feature, as this could prevent them from using other valuable services [1]. WeChat's core product tenet is that it is a "tool" with utility-oriented qualities. Zhang compared this to Google: "once the user finds the right link, Google sends the user directly to the desired site" [1].

The team limits marketing content on Moments and restricts broadcast messages from Subscription Accounts to one per day, without push notifications [1].

Despite the deep feature set, WeChat maintains only 4 tabs: chat, contacts, discover, and me [1]. They have been "very disciplined in keeping the app to only 4 tabs, as whenever they experiment with adding one, the left-most tab sees conversion rates drop by 20-30%" [1].
