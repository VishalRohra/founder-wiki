# Lambda School (BloomTech): Reinventing CS Education with Income Share Agreements

Lambda School (YC S17), later renamed Bloom Institute of Technology, provides a CS education that is free until the student gets a job. The company was founded by [[Austen Allred]] and uses income share agreements (ISAs) as its core business model: students pay nothing upfront and repay a percentage of their income after they are employed [1].

## Origin: Human Capital as a Mispriced Asset

Allred's founding insight came from moving to San Francisco from rural Utah. He observed that people of equivalent capability were earning $30,000 in Utah and $150,000 in San Francisco. The gap was not intelligence or work ethic but access to opportunity [1]. He frames the problem in financial terms: "If you look at any asset class in the United States, it's being traded on Bloomberg terminals instantly all over the world. You can trade a hundred thousand pounds of pork bellies without anything moving. For some reason humans don't have that" [1]. Human capital, in his view, is "the most unoptimized asset class we've ever had" [1].

The ISA model emerged from this observation. Asking someone making $30,000 a year to spend $10,000 on education with 7% interest represents spending half their annual income on a bet. Most cannot afford that risk [1]. By deferring payment until after employment, Lambda School removed the primary barrier to entry.

## The Anti-University Model

Allred is not a college graduate. He argues that the conventional advice of "go to college, doesn't matter what it costs, get a degree, doesn't matter what you study" is "not only not good advice, it's probably bad advice, net-net" [1]. He points to the structural misalignment of incentives in traditional education: University of Phoenix gets paid $80,000 whether or not a student succeeds because loans are backed by the federal government [1].

Lambda School's nine-month program compresses a four-year degree into one focused period. Allred calculates that by the time a university student graduates, a Lambda School student already has three years of work experience, has paid off the ISA, and is at least a quarter-million dollars ahead [1].

The school is entirely vocational. Allred sees value in liberal arts education but believes it should come after financial stability, not before: "We want to help you make as much money as you can and then when you're making a lot of money, it's a whole lot easier to make decisions around what you'll learn" [1].

## The ISA Is the Easy Part

When competitors began copying the ISA model, Allred argued the business model itself is not the moat. "Plugging, throwing an ISA on the front end is the easy part" [1]. The hard parts are:

1. **Underwriting student risk.** No credit score exists for human potential. Lambda School had to build proprietary models to predict which students would succeed. "Nobody knows how to underwrite student risk" [1].
2. **Training students effectively online.** The entire code bootcamp industry believed online-only education was ineffective. Allred built the instructional design from first principles [1].
3. **Placing students nationally at scale.** Lambda School hired 37 interview sourcers to function as a talent agency for its graduates. Networking skills were built directly into the curriculum starting at week three [1].
4. **Reducing dropout rates.** Initially, dropout was "what was killing us." By the time of this interview, Lambda School was graduating over 85% of students who start day one [1].

## Online Education: A Secret Advantage

Lambda School is fully remote by design. Allred sees this not as a limitation but as a competitive advantage. In-person classes rely on the "crutch" of physical presence, while Lambda School must use "data and software and instructional design to make sure everybody's working on the right thing at the right time" [1].

Allred observed that in his own college experience, at least 50% of students in any lecture were on Facebook. Lambda School's approach flips the traditional CS curriculum: instead of starting with C and memory management (which produces high dropout), students have a site they can see and interact with on day one. "You do need to know how to write C to be a good engineer. You do need to know how memory management works, but maybe let's not start with memory management as an entry level point to software engineering" [1].

## From Bootstrapped to VC-Backed

Lambda School was profitable before joining YC. Allred had sworn off venture capital after a traumatic experience with his first company, where a Series A fell through on December 23rd with docs already signed. The VC's secretary called to cancel, and Allred had to call his entire team to tell them "I think it's over" [1].

Despite this history, Allred chose to raise for Lambda School after modeling the trade-off with a YC partner: the dilution (roughly 15%) would enable 10x growth. By the time of this interview, Lambda School had raised $48 million [1]. The decision framework was explicit: "If you have a company that can grow at extreme scale and capture a market and create way more value, that's why you raise VC" [1].

## Product Quality as Unmeasurable Moat

Allred argues that product quality is systematically undervalued by analysts and investors because it cannot be quantified. "Revenue is a very, very trailing indicator of product" [1]. He points to Airbnb, Stripe, and Dropbox as companies that won despite competitors with more money or market share, specifically because product quality compounds over time through "a whole lot of subtlety built up" [1].

He uses Tesla as a canonical example: "The materials are worse, the build is probably not as good. They're not as good at assembling the pieces, but just the experience of that car versus the other one, it's not even close" [1].

## Expansion Plans

At the time of the interview, Lambda School was exploring expansion into other fields with labor shortages. Nursing was the leading candidate: there is a massive shortage of nurses due to an aging population, but nursing schools are capped by the federal 90-10 rule (only 90% of revenue can come from federal funds). Lambda School's ISA model could bypass this constraint entirely [1].
