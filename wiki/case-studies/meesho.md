# Meesho: Social Commerce in India

Meesho (YC S16) is a platform that enables anyone in India to start and grow a social store on WhatsApp, Facebook, or Instagram. By 2019, the platform had close to half a million monthly active social store owners selling across fashion, food, travel, cosmetics, and other categories [1]. The company was co-founded by Vidit Aatrey and Sanjeev Barnwal.

## Origin: From Hyperlocal Fashion to WhatsApp Commerce

Aatrey and Barnwal met at IIT Delhi (class of 2008-2012). They did not plan to start a company in college. In 2015, three years after graduation, Barnwal called Aatrey from Sony in Japan, asking for a reference check on a startup he wanted to join. Aatrey's response: "If you want to work with a startup, let's do something ourselves first. If it doesn't work, then you go there" [1]. They agreed to start a company on that call, with no idea in mind. The next day they created a Google spreadsheet listing ideas and started talking to people around them [1].

Their first idea was FashNear, a hyperlocal fashion delivery service (similar to what Swiggy is for food, but for fashion). They built it because "doing O2O, hyperlocal, was very popular in India" in 2015 [1]. After four to five months, they learned a hard lesson: "In fashion, fulfillment is like the third thing that is most important. The first thing is selection, which you can never have around you" [1].

The discovery that led to Meesho came from the same work. While onboarding small shops in Bangalore as suppliers for FashNear, Aatrey's pitch was "I'm going to take you online." The shopkeepers replied: "We're already online... I sell on WhatsApp" [1]. They had created WhatsApp groups of existing customers and posted photos of new stock, taking orders through the chat. One shopkeeper reported selling 30-40% of his total monthly business through WhatsApp [1].

Aatrey and Barnwal built a mobile-only, India-localized version of Shopify, tailored for doing commerce on WhatsApp and Facebook. They applied to YC and raised an angel round with this product [1].

## The Second Pivot: From Tools for Shops to a Reseller Marketplace

Six months after the tool launched, retention was lower than expected. Speaking to users revealed that more than half were not the intended audience of offline shopkeepers. They were housewives running boutiques on WhatsApp -- creating logos, curating products from suppliers, and selling to their own networks [1]. Most were women based in Gujarat (a manufacturing hub) who sourced products via WhatsApp from local suppliers.

Aatrey created an internal experiment. He split the company into two teams in two rooms: one continuing the original Meesho product, the other building "Meesho Supply" -- a product to enable anyone to run a boutique like these women. Critically, Meesho Supply launched on WhatsApp with no app or website [1]. In six months, the new product was doubling every month. The original product's retention remained stagnant [1].

The decision to shut down the original product and rename Meesho Supply to Meesho was difficult. Aatrey attributes his ability to pivot to co-founder trust: "I have seen so many friends around me who were just not able to pivot because it was a hard conversation with their co-founder... Just because you have a co-founder you trust blindly, it was easier" [1]. The team initially resisted ("You keep changing what we do every six months"), but three months after the pivot, growth was so strong that "everyone was very clear that we made the right decision" [1].

Financial pressure also helped. Meesho was one of the few YC companies that did not raise significant money on Demo Day. The new product generated revenue by charging suppliers; the old product was free. "Everything was pointing in one direction" [1].

## Macro Tailwinds: Jio, UPI, and WhatsApp

Aatrey identifies three macro trends that drove Meesho's growth [1]:

1. **Reliance Jio** (launched 2016): Drastically reduced data costs in India. "Hundreds of millions of people in India have come online just the last two, three years" -- primarily in tier 2 and tier 3 cities [1].
2. **WhatsApp adoption**: WhatsApp was generally the first app most people used when they came online [1].
3. **UPI (Unified Payments Interface)**: Taught millions to transact online for the first time [1].

These new internet users were predominantly in smaller cities with lower incomes. They bought mostly unbranded products -- a category that destination marketplaces like Amazon and Flipkart had never figured out how to sell at scale in India [1].

## The Trust Deficit and Social Selling

India operates as a "trust deficit market." Aatrey explains: "Most people do not believe in corporate companies. They don't believe in their own government. They don't believe in the judicial system. Court cases run for hundreds of years" [1]. Trust is placed in personal communities. For unbranded products -- where quality is uncertain and there is no brand guarantee -- buyers trust people in their community over unknown merchants [1].

This is why 90% of Indian commerce (as of 2019) happens through small mom-and-pop stores, and why social selling through personal networks is a natural distribution channel for unbranded goods [1]. Meesho digitizes the same trust relationship that existed in offline community commerce.

## User Profile: Aspiring Entrepreneurs

Ninety percent of Meesho's resellers are women. More than 80% are from tier 2, tier 3, and smaller cities. Seventy-five percent are housewives [1]. The typical average user earns $150-200 per month; the top 5-10% earn $400-500 [1]. Most use it as secondary household income (15-30% of primary income) [1].

The economic impact matters less to users than the identity it provides. Aatrey describes a woman who had asked her husband for money to start a boutique every year for 20 years and never received it. On Meesho, she started a store with zero investment [1]. Aatrey says: "Most of our most impactful stories are not just about how much money this person makes. It's a lot about what were they doing before... A lot about how you empower me, what identity do you give me, people start giving me more respect" [1].

## Building for Users You Are Not

Aatrey identifies a core challenge: "I am not a woman and I am not a housewife" [1]. His response has been systematic user closeness:

- **CEO as customer support**: From day one, every new user was added to Aatrey's personal WhatsApp. "Any problem, you tell me." By 2019, 100-200 top users were on his WhatsApp [1].
- **"We Hear You" monthly videos**: Aatrey records a monthly video for users describing what Meesho did last month, what it plans to do, and what is not working. Users respond with detailed feedback. "It's like a town hall for our users" [1].
- **Management call center rotations**: All managers sit in the call center for half a day every month, listening to user calls. "Everyone comes out with something new that they never thought about" [1].
- **User celebrations**: Users in Bangalore are invited to company events, including funding round parties [1].
- **Community mentorship**: A Reddit-type community in the app connects new users with experienced sellers. Top 5% of users serve as mentors. "These people are helping each other out" [1].

Aatrey frames this as a general principle: "The best thing to do is build a product that solves your own problem. The second best thing to do is build a product that solves a problem for someone who can't solve their own problem" [1].

## Growth and Quality Cycles

Meesho's growth came in six-month cycles alternating between expansion and quality improvement. "You grow very fast, and then you realize you've carried so much debt around -- a lot of rework, a lot of manual things -- and then you start focusing on how do you improve quality" [1].

During high-growth periods, quality was deliberately compromised. During lean seasons (e.g., post-Diwali), the company focused on fixing accumulated technical and operational debt [1].

On customer support specifically: "If you need customer support, that means something is wrong with the product. You have to go back to the drawing board and understand: why are people calling me?" [1].

## Competing with Facebook and Amazon

Aatrey argues that Facebook (WhatsApp's parent) will not directly compete because social commerce is one of many use cases on the platform, and operating a supply chain is "not in their DNA" [1]. He draws a parallel to Tencent, which invested in commerce use cases on WeChat rather than building them in-house [1].

For Amazon and Flipkart, the issue is different: those marketplaces primarily sell branded products. Selling unbranded, long-tail products at scale requires trust-based distribution -- the reseller model -- which destination marketplaces have not cracked [1].

## CEO Role Evolution

Aatrey describes three phases of his role [1]:

1. **Pre-PMF**: "My whole job was just to figure out what the business model will be in the long term." He did everything on growth and product. Meesho hired its first product manager after Series A and its second after Series B [1].
2. **Post-PMF**: Role shifted to hiring, culture, and course correction. "Nothing else" [1].
3. **At 700 employees**: Ensuring the right people are in place, motivating the team, and occasional direction changes [1].

## The Indian Startup Ecosystem

Aatrey observes a shift in India's startup culture. When Meesho started, "everyone was like, tell me what are you a copy of, either in US or in China." Meesho struggled because there was no obvious Western or Chinese parallel [1]. By 2019, new companies were building bottom-up for tier 2 and tier 3 users, "not thinking top-down, this is how it should be because it exists in US" [1].

He predicts the next decade of Indian startups will focus on serving the newly-online tier 2-4 population across housing, education, healthcare, and entertainment [1].

## Key Mistakes and Lessons

**Listening to VCs instead of users**: Aatrey's biggest early mistake. After deciding to start up, "we just kept listening to VCs." They spent four months chasing VC-defined milestones that kept moving. "After that, I've never gone to them asking what should I do, because I know they don't know" [1].

**Best decision**: Starting the company with Sanjeev Barnwal. "A lot of other mistakes that I've done have not been such big mistakes because I started with him" [1].

**Reversed opinion**: Aatrey initially believed the common advice that founders must solve their own problems. After Meesho's discovery process, "I don't hold that opinion anymore. You can build for others. It's hard, definitely, much, much harder than building a product for yourself" [1].
