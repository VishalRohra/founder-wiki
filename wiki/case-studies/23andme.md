# 23andMe: Disrupting Healthcare by Empowering the Consumer

23andMe provides direct-to-consumer genetic testing. Co-founded by [[Anne Wojcicki]], the company built the world's largest genetic dataset with health information (over 5 million people at the time of this interview) and navigated years of regulatory tension with the FDA. [[Sam Altman]] interviewed Wojcicki for YC's "How to Build the Future" series [1].

## Origin: Wall Street to Genetics

Wojcicki spent 10 years on Wall Street investing in healthcare before founding 23andMe. She covered small-cap biotechs, pharma companies, hospital systems, and insurance companies, developing a broad understanding of the healthcare landscape [1].

Her founding insight: healthcare is a system that "really effectively monetizes illness." She frames the misalignment starkly: "If I successfully get you to never be diabetic, no one makes money. But if you do become diabetic, there's all kinds of ways I can make money" [1]. No entity in the system was focused on keeping people healthy.

Two converging trends made 23andMe possible. First, the cost of genome sequencing was plummeting. Wojcicki had invested in genetics since 1996 and saw that consumers would soon be able to afford a scan of their entire genome [1]. Second, social networking proved that crowdsourcing worked. "If I just empowered everyone with their genetic information and I crowdsourced all this information... if I have the world's health information, what could I do?" [1].

Altman observes that many transformative companies emerge because "the founder sees an intersection of two pretty different but really important trends a few years before everybody else does" [1]. Wojcicki adds another dimension: the founder personally wants the product. "To me it was totally obvious" [1].

## The Trough of Sorrow

23andMe sold well on launch day but then slowed to 15-25 kits per day. The problem: "People don't understand genetics" [1]. Basic market research could have revealed this, but the team had to learn through the trough.

Wojcicki's response was relentless speaking. She accepted every conference invitation. She never gave the same talk twice, iterating based on audience feedback. Over time she accumulated roughly 500 different talks [1]. Through this process, she identified three barriers to adoption: people did not understand genetics at a basic level, they did not know the medical utility, and they needed social validation that genetic testing was normal [1].

The conversion breakthrough: when Wojcicki started leading with the statistic that over 50% of customers get a medically meaningful result, conversion improved [1].

## Navigating Regulation

23andMe went from unregulated to FDA-regulated, a transition Wojcicki describes as significant. The company's culture of hiring smart people who question everything clashed with the regulatory world's emphasis on rules and obedience [1].

Her view evolved from adversarial to respectful: "People who work at the FDA or people who work in government, it's a public servant job. They care about public safety" [1]. The key communication lesson for science companies: "You can't just tell you, 'Oh we're great, we have great science.' No one believes that. What you need to do is you need to publish" [1]. She never argues with scientists; she hands over publications.

Regulation, she acknowledges, also has an upside: "When you're regulated, it means there's rules. There's guidelines and you can figure out exactly what that means. In some ways, there's more of a path about actually how you have to execute" [1].

## Hiring for Humility

Wojcicki's first two hires were founding scientists found through a friend-of-a-friend network. They had a similar idea, which made the partnership work [1]. These early hires set the bar for talent throughout the company. Altman reinforces this: "If you screw up the first five, seven hires, it's almost impossible to get through that" [1].

Wojcicki describes a deliberate hiring philosophy: "There are a lot of smart people who have humility and there's a lot of smart people who want to tell you how smart they are. And we have opted for the humility" [1]. She looks for people who are "constantly open to feedback and constantly open to constructive criticism and learning about other areas" [1].

Altman adds his own framework: evaluate the net output a person has on the entire organization, not just their individual contribution. "Even if they do a lot themselves, if they make everybody else miserable" the hire is a failure [1]. Wojcicki reinforces with the Steve Jobs example: hiring the wrong senior leader (Sculley) was disastrous. "If you hire the wrong senior leadership and you don't fix it very quickly, it can sink you" [1].

## Persistence as Strategy

Wojcicki describes persistence as the defining characteristic of 23andMe's success. The company's Series A OKRs from 2007 were "remarkably similar" to current goals. One investor told her: "What's amazing, I look back on everything, you're a broken record" [1].

This consistency is a feature, not a bug: "One of the most important things I've learned at doing this now is the importance of persistence. You have to stick with it" [1]. She sees the long-term payoff of persistence -- kids who saw her speak years ago now tell her she inspired them to pursue PhDs in genetics. "It takes a decade to really see the impact" [1].

Altman summarizes: "That is the secret to overnight success is ten years of persistence" [1].

## Vision: Healthy at 100

Wojcicki's personal success metric is being healthy at 100. 23andMe's five-million-person dataset enables intervention studies at unprecedented scale: a six-arm weight loss study with 70,000 participants, research into whether behavior changes can lower Parkinson's risk in genetically high-risk individuals [1].

The ultimate promise: "At the end of this decade, I will tell you what to do" -- personalized, data-driven health recommendations based on genetics and behavior [1].
