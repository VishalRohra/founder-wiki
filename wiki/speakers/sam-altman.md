# Sam Altman

Sam Altman is the former president of Y Combinator. He also taught the Stanford course "How To Start a Startup." His YC Library posts cover pre-growth product love, later-stage operations, and navigating outcomes that are not billion-dollar companies.

## Worldview

Altman's startup advice is organized around stages and transitions:

- **Product love before growth.** His clearest message: "If you focus on trying to grow before you make a product people love, you are unlikely to succeed." He cites Facebook (obsessive from the start) and Airbnb (1000 days of slogging before PMF). When a product is loved, users become "a free marketing and sales force for you." He warns against papering over a mediocre product with capital.

- **The management transition.** Around 25 employees, the founder's job shifts from building a great product to building a great company. Every employee must know their manager. Delegation is essential but must be real delegation -- empower employees to own decisions, not just do grunt work. Clear communication in writing is "one of the highest leverage things you can do."

- **The alignment test.** Altman asks companies: "If I asked 10 employees what the top three goals are for the company, would they say the same thing?" Founders always say yes. Employees never agree. The fix: reiterate roadmap and goals frequently.

- **Equity generosity.** Successful YC companies "give out a lot of equity" -- plan for 3-5% per year for ten years. Refresher grants before vesting cliffs are critical.

- **The airplane vs. spaceship framework.** Not every startup will be a billion-dollar company. An "airplane" (profitable, on track for $100M) is a triumph. The danger: raising too much at too high a valuation when you know you have an airplane, which "severely limits your optionality" and "often precludes your opportunity for a good acquisition later."

- **10-year commitment.** "Have a strategy that assumes you will work on your company for the next 10 years." Very few founders make this commitment, and those who do "have a huge advantage."
