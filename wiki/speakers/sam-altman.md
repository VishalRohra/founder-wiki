# Sam Altman

Sam Altman served as president of Y Combinator, was part of the first YC batch in 2005 (with Loopt, a geolocation app), and later became CEO of OpenAI. Within the YC canon, his writing covers the full lifecycle of a startup from pre-growth through later-stage scaling, with particular emphasis on the transitions founders must navigate as companies grow. In a 2024 interview with [[Garry Tan]], Altman describes the founding of OpenAI, the scientific bets behind the GPT series, and why the current AI moment is the best time yet to start a company [4]. Beyond the YC library, his personal blog (2016-2023) produces a compact parallel canon — on idea generation [16], hard startups [15], investor behavior [14], productivity [12], career-level success [13], cofounders [11], the pre-growth phase [10], and the strategic value of being misunderstood [17].

## Worldview

Altman's advice centers on sequencing and discipline. His recurring theme is that founders fail by doing the right things at the wrong time -- pursuing [[Growth]] before the product is loved, ignoring management structure until it is painfully needed, or raising too much money when the company is an airplane rather than a spaceship.

He is pragmatic about outcomes. While much of YC culture emphasizes the billion-dollar path, Altman explicitly validates mid-range outcomes ($100M companies) and warns that forcing a spaceship trajectory on an airplane company causes heartache and limits optionality [3].

In his OpenAI experience, Altman extends this pragmatism to the realm of scientific research. He describes OpenAI's early years as deeply uncertain -- working on robots, video games, and agents before language models emerged as the path. The critical lesson: "Conviction is great until the moment you have data one way or the other. And there are a lot of people who hold on past the moment of data" [4]. You must operate on conviction when data does not yet exist, but fully embrace being wrong when data arrives.

His 2023 formulation of the starting-energy question is terser: "Optimism, obsession, self-belief, raw horsepower and personal connections are how things get started" [9]. None of the five are about skill or domain expertise -- all are about disposition and relationships, which is consistent with his long-running view that the scarcest resource is people willing to run at an ambitious problem. He pairs this with a constraint: "Don't fight the business equivalent of the laws of physics" [9]. Conviction-at-uncertainty is a virtue; conviction-against-physics is a death wish, and separating the two is a core operator skill.

Altman's 2020 essay *The Strength of Being Misunderstood* reframes the whole question of social noise for founders. The question is not how to stop caring what people think — almost everyone cares, and a little of it is load-bearing. The two degrees of freedom are *which* people's opinions you track and *on what timescale* you track them [17]. Most people figure out the first; almost no one figures out the second. The most impressive people he knows care intensely about what others think, even haters (several keep screenshot folders of hater tweets), but they care on history-book timescales rather than newspaper timescales: "trade being short-term low-status for being long-term high-status, which most people seem unwilling to do" [17]. The position is structurally the same one he takes on [[Startup Outcomes and Optionality]] — decisions that look wrong over weeks can be correct over decades, and holding both frames at once is the discipline.

## Key Frameworks

**The Leaky Bucket**: Growing before the product is loved creates a leaky bucket -- users come in but do not stay. The fix is to build something people love first, then grow [1]. His 2016 essay *Before Growth* gives this its canonical binary test: "Do any users love our product so much they spontaneously tell other people to use it?" Until that answer is yes, founders are better off ignoring growth targets entirely [10]. Startups that target weekly growth before product-love end up "making a nebulous product that some users sort of like and papering over this with 'growth hacking' ... that sort of works — at least, it will fool investors for awhile until they start digging into retention numbers — but eventually the music stops" [10].

**The Alignment Test**: Ask 10 employees what the company's top three goals are. If they do not agree, the founder is not communicating effectively. Altman reports that 100% of founders predict alignment and 100% of the time, employees disagree [2].

**Airplane vs. Spaceship**: Most successful startups are airplanes (on track for ~$100M), not spaceships (billion-dollar outcomes). Founders and investors get misaligned when both pretend the airplane is a spaceship [3].

**10-Year Commitment**: Having a strategy that assumes 10 years of work on the company is "an extreme advantage that few founders leverage" [2]. His 2020 *Hard Startups* framing sharpens the math: "In a world of compounding advantages where most people are operating on a 3 year timeframe and you're operating on a 10 year timeframe, you'll have a very large edge" [15]. His 2023 restatement compresses the cadence: "Plans should be measured in decades, execution should be measured in weeks" [9]. The order-of-magnitude gap between vision horizon and accountability horizon is the point -- it is what makes fast iteration protective rather than chaotic.

**Concentration Over Diversification**: At OpenAI, being less resourced than DeepMind forced a strategic choice: "They're going to try a lot of things, and we've just got to pick one and really concentrate. And that's how we can win here" [4]. This is "totally the right startup takeaway." Altman gives the same principle its most operational form in 2023: "Concentrate your resources on a small number of high-conviction bets... You can delete more stuff than you think" [9]. The second clause carries the real weight -- founders systematically underestimate what is deletable, and the gap between what is deletable and what they believe is deletable is where diffusion of focus lives.

**Compounding Advantage with Scale**: "Compounding exponentials are magic. In particular, you really want to build a business that gets a compounding advantage with scale" [9]. The "with scale" clause is the distinguishing claim -- it is what separates a business worth building from one that just grows. It is the structural form of his Airplane vs. Spaceship framework: spaceships compound, airplanes do not. Paired with: "Scale often has surprising emergent properties" [9]. At sufficient size, capabilities appear that were not visible in the smaller version of the same system -- a pattern he encountered at OpenAI with language models, and one founders should expect (and underwrite) if the structural compounding is genuine. His 2020 investor essay names this as the central career lesson: "You can and should scale up things that are working. The power of scale, and the emergent behavior that sometimes comes from it, is tremendous. I think about the potential energy of future scale for every investment I make" [14].

**The Five Levels of AI**: A framework to replace the overloaded term "AGI": (1) Chatbots, (2) Reasoners, (3) Agents, (4) Innovators, (5) Organizations. OpenAI believes it reached level 2 with O1 in 2024 [4].

**Trend Intersection as Founding Insight**: "Many of the most transformative companies come because the founder sees an intersection of two pretty different but really important trends a few years before everybody else does" [6]. He identifies 23andMe as sitting at the intersection of plummeting genome costs and the rise of social networking/crowdsourcing.

**Founder/Company Fit**: From the 2020 *Idea Generation* essay: "I've come to believe that founder/company fit is as important as product/market fit" [16]. The founder is not just the executor of an idea — the idea itself has to match what the founder is well-suited for. Introspection is insufficient for this diagnosis; Altman recommends asking mentors or people you've worked with what you are particularly good at. This framing quietly reorders the standard startup advice: choose the idea that fits *you*, not the idea that sounds best in the abstract.

**Rate of Improvement > Absolute Ability**: Applied symmetrically to hiring and investing. "If you meet someone three times in three months, and notice detectable improvement each time, pay attention to that. The rate of improvement is often more important than the current absolute ability (in particular, younger founders can sometimes improve extremely quickly)" [14]. In the career-level version: when building a network, "you are mostly looking for rate of improvement, and don't overvalue experience or current accomplishment" [13]. The heuristic compounds with his 2023 restatement that hiring should target "high-potential people with a fast rate of improvement" [9].

**The 1x/100x Bet**: On risk-taking: "Look for small bets you can make where you lose 1x if you're wrong but make 100x if it works. Then make a bigger bet in that direction" [13]. The framing is explicit option-theory for career decisions — downside is bounded (you lose the wager), upside is open-ended. Paired with the warning against the golden-handcuffs treadmill at Google or Facebook: "People have an incredible ability to always match their lifestyle to next year's salary," which is how risk-taking capacity gets quietly destroyed [13]. See [[Leaving Big Tech]].

**Force of Will and Chief Optimist**: From his 2017 profile of Greg Brockman: "Every successful startup I know has at least one person who provides the force of will to make the startup happen" [11]. The force-of-will role includes being what he calls the "Chief Optimist" — the person who "rallies everyone to press on despite the difficulties, and it's always hard on that person because they can't really lean on anyone else in the hardest times" [11]. Without someone dedicated to solving every problem "no matter how difficult, eventually a large problem will come along and kill you while you're still weak" [11]. The framework is a direct prescription for [[Co-Founders]] composition: technical talent is necessary but not sufficient, and a team of pure technical co-founders without a Chief Optimist is structurally fragile.

**Short-Term Low-Status for Long-Term High-Status**: The core move from *The Strength of Being Misunderstood*. The trade is almost universally refused: most people optimize for today's newspaper, not the history book. "As long as you are right, being misunderstood by most people is a strength not a weakness. You and a small group of rebels get the space to solve an important problem that might otherwise not get solved" [17]. The "as long as you are right" clause does the structural work — the framework is not a license for contrarianism; it is a license for *correct* contrarianism, which is rarer.

## On Hiring

Altman's hiring framework centers on net organizational impact rather than individual output. "The big hire mistakes I've made are people who are really smart, but just awful to work with because they want to tell you how smart they are. The framework that I've finally figured out, is you have to look at the net output that a person has on the organization as a whole" [6]. Even high individual performers are a failure if they make everyone else miserable.

He calls generous early employee equity "the most important piece of advice" from his conversation with [[Vinod Khosla]]: "Being super generous with early employee equity in getting founder quality people as the first 10 employees. All the evidence is on the side of doing this, and yet almost no one does, so there's a huge edge if you're willing to do it" [5]. The best recruits always take months because "they always have something great to walk away from" [5].

On early hire criticality: "If you screw up the first five, seven hires, it's almost impossible to get through that" [6].

His 2023 restatement is more directive: "Spend more time recruiting. Take risks on high-potential people with a fast rate of improvement. Look for evidence of getting stuff done in addition to intelligence" [9]. Time allocated is the input variable (more hours, not better process); trajectory beats current state (rate of improvement over raw intelligence); shipped work beats credentials. He completes the framing with a restatement of the net-impact rule: "Superstars are even more valuable than they seem, but you have to evaluate people on their net impact on the performance of the organization" [9]. Both clauses are load-bearing -- the first argues against safely average hiring, the second against individual-output worship. His 2023 essay closes on the payoff: "Working with great people is one of the best parts of life" [9].

His 2020 *Hard Startups* essay reframes the whole recruiting problem in terms of mission gravity. A startup eventually has to get a lot of people to join: "It's usually reasonably easy to get the first five or ten people to join — you can offer large equity grants and areas of responsibility. But eventually, what you have to recruit with are the mission of the company, the likelihood of massive success, and the quality of the people there" [15]. The single most powerful recruiting message, when true, is: "the world needs this, it won't happen any time soon if we don't do it, and we are much less likely to succeed if you don't join" [15]. He names a Peter-Principle derivative: "Your startup will rise to the level where it can no longer attract enough talented people" [15]. The implication flips the conventional wisdom — a hard startup is easier to staff than an easy one, because talent follows meaning.

His profile of Greg Brockman makes the recruiting-as-founding-role argument concrete: "Greg is a world-class recruiter (he plans every detail of interviews, heavily researches candidates' backgrounds, sends thoughtful and persistent followups, and so on), and I now believe even more strongly that someone on the founding team has to be an amazing recruiter" [11]. The claim is not that founders should be good recruiters on average — it is that recruiting capability is a founding-team requirement, no different from technical capability.

## On Idea Generation

*Idea Generation* (2020) is Altman's most compact statement of the ideas-problem. Founders without ideas are not a category to be funded and fixed: "YC once tried an experiment of funding seemingly good founders with no ideas. I think every company in this no-idea track failed. It turns out that good founders have lots of ideas about everything, so if you want to be a founder and can't get an idea for a company, you should probably work on getting good at idea generation first" [16]. Idea-generation is an input skill, not an output.

His recommended environment: "people who have a good feel for the future, will entertain improbable plans, are optimistic, are smart in a creative way, and have a very high idea flux" [16]. The filter is social — most people belittle ambitious plans, hold onto the past, and kill fragile ideas before they can be explored. "The best ideas are fragile; most people don't even start talking about them at all because they sound silly" [16].

Two methods for generating ideas:
1. **20-year-out-then-backward**: "You want to be able to project yourself 20 years into the future, and then think backwards from there. Trust yourself — 20 years is a long time; it's ok if your ideas about it seem pretty radical" [16].
2. **Tectonic-shift scanning**: "Think about the most important tectonic shifts happening right now. How is the world changing in fundamental ways? Can you identify a leading edge of change and an opportunity that it unlocks? The mobile phone explosion from 2008-2012 is the most recent significant example of this — we are overdue for another!" [16]. In a tectonic shift, "the world changes so fast that the big incumbents usually get beaten by fast-moving and focused startups." See [[Why Now]].

A clean heuristic for distinguishing real from fake trends: "A key differentiator is if the new platform is used a lot by a small number of people, or used a little by a lot of people" [16]. Real trends have obsessive early adopters. This is the same test he gave in his 2018 *How to Succeed with a Startup* lecture [8] and restates in the 2020 investor essay [14]. The consensus across three sources and six years makes it one of his most load-bearing frameworks.

Three further tests for an idea:
- **Possibility-delta**: "Any time you can think of something that is possible this year and wasn't possible last year, you should pay attention. This is especially true if next year will be too late" [16].
- **Huge-if-works**: "A good question to ask yourself early in the process of thinking about an idea is 'could this be huge if it worked?' ... Think early about why an idea might have that property" [16]. Most businesses don't generate accumulating advantage with scale; the ones that do are the ones worth starting.
- **Contrarian-correct**: "A good test for an idea is if you can articulate why most people think it's a bad idea, but you understand what makes it good" [16].

He closes the essay with a note added after re-reading: "I wrote it when I thought mostly about startups; now I think mostly about AI development. I am struck by how much of it applies, particularly paragraphs 5-9" [16]. The paragraphs he flags are on environment, future-projection, tectonic shifts, and real-vs-fake trends — the frames he considers transferable from startup-ideation to AI-platform-thinking. See [[AI and Startups]].

## On Productivity

The 2018 *Productivity* essay is the unified statement of his working-habits framework. The opening claim is the ordering of the problem: "It doesn't matter how fast you move if it's in a worthless direction. Picking the right thing to work on is the most important element of productivity and usually almost ignored" [12]. Everything downstream — lists, meeting lengths, sleep — is secondary to this.

**What to work on.** Independent thought is the foundation. "The most impressive people I know have strong beliefs about the world, which is rare in the general population. If you find yourself always agreeing with whomever you last spoke with, that's bad" [12]. He protects schedule space for this — reading books, time with interesting people, time in nature. Working on things you don't care about is "a painful drag on morale and momentum" and should be delegated, avoided, or exited. On delegation: "Remember that everyone else is also most productive when they're doing what they like ... try to figure out who likes (and is good at) doing what, and delegate that way" [12]. See [[Startup Management]].

**Prioritization.** The three-pillar system: "Make sure to get the important shit done. Don't waste time on stupid shit. Make a lot of lists" [12]. Lists are paper (easy to add/remove, accessible in meetings without rudeness), re-transcribed frequently (forces re-thinking), uncategorized (most he does is a star). He prioritizes for momentum — "start and end each day with something I can really make progress on." He is ruthless about saying no, is "terse to the point of rudeness" in email, and generally avoids meetings because "I get the most value out of time in my office." But he keeps 10% of meetings open for chance encounters: "Though probably 90% of the random meetings I take are a waste of time, the other 10% really make up for it" [12].

Meeting-length heuristic: "Most meetings are best scheduled for 15-20 minutes, or 2 hours. The default of 1 hour is usually wrong, and leads to a lot of wasted time" [12].

The anti-optimization warning: "Don't fall into the trap of productivity porn — chasing productivity for its own sake isn't helpful. Many people spend too much time thinking about how to perfectly optimize their system, and not nearly enough asking if they're working on the right problems" [12]. Summary principle: "The right goal is to allocate your year optimally, not your day" [12].

**Physical factors.** Sleep is the top input (cold dark room, great mattress, no late eating, minimal alcohol, a [Chili Pad](https://www.chilitechnology.com) for cooling, an [eye mask](https://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B00GSO1D9O/) and [ear plugs](https://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B001J4HB1C) when traveling). He takes very low-dose sleeping aids or cannabis when necessary. A full-spectrum LED light for 10-15 morning minutes is "the thing I'd try" if you try nothing else [12]. Exercise second (heavy weights 3x/week plus occasional HIIT). Nutrition third (no breakfast for ~15 hours of fasting; avoid sugar; espresso morning and after lunch ≈ 200mg caffeine; vegetarian with supplements calibrated by quarterly blood tests through [WellnessFX](https://www.wellnessfx.com)).

**Other.** Natural-light workspace, custom software for repetitive tasks, fast typing. He experiences 1-2 week motivation fogs and has not found anything to do but wait. He suggests slightly overcommitting to force efficiency, but not drastically. And the non-negotiable: "Don't neglect your family and friends for the sake of productivity — that's a very stupid tradeoff (and very likely a net productivity loss, because you'll be less happy)" [12].

The essay ends by returning to the opening: "Productivity in the wrong direction isn't worth anything at all. Think more about what to work on" [12]. See [[Founder Productivity]].

In his 2019 *How to Be Successful* essay, several principles sharpen the 2018 framing. On compounding: "A medium-sized business that grows 50% in value every year becomes huge in a very short amount of time ... you also want to be an exponential curve yourself — you should aim for your life to follow an ever-increasing up-and-to-the-right trajectory" [13]. The career-level rule: "You don't want to be in a career where people who have been doing it for two years can be as effective as people who have been doing it for twenty — your rate of learning should always be high." On focus: "I have yet to meet a slow-moving person who is very successful" [13]. On work ethic: "You can get to about the 90th percentile in your field by working either smart or hard ... But getting to the 99th percentile requires both" [13]. And the career-sequencing note: "Do it at the beginning of your career. Hard work compounds like interest, and the earlier you do it, the more time you have for the benefits to pay off" [13].

## On Investing

The 2020 essay *How to Invest in Startups* decomposes investor skill into three jobs: "Get access to good investment opportunities, make good decisions about what to invest in, and get the companies you want to invest in to choose you as an investor. That's it!" [14]. The deflationary note follows: "You can often help the companies you invest in become bigger than they otherwise would have been, but the sad reality is that your best investments will do quite well without you."

**Access.** The exploitable bug: "It's surprising that most investors don't work hard, but it's true, and a bug that you can exploit" [14]. The operational form is: stop asking contacts for deal-flow; instead, "ask them if you can spend a day per week helping their best company." And brand is the other lever, built by doing hard things few investors do — "write long-form content (hard, few people do a good job at it) instead of tweeting (easy, everyone does a pretty good job at it)" [14].

**Decisions.** Founder-prediction is the core skill. "The rate of improvement is often more important than the current absolute ability" [14]. The two questions he asks when meeting a founder: "If I'd work for that person," and "If I can imagine them taking over their industry" [14]. The founder trait list is densely specified: "Scrappy and formidable at the same time (a rarer combination than it sounds); mission-oriented, obsessed with their companies, relentless, and determined; extremely smart (necessary but certainly not sufficient); decisive, fast-moving, and willful; courageous, high-conviction, and willing to be misunderstood; strong communicators and infectious evangelists; and capable of becoming tough and ambitious" [14]. Some traits are mutable (toughness, ambition); some are nearly fixed (fast-moving vs slow-moving). The tell for the fixed one: "I have almost never made money investing in founders who do not respond quickly to important emails" [14].

On market sizing, he calls out investor incompetence directly: "I don't know why most investors are so obsessed with the current size of a market instead of how big they think it will be in ten years, but it's an opportunity for you" [14]. See [[Why Now]].

The "spectral signature" of his best investments — seven characteristics: "Compelling founders, a mission that attracts talented people into the startup's orbit, a product so good that people spontaneously tell their friends about it, a rapidly growing market, a network effect and low marginal costs, the ability to grow fast, and a product that is either fundamentally new or 10x better than existing options" [14]. See [[Product-Market Fit]].

The power-law discipline: "You should try to limit yourself to opportunities that could be $10 billion companies if they work ... the failures don't matter much, the small successes don't matter much, and the giant returns are where everything happens" [14]. Altman admits to repeatedly violating this principle, which is itself informative — it is easy to state, hard to hold. See [[Startup Outcomes and Optionality]] for the founder-side of the same asymmetry.

Two chronic mistakes he names: investing in bad founders with good ideas, and "chasing investments primarily because other investors like them" [14]. Both are failures to weight the founder above consensus social signals.

**Close rate.** The better the opportunity, the harder to win it. "A reputation for being above-and-beyond helpful and accessible is worth a lot here, and rare among all but the best investors" [14]. Decisiveness matters — his two most recent significant investments were both decided before meetings concluded (one before the founder finished describing the idea). "I don't recommend doing that very often, but when your conviction is strong, let it show" [14]. And the peer test: "If you're picking well, you should be investing in founders that you think of as your peers at least. Founders have a sixth sense for who is going to treat them like a peer and who is going to treat them like a boss. And if they're good, they know you're failing an intelligence test if you act like their boss" [14]. See [[Investor Advice Pitfalls]].

**Help them.** The most important help an investor provides is ambition: "Help them set ambitious but achievable goals. Momentum is important and self-reinforcing — most people set goals that they expect to be just out of reach, which is usually demotivating. It's better to continuously set goals that you can just barely hit" [14]. Second is specific-tactical advice over general-strategic advice. Third is introductions and logistical help, but usually on-demand, not proactively. One important exception: proactively flag very-high-conviction concerns about mistakes, especially once things are working.

A self-aware note on idea-generation-help: "The first investor I ever watched in action was PG and so I assumed this was something all investors were fantastic at. But it turns out he is a sui generis idea generator, and even most great investors are usually still bad at telling founders what to work on" [14]. See [[Paul Graham]]. And the grounded observation that most "vague help" requests are emotional support requests: "Invite them over to your house, make them tea or pour them a drink, and start listening to their struggles" [14].

## On the Ideal Cofounder

The 2017 essay *Greg* is Altman's fullest statement of what a founding partner looks like, written as a profile of Greg Brockman during OpenAI's formation. "A lot of people ask me what the ideal cofounder looks like. I now have an answer: Greg Brockman" [11]. See [[OpenAI: From Flip Chart to AGI]].

Five traits, illustrated concretely:
1. **Force of will.** "Every successful startup I know has at least one person who provides the force of will to make the startup happen ... OpenAI wouldn't have happened without Greg" [11].
2. **Full commitment, fast.** Greg decided to join mid-drive after a group dinner about what OpenAI might look like — "asked me questions for the first half of the drive back to San Francisco, then declared he was in, and started planning logistics for the rest of the drive" [11]. From then on, his average email response time was "about 5 minutes to anything" — the same fast-mover heuristic Altman applies when investing [14].
3. **World-class recruiting.** "He plans every detail of interviews, heavily researches candidates' backgrounds, sends thoughtful and persistent followups ... someone on the founding team has to be an amazing recruiter" [11].
4. **Open to feedback.** "Never gets offended, and processes it very quickly." When Altman flagged that his vision communication wasn't bold enough, the next time Greg talked about it, "it was a perfectly calibrated explanation of how we were going to succeed at something that really mattered" [11].
5. **Does the non-technical founder work.** Defining culture, making offers, organizing offsites, letting people work out of his apartment, ordering supplies, cleaning up after meals. "It's important to have someone great in this role at a small startup — many people gloss over it" [11].

The structural claim underneath: founding teams need a Chief Optimist, and technical talent alone is not enough. "If they're the same person, then you've hit the jackpot" [11]. See [[Co-Founders]].

## On the Current AI Moment

Altman views the AI platform shift as comparable to mobile, internet, and semiconductor revolutions: "Big companies have the edge when things are moving slowly. When something like this happens, that's when upstarts have their edge" [4].

His advice to current founders: bet on the AI trend ("we are not near the saturation point"), exploit speed as the startup advantage (build the day a new capability appears, rather than putting it into a quarterly planning cycle), but remember that "the rules of business still apply. Everyone can build an absolutely incredible demo right now. But building a business, man, that's the brass ring" [4].

His 2020 *Idea Generation* epilogue explicitly transfers the startup-ideation frameworks to the AI era: he notes that paragraphs 5-9 of the essay — on environment, 20-year projection, tectonic shifts, and real-vs-fake trends — "apply" directly to thinking about AI development [16]. The continuity is the point: the tools for seeing the future are the same whether the future is a startup or a research program.

## On Peer Groups and YC

Altman credits YC with providing the peer group that shaped his ambitions. At Stanford, the peer competition was over investment banking internships. At YC, it was over building companies. "No one is immune to peer pressure and so all you can do is pick good peers" [4]. He echoes [[Paul Graham]]: "Your 20s are always an apprenticeship but you don't know for what, and then you do your real work later" [4].

In *How to Be Successful* he extends this into a general network-building principle: "The size of the network of really talented people you know often becomes the limiter for what you can accomplish" [13]. The operational form is long-term generosity: "Doing this, over a long period of time, is what lead to most of my best career opportunities and three of my four best investments. I'm continually surprised how often something good happens to me because of something I did to help a founder ten years ago" [13]. And the reputation multiplier: "Be overly generous with sharing the upside; it will come back to you 10x" [13].

His benchmark for internally-driven motivation is [[Jessica Livingston]] and [[Paul Graham]]: "YC was widely mocked for the first few years, and almost no one thought it would be a big success when they first started. But they thought it would be great for the world if it worked, and they love helping people, and they were convinced their new model was better than the existing model" [13]. The pattern matches his *Strength of Being Misunderstood* frame [17] — YC traded short-term low-status for long-term high-status.

## On Persistence and Cross-Domain Learning

Altman frames persistence as compound growth: "It's like compound growth. It's always misunderstood. It always takes longer and then the magnitude in the out years is always bigger than you think" [6]. He and [[Anne Wojcicki]] agree: "That is the secret to overnight success is ten years of persistence" [6]. In 2023 he compresses this into a single imperative: "Get back up and keep going" [9]. Paired with his framing of inaction as risk -- "Inspiration is perishable and life goes by fast. Inaction is a particularly insidious type of risk" [9] -- the position is that founder failure is almost never losing; it is stopping.

In *How to Be Successful* he names Airbnb as his benchmark for willfulness — "keeping maxed-out credit cards in those nine-slot three-ring binder pages kids use for baseball cards, eating dollar store cereal for every meal, battle after battle with powerful entrenched interest, and on and on — but they managed to survive long enough for luck to go their way" [13]. The throughline: "Almost always, the people who say 'I am going to keep going until this works, and no matter what the challenges are I'm going to figure them out', and mean it, go on to succeed. They are persistent long enough to give themselves a chance for luck to go their way." And the character note: "To be willful, you have to be optimistic ... I have never met a very successful pessimistic person" [13]. See [[Dealing with Setbacks]].

Altman shares that he has "learned the most from studying businesses that are not in the technology industry, and I don't think people ever get enough of that" [7]. He mentions visiting the Vatican to think about building YC: "They think on thousand-year time frames, and about a billion people, and it's a very different perspective, but it was really good" [7].

## Distinct Perspective

Altman is unusual in the YC canon for explicitly addressing what happens when things go well but not spectacularly. Most YC advice assumes either the company will become huge or will fail. Altman fills the gap for the large middle ground: companies that work but are not on a path to massive scale. His advice to be frank with investors about this, rather than deluding them, is uncommon in startup culture.

On management, he is firmly in the "keep it simple" camp -- no innovation on org structure, clear single-manager reporting, compensation bands, and frequent written communication as the highest-leverage management tool. His 2023 restatement makes the position more directive: "Communicate clearly and concisely" [9], "Fight bullshit and bureaucracy every time you see it and get other people to fight it too. Do not let the org chart get in the way of people working productively together" [9], "Outcomes are what count; don't let good process excuse bad results" [9], and "Incentives are superpowers; set them carefully" [9]. The throughline: organizations do what they reward and tolerate what they leave in place, and bureaucracy-fighting has to be deputized -- if it stays a CEO-only responsibility, it does not survive the transition past a few hundred employees.

He also names the team-level version of the personal discipline he has long emphasized: "Cohesive teams, the right combination of calmness and urgency, and unreasonable commitment are how things get finished" [9]. The pairing of calmness *and* urgency is the difficult part -- most teams default to one. And on ambition as a recruiting and retention lever: "It is easier for a team to do a hard thing that really matters than to do an easy thing that doesn't really matter; audacious ideas motivate people" [9]. The 2023 formulation sharpens the 2018 claim that hard startups are easier to staff because talent follows meaning.

His 2019 *How to Be Successful* adds the motivation-diagnosis frame that sits underneath all of his hiring and investing heuristics. "Most people are primarily externally driven; they do what they do because they want to impress other people" [13]. The two failure modes of externally-driven behavior: work on consensus ideas and consensus career tracks, and miscalculate risk out of fear of falling behind. "The most successful people I know are primarily internally driven; they do what they do to impress themselves and because they feel compelled to make something happen in the world" [13]. Diagnostic question: "Is this person a force of nature?" And the career frame: "Eventually, you will define your success by performing excellent work in areas that are important to you. The sooner you can start off in that direction, the further you will be able to go. It is hard to be wildly successful at anything you aren't obsessed with" [13]. See [[Conformists vs. Non-Conformists]].

On getting rich, he flatly breaks with salary-as-path thinking: "The biggest economic misunderstanding of my childhood was that people got rich from high salaries ... you get truly rich by owning things that increase rapidly in value ... But somehow or other, you need to own equity in something, instead of just selling your time. Time only scales linearly" [13]. See [[Wealth vs. Money]].

His OpenAI experience adds a dimension missing from his earlier YC-era advice: what it looks like to build a research organization that becomes a product company, how to navigate being badly wrong many times while maintaining forward momentum, and how clarity of direction (once achieved) is the primary determinant of speed.

## How to Succeed with a Startup (2018 Talk)

In a 2018 lecture, Altman provides a comprehensive overview of startup success factors [8]. His central claim: "The degree to which you're successful approximates the degree to which you build a product that is so good people spontaneously tell their friends about it." This is the bar -- "something that people love so much, they tell their friends about it" [8].

A product that works at this level must be "simple to explain and easy to understand. If you can't explain it in a few words what you do... that's usually a sign of unclear thinking or a need that is not big enough" [8].

Altman argues startups should target markets undergoing exponential growth, and that evaluating markets by current size is the biggest investor mistake: "11 years ago, the market for iPhone apps: absolute $0" [8]. See [[Why Now]].

His test for real vs. fake trends: real trends feature early adopters who use a product obsessively. The iPhone was a real trend. VR (as of 2018) was a fake trend [8].

On difficulty: "In 2018, at least in Silicon Valley, it's easier to start a hard startup than it is to start an easy startup." The logic: ambitious projects attract talent, and talent is the scarcest resource [8]. His 2020 essay *Hard Startups* makes this the centerpiece — the counterintuitive claim is restated as an explicit tailwind-vs-headwind argument and then generalized: "If people care about your success because you seem committed to doing something significant, it's a background force helping you with hiring, advice, partnerships, fundraising, etc." [15]. Silicon Valley adds a cultural multiplier — "people default to taking you seriously if you're willing to be serious — they've learned it's a very expensive mistake, in aggregate, not to" [15].

On competitive advantage: YC applicants are frequently stumped by "What is the long-term monopoly effect here?" All great businesses have an answer. "The better they are, the more they pretend not to" [8].

Three structural advantages for startups: (1) ideas that sound bad but are good favor startups (one "yes" vs. needing all "yeses"), (2) fast-changing markets compound speed advantage, (3) big platform shifts catch large companies flat-footed [8]. See [[Competing with Big Companies]].

## Key Frameworks (from 2018 Talk)

- **Product So Good People Tell Friends**: The single most important success factor [8]. Altman's 2016 *Before Growth* essay gives this the same binary test in different words: "Do any users love our product so much they spontaneously tell other people to use it?" [10]. Across two sources and two years, this is the single most consistently restated test in his canon.
- **Real Trends vs. Fake Trends**: Real trends have obsessive early adopters [8].
- **The Evangelical Founder**: Every startup needs one person who "infects with enthusiasm the whole world" [8].
- **Frugality, Focus, Obsession, Love**: Paul Buchheit's distillation of the best founder traits [8].
- **Never Lose Momentum**: "If you lose momentum, it's very difficult to get it back" [8].
