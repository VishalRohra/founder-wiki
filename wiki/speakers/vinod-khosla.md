# Vinod Khosla

Vinod Khosla is the founder of Khosla Ventures and the founding CEO of Sun Microsystems. Before Sun, he co-founded Daisy Systems, which went public in the 1980s and raised $100 million. At Sun, the first 15-20 employees included Eric Schmidt (later CEO of Google), Carol Bartz (later CEO of Autodesk and Yahoo), Bill Joy, and Andy Bechtolsheim -- each of whom went on to build or lead billion-dollar companies. Khosla's interview with [[Sam Altman]] in the "How to Build the Future" series is a concentrated argument that company-building is almost entirely about people selection.

## Worldview

Khosla's central thesis is that "a company becomes the people it hires, not the plan it makes" [1]. A business plan is "completely irrelevant, other than to judge how somebody's thought about a problem. Not what they're going to do" [1]. The people you assemble determine not just execution but the quality of future decisions, because those people form the "kitchen cabinet" -- the group you turn to when facing hard, ambiguous, uncertain decisions [1].

This conviction drives everything else in his philosophy: how to pick investors, how to allocate equity, what to look for in hires, and how to think about ambition.

## The $0 Million vs. $0 Billion Mindset

Khosla's framework for founding ambition: a company at zero revenue with a $0 million mindset will hire tactically, make short-term deals, and optimize for near-term survival. The same company at zero revenue with a $0 billion mindset will build the team and infrastructure needed for a decades-long journey. "When you set out on a journey, your mindset determines who you bring onboard, how you approach it, what you set up, what deals you do, which investors you've got" [1].

He uses the Mt. Everest analogy: you must be "obstinate about your vision" (reaching the summit) but "flexible about tactics" (the route to base camp, camp one, camp two). The danger is setting up base camp in the wrong place -- generating revenue and stability that does not position you for the actual summit [1].

## Gene Pool Engineering: Equity as a Weapon

Khosla argues that insufficient equity allocation is the single most important failure mode in the first six months of a company [1]. His positions are specific:

**At Sun**, the first three or four founders kept less than half the common stock -- roughly 25-27% -- with an equal or larger chunk reserved for future hires and about 40% for investors after the Series A [1].

**His son's company** (Curai, an AI healthcare company): Khosla advised his son to keep 15% for himself instead of 45%, recruit a co-founder at 15%, and leave the rest for hiring great engineers. In AI, where the best engineers make million-dollar salaries at Google and Uber, you have to offer 3-5% each. "If you don't do that, you're including only the people who couldn't start their own company, who won't help you evolve your plan" [1].

**The magnet principle**: Some hires are worth their equity even if they did zero work, because they attract other exceptional people. Bill Joy at Sun was "an incredible magnet." Khosla does not think Eric Schmidt would have joined him at 25 years old without the draw of working alongside Joy and Bechtolsheim [1].

[[Sam Altman]] reinforces this: "Being super generous with early employee equity in getting founder quality people as the first 10 employees. All the evidence is on the side of doing this, and yet almost no one does, so there's a huge edge if you're willing to do it" [1].

Khosla calls insufficient option pools his "single biggest beef with YC" [1]. He acknowledges it is counterintuitive: both founders and investors want to minimize the pool and maximize their own percentage. But "they would like to own a higher percentage, but the pie is much smaller" [1].

## Whose Advice to Trust on What Topic

Khosla told Joe Kraus (founder of Excite): "The single hardest decision you'll make is whose advice to trust on what topic" [1]. This extends to investors, board members, and executives.

The qualification to advise an entrepreneur is having built a large company yourself. "Just because you got an MBA and joined a venture firm doesn't mean you're qualified to advise an entrepreneur" [1]. Most investors fail this test. Khosla's assessment: "90% of investors add no value. 70% of investors add negative value to a company" [1]. They give contradictory advice ("improve quality," then "ship faster," then "spend less money") without understanding the trade-offs, because they have never faced gut-wrenching decisions under ambiguity [1].

He describes the near-bankruptcy experience at Sun: monitors from Philips were breaking every 30 days, and for a month or two the earliest he went home was 3 AM and the latest he was back was 7 AM. "Unless you've felt that gut-wrenching decision, you can't advise an entrepreneur" [1].

## Hiring for Rate of Learning, Not Experience

The most important quality in a hire is rate of learning, not domain experience. "Experience doesn't matter, the rate of learning matters. First principle's thinking matters. Pick for the best athlete, not the person who's the most established wide receiver who knows how to run one pattern" [1].

Khosla's evaluation method: give candidates a brand-new problem they have no experience with and observe how they think through it from first principles. He also uses the question: "If I gave you $10 million today, what three startups would you consider, and what are the reasons as an investor you wouldn't want to invest in those?" [1].

For startup founders, the strongest signal is rate of change during a defined period. Altman confirms this is his "number one" indicator: "How fast did they evolve their plan, change their plan?" [1].

When hiring senior executives, the functional competence question ("can you do marketing?") is secondary to the cross-functional question: "What are the questions he will ask? How will he make the CFO better or the VP of engineering better through the questions they ask?" [1].

A specific example: Scott McNealy started at Sun as VP of Manufacturing. Khosla made the gut call to move him to VP of Sales based on behavioral signals, not background. "You have to make those gut calls. It's a very non-intuitive gut call" [1].

## On Investors

"An investor is an employee who you can't fire, and that's how you should think about it" [1]. Khosla advises founders to optimize for investors who care about their vision and understand their technical approach, not those who want 3-4x returns and early liquidity. To evaluate: talk to founders who had hiccups on ambitious paths and ask how the investor behaved when things went wrong [1].

Altman adds that the worst board behavior is adding stress during the moments that are already the most stressful -- sending press clippings of competitors, catastrophizing about death. The best investors are "calming you down, supporting you" during hard risk decisions [1].

## Ambition and Unreasonable People

Khosla has never had a founder pitch an idea he considered too ambitious. His response to ambitious ideas: "That's ambitious. That's awesome. Build the team for it. Now, what is step one, two and three?" [1]. Vision and tactics must coexist: "The only recipe I've ever seen work for making really impactful companies is both the giant vision and a good step one, two, and three," Altman confirms [1].

Khosla's position on management: "If you're reasonable, you won't do unreasonable things. It's just by definition." Great managers will take a great idea and turn it into "a good one, and execute a decent idea at lower risk." For ambitious companies, you need a "think tank of unreasonable people" at the top, with reasonable people layered below to optimize within the macro ideas [1].

## Reinventing Societal Infrastructure

At 60, Khosla wrote a 50-page document called "Reinventing Societal Infrastructure with Technology." The exercise: identify which parts of non-governmental US GDP could be reinvented with technology. He expected to find a small fraction. Instead, "there's no part of non-governmental US GDP that one can't innovate on in ways that are not five, 10% improvements, but are 100 to 1,000% improvements" [1].

The goal: give seven billion people the lifestyle of the top 700 million (energy-rich, healthcare-rich, education-rich, transportation-rich) without destroying the planet. He reports being able to envision a path for every major sector. "Only thing I'm short is entrepreneurs" [1].

Examples span food (Impossible Foods -- "I said yes in our very first meeting. It was just a vision too big to not attempt"), rockets (Rocket Lab), construction (3D printed buildings with 80% less material), and everything computation enables [1].
