# Vidit Aatrey

Vidit Aatrey is the co-founder and CEO of [[Meesho: Social Commerce in India|Meesho]] (YC S16), one of India's largest social commerce platforms. He graduated from IIT Delhi (2008-2012) and worked at MobiHerd before co-founding Meesho with Sanjeev Barnwal in 2015. The company had 700 employees by 2019 and half a million monthly active social store owners [1].

## Worldview

Aatrey's thinking centers on three convictions:

**Stay close to users, especially when they are not you.** Aatrey builds for an audience he is not part of -- women, mostly housewives, in tier 2-3 Indian cities. His response is systematic proximity: 100-200 top users on his personal WhatsApp, monthly "We Hear You" video town halls for users, management call center rotations, and inviting users to company celebrations. "The day you start taking assumptions, start thinking on behalf of your users, you start doing mistakes" [1]. See [[Customer Relationships]] and [[Talking to Users]].

**You can build for others; it is just harder.** Aatrey initially believed the common wisdom that founders must solve their own problems. After Meesho's discovery process, he reversed this view: "The best thing to do is build a product that solves your own problem. The second best thing to do is build a product that solves a problem for someone who can't solve their own problem" [1].

**Do not listen to VCs about what to build.** Aatrey's biggest early mistake was spending four months chasing VC-defined milestones. "After that, I've never gone to them asking what should I do, because I know they don't know" [1]. See [[Investor Advice Pitfalls]].

## Distinct Perspective

Aatrey offers a rare view of building a technology company for emerging-market users who are fundamentally different from the founder. His insights on India's trust deficit, unbranded commerce, and social selling through community networks are specific to contexts where institutional trust is low and personal relationships drive economic activity. He is also distinctive in framing the value of his product not primarily in economic terms but in identity and empowerment -- the professional recognition his users gain matters more to them than the $150-200/month income [1].

His co-founder relationship with Sanjeev Barnwal is a case study in itself. They committed to start a company with no idea, on a phone call, based on a decade of friendship. Aatrey credits this trust for enabling two pivots that he believes would have been impossible with a less trusted co-founder [1].

## Key Frameworks

- **Growth-Quality Cycle**: Alternate six-month periods between growth and quality improvement. Do not try to optimize quality during growth spurts [1].
- **The Second Best Thing**: If you cannot solve your own problem, solve the problem of someone who cannot solve it themselves [1].
- **User Town Halls ("We Hear You")**: Monthly CEO-produced videos for users reporting what the company did, plans to do, and what is not working [1].
- **Founder as Customer Support**: CEO personally adds early users on WhatsApp and provides direct support [1].
