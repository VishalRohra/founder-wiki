# Alan Kay

Alan Kay is a computer scientist, Turing Award winner, and one of the pioneers of personal computing. He was a key researcher at Xerox PARC, where he helped develop Smalltalk, the graphical user interface, and the Dynabook concept. He delivered a two-part lecture in Sam Altman's Stanford "How to Start a Startup" series on how transformational technology gets invented. See [[Inventing the Future]] for the full treatment [1].

## Worldview

**Context beats IQ.** Kay's most fundamental principle: "Context or point of view is worth 80 IQ points." A person who learns calculus with an IQ of 110 can do things the greatest geniuses of antiquity cannot. Leonardo da Vinci could not invent a motor; Henry Ford built millions of cars. The difference was the century they were born into, not their intelligence [1].

**Reality is a low-pass filter.** Ideas get degraded as they pass through human institutions. "You have to have enormous ideas in order so that people not understanding them will retain something. You don't want the low pass filter to give you back a dial tone" [1].

**Humans are fundamentally blind.** Kay uses hands-on demonstrations (blind spot experiments, size constancy illusions, Shepard tables) to show that human perception routinely manufactures false information. "The natural state of humanity is to be crazy, because crazy is not having good models in our mind for what's going on out there." The most dangerous version: people who think they can see. "We can't learn to see until we realize and admit that we're blind" [1].

**Good funders give gold medals ahead of time.** The quality of results in technology research correlates most strongly not with the researchers but with the funders. Good funders award resources before results, accepting that 70% will fail [1].

## Distinct Perspective

Kay is unique among YC speakers in that he comes from the research tradition rather than the startup tradition. His critique runs deeper than product advice -- he challenges the assumptions underlying modern computing itself. He argues that the web browser is "one of the worst things ever done in computing" because it ignores the fact that users are on a computer, and that all three major operating systems are based on ideas from before 1965 [1].

His challenge to entrepreneurs: the easiest way to make money is to build "technological amplifiers" for genetic human cravings (social connection, stories, revenge, news). The harder but more important path is to build technology that serves humanity's invented needs (progress, scientific thinking, legal systems) without appealing to the parts of human nature "that they're helpless in front of" [1].
