# Diane Greene

Diane Greene is a serial founder and technology executive. She co-founded and served as CEO of three startups: Vxtreme (sold to Microsoft for $75 million in 1997), VMware (IPO in 2007 with $19.1 billion market cap at close), and Bebop (sold to Google for $380 million in 2015). She later served as SVP of Google Cloud. Before her startup career, she worked at Sybase, Tandem, and SGI. She was also a tech investor who put seed money into Cloudera, Pure Storage, and Unity Technologies [1].

## Worldview

Greene's advice draws on a recurring analogy between sailing and company-building. A competitive sailor (she won the women's national dinghy sailing championships, raced long distance to Bermuda, and windsurfed across the open ocean from Maui to Molokai), she finds direct parallels between navigating a race and navigating a startup [1].

**Plan, then adapt in real time.** "Building a company is so much like racing a sailboat. You need a plan and then the ability to reevaluate the plan in real time as new information is acquired. Suddenly, the wind changes or competitors are stronger than you thought they'd be" [1].

**Presence and decisiveness.** "When you campaign a sailboat, you need to be in the moment. You need to let go of your self-doubt, and yet you can't lose the ability to question your decisions. Most of all, when it's time to make a decision -- a competitor's come upon you or the wind shifted -- you make it. The race won't wait for you" [1].

**Crew selection is paramount.** "It's impossible to be an effective skipper if you don't have the right people working harmoniously in the right roles" [1].

## Lessons from Each Company

### Vxtreme (1995)

Greene learned that founders need to be solidly aligned and that limiting the number of founders improves outcomes: "Too many cooks can spoil the broth." The most important lesson: "No one is indispensable, and it's essential to get the right mix of people, talents, and personalities" [1].

### VMware (1998)

VMware taught Greene what it takes to bring a disruptive innovation to market. The critical insight: "It's essential to design and market a major innovation in a way that makes its adoption non-disruptive. That makes it easy to integrate with an established order, an established way of doing things, even if the innovation ultimately supplants that order" [1].

A concrete example: VMware built a tool that would take a workload running on a physical machine and place it in a virtual machine where everything ran identically. "That was a phenomenal sales tool for us" [1].

When VMware learned it could not sell through IBM due to IP issues, the team turned the setback into an advantage by enlisting IBM's star resellers to take VMware to market instead [1].

VMware also taught Greene the importance of corporate culture: fostering a sense of importance and value for each person and their role, and creating an open culture for communication and collaboration. "We designed our corporate campus with exactly these goals in mind" [1].

### Bebop (2013)

At Bebop, Greene's original co-founder departed, and the team's first design attempts were "just off." Greene credits tenacity: "We persevered, and Bebop became a powerhouse for design thinking and for tools to build enterprise applications. Tenaciousness really pays off" [1].

### Google Cloud

Running Google Cloud taught Greene that "it's possible to build an enterprise startup inside a mega company." The same principles applied: culture, getting the right people in the right roles, and never losing focus on creating value for customers and partners [1].

## Core Philosophy

Greene distills her philosophy: "The bottom line in sailing a boat or building a company is that you give it your all. You give it your all, not because you're supposed to, not because that's what makes you win, but because you have respect for your goal and you enjoy the process" [1].

She reframes success as a byproduct: "You may make a fortune, but the fortune will be incidental. If your goals are worthy, it's the process that will count, and that understanding will be your greatest source of strength no matter what the adversity" [1].
