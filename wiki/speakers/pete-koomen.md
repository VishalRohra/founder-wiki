# Pete Koomen

Pete Koomen is a Group Partner at Y Combinator. He was previously co-founder of Optimizely, which helps thousands of companies run A/B tests to measure which design changes improve user behavior [1]. His background gives him a distinctive data-driven lens on conversion optimization, and his startup Optimizely was itself a YC company (Winter '10). He has also built internal AI tools for YC's finance and legal teams and wrote an influential essay arguing that most AI apps are "horseless carriages" -- powerful technology trapped in outdated interfaces [3].

## Worldview

Koomen's advice is empirical and experiment-driven. His core themes:

**Small changes can have big impact.** From his Optimizely experience: "I saw firsthand how small changes can sometimes have a big impact on how your users behave" [1]. This carries through his advice on landing page design, CTA placement, and signup flows.

**Every page must answer two questions.** Koomen evaluates websites by asking: (1) "Does this convince me that I want to sign up?" and (2) "Does the signup process talk me out of it?" [1]. The first question tests value communication; the second tests friction.

**Map and shorten the conversion funnel.** The aha moment -- the point where users experience the core value -- should be as few clicks as possible from landing. Koomen describes working with founders on whiteboards: "The aha moment is at like step seven. And they realize they don't actually need steps two, four, five. And they cut them out. And all of a sudden, the aha moment becomes like step two" [1].

**Post-launch iteration is analytical, not emotional.** In the Launching discussion, Koomen advises founders whose product gets no traction to treat it as a diagnostic problem: "Week by week, you're like, 'Okay, I'm going to tweak one variable'" -- messaging copy, target audience, company size [2]. It is "more art than science" but the frame is analytical: "Something isn't working. Which one of your assumptions was wrong?" [2].

**AI should offload work, not create it.** Koomen's central argument on AI product design: the right question is not "How do I insert AI into my tool?" but "How would I design this tool from scratch to offload as much repetitive work from the user as possible?" [3].

**System prompts should be user-editable.** Developers treat system prompts like code -- hidden, one-size-fits-all, owned by the developer. Koomen argues they should be visible to and editable by users, since they are written in natural language and represent the user's logic, not the developer's. "Because this is all just English, we no longer have to treat these things like black boxes" [3].

**Developers should build tools, not prompts.** If users program agents through natural language system prompts, the developer's job shifts to building the tools (actions, integrations) that agents need to accomplish work in the world [3].

## Distinct Perspective

Koomen is the most conversion-specific voice in the YC canon. Where [[Kevin Hale]] provides the diagnostic framework (seven questions for every page, the knowledge gap), Koomen provides live examples of what goes wrong: competing CTAs, hidden aha moments, carousels that auto-scroll past the user, and forms that introduce unnecessary friction.

His Optimizely background makes him uniquely credible on A/B testing and experimentation. He is also one of the earliest case studies of the [[MVP Planning|prototype stage]]: the Optimizely MVP was built in days as a JavaScript file on S3 that only the founders could operate, shown to marketers to validate demand [2].

On AI, Koomen brings the AB testing mindset to AI product design. His essay includes working demos where readers can edit system prompts and see results in real time. His practical experience building AI tools at YC -- sitting next to finance and legal team members, watching their workflows, writing system prompts together -- gives his advice concrete grounding. "Not everyone has a Pete next to them, but you could have an AI system prompt writer" that translates feedback into prompt edits [3].

## Key Frameworks

- **Two-Question Page Evaluation**: (1) Does this convince me to sign up? (2) Does the signup process talk me out of it? [1]
- **Aha Moment Mapping**: Count the steps from landing to core value experience, then eliminate unnecessary steps [1]
- **Single-Variable Iteration**: When nothing works post-launch, change one variable per week and measure [2]
- **Horseless Carriage Test**: Is the AI app just inserting AI into an old interface, or is it redesigning from the ground up to offload work? [3]
- **Editable System Prompts**: Let users program agents in natural language; developers build tools, not prompts [3]
