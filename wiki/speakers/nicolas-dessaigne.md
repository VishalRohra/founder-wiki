# Nicolas Dessaigne

Nicolas Dessaigne is a Group Partner at Y Combinator. He was the co-founder and CEO of Algolia, a search API used by millions of developers that powers over 1.75 trillion searches annually for 17,000+ customers worldwide [1]. His YC content focuses primarily on building and selling dev tools.

## Worldview

Dessaigne brings a practitioner's perspective from building Algolia from two technical co-founders with no business background to a major enterprise product. His core themes:

**Technical founders are the best salespeople for dev tools.** 74% of YC dev tool companies have only technical co-founders [1]. Dessaigne lived this: "We ended up creating Algolia with two tech co-founders. It crossed our mind: 'Hey, we don't know how to sell.' No. It's actually way easier. Learning to sell is way easier than learning how to speak with a developer" [1].

**Developers are the best marketers for developer products.** "I don't know a single dev tool company that is happy with their CMO when they have a traditional marketing background" [1]. At Algolia, every engineer did a "marketing hack" every month. "All of our best marketing at Algolia was done by developers" [1].

**Documentation is product.** Dessaigne treats documentation as first-class: "Documentation should never be an afterthought. It should be part of the product. Documentation is marketing" [1]. At Algolia, features were not considered done until docs were done, and the documentation team existed to help developers write better docs, not to write docs for them [1].

**Support is marketing.** Engineers should do support, which produces two benefits: satisfied developer-customers who interact with peers who speak their language, and engineers who better understand customer pain [1]. Algolia waited until they had hundreds of employees before creating a dedicated support team [1].

**Runtime ideas beat build-time ideas.** Build-time tools (docs, QA, testing) are nice-to-have and crowded. Runtime tools (APIs, infrastructure) are must-have and have usage-based growth dynamics that align incentives with customers [1].

**Open source is a go-to-market strategy.** For libraries, frameworks, and data-sensitive products, open source is mandatory. For others, it is a powerful differentiator. But monetization must be planned: hosting/cloud, open core, or (least recommended) support services [1].

## Distinct Perspective

Dessaigne is the most specific voice on dev tools in the YC canon. Where others give general startup advice, he addresses the unique dynamics of selling to developers: no sales decks (show demonstrations), bottom-up adoption, renamed sales roles ("product specialists"), and the build-time vs. runtime distinction.

He is also notable for his emphasis on Hacker News as the primary launch platform for dev tools and his detailed analysis of open source monetization models. His Algolia examples -- from closing the first $2,000/month contract with a command-line demo to not having a sales deck until $10 million ARR -- provide concrete benchmarks.

## Key Frameworks

- **Build-Time vs. Runtime**: Runtime ideas are must-have, with usage-aligned growth; build-time ideas are nice-to-have and crowded [1]
- **Good/Better/Best Pricing**: Self-serve for developers (low price), team tier for engineering managers (collaboration), enterprise tier for CTOs (security, SLAs, sales-led) [1]
- **Open Source Decision Tree**: Must be open source (libraries, frameworks, sensitive data products), could be open source (APIs, applications), monetized via hosting, open core, or support [1]
- **Documentation as Product**: Features are not done until docs are done; documentation team helps engineers write better docs [1]
- **Founder-Led Sales Until $1M ARR**: Do not hire salespeople until roughly $1M in annual recurring revenue [1]
