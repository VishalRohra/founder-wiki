# David Lieb

David Lieb is a YC Group Partner, Product Lead for Google Photos, and co-founder and former CEO of Bump (YC S09). Before Bump, he was an engineer at Texas Instruments who went to business school. He dropped out to pursue Bump full-time during YC. Bump reached 150 million downloads and 10 million monthly active users before being acquired by Google in 2013. At Google, Lieb combined Bump's product insights with Google's AI capabilities to create Google Photos, now one of 17 billion-user apps at Google. He also hosts episodes of the "How to Build the Future" series, interviewing founders like [[Aravind Srinivas]] of Perplexity, co-hosts "The Breakdown" podcast, and gave a foundational lecture on [[Cohort Retention]] [3].

## Worldview

Lieb's thinking is organized around the principle that product insight matters more than technology, and that user needs are discovered through direct contact rather than analytics dashboards.

He believes [[Product-Market Fit]] is not binary but staged. Even Google Photos, with over a billion users, has PMF for some user segments and not others. Each new user who joins a product at scale is fundamentally different from the first user, and the product must evolve to serve them [1].

He views the founder's job as interpreting user behavior rather than simply measuring it. His career inflection points all came from directly talking to users -- calling the top 100 Bump users to discover they used it for photo sharing, talking to YC founders to understand why Flock was not getting traction [1].

## Key Frameworks

**The Human Assistant Test.** When designing a product, imagine a human being with unlimited time, intelligence, and resources doing the job your product will do. List everything that person would do for the user. This list becomes the product roadmap. Lieb used this to design Google Photos: "If my co-founder, Andy, was my photo assistant, and all he did all day long was help me with my photos, what would he do?" [1].

**Two Vectors for AI.** AI startups should identify which of two vectors they pursue: (1) scaling a task that skilled humans can do but cannot do affordably at scale (Google Photos' approach), or (2) solving problems beyond human cognitive capacity. The first is lower risk; the second has higher payoff but is harder to validate [1].

**Cohort Retention as the Answer to "Have We Made Something People Want?"** Lieb calls cohort retention the single best quantitative method for answering YC's fundamental question. The only thing that matters is whether retention curves get flat -- the shape, not the absolute number. Google Photos retained 20-40% of weekly cohorts, which was enough to build to a billion users [3]. See [[Cohort Retention]] for the full treatment.

**Durable Human Needs vs. Novelty.** Consumer products succeed when they amplify a fundamental human behavior 10x or 100x. Facebook amplified social connection. Instagram amplified self-expression. Products that introduce novel formats without addressing a durable need fade once the novelty wears off [1].

**Come for the Tool, Stay for the Network.** Learned from Chris Dixon's framework after Flock failed. The first user must get standalone value; if value depends on friends also adopting, 99% of users will never activate [1].

## Distinct Perspective

Compared to other YC speakers who focus on the early-stage founder journey, Lieb bridges the startup-to-big-company transition. His experience selling Bump to Google and then building a billion-user product inside a large organization gives him a rare dual perspective.

He is unusually candid about what went wrong at Bump: not understanding cohort retention (a mistake he now teaches others to avoid [3]), not differentiating their business from Facebook and Twitter's dynamics, raising too much money without a clear plan, and focusing on system performance microseconds when the fundamental business model was the real problem [1].

His advice on acquisitions is specific and uncommon: the most important question is mission alignment, not financial terms. He recommends founders spend far more time with the people they will work with daily and far less time negotiating price [1].

## On Investing and Evaluation

As an angel investor, Lieb asks founders: "If you could go acquire a big company with the purpose of furthering your vision, which big company would you acquire?" The answer reveals both ambition and long-term product thinking [1].
