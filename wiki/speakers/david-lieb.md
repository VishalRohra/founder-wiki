# David Lieb

David Lieb is a YC Group Partner. He went through YC in Summer 2009 with his startup Bump, which let users share contact information and photos by physically bumping their phones together. Bump was one of the first mobile apps to reach more than 100 million users, but it did not work as a business [1]. After pivots into photo sharing and photo management, Bump was acquired by Google. Lieb's last app formed the basis for Google Photos, which he worked on for nearly a decade. Google Photos serves well over a billion users [1].

## Worldview

Lieb's advice is grounded in the painful experience of building a product with impressive surface metrics (100 million users) that masked fundamental retention problems. His central framework -- [[Cohort Retention]] -- reflects a hard-won conviction that the only thing that matters is whether users keep using the product over time. He describes Googling "cohort retention" after a VC meeting where he gave "a very hand-wavy answer" to the question -- a mistake he aims to save other founders from [1].

His perspective is quantitative but practical. He warns against the most common self-deception patterns in retention analysis because he committed every one of them at Bump: widening time periods to make curves look better, picking too-easy actions, and trusting analytics dashboards without understanding what they actually measure [1].

## Key Frameworks

- **Cohort Retention Curves**: The shape matters more than the absolute number. Curves that get flat signal PMF; curves heading to zero do not. See [[Cohort Retention]] for the complete framework [1].
- **The Triangle Chart and Layer Cake**: Visual tools for tracking retention by cohort over time [1].
- **The Sitting-Next-To-Your-Customer Rubric**: For choosing what action to track, imagine sitting next to a customer and watching them use the product. Whatever signals real value to you in that moment is the right metric [1].

## Key Example: Google Photos

Google Photos had weekly cohort retention curves that dropped off quickly but flattened between 20-40%. "80% of my users leave pretty immediately" sounds bad, but the 20% who stayed demonstrated they would use the product every week "for basically forever." Six weeks after launch, Lieb was confident Google Photos would eventually reach 20% of all humans on the planet. Four years later, it had exceeded a billion users [1].
