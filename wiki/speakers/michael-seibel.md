# Michael Seibel

Michael Seibel is CEO of Y Combinator and a former founder. He cofounded Justin.tv (which became Twitch) and Socialcam. His writing and advice draw heavily on concrete operational lessons from these experiences rather than abstract principles.

## Worldview

Seibel's advice is grounded in a belief that startups are primarily execution problems, not idea problems. His recurring themes are:

**Process over talent.** His [[Product Development]] guide is built on the premise that a well-structured development cycle with clear goals, inclusive brainstorming, difficulty grading, and consensus-based prioritization can make even an imperfect team productive and aligned. The enemy is not lack of talent but lack of structure: "meandering product meetings where we didn't write down our decisions," loose specs where team members had different ideas about what was being built, and founders making non-transparent decisions [1].

**Urgency about time.** Seibel places high value on the 20s as the prime working decade. He frames the decision to delay starting a company in explicit terms: "Every year you are not doing a startup you are losing 10% of this prime time" [2]. He disagrees with the cultural norm of using your 20s for self-discovery, arguing that low obligations and low burn rate make these the best years for building [2].

**Relationships as infrastructure.** Across multiple posts, Seibel treats personal relationships as the primary infrastructure for career success. Press strategy is about building relationships with reporters, not sending press releases [3]. College is valuable primarily for making friendships with smart people [2]. Cofounder relationships are among the most important determinants of startup success [4].

**PR as business development.** Seibel's press strategy is built on four words from Mike Arrington and M.G. Siegler: "Treat PR like biz dev." This reframes press from a specialized activity with special rules into relationship-based deal-making: warm intros, exclusive offers, consistent cadence, and fair value exchange [3].

## Distinct Perspective

Compared to other YC advisors, Seibel's advice tends to be more operational and process-oriented. Where [[Paul Graham]] writes about abstract principles (schlep blindness, do things that don't scale), Seibel writes about how to run a product meeting, what to put in a press pitch email, and how to structure a two-week development cycle. His posts often follow a format of "here is the broken version, here is what we learned, here is the process that fixed it."

Seibel is also unusually direct about the costs of different career paths. His [[Becoming a Founder|three paths framework]] (founder, executive, employee) presents each with explicit pros and cons, including the observation that being a founder "probably won't maximize your personal earnings" and that the founder path's commitment level "can significantly hurt personal relationships" [5].

## Key Frameworks

- **Product Development Cycle**: Two-week cycles with one product meeting per cycle, three possible goals (content creation, new users, retention), inclusive brainstorm with difficulty grading (easy/medium/hard), consensus-based selection, and mandatory analytics for every feature [1].
- **Four Categories of Startup News**: Product launches and features, product/sales milestones, significant BD deals or customers, fundraising [3].
- **Three Paths in Tech**: Founder, executive, and employee, each with distinct strategies and trade-offs [5].
- **Founder Prerequisites**: Teammates with technical skills, financial plan, passionate problem, and an idea (in roughly that order of importance) [5].
