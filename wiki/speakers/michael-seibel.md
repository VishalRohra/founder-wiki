# Michael Seibel

Michael Seibel is CEO of Y Combinator and a former founder. He cofounded Justin.tv (which became Twitch, sold to Amazon for ~$1B) and Socialcam. His writing and talks draw heavily on concrete operational lessons from these experiences. With 20+ entries in the YC library -- both solo talks and the "Dalton & Michael" series with [[Dalton Caldwell]] -- he is one of the most prolific contributors to the YC canon, covering the full founder journey from "should I start a startup?" through PMF and fundraising.

## Worldview

Seibel's advice is grounded in a belief that startups are primarily execution problems, not idea problems:

**Start with problems, not ideas.** "There's a common misconception that your idea has to be great in order to start a company" -- Justin.tv's original concept was an online reality TV show, which eventually became Twitch [7]. He recommends keeping "problem books" rather than idea books [7].

**Process over talent.** His [[Product Development]] guide is built on the premise that a well-structured development cycle with clear goals, inclusive brainstorming, difficulty grading, and consensus-based prioritization can make even an imperfect team productive [1]. The enemy is not lack of talent but lack of structure.

**Market first, solution second.** "Founders often hold too tightly onto solutions and too loosely onto problems. The problem, i.e. the market, is the real opportunity" [8]. The founder's v1 idea is usually wrong; "the market pulls product out of the startup" [8].

**Products are not art.** "If users don't find products useful, then the products are by definition not useful, and they're a waste of your time to build" [9]. The startup world is unforgiving to artists who refuse to listen to users [9].

**Find desperate customers.** "How do you find people who are willing to use a bad product? They have to be the most desperate" [9].

**Equal equity as motivation.** Seibel advocates equal or near-equal co-founder equity splits as "what we almost always recommend at YC." His reasoning: "Your first thought has to be, 'How do I come up with an equity split that's going to maximize the motivation of my teammates?'" [6].

**Urgency about time.** Seibel places high value on the 20s as the prime working decade. "Every year you are not doing a startup you are losing 10% of this prime time" [2].

**Users you don't want.** Seibel introduces the concept of "users you don't want" -- people who try to hijack your product to solve a problem you did not intend to solve [10].

**Investor as boss is fatal.** One of the five things that kill post-seed startups. "All they can do is use words. If you do not want your investors to have power over you, don't give it to them" [11].

**Three things saved Justin.tv/Twitch** [9]: (1) an extremely technical founding team, (2) spending almost nothing ($500/month per person walking-around money), and (3) ego fully tied to the startup's success. Remove any one and the company would have died [9].

## Key Frameworks

- **Product Development Cycle**: Two-week cycles, one product meeting per cycle, three possible goals, inclusive brainstorm with difficulty grading, consensus-based selection, mandatory analytics [1].
- **Five Things That Kill Post-Seed Startups**: Fake PMF, investor as boss, co-founder conflict, reaching for ordinary, slow product development [11].
- **The Real PMF Test**: Not headcount or fundraising, but "a frantic founding team trying to deal with ever-growing numbers of happy, loyal, and ideally paying customers" [8].
- **Hair on Fire**: If someone's hair is on fire, they will use even a brick to put it out. Find problems that dire [8].
- **Customer Pitch vs. Investor Pitch**: Two fundamentally different pitches for two fundamentally different audiences [12].
- **Problem Quality**: Evaluate problems on frequency, intensity, willingness to pay, and reachability of customers [9].
- **Cold Email Formula**: 60 seconds or less. Problem, solution, traction, market, team, controversial insight [13].
- **Equal Equity Split**: Vesting with a one-year cliff is the safety net; equity inequality kills motivation [6].
- **Order of Operations**: Problem, unique qualification, MVP, hand-picked users, iterate [7].
- **Three Paths in Tech**: Founder, executive, employee -- each with distinct strategies and trade-offs [4].
- **Users You Don't Want**: Not every user who shows up is your user [10].
- **Silicon Valley Fundraising Advantage**: Investors who have missed big deals are psychologically different from investors who have not [14].

## Distinct Perspective

Compared to [[Paul Graham]]'s conceptual architecture and [[Dalton Caldwell]]'s psychological focus, Seibel is the most operational voice in the YC canon. His posts follow a pattern of "here is the broken version, here is what we learned, here is the process that fixed it." He provides specific playbooks -- how to run a product meeting, how to structure a press pitch, how to evaluate whether an early hire is essential.

Seibel is unusually direct about the costs of being a founder: "It probably won't maximize your personal earnings" and the commitment level "can significantly hurt personal relationships" [4].
