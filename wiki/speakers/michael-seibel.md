# Michael Seibel

Michael Seibel is CEO of Y Combinator and a former founder. He cofounded Justin.tv (which became Twitch, sold to Amazon for nearly $1 billion) and Socialcam. His writing and advice draw heavily on concrete operational lessons from these experiences rather than abstract principles.

## Worldview

Seibel's advice is grounded in a belief that startups are primarily execution problems, not idea problems. His recurring themes are:

**Process over talent.** His [[Product Development]] guide is built on the premise that a well-structured development cycle with clear goals, inclusive brainstorming, difficulty grading, and consensus-based prioritization can make even an imperfect team productive and aligned [1].

**Urgency about time.** Seibel places high value on the 20s as the prime working decade. He frames the decision to delay starting a company in explicit terms: "Every year you are not doing a startup you are losing 10% of this prime time" [2].

**Relationships as infrastructure.** Across multiple posts, Seibel treats personal relationships as the primary infrastructure for career success. College is valuable primarily for making friendships with smart people [2]. Cofounder relationships are among the most important determinants of startup success [4].

**Ideas don't need to be great.** Seibel uses his own experience -- Justin.tv started as an online reality TV show and became Twitch -- to argue that the initial idea is a starting point, not a destination [10]. He advises starting with problems rather than ideas, and treating the MVP as a learning tool rather than a product to fall in love with [10].

**Equity as motivation tool.** On [[Equity Split]], Seibel argues that the primary purpose of the equity split is to maximize long-term co-founder motivation, not to reward past contributions [9]. He advocates equal or near-equal splits as the default, using vesting and a one-year cliff as the safety mechanism [9].

**Fundraising is about leverage, not persuasion.** Across multiple videos, Seibel returns to the theme that the best fundraising strategy is to have a product customers want. He distinguishes between Silicon Valley investors (who have learned humility by passing on successes) and investors elsewhere (who may have been right every time they said no) [7]. His advice on [[Cold Emailing Investors]] focuses on brevity and substance: get the investor to reply, not to invest, in the first exchange [8].

## Distinct Perspective

Compared to other YC advisors, Seibel's advice tends to be more operational and process-oriented. Where [[Paul Graham]] writes about abstract principles (schlep blindness, do things that don't scale), Seibel writes about how to run a product meeting, what to put in an investor email, and how to structure an equity conversation. His posts often follow a format of "here is the broken version, here is what we learned, here is the process that fixed it."

Seibel is also unusually direct about the costs of different career paths. His [[Becoming a Founder|three paths framework]] (founder, executive, employee) presents each with explicit pros and cons [5].

On fundraising, Seibel frequently partners with [[Dalton Caldwell]] to diagnose common mistakes. Their joint analysis of [[Fundraising Mistakes]] combines Seibel's operational lens with Caldwell's psychological analysis [11].

## Key Frameworks

- **Product Development Cycle**: Two-week cycles with one product meeting per cycle, three possible goals, inclusive brainstorm with difficulty grading, consensus-based selection, and mandatory analytics [1].
- **Hair on Fire Test**: The problem you solve should be so urgent that customers will use a half-baked v1 product -- like someone whose hair is on fire grabbing any object to extinguish it, even a brick [6].
- **Four Categories of Startup News**: Product launches, milestones, BD deals, fundraising [3].
- **Three Paths in Tech**: Founder, executive, and employee, each with distinct strategies [5].
- **Founder Prerequisites**: Teammates with technical skills, financial plan, passionate problem, and an idea (in roughly that order of importance) [5].
- **The 80/90% Rule**: If 80-90% of your waking hours are spent talking to customers and building product, you are doing it right. If it is 20%, something is very wrong [11].
- **60-Second Email Rule**: Cold investor emails should be readable in 60 seconds or less. The goal is a reply, not a check [8].
