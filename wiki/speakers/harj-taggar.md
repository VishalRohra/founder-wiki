# Harj Taggar

Harj Taggar is cofounder of Triplebyte (YC S15), a hiring marketplace that helps engineers find work at fast-growing companies. His advice on hiring comes from both sides of the market: helping companies close engineers and observing what candidates experience across hundreds of hiring processes.

## Worldview

Taggar views engineering hiring as a sales and optimization problem, not an HR process. His consistent theme is that companies dramatically underinvest in the candidate experience and then blame candidates for not being interested. From Triplebyte, he observes that offer acceptance rates range from as low as 10% to as high as 90% across companies, and the variance comes down to process quality, not company prestige [1].

**Personal networks first, always.** Taggar ranks hiring channels and puts personal networks at the top by a large margin, especially for the first three hires. The advantage is not just access but predictability: "you already know if you'll like working with them." He acknowledges the emotional difficulty -- asking friends to leave their jobs is scary and rejection from friends is more awkward -- but frames it as a necessary cost of optimizing for the startup's success [2].

**Speed as competitive advantage.** For startups competing against larger companies, speed is one of the biggest advantages. He identifies two failure modes: entropy (candidate tracking systems naturally trend toward disorder) and psychological bias (rationalizing that candidates who dropped out were not interested anyway). Both must be actively fought [1].

**The hiring funnel is expensive.** Five onsite interviews produce one offer, consuming roughly 30 hours of engineering time. Given this cost, maximizing the offer-to-acceptance conversion rate has high ROI [1].

## Distinct Perspective

Taggar's advice is notable for its specificity and its ranking of channels. While most hiring advice treats all channels as roughly equivalent ("try everything"), Taggar explicitly ranks them by effectiveness for early-stage companies and argues that founders should focus exclusively on personal networks for the first three hires before supplementing [2].

He is also unusually practical about competing with large companies like Facebook and Google, identifying four specific arguments (learning, career progression, opportunity cost, mentorship) and noting which resonates with different types of candidates [1].

His advice against exploding offers and for expiring signing bonuses as a softer alternative reflects Triplebyte's observations across many hiring processes [1].

## Key Frameworks

- **Hiring channel ranking**: Personal networks > hiring marketplaces > inbound generation > cold outreach > recruiters > meetups > agencies [2]
- **Two-step offer presentation**: Excitement call same day, details call next day [1]
- **Closing with the whole team**: Everyone who met the candidate sends follow-up emails with specific reasons; involve investors at early stage [1]
- **Four arguments against big companies**: Learning, career progression, opportunity cost, mentorship [1]
