# Harj Taggar

Harj Taggar is a YC partner and cofounder of Triplebyte (YC S15), a hiring marketplace that helps engineers find work at fast-growing companies. His advice on hiring comes from both sides of the market: helping companies close engineers and observing what candidates experience across hundreds of hiring processes. He also contributed foundational lectures on finding co-founders and on whether to start a startup [4][5].

## Worldview

Taggar views engineering hiring as a sales and optimization problem, not an HR process. His consistent theme is that companies dramatically underinvest in the candidate experience and then blame candidates for not being interested [1].

**Personal networks first, always.** Taggar ranks hiring channels and puts personal networks at the top by a large margin, especially for the first three hires [2].

**Speed as competitive advantage.** For startups competing against larger companies, speed is one of the biggest advantages [1].

**Think like an investor when joining.** In [[Choosing a Startup to Join]], Taggar advises job seekers to evaluate startups the same way investors do -- looking at growth trajectory, founder quality (relative to starting point, not credentials), and depth of user passion rather than market size [3].

**Resilience is the core founder trait.** After 15 years of starting and investing in startups, Taggar identifies resilience as the quality most predictive of founder success. He has abandoned earlier proxies: confidence is unreliable ("someone could appear very confident initially, but not be resilient when their startup hit roadblocks"), credentials are unreliable, and motivations are unreliable [4]. He cites Benchling's founder Sajith as a case study in hidden resilience: quietly spoken, struggled at Demo Day, made no revenue for over a year, and now runs a $6 billion company [4].

**Stress response is the #1 co-founder criterion.** Taggar identifies how someone handles stress as the single most important quality in a co-founder, above complementary skills, shared interests, or pedigree. "Even if they have the perfect set of complementary skills, if they don't handle stress well, it's very unlikely your relationship is going to last" [5].

## Distinct Perspective

Taggar's advice is notable for its specificity and its ranking of channels. He explicitly ranks hiring channels by effectiveness and argues that founders should focus exclusively on personal networks for the first three hires [2].

His advice on joining startups is distinctive for warning against evaluating market size -- "the most common mistake investors make when they're trying to pick a startup" [3]. Instead, he recommends asking to talk to users and gauging the depth of their passion for the product, citing Airbnb as the classic example of a small but fanatical user base [3].

His co-founder advice emphasizes the always-be-scanning approach: work on projects with potential co-founders long before planning a startup, because "the best time to find a co-founder is when you're not actually looking for a co-founder." Phil Libin (Evernote founder) took this to an extreme: he would only make friends with people who could someday be co-founders [5].

On whether to start a startup, Taggar provides a practical worst-case framework: figure out the specific downside, decide if you can handle it, and factor in the learning value. "I don't think enough people factor in how valuable this learning is when they're doing their worst case scenario analysis" [4]. He also treats the search for ideas and co-founders as a single process: "It's hard to think up good ideas yourself... Having someone to talk through that initial idea or impulse with and debate and discuss is important" [4].

## Key Frameworks

- **Hiring channel ranking**: Personal networks > hiring marketplaces > inbound generation > cold outreach > recruiters > meetups > agencies [2]
- **Two-step offer presentation**: Excitement call same day, details call next day [1]
- **Three-factor startup evaluation**: Founders (relative impressiveness), user passion (depth over breadth), growth (trajectory over absolute numbers) [3]
- **Paul Buchheit Test**: "If you're happy working where you are and you don't have a strong desire to do anything else, you're probably going to get paid less and have to work more if you leave for a startup" [3]
- **Resilience Over Confidence**: Quiet, persistent founders often outperform visibly confident ones [4]
- **Worst-Case Analysis**: Make the decision concrete by identifying the specific downside and deciding if it is tolerable [4]
- **Ideas and Co-Founders as One Search**: Don't separate the two; the best co-founder emerges from conversations about ideas [4]
- **Co-founder Branching Algorithm**: Ask your top candidates; if they say no, ask who they would co-found with, get introductions, repeat [5]
