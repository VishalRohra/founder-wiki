# Harj Taggar

Harj Taggar is a YC partner and cofounder of Triplebyte (YC S15), a hiring marketplace that helps engineers find work at fast-growing companies. His advice on hiring comes from both sides of the market: helping companies close engineers and observing what candidates experience across hundreds of hiring processes.

## Worldview

Taggar views engineering hiring as a sales and optimization problem, not an HR process. His consistent theme is that companies dramatically underinvest in the candidate experience and then blame candidates for not being interested [1].

**Personal networks first, always.** Taggar ranks hiring channels and puts personal networks at the top by a large margin, especially for the first three hires [2].

**Speed as competitive advantage.** For startups competing against larger companies, speed is one of the biggest advantages [1].

**Think like an investor when joining.** In [[Choosing a Startup to Join]], Taggar advises job seekers to evaluate startups the same way investors do -- looking at growth trajectory, founder quality (relative to starting point, not credentials), and depth of user passion rather than market size [3].

## Distinct Perspective

Taggar's advice is notable for its specificity and its ranking of channels. He explicitly ranks hiring channels by effectiveness and argues that founders should focus exclusively on personal networks for the first three hires [2].

His advice on joining startups is distinctive for warning against evaluating market size -- "the most common mistake investors make when they're trying to pick a startup" [3]. Instead, he recommends asking to talk to users and gauging the depth of their passion for the product, citing Airbnb as the classic example of a small but fanatical user base [3].

## Key Frameworks

- **Hiring channel ranking**: Personal networks > hiring marketplaces > inbound generation > cold outreach > recruiters > meetups > agencies [2]
- **Two-step offer presentation**: Excitement call same day, details call next day [1]
- **Three-factor startup evaluation**: Founders (relative impressiveness), user passion (depth over breadth), growth (trajectory over absolute numbers) [3]
- **Paul Buchheit Test**: "If you're happy working where you are and you don't have a strong desire to do anything else, you're probably going to get paid less and have to work more if you leave for a startup" [3]
