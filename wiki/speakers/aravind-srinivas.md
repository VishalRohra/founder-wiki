# Aravind Srinivas

Aravind Srinivas is the co-founder and CEO of Perplexity, an AI-powered search engine valued at over $9 billion in less than three years. He holds a PhD from UC Berkeley in AI and deep learning, with internships at OpenAI (where he worked under Ilya Sutskever) and Google. His co-founder and CTO, Dennis, was a visiting student in his Berkeley lab.

## Worldview

Srinivas operates from two core beliefs: that AI and product development must form a flywheel (better product generates data that improves AI, which makes the product better), and that consumer product taste is the ultimate competitive advantage against larger, better-resourced competitors [1].

He chose search as a domain because it sits on the "AI completeness path" -- the company gets better as AI gets better, rather than being disrupted by it. This was a deliberate strategic decision made before founding: "Your company gets better as AI gets better, as opposed to your company gets run over by somebody else" [1].

He embraces productive ignorance. Perplexity only realized its core competitive insight -- that AI answers that eliminate link-clicking would erode the ad economy -- after launching the product. "There's some benefit to being ignorant. Ignorance is bliss" [1].

## Key Frameworks

**The User Is Never Wrong.** Borrowed from Larry Page, this principle dictates that when a product fails for a user, the product should clarify ambiguity rather than blame the user. The opposite approach -- teaching users to be better prompt engineers -- is the enterprise model. Consumer products must adapt to the user [1].

**The Dumb Approach.** When choosing between a sophisticated agentic approach (RL agents that browse, click, scroll) and a simpler heuristic approach (always take top-K links, always use cached snippets, always feed everything to the LLM), Srinivas chose simple. The bet was that model improvement would make the dumb approach work, and it did. Timing was the key variable [1].

**Queries Per Day as North Star.** Srinivas uses the same primary metric Google used in its early days. He reviews it weekly at All Hands, analyzes growth and decline, and freaks out if it declines. He rejected having a constant TV dashboard as distracting [1].

**Fight the Entropy.** As Perplexity scales, Srinivas acknowledges the company is already slower than in its earliest days. Production breakdowns erode trust and make people cautious. His response: maintain personal knowledge of who is working on what, file 50 bugs a day, and rely on co-founders who share obsessive detail-orientation. "We're trying to fight the entropy. That's the only thing you can try" [1].

## Distinct Perspective

Srinivas offers a rare inside view of competing with Google from a position of structural advantage rather than resource advantage. His argument: Google cannot build Perplexity's exact product on its homepage because doing so would erode its $200 billion annual search ad revenue. This innovator's dilemma is Perplexity's moat [1].

He is also unusual in his transparency about fear and uncertainty. When Bing Chat leaked and Google announced Bard in the same period, Srinivas seriously considered selling the company. The investor who had handshaked held firm, which Srinivas describes as "pretty impressive" [1].

His vision for the next decade is an "orchestrator" that combines small models, knowledge graphs, widgets, LLM streaming, multi-step reasoning, and transactional capabilities. The user should never need to specify which tool to use. This is explicitly modeled on what Google already does, but rebuilt for an AI-native paradigm [1].

## On Feedback Channels

Srinivas ranks feedback channels by honesty: X (Twitter) is the most brutally honest and therefore the most useful. Email is too polite. In-person is the worst -- "you go show someone something and they're just going to tell you good things, even if they hate it" [1].
