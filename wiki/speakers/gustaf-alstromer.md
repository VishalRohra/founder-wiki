# Gustaf Alstromer

Gustaf Alstromer is a Group Partner at Y Combinator. Before YC, he was Head of Growth at Airbnb. He appears across multiple YC sources, primarily on topics related to early-stage customer acquisition, sales, and launching.

## Worldview

Alstromer's advice is grounded in the belief that early-stage growth is a founder execution problem, not a marketing or product problem. His recurring themes:

**Sales is a numbers game that founders must do themselves.** Alstromer argues that "learning how to do sales actually gives you full control of your destiny as a startup" [1]. He teaches founders to work backwards from goals through a sales funnel, track conversion rates, and accept that most recipients of outbound emails are not early adopters. The most common mistake he sees: founders send too few emails, get zero customers, and conclude that "sales is not working" -- when the real problem is insufficient volume [1].

**Founders can learn sales easily.** "It's probably the easiest job to learn in a startup if you know the problem you're solving, if you know your product intimately, if you know the market" [1]. He cites Tony Xu (DoorDash), Tracy Young (PlanGrid), and Steve Jobs as founders who learned to sell [1].

**Start with the easiest customers.** "Your first customers should be your easiest" [1]. Sell to people you know first, then to startups (short decision-making lines, no procurement departments), and accept that most people are not early adopters [1].

**Charge from day one.** "If you don't charge your customers, they are not a customer, and you don't have a company" [1]. For B2B, Alstromer recommends money-back guarantees over free trials [1].

**Launching and talking to customers serve the same purpose.** In the Launching video, Alstromer reinforces that programmers avoid customer interaction because coding is comfortable: "Very comfortable sitting behind a computer writing code" solving "discrete, well-defined problems" where "the code compiles or the code does not compile" [2]. Customer feedback is ambiguous and feels personal. But launching early is how founders discover if they are even talking to the right users [2].

## Distinct Perspective

Compared to other YC partners, Alstromer brings a uniquely data-driven approach to early-stage sales. His sales funnel math -- showing that 100 emails yields zero customers while 500 yields two -- is one of the most concrete tools in the YC curriculum. He is also self-identified as an early adopter: "I remember being one of those people when I worked at Airbnb. I would love when founders emailed me with new products" [1].

His advice bridges the gap between the philosophical ("do things that don't scale") and the tactical (here is exactly how to write the email, here is the CRM tool, here is the math on how many emails to send).

## Key Frameworks

- **Sales Funnel Math**: Work backwards from your goal. If each funnel stage has known drop-off rates, you can calculate exactly how many outreach emails you need to send [1].
- **Early Adopter Search**: Since most people are not early adopters, volume is the only way to find them. "You don't have time to convince anybody to become an early adopter. You have to find early adopters and just go for them" [1].
- **Unscalable-to-Scalable Bridge**: Even if your eventual growth channel is SEO or word of mouth, "that's not how Airbnb started." Every company begins with unscalable founder-led tactics [1].
