# Tom Blomfield

Tom Blomfield is a YC Group Partner who founded two billion-dollar companies: GoCardless (a recurring payments company, similar to Stripe) and Monzo (an online bank with 8 million customers in the UK). He also worked at Grouper, a YC-backed dating/social startup that ultimately failed.

## Worldview

Blomfield approaches startups through the lens of metrics, unit economics, and retention. His lectures are among the most data-rich in the YC canon, providing specific benchmarks rather than general principles. He draws heavily from his experience at Monzo, where he saw firsthand how negative unit economics can threaten a scaling consumer company and how they can be fixed.

His central belief: metrics are instruments in a cockpit. Without them you fly blind. But too many metrics, or the wrong ones, are equally dangerous. The ideal is a blend of four to five key metrics, direct customer conversations, and product intuition [1].

## Key Frameworks

**The Layer Cake vs. Leaky Bucket**: Blomfield's most distinctive visualization. High-retention businesses stack monthly cohorts into a "layer cake" of growing revenue; low-retention businesses are "pouring water into a leaky bucket" [1]. He argues that 20% retention that flattens is better than higher initial retention that decays to zero [1].

**Value-Based Pricing**: Price at 25-50% of the value delivered to the customer. Never start with cost (that leads to underpricing). Cost is only a floor. The value equation also defines success metrics for pilots [3].

**Organic vs. Paid Growth**: For consumer companies, at least 50% organic growth is necessary. The best consumer companies achieve 80-100% organic growth. Relying on paid acquisition enriches Google and Meta while driving your margins to zero [2].

**The Magic Moment**: A specific user behavior that predicts long-term retention. Facebook: adding 7 friends in 10 days. Monzo: adding 3 friends to send/receive money. Once identified, re-engineer onboarding to push users toward it [2].

## Distinct Perspective

Blomfield is unusually candid about failures. He discusses Monzo's negative unit economics (losing 30-40 pounds per customer), his dating startup Grouper's failure, and specific measurement mistakes. He provides concrete numbers where most speakers offer generalities: retention benchmarks by vertical, growth rate thresholds, NPS targets, and net dollar retention ranges.

He is also the most explicit YC voice on the danger of AI costs for startups: foundation model credits from OpenAI/Anthropic are real COGS, not free money, and will eventually be billed at full price [1].
