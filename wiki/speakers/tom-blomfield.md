# Tom Blomfield

Tom Blomfield is a YC Group Partner and serial founder. He co-founded GoCardless (a multi-billion dollar recurring payments company, YC S11), founded Monzo (a UK online bank with 8 million customers), and worked at Grouper (a YC-backed dating/social app). His YC contributions focus on practical, operational guidance for both B2B and consumer founders, particularly around pricing, metrics, and leveraging AI development tools.

## Background and Path to YC

Blomfield was fascinated by computers from age seven, playing a lemonade stand game on an Apple II. At fifteen, he started making money building websites for local businesses [6]. His parents directed him toward Oxford to study law and become a corporate lawyer -- "a pretty safe, well-paying job, pretty respectable, with a pretty predictable linear career path" [6].

At Oxford, he met his first co-founders (including [[Harj Taggar]]) and started an online student marketplace. They did YC, but Blomfield dropped out of the startup to finish his exams. "I always think about how my life might have been different if I hadn't taken that decision" [6].

After his law degree, he and two other Oxford graduates planned to start a dating website. They were convinced to start a bill-splitting app instead -- "one of YC's classic tarpit ideas" [6]. YC accepted them but reset their expectations: "We thought the bar was here, and YC showed us the bar was like up here. We were just play-acting at running a startup" [6].

The bill-splitting app pivoted to B2B payments, becoming GoCardless [5][6]. Growth was slow for the first year or two, but they "ground away at it" [6]. The biggest challenge for Blomfield was that he could not put himself in his customers' shoes -- small businesses collecting money. "The problem we were solving just wasn't close enough to my heart" [6]. After three years, he left. Twelve years later, co-founder Heroji still runs GoCardless, processing tens of billions of dollars of payments in dozens of countries [6].

## Monzo: Building and Nearly Losing a Bank

After GoCardless, Blomfield "wanted to swing bigger" [6]. He saw that "banking was excruciatingly painful... The people running these banks did not understand what software could do." The naive view: rewrite the software from scratch and the bank would work better. "It turns out that's true. It's miraculous" [6].

Monzo launched a prototype in three to four months on a prepaid debit card, leaning on YC principles. Blomfield's notes from YC: "Launch early and talk to users. Do things that don't scale" [6]. The product had a distinctive hot coral card. When someone tapped to pay, a notification popped on their phone. This drove organic word of mouth. Monzo grew from zero to one million customers in about two and a half years without spending on advertising [6].

Blomfield describes the first time he saw a stranger use a Monzo card: a customer in a coffee shop pulled out the card, and the barista said "Is that a Monzo card? I've been waiting in line to get one" [6].

### Taking on Hard Problems Makes Some Things Easier

Blomfield identifies a counterintuitive advantage: "Taking on a really hard problem actually makes some things easier. The smartest, most ambitious people want to come and work with you... The press wants to write about you... Customers want to sign up because you've got this bold ambitious vision" [6].

### The Crisis

Monzo processed fifty to one hundred billion pounds of payments annually. Outages -- sometimes minutes, sometimes hours -- meant tens of thousands of payments failed. The BBC commissioned an ice sculpture of a frozen Monzo card and filmed it outside Monzo's office [6].

When COVID hit, revenue dropped 50% (much of it came from overseas card usage). A hundred-million-pound funding round, nine months in negotiation with paperwork agreed, was due to close on a Monday. On Friday afternoon, the world went into lockdown, and investors paused all investments [6].

Financial regulators told Monzo to "start preparing to wind the company down because it was losing a hundred million pounds a year" [6]. Blomfield worked seven days a week, sixteen to seventeen hours a day. He held all-hands in front of two thousand people, openly sharing what was going wrong. "The vulnerability created this immense bond" [6].

Eventually, Blomfield could no longer function. His identity and the company were "so intertwined" that stepping away "was like letting all of that go." His board and investors kept saying he could step back "in two or three years." He told them: "Maybe you've got two or three more weeks, but that's all I can do" [6].

He stepped down as CEO. A new CEO and COO, along with co-founder Jonas (CTO) and the existing team, carried Monzo through. It is now profitable with over eight million UK customers (15-20% of the adult population) [6].

### Recovery and Transition to YC

After leaving Monzo, Blomfield took a year off. An investor invited him to angel-invest in a fintech and have a conversation with the founder. "I could feel my brain like coming back to life" [6]. He went from one investment to seventy-five in nine months. YC then recruited him, first as a visiting partner, then as a full Group Partner [6].

Blomfield describes rejoining YC as "coming home" -- [[Harj Taggar]], [[Garry Tan]], and others from his 2011 cohort are back [6].

## Worldview

**Start from value, not cost.** His [[B2B Pricing]] framework centers on the value equation: calculate the dollar value of what the product delivers to the customer, then price at 25-50% of that value. Cost is only a floor, never a starting point. Competition is addressed through differentiation, not price wars [1].

**Metrics discipline over metrics obsession.** Blomfield holds two positions simultaneously: you must track key metrics from day one (launching without metrics is "flying blind"), but you must not hide behind metrics or try to A/B test everything with insufficient data. Four or five metrics, tracked accurately, with agreed-upon definitions, is the standard [3].

**Fix unit economics before scaling.** This lesson comes from Monzo's early experience of losing 30-40 pounds per customer while scaling to half a million users. "Don't scale your customer base, don't try and grow as quickly as possible whilst you have negative unit economics. You fix them first, and then you scale" [3].

**Organic growth over paid growth.** Blomfield got Monzo to one million customers before spending on marketing. Benchmark: great consumer companies get 80%+ of growth organically [4].

**AI as a professional engineering practice.** Blomfield treats vibe coding not as a gimmick but as a new development methodology that follows the same principles good software engineers use: planning before coding, version control, testing, modular architecture, and refactoring [2].

**Everyone has the choice to create.** Blomfield frames entrepreneurship as a choice between following existing structures and creating new ones: "All of these things were created by people no smarter or harder-working than you or me. You have the chance to create something, to define the rules and make the world feel different" [6].

## Distinct Perspective

Blomfield's unique contribution is bridging B2B and consumer metrics from personal experience. Having operated a B2B payments company (GoCardless), a consumer bank (Monzo), and a consumer social app (Grouper), he can speak to the differences between these business models with operational detail rather than theory. His retention layer-cake visualization -- showing how sticky cohorts compound into unstoppable revenue -- and his unit economics warnings from Monzo's near-negative-margin scaling both come from lived consequences rather than observation.

He also contributes a pivot story: his first company started as a consumer bill-splitting app (Group.io), pivoted to business payments after [[Paul Graham]] told them to stop building features and start getting users, and became GoCardless [5].

## Key Frameworks

- **The Value Equation**: Price at one-third of the value delivered to the customer [1].
- **The 5:1 Rule**: Salespeople should close 5x their total compensation in new ARR annually [1].
- **The Ratchet Method**: Start at a reasonable price and increase by 50% per customer until 25% of deals are lost on price [1].
- **Plan-Implement-Test-Commit**: For vibe coding, create a plan in markdown, implement section by section, test, commit to Git [2].
- **Four or Five Key Metrics**: Pick before launch, agree on definitions, never change them to improve numbers [3].
- **Revenue / Burn Rate / Runway**: The three numbers at the top of every investor update [3].
- **The Layer Cake vs. Leaky Bucket**: Visual model for whether retention is building compounding revenue or draining it [3].
- **Net Dollar Retention benchmarks**: 125-150% early stage, 110-120% mature, below 100% is a red flag [3].
- **Organic/Paid Growth ratio**: 80%+ organic for great consumer companies [4].
- **Magic Moment**: Early user behavior that predicts long-term retention (Facebook's 7 friends in 10 days, Monzo's 3 friends added) [4].
- **NPS minimum**: +50 for consumer startups [4].
