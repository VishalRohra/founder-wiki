# Elon Musk

Elon Musk is co-founder of PayPal, founder and CEO of SpaceX, CEO of Tesla Motors, co-founder of OpenAI, and founder of xAI and Neurolink. In a 2016 interview with [[Sam Altman]] [1] and a 2025 fireside chat with [[Garry Tan]] at AI Startup School [2], Musk discusses what problems are worth working on, how to evaluate impact, first principles thinking, and how to act in the face of fear.

## Worldview

**Usefulness as the optimization function.** Musk frames his career decisions around utility rather than prestige or financial return: "Whatever this thing is that you're trying to create, what would be the utility delta compared to the current state of the art times how many people it would affect" [1]. In 2025, he elaborates: "Try to be as useful as possible. It may sound trite, but it's so hard to be useful, especially to be useful to a lot of people. Where the area under the curve of total utility is like, how much have you been useful to your fellow human beings times how many people? It's like the physics definition of true work" [2].

**Technology does not automatically improve.** Musk pushes back against the assumption that technology naturally gets better over time: "It only gets better if smart people work like crazy to make it better. By itself technology, if people don't work on it, it actually will decline" [1]. He cites ancient Egypt (forgot how to build pyramids), Rome (forgot roadways and aqueducts), and rocketry (Saturn V to moon, then Space Shuttle to low orbit, then Space Shuttle retired -- "that trend basically trends to zero") [1].

**Ego-to-ability ratio.** In 2025, Musk introduces a failure mode framework: "A major failure mode is when ego to ability ratio is greater than one. If your ego to ability ratio gets too high, then you're going to break the feedback loop to reality. In AI terms, you'll break your reinforcement learning loop." The prescription: internalize responsibility, minimize ego, do whatever the task requires regardless of whether it is grand or humble [2].

**Fatalism reduces fear.** Musk says he feels fear "quite strongly" but acts despite it when "something is important enough, you believe in it enough" [1]. He describes a deliberate practice of fatalism: "If you just accept the probabilities, then that diminishes fear." When starting SpaceX, he thought the odds of success were less than 10%: "just accepted that actually probably I would just lose everything" [1].

## The Origin Stories

### Zip2 (1995)

Musk's first startup. He chose between a Stanford PhD in material science (working on ultra capacitors for EVs) and building something on "this thing most people have never heard of, called the internet." He asked his professor to defer for a quarter; the professor replied, "This is probably the last conversation we'll have." He was right [2].

Musk wrote the first (or one of the first) maps, directions, and internet yellow/white pages. He could not afford a web server, so he read the port directly. Could not afford a T1 line, so he drilled a hole through the floor of his $500/month office and ran a LAN cable to the ISP below. He and his brother slept in the office and showered at the YMCA [2].

He did not intend to start a company: "I tried to get a job at Netscape. I sent my resume in. Nobody responded. Then I tried hanging out in the lobby to see if I could bump into someone. But I was too shy to talk to anyone. So I'll just write software myself" [2].

Zip2 sold for $300 million. Musk received $20 million. The key mistake: too much board control from legacy media companies who "kept wanting to use our software in ways that made no sense" [2].

### X.com / PayPal

Musk reinvested almost all of his Zip2 proceeds. "I kept the chips on the table." He wanted to go direct to consumer after feeling Zip2's technology was "constrained by our customers" [2].

### SpaceX (2002)

Musk went to NASA's website to find out when people were being sent to Mars. "There was no date." He initially planned a philanthropic mission (greenhouse on Mars) but after learning more -- including trips to Russia to buy ICBMs ("that's an adventure -- you go and meet with Russian high command and say, 'I'd like to buy some ICBMs'") -- realized the problem was not insufficient will but insufficient affordable technology [2].

He started SpaceX expecting to fail: "Maybe a less than 10% chance of being successful, maybe 1%." When recruiting: "I said we're probably going to die. But there's a 12% chance we might not die." He became chief engineer "not because I wanted to, but because I couldn't hire anyone who was good. None of the good chief engineers would join because they're like, 'This is too risky'" [2].

The first three launches failed. "If the fourth one hadn't worked, I had no money left, and that would have been curtains." The fourth launch succeeded. NASA awarded a resupply contract right before Christmas. "I literally blurted out, 'I love you guys,' which is not normally what they hear" [2].

### Tesla and the 2008 Crisis

Simultaneously, Tesla's financing round failed mid-2008 after SpaceX's third launch failure. "Tesla financing round closed on the last hour of the last day that it was possible, which was 6 p.m. December 24th, 2008. We would have bounced payroll two days after Christmas if that round hadn't closed" [2].

The press ridiculed him: "'Internet guy aka fool is attempting to build a rocket company.' I was like, 'Yeah, admittedly it does sound improbable and I agree that it's improbable'" [2].

## First Principles Thinking

Musk provides a detailed example of first-principles analysis applied to rockets [2]:

The typical approach: look at historical rocket costs and assume new rockets cost similarly.

The first-principles approach: list constituent materials (aluminum, copper, carbon fiber, steel), determine their weight and cost per kilogram. "The raw materials of a rocket are only maybe one or two percent of the historical cost of a rocket. So the manufacturing must necessarily be very inefficient" [2].

An AI infrastructure example: in early 2025, xAI needed 100,000 H100s to train coherently. Suppliers estimated 18-24 months. Musk needed it in 6 months. Breaking it down: need a building (found a disused Electrolux factory in Memphis), need power (input was 15 MW, needed 150 MW -- rented generators), need cooling (rented a quarter of the mobile cooling capacity of the US), need power smoothing (modified Tesla Megapack software to handle 50% power drops in 100 milliseconds). They ran cabling operations in four shifts, 24/7, with Musk sleeping in the data center and doing cabling himself [2].

## Five Areas That Affect Humanity's Future

Identified in college 25 years before his 2016 interview [1]:

1. **AI** -- "Probably the single biggest item in the near term that's likely to affect humanity"
2. **Genetics** -- solving genetic diseases
3. **Brain-computer interface** -- solving the bandwidth constraint
4. **Making life multi-planetary**
5. **Accelerating the transition to sustainable energy**

## On Democratization of AI

Musk argues that the primary danger of AI is concentration of power: "No one company or small set of individuals has control over advanced AI technology. I think that is very dangerous" [1]. This was the stated motivation for co-founding OpenAI with Altman: "to help spread out AI technology so it doesn't get concentrated in the hands of a few" [1].

## On Digital Superintelligence (2025)

Musk predicts digital superintelligence (smarter than any human at anything) will arrive in 2025 or 2026. He estimates 10-20% chance of annihilation ("But look on the bright side, that's 80 to 90% probability of a great outcome"). His primary AI safety prescription: "A rigorous adherence to truth is the most important thing for AI safety." Forcing AI to believe things that are not true is what could make it dangerous [2].

He expects roughly 5-10 "deep intelligences" globally, perhaps 4 in the US. He predicts 5-10x as many humanoid robots as humans within 100 years [2].

On the Kardashev scale: humanity has harnessed 1-2% of Earth's energy. Mars self-sustainability is roughly 30 years away. "Being a multiplanetary species greatly increases the probable lifespan of civilization or consciousness or intelligence, both biological and digital" [2].

## On Neurolink

Not necessary for digital superintelligence but solves the input-output bandwidth constraint. Sustained human output is less than one bit per second (fewer than 86,400 symbols per day). Neural interfaces could enable multispectral vision (infrared, ultraviolet, radar) and dramatically augmented intelligence. Five humans have received read-only implants; vision implants (writing to the visual cortex) are expected within 6-12 months [2].

## On PhDs and Being Useful

Asked whether people who want to be useful should get PhDs, Musk answers: "Mostly not" [1]. He himself left a Stanford PhD program to start an internet company in 1995 because the timing seemed right and he "didn't want to do a Ph.D. at Stanford and watch it all happen" [1].

## How Musk Spends His Time

As of 2016, Musk spent 80% of his time on "engineering and design" and "developing next-generation product" at SpaceX and Tesla [1]. At SpaceX, Gwynne Shotwell manages legal, finance, sales, and general business while Musk works with engineering. His biggest realization that year: "What really matters is the machine that builds the machine -- the factory. And that is at least two orders of magnitude harder than the vehicle itself" [1].
