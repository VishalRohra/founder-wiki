# Elon Musk

Elon Musk is co-founder of PayPal, founder and CEO of SpaceX, CEO of Tesla Motors, and co-founder of OpenAI. In a 2016 interview with [[Sam Altman]] for the YC "How to Build the Future" series, Musk discusses what problems are worth working on, how to evaluate impact, and how to act in the face of fear [1].

## Worldview

**Usefulness as the optimization function.** Musk frames his career decisions around utility rather than prestige or financial return: "Whatever this thing is that you're trying to create, what would be the utility delta compared to the current state of the art times how many people it would affect" [1]. A small difference affecting vast numbers of people and a big difference affecting a moderate number both produce similar area under the curve [1].

**Technology does not automatically improve.** Musk pushes back against the assumption that technology naturally gets better over time: "It only gets better if smart people work like crazy to make it better. By itself technology, if people don't work on it, it actually will decline" [1]. He cites ancient Egypt (forgot how to build pyramids, forgot how to read hieroglyphics) and Rome (forgot how to build roadways, aqueducts, indoor plumbing) as evidence that "entropy is not on your side" [1].

In rocketry, he notes the same decline: "In '69 we were able to go to the moon with Saturn V and then the space shuttle could only take people to low Earth orbit and then the space shuttle retired. That trend basically trends to zero" [1].

**Fatalism reduces fear.** Musk says he feels fear "quite strongly" but acts despite it when "something is important enough, you believe in it enough" [1]. He describes a deliberate practice of fatalism: "If you just accept the probabilities, then that diminishes fear." When starting SpaceX, he thought the odds of success were less than 10% and "just accepted that actually probably I would just lose everything. But that maybe would make some progress" [1]. The same applied to Tesla: "I thought the odds of a car company succeeding were extremely low" [1].

## Five Areas That Affect Humanity's Future

When asked what he would work on if starting over at age 22, Musk lists five areas he identified in college 25 years earlier [1]:

1. **AI** -- "Probably the single biggest item in the near term that's likely to affect humanity." The risk is not that AI develops autonomous will but that "someone may use it in a way that is bad" or it gets stolen by "some evil dictator" [1].
2. **Genetics** -- Solving genetic diseases, preventing dementia or Alzheimer's through genetic reprogramming [1].
3. **Brain-computer interface** -- Solving the "bandwidth constraint" between the cortex and digital tools. Musk argues humans already have a "digital tertiary self" through phones and computers but the interface is "extremely bandwidth constrained" [1].
4. **Making life multi-planetary** [1].
5. **Accelerating the transition to sustainable energy** [1].

## On Democratization of AI

Musk argues that the primary danger of AI is concentration of power: "No one company or small set of individuals has control over advanced AI technology. I think that is very dangerous" [1]. This was the stated motivation for co-founding OpenAI with Altman: "to help spread out AI technology so it doesn't get concentrated in the hands of a few" [1].

His proposed long-term solution: if humans can merge with AI through improved brain-computer interfaces, "then effectively you become an AI human symbiote. And if that then is widespread, with anyone who wants it can have it, then we solve the control problem as well. We don't have to worry about some evil dictator AI because we are the AI collectively" [1].

## On PhDs and Being Useful

Asked whether people who want to be useful should get PhDs, Musk answers: "Mostly not" [1]. He himself left a Stanford PhD program to start an internet company in 1995 because the timing seemed right and he "didn't want to do a Ph.D. at Stanford and watch it all happen." He adds: "You can get a doctorate on many things that ultimately do not have a practical bearing on the world" [1].

## How Musk Spends His Time

As of 2016, Musk spent 80% of his time on "engineering and design" and "developing next-generation product" at SpaceX and Tesla [1]. At SpaceX, Gwynne Shotwell manages legal, finance, sales, and general business while Musk works with engineering on improving the Falcon 9 and Dragon spacecraft and developing the Mars colonial architecture [1]. His biggest realization that year: "What really matters is the machine that builds the machine -- the factory. And that is at least two orders of magnitude harder than the vehicle itself" [1].
