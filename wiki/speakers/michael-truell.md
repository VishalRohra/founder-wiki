# Michael Truell

Michael Truell is co-founder and CEO of Cursor (Anysphere), an AI-powered code editor that reached $100M ARR in 2024 when Truell was 24. He began programming in middle school, built robots with reinforcement learning in high school, studied at MIT, and co-founded Anysphere with three MIT classmates in 2022. See [[Cursor: From Failed CAD Co-Pilot to $100M ARR Code Editor]] for the full company story [1].

## Worldview

**Conviction over competition.** Truell's team avoided working on AI for coding because GitHub Copilot was already generating $100M+ in revenue. They only entered the space after exhausting other ideas and realizing that no one was building toward the end state where "all of software development flows through models." The lesson: competition is less important than whether existing players are aiming at the right future [1].

**Product quality is the growth engine.** Cursor grew almost entirely through word of mouth. Attempts at growth engineering "kind of washed away" compared to product improvements. Each improvement -- codebase awareness, faster predictions, agent-like behavior -- showed up directly in the numbers [1].

**The long messy middle.** Rather than believing AI will instantly automate all coding or that the current paradigm is permanent, Truell holds a middle position: professional software engineering will transform over decades. "There will be this long messy middle where you will be working with the AI more and more" [1].

## Distinct Perspective

Unlike many AI founders who came from research or large tech companies, Truell represents the builder-from-childhood archetype -- programming since middle school, implementing neural networks from scratch in high school without understanding calculus, spending years on failed projects with his co-founders before finding the right idea. His trajectory validates both the value of starting young (see [[Starting Young]]) and the value of failed projects as education [1].

His most distinctive advice: do not pick an idea because it seems "boring and sleepy and uncompetitive" (their CAD co-pilot failed for exactly this reason). Instead, pick something you are "inherently excited about" even if the competition is intimidating [1].
