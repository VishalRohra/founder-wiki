# Bob McGrew

Bob McGrew is the former Chief Research Officer at OpenAI. Before OpenAI, he spent years at Palantir, where he gained management experience and learned about forward-deployed engineering. He joined OpenAI in 2016 not to work at a research lab but because "it was a place full of very smart people with big ambitions" where he could become an expert in deep learning [1].

## Worldview

**Start with the best model, work backwards.** McGrew's advice to AI startup founders is specific: "Start with the very best model you can. Your startup is only going to be successful if it exploits something about AI that realistically is going to be on the frontier." Once it works, distill to cheaper models. "The most important thing in a startup is actually your time. You don't want to be taking three years to get to market" [1].

**Scale is the central problem.** McGrew identifies scale as the common thread across OpenAI's breakthroughs. Getting from zero to a working model is a separate challenge from scaling it, but once something works, scaling is "probably the practical problem in any sort of model building" -- a systems problem, a data problem, and an algorithmic problem simultaneously [1].

**Software needs forward-deployed engineers.** Drawing from Palantir, McGrew argues that the gap between AI capability and AI adoption exists because nobody is doing the integration work. "We just need more software engineers who are like that forward-deployed engineer to link up the intelligence." Palantir was initially criticized for needing forward-deployed engineers; a decade later, it is a highly valuable company and everyone is building that function [1].

## On OpenAI's Culture

McGrew contrasts OpenAI's culture with DeepMind (top-down plan from Demis) and Google Brain (pure academia, let researchers do whatever they want). OpenAI found a middle ground: "There was no big centralized plan. But at the same time, people didn't just let a thousand flowers bloom. Instead, we had opinions about what needed to be done" -- particularly around scale as the path to improvement [1].

A cultural decision: early papers credited "cite as OpenAI" rather than individual authors, avoiding the academic obsession with credit assignment. "The ability to accept that people want credit, but to channel it into internal reputation rather than paper position, was a really important cultural piece" [1].

## On the Path to AGI

McGrew outlines the progression: GPT-1 (Alec Radford's insight that predicting the next token would produce coherent text), combined with the scale learnings from Dota 2 and the robot hand project, led to GPT-2, GPT-3, and GPT-4. The current frontier: reasoning and test-time compute (O1, O3 models), which McGrew sees as cracking the final piece needed for a clear path to scaling toward AGI [1].

He frames AI progress using the Moore's Law analogy: "One big exponential curve, but it's actually the sum of a bunch of little S-curves." Pre-training data is one S-curve that is approaching its ceiling. Reasoning is the new S-curve [1].

## Two Jobs of the Future

McGrew predicts two dominant roles in an AI-heavy future: "genius" (the lone innovator leveraging AI to amplify individual capability) and "manager" (the CEO of a firm that is mostly AI). "Those are two things that would be really fun jobs, honestly" [1].

On why he teaches his eight-year-old son to code despite AI's coding ability: "Even once the computer can do the programming for you, there's still something to having had your hands in it yourself and knowing what's possible and what's not possible." He cites [[Paul Graham]]'s concept of "the resistance of the medium" [1].
