# Drew Houston

Drew Houston is the co-founder and CEO of [[Dropbox: From Thumb Drives to 500 Million Users|Dropbox]] (YC W07), a cloud file storage and sharing company that grew to over 500 million users. He studied at MIT, applied to YC once (rejected with Accolade, an SAT prep company) and a second time (accepted with Dropbox as a solo founder, before finding co-founder Arash Ferdowsi through Kyle Vogt) [1].

## Worldview

**The CEO job changes every six months, and nobody tells you.** Houston identifies this as the hardest part of scaling: "Some of the things that made you good at the prior stage become a hindrance for the next stage." He wrote most of the first version of Dropbox but had to stop coding as the company grew [1].

**Learning to be CEO is a systematic project.** Nobody is born a CEO. Houston's approach: reading (Andy Grove's "High Output Management," Drucker's "The Effective Executive," Munger's "Poor Charlie's Almanack"), mentoring from entrepreneurs at different stages, and studying excellence outside tech (Phil Jackson's coaching, government problem-solving, consumer packaged goods) [1].

**Thick skin and thin skin simultaneously.** Founders must tune out external noise (press, Hacker News critics, market hype cycles) while remaining highly responsive to customer and team signals. "That's a weird dynamic that you have to handle" [1].

**Focus means killing things you love.** After expanding Dropbox into photos (Carousel) and email (Mailbox), Houston contracted -- killing both products after reading Andy Grove's "Only the Paranoid Survive." "Everybody loves the idea of focus, but what focus actually means is shooting stuff you love and turning down things you know you can make work" [1].

## Distinct Perspective

Houston's contribution to the YC canon is the engineer-to-CEO transition story told with unusual specificity. His early meeting with a more experienced CEO -- where he tuned out talks about mission and values while mentally reviewing unfixed bugs -- is a canonical moment. "After hearing that 5 or 6 or 10 times, you realize, 'The only one here who's confused is me'" [1].

His insight that founders transition "from designing a system of code and algorithms, which is very orderly and discrete, to a system of people, and things like culture, values, all kinds of other things, are the programming that makes that work" captures a realization many technical founders struggle to articulate [1].

Houston also offers a clear model for viral growth at scale. The Dropbox referral program (two-sided free storage incentive) drove 30% of signups; shared folders drove another 20%. Half of all growth was viral -- a benchmark for consumer product growth [1].

## Key Frameworks

- **AARRR Metrics (via Dave McClure)**: Acquisition, Activation, Revenue, Retention, Referral. Houston used this as the operating framework for managing Dropbox's growth [1].
- **The 1/2/5-Year Question**: "What will I wish I had been learning, or what would I wish I had been doing?" Applied regularly to identify CEO skill gaps before they become crises [1].
- **People as Code**: Culture and values are the programming language for a system of people, analogous to code and algorithms for a system of software [1].
- **The Triangulation Method**: Learning from multiple domains (tech CEOs, sports coaches, government leaders, board members) to internalize management lessons faster than reading alone [1].
- **Strategic Contraction**: When expansion creates identity confusion, "put all your eggs in one basket and watch that basket" [1].
