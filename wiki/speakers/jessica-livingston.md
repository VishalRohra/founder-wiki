# Jessica Livingston

Jessica Livingston is a co-founder of Y Combinator (with [[Paul Graham]], Robert Morris, and Trevor Blackwell). She is the author of "Founders at Work" (2007), a book of interviews with early-stage startup founders. Graham gave her the nickname "the social radar" for her focus on the personal and personality dimensions of founders, complementing his more technical evaluation [2]. After 11 years of working on YC and seeing over 1,000 companies go through the program, she delivered a synthesis of her observations in a 2017 talk called "How Not to Fail" [1].

## Worldview

Livingston's advice is structured as seven things to remember. Her recurring themes:

**Make something people want, above all else.** "Nothing else you do will matter if you are not making something people want. You can be the best spokesperson, the best fundraiser, the best programmer, but if you aren't building a product that satisfies a real need, you'll never succeed" [1].

**Focus is what separates winners.** "One of the most conspicuous patterns we've seen among the thousand startups we've funded is that the most successful founders are always totally focused on their product and their users. To the point of being fanatical" [1].

**Denial is the silent killer.** Growth targets force honesty: "The numbers, good or bad, are staring you in the face" [1]. Many founders say "we're not focusing on growth right now" -- and the outcome is usually predictable [1].

**Overhiring kills.** "Overhiring is the big mistake in the second phase of a startup, as making something no one wants is in the first phase" [1].

## Distinct Perspective

Livingston provides a list of distractions she has seen derail founders, which she calls "the startup equivalent of wolves in sheep's clothing": grabbing coffee with investors, talking with acquirers, networking, recruiting boards of advisors, doing partnerships, spending time on PR before PMF, arguing on social media, going to conferences, and worrying about being a woman in tech [1].

On the last point, she is direct: "While I'll tell you that it is going to be harder for you as a woman, it's not going to be so much harder that it will make the difference between success and failure. If you want to start a startup, just go ahead and do it" [1].

## Role in YC's Founding

YC originated from a failed attempt to join a Boston VC firm. The firm took so long in their process that during the waiting period, Livingston and Graham spent nightly dinners brainstorming what a better early-stage funder would look like [2]. Graham would suggest ideas and eventually said, "Let's just start our own thing" [2].

The original plan was to fund companies asynchronously under the name "Cambridge Seed." The batch model came later. Key decisions that shaped YC's DNA [2]:
- **Standardized legal paperwork** -- unprecedented at the time, eliminating the need for expensive legal representation.
- **Standard deal terms** -- a single, non-negotiable deal structure.
- **Weekly dinners as events** -- VCs did one or two events per year; YC did them weekly.
- **Believing in founders before success** -- Livingston describes this as the core of YC's DNA: "We love founders. It's not about the returns. It's about the founders themselves" [2].

## The First Batch (Summer 2005)

The first batch included eight companies. Among them: [[Sam Altman]], Steve Huffman and Alexis Ohanian (Reddit), Justin Kan and Emmett Shear (Twitch), and Aaron Swartz [2]. Paul cooked chili in crock pots for the weekly dinners [2].

Livingston recalls: "It was like one of the most fun times in my life because everyone really wanted to be there and really wanted to start a startup" [2]. Steve Huffman's mother was convinced YC was a scam [2].

## The DNA of YC

Livingston identifies elements that have remained constant for nearly 20 years [2]:

**Community.** Alumni returned to help new batches, creating a flywheel of knowledge and support.

**Events as core infrastructure.** "Any time you can bring people together with an event, good things always happen" [2].

**Believing in people pre-success.** Most investors prefer to socialize with famous founders. YC partners engage most deeply with founders before they have achieved anything. "Confidence is a huge thing that YC brings to the table" [2].

**Resisting bureaucracy.** Graham resisted: "I don't do meetings." Livingston describes 99% of incoming requests as "useless time-wasting" [2].
