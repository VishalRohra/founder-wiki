# Diana Hu

Diana Hu is a Group Partner at Y Combinator. She was co-founder and CTO of Escher Reality, a startup building an augmented reality SDK for game developers. Escher Reality was acquired by Niantic (makers of Pokemon Go), where Hu served as director of engineering heading the AR platform. Her advice draws on the full arc from idea to prototype to MVP to scaling systems to millions of users [1].

## Worldview

Hu's advice is centered on the specific challenges technical founders face. Her core themes:

**The technical founder is a partner, not a contractor.** When non-technical founders say "I need somebody to build my app," Hu responds: "That isn't going to cut it" [1]. A technical founder requires intense commitment and is involved in talking to users, not just building [1].

**Speed is the only thing that matters early.** Every principle Hu teaches serves the goal of moving faster. Prototypes in days, MVPs in weeks, tech choices optimized for iteration speed. "If there's only one takeaway from this whole talk, it is that startups move quickly" [1].

**Good enough beats perfect.** At a big company, you may be rewarded for the perfect architecture. "But not for a startup. You're going to have a bias towards action and moving quickly, and actually deciding with a lot of incomplete information" [1]. Technical debt is acceptable. Ugly code is acceptable. Chaos is the normal state [1].

**The Jedi Master and the beginner choose the same tools.** Hu presents the "midwit meme" applied to tech stacks: the beginner picks PHP/JavaScript because that is all they know; the mid-level engineer reaches for Kafka and Kubernetes; the expert picks PHP/JavaScript again because they recognize it enables faster iteration. "If you build a company and it works and you get users, you can solve your way out of it" -- as Facebook did by building HipHop to transpile PHP to C++ [1].

**Don't hire to build the MVP.** Hiring from an unknown pool takes over a month, slows you down, and prevents founders from developing product insights [1]. Justin.tv/Twitch was built by three technical founders before any hires were made [1].

**Tech debt is fine pre-PMF.** "You gotta get comfortable, a little bit, with the heat of your tech burning" [1]. Pokemon Go could not handle its launch traffic and nobody could log in. "That did not kill the company at all. In fact, to this day, Pokemon Go generates over a billion dollars in revenue" [1].

**The technical founder must stay close to users.** After launching, the temptation is to hunker down and build. Hu pushes back: "Your role as a technical founder is very different. You got to be involved in the journey and really understand the insights of why users stay or leave your products" [1].

## Distinct Perspective

Hu speaks specifically to engineers who are used to big-company standards. Her advice is the technical founder's counterpart to [[Michael Seibel]]'s operational advice -- where Seibel addresses product process and customer interaction, Hu addresses architecture decisions, tech stack choices, and the evolving role of the CTO from coder to manager.

She is unusually concrete about what tools to use and not use at each stage (Figma for prototypes, Firebase for backend, Google Analytics for post-launch, not Kafka or Prometheus). Her examples are drawn from real YC companies (Optimizely, DoorDash, Stripe, WePay, Segment, Justin.tv, Escher Reality, Pokemon Go) with specific technical details.

In the Launching video, Hu addresses the fear of launching from a technical founder's perspective: the fear that "nobody shows up" means "I'm dumb" or "I'm wrong" [2]. She also identifies the comforting alternative to launching -- writing code -- as a form of avoidance [2].

## Key Frameworks

- **Three-Stage Model**: Ideating (prototype in days), Building to Launch (MVP in weeks), Launch to Iterate (ongoing iteration toward PMF) [1]
- **90/10 Solution**: Paul Buchheit's principle applied to MVPs -- restrict the product to limited dimensions, expect to rewrite 90% of the code [1]
- **Choose Tech for Iteration Speed**: The only tech choices that matter are those tied to customer promises; everything else should optimize for speed [1]
- **The Midwit Meme for Tech Stacks**: Beginners and experts converge on simple tools; intermediate engineers over-engineer [1]
- **Building vs. Fixing Balance**: Post-launch, technical founders must balance bug fixes, features, and tech debt -- fear the right things (getting to PMF), not rendering bugs [1]
