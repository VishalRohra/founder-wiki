# Ooshma Garg

Ooshma Garg is the founder and CEO of [Gobble](http://www.gobble.com) (YC W14), the dinner kit delivery service that invented the 10-minute, 1-pan dinner kit. She is a mechanical engineer by training and previously ran a startup at Stanford helping women get jobs at banks and consulting firms [1].

## Worldview

Garg's worldview is defined by persistence through prolonged uncertainty. She spent five and a half years iterating through four different product versions before finding [[Product-Market Fit]]. [[Paul Graham]] [tweeted](https://twitter.com/paulg/status/659749829040410624): "Ooshma Garg does not give up" [1].

She describes building a startup as "a daily game of Snakes and Ladders" -- two steps forward, two steps back. The snakes do not go away as the company grows; they become obstacles the founder can isolate and work around rather than existential threats [1].

She deliberately separated the company from the product: "A company is not just a product, a company is solving a problem. It's a mission. And if the mission is alive, you should keep working on it" [1]. This allowed Gobble to launch four different products while maintaining the same underlying mission.

## Key Frameworks

**GEM Framework**: Garg's mnemonic for founder resilience:
- **Grit** (from your core): The internal compass that tells you to keep going when no one else believes
- **Experiment** (from your head): Use data and iteration to find what works; a company can launch multiple products in service of one mission
- **Mission** (from your heart): The mission outlasts any product. Gobble's mission -- "create love through food" -- survived four pivots [1]

**The Startup Curve in Practice**: Garg maps Gobble's five-year journey onto [[Paul Graham]]'s startup curve: launch, trough of sorrow, wiggles of false hope, and (eventually) the promised land. She compares it to Airbnb's 2008-2010 graph, which showed a similar pattern before becoming smooth in retrospect [1].

## Distinct Perspective

Garg is unusual for openly describing the financial desperation of near-death -- showing a screenshot of her bank balance before YC money arrived, noting she had just made the last full payroll. "Starting and running a company, as a founder, is a very lonely experience. Sometimes, only you know the reality of the low times" [1].

She also demonstrates that joining YC at a later stage (with an existing product and team) can be valuable. "YC helped us get back to the basics. Even when you have a product and a team, founders focusing on the basics is amazing at any point and anytime in your company" [1].

Her fundraising lesson is concrete: both Trinity Ventures and Andreessen Horowitz said no twice before leading her Series A. "It's important to accept rejection with grace" [1].
