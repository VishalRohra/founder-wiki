# Garry Tan

Garry Tan is President and CEO of Y Combinator. Prior to leading YC, he was a partner at YC (2011-2015), co-founded Initialized Capital (an early-stage venture fund), and co-founded Posterous (YC S08). He was early at Palantir, which directly informs his advocacy for the [[Forward Deployed Engineers]] model. His content bridges the technical founder and investor perspectives.

## Worldview

**The "Why Now" question is paramount.** Tan's central thesis is that the most important question for any startup is "Why now?" [2]. Startup opportunities arise from three forces: new technology (orders-of-magnitude improvements), behavior change (mass adoption of new platforms), and regulation (government mandates creating waves of demand) [2].

**Utility is the only real measure.** Tan urges founders to "think about the area under the curve of utility that you could contribute to society" and warns that status-seeking without substance is the path to disgrace: "When you think about SBF, when you think about Theranos, when you peel back a little bit, you realize there's nothing. It was a lie" [8].

**The forward deployed engineer is the ideal founder.** Drawing on his Palantir experience, Tan argues that the best founders combine deep technical ability with the willingness to sit next to end users and understand their workflows at the most granular level. This is how "a really good engineer with a weak handshake" beats Salesforce, Oracle, or Booz Allen [7].

**First principles over analogy.** The educational and professional systems are designed to keep people "in the box" [1]. The three qualities needed to break out: curiosity (asking why repeatedly), empathy (direct experience with users), and serenity (fighting battles you can win) [1].

**Platform neutrality matters.** Tan draws parallels between net neutrality fights and the current AI platform landscape, arguing that government intervention to prevent self-preferencing is necessary for a healthy AI ecosystem [6].

## Key Frameworks

- **The "Why Now" Test**: Every startup idea should have a clear answer to why this can succeed now when it could not before. Instacart succeeded where Webvan ($800M loss) failed because smartphone penetration enabled an on-demand labor force [2]. General Magic failed at smartphones in 1994 because the technology was 16,000x worse than what the iPhone delivered 13 years later [2].
- **First Principles via Curiosity, Empathy, Serenity**: DoorDash exemplifies serenity -- retreating to suburbs where competitors were not, finding profitability, then using those profits to win cities [1]. Airbnb exemplifies empathy -- the founders' experience of literally hosting guests gave them direct conviction [1].
- **Collapsed marketplaces**: AI turns three-sided or four-sided marketplaces into two-sided ones by replacing the human intermediary [6].
- **The FDE test**: Can you sit next to the end user, understand their workflow, and return with a demo that makes them say "I've never seen anything like that"? [7]
- **Follow curiosity, not customer discovery**: In the AI era, living at the edge of technology and following genuine curiosity yields better ideas than extensive pre-build customer discovery [6].
- **Underserved Audiences in Fintech**: Through Kai Stinchcombe (True Link Financial), choosing a specific underserved customer yields lower CAC, higher LTV, and distribution advantages [3].

## Distinct Perspective

Tan repeatedly argues that the best investments come from understanding technology curves and timing -- a skill he developed by funding Instacart ("I saw the future literally right before my eyes in my iPhone") [2]. He is more explicit than most YC partners about the role of government and regulation in creating startup opportunities [2].
