# Surbhi Sarna

Surbhi Sarna is a Group Partner at Y Combinator, where she is YC's first healthcare and biotech partner. Before YC, she founded nVision Medical (also called Envision Medical), which developed the first and only FDA-cleared device for collecting cells from the fallopian tube for cancer evaluation. Boston Scientific acquired the company for $275 million [1]. See [[nVision Medical: From Ovarian Cyst to $275M Acquisition]].

## Worldview

**Founders do not need advanced degrees to build healthcare companies.** Sarna started nVision at 24 with a bachelor's degree, no MBA, PhD, or MD. She was rejected by at least 50 investors. Her evaluation framework as a YC partner: "As long as you really know your stuff, I am not going to look at whether or not you have an MD or PhD and make my decision exclusively on that. I want to know that there's a big problem that needs solving and you are the founder to do it" [1].

**Personal experience creates founder conviction that others cannot replicate.** Sarna's ovarian cyst at age 13, and the diagnostic limitations her family faced, drove her to write in her college personal statement that she would one day start a company in women's health. That conviction sustained her through 50 fundraising rejections and years of device development [1].

**Ignore bias; find the believers.** Sarna acknowledges that fundraising is harder for women and people of color. Investors called women's health "bikini medicine" and said it was not a big enough market. Her advice: "These days I sincerely believe that there are enough people who believe in your potential that you can just ignore the folks who are being biased for whatever reason. Don't let it impact your confidence" [1].

## Distinct Perspective

Sarna's story bridges healthcare founder experience and YC advising. Her path from patient (at 13) to engineer (at Abbott Vascular) to founder (at 24) to $275M exit to YC Group Partner illustrates how personal experience with a problem creates the conviction needed to survive the long, capital-intensive journey of medical device development.

Her customer discovery story -- pretending to have a stomach ache to get a gynecologist appointment, then pitching a catheter concept during a pelvic exam -- is one of the more memorable [[Talking to Users]] examples in the YC canon. It worked: the physician introduced her to other doctors at UC Davis and Stanford [1].

She is also distinctive in explicitly addressing the fundraising experience of women founders and founders of color, including specific investor quotes that reveal bias in healthcare investing [1].

## Key Frameworks

- **Use Your Existing Network**: You do not need a Rolodex. Start with people you know and ask for introductions. Persistence and time get you to the right people [1].
- **Problem Conviction Over Credentials**: The most important qualifier is that "no one believes in the solution as much as you do," not degrees or pedigree [1].
- **Ignore the Biased, Find the Believers**: Do not let rejection from biased investors erode confidence. There are enough believers to build the company [1].
