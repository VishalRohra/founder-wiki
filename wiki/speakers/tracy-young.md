# Tracy Young

Tracy Young is a co-founder and former CEO of [PlanGrid](https://www.plangrid.com/) (YC W12), a construction productivity software company acquired by Autodesk for $875 million in 2018. She trained as a construction engineer with "muddy boots, hard hats, safety vests" and performed quality control on job sites before co-founding PlanGrid [1].

## Worldview

Young's advice centers on authenticity, emotional management, and the importance of understanding your users at a visceral level. She argues that deep domain expertise -- having been the actual user of the product you are building -- creates an advantage that cannot be hired for [1].

She is candid about the psychological toll of scaling a startup. She describes a recurring fear before every board meeting that the board would fire her, even when the company was performing well. Her executive coach diagnosed "negative ego" -- not arrogance, but constant self-diminishment. "I felt so guilty about the mistakes that we were making instead of focusing on making it better" [1].

Her advice to her younger self: be authentic earlier. In construction she smoked cigarettes and tried chewing tobacco to fit in. As CEO she tried to match an imagined template of what a CEO looks like. Neither worked. "I just became more and more and more of myself. And the last few years have been the happiest for me" [1].

## Key Frameworks

**Pain Tolerance as a Hiring Signal**: For early-stage hires (sub-50 employees), look for people who have demonstrated tolerance for rejection and discomfort. Door-to-door canvassers, pencil salesmen -- people who have done hard, unglamorous work and still speak positively about it [1].

**Firing Framework**: The moment you identify someone is not working out, time-box it. State expectations, specify what needs to change, and give a clear deadline. "That is what you owe your team member." Surprises in terminations are a leadership failure [1].

**The 150-Person Breaking Point**: Everything breaks at 150 employees (Dunbar's number). Before 50, hire generalists. After 50, specialize. After 150, organizational structure becomes essential [1].

**Executive Scale Mismatch**: The most common executive hiring mistake is hiring someone from a company at a very different scale. An executive from a 10,000-person company may have never operated at startup scale where there are no recruiters, no support teams, and constant pain [1].

## Distinct Perspective

Young is one of the few YC founders who built software for an industry whose workers had never used any software. This required not just building a product but educating an entire industry on mobile devices, then on the software itself. Her approach -- loaning out iPads, walking users through the App Store, calling every week to check on updates -- is among the most extreme examples of [[Doing Things That Don't Scale]] in the YC canon [1].

She is also notable for openly discussing the experience of being pregnant, giving birth, and nursing while leading a 400+ person company through a Series C and acquisition. "You can do all of that. I'm living proof" [1].
