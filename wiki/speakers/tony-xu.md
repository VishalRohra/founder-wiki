# Tony Xu

Tony Xu is the co-founder and CEO of DoorDash (YC S13), the largest food delivery platform in the United States with mid-60% market share and over seven million drivers. Before DoorDash, he worked at eBay under CEO John Donahoe, who recommended he attend Stanford GSB. He met his co-founders (Andy Fang, Stanley Tang, Evan Moore) at Stanford.

## Worldview

Xu's thinking is organized around a single principle: customer obsession over competitor focus. This is not abstract -- it is operationalized through the specific practice of doing the work yourself, continuously and indefinitely. All four DoorDash co-founders did every delivery for the first period. Xu personally did deliveries for two to three years. Every person at DoorDash, including Xu, still does deliveries every year, twelve years after founding [1].

He believes that doing the work produces insight that no analytics dashboard can replicate. His geographic strategy (suburbs over cities), his understanding of driver demographics, and his ability to weather fundraising rejection all trace back to direct operational experience [1].

## Key Frameworks

**The Three-Question Market Validation.** Before scaling, Xu needed positive answers to three questions: (1) Will consumers pay? (2) Will merchants pay? (3) Will drivers show up? Each required a different method of validation -- consumer retention without marketing spend, door-to-door merchant sales, and a controlled driver switching experiment [1].

**Customer Obsession, Not Competitor Focus.** This became DoorDash's core company value, originating from the Stanford football incident where the founders refunded all orders (costing 40% of their near-zero bank balance) and baked cookies delivered at 5 AM. The principle: do things the right way for customers even if the company might not survive [1].

**Market Creation vs. Market Capture.** Xu distinguishes between companies that capture an existing market (GrubHub partnering with the 20,000 restaurants that already offered delivery) and companies that create a new market (enabling the other 980,000 restaurants to offer delivery by building a logistics network). DoorDash was in the business of creating a market, which investors initially could not evaluate with standard frameworks [1].

**Crisis Simplifies Decision-Making.** In both DoorDash's near-death in 2013 and during COVID-19 in 2020, Xu found that crisis made priorities obvious and execution simpler. "Sometimes in crisis, I actually find that it's a lot easier operating a company because it's very clear what to do" [1].

## Distinct Perspective

Xu is notable for the extreme patience required by his journey. DoorDash almost died after demo day, received hundreds of investor rejections across three consecutive years (2016-2018), took a down round on the Series C, and still emerged as the category leader by 2019. His framework for understanding investor rejection: timing matters more than metrics. "There are moments where it's just before that moment" -- when data exists but is not yet overwhelming enough for growth investors to act [1].

His suburban strategy was contrarian at a time when all competitors targeted urban density. The insight came from doing deliveries, not from spreadsheet analysis: suburbs had families with larger baskets, easier parking, simpler deliveries to single-family homes, and higher customer need. "The first instinct was just listening to what the customers had told us" [1].

On COVID-19 decision-making, Xu cut commissions in half (costing $100+ million) and ran a national TV campaign advertising competitors, while DoorDash was unprofitable and preparing for an IPO. He justified this by appealing to mission duration: the company exists to grow the GDP of every city over decades, not to optimize for a single year's financials [1].

## On Becoming an Expert

Xu's advice to his 18-year-old self: "Especially when you're on the cusp of something new -- take AI -- there's no better way to be the expert than just to do the work. And you might be surprised at how quickly you get to become the expert" [1]. He had no logistics or delivery background but became an authority through years of hands-on experience.
