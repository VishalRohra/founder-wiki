# Jared Friedman

Jared Friedman is a YC partner who focuses on biotech and life science companies. He suggested the creation of YC's seed deck template (per [[Aaron Harris]]'s acknowledgment) [2].

## Worldview

Friedman draws a direct parallel between the [[Biotech Startup Funding|biotech funding landscape]] of 2019 and the tech funding landscape of 2005 [1]. He argues that the same structural forces [[Paul Graham]] predicted for tech (lower costs, new investors, founder control) are now playing out in biotech. His thesis is that new infrastructure (low-cost CROs, lab-as-a-service, computational biology tools) has dropped the cost of starting a biotech company to as little as $100K, enabling the same incremental fundraising model that transformed tech startups [1].

## Key Contributions

- **Biotech-tech analogy**: The venture creation model in biotech (VCs create companies, hire executives, take control) parallels the pre-2005 tech model. Both are disrupted by falling costs to start [1].
- **Infrastructure catalog**: Detailed list of tools reducing biotech startup costs (WuXi Biologics, Science Exchange, MBC BioLabs, OpenTrons, Atomwise, Cognition IP, Enzyme) [1]
- **New biotech founders**: Grad students and postdocs who previously had only two career paths (academia or big pharma) now have a third option [1]
- **[[University Spinouts|University spinout]] mechanics**: Comprehensive advice on who should leave the university, when to spin out, equity splits, and TTO negotiations [3]. YC has funded 75+ companies from academic research. Core messages: someone from the lab must leave full-time, hiring an outside CEO is almost always a mistake, business experience is not required ("People who work in business like to make it sound hard, as if business were like quantum physics. The fact is, it's not even close"), and equity should reward future contributions, not past ones [3]. Academic [[Cofounders|cofounders]] staying behind should own no more than 10% [3].
