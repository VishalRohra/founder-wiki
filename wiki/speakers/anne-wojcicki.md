# Anne Wojcicki

Anne Wojcicki is the co-founder and CEO of [23andMe](https://www.23andme.com/), a direct-to-consumer genetic testing company. Before founding 23andMe, she spent 10 years on Wall Street investing in healthcare -- small-cap biotechs, pharma, hospital systems, and insurance companies. She got her first Wall Street job by accident after attending a job fair for the free frequent flyer miles [1].

## Worldview

Wojcicki's thinking is shaped by a fundamental critique of healthcare incentives. The system monetizes illness; no one profits from keeping people healthy. "If I successfully get you to never be diabetic, no one makes money" [1]. Her response is to put consumers in the "power seat" by making them direct purchasers of their own health data, bypassing the insurance-doctor-pharmacy chain that typically makes decisions on their behalf [1].

She believes in persistence above almost everything else. 23andMe's Series A OKRs from 2007 were nearly identical to the company's goals a decade later. "One of the most important things I've learned at doing this now is the importance of persistence. You have to stick with it" [1]. She and [[Sam Altman]] agree that the secret to overnight success is ten years of persistence [1].

On regulation, her view evolved from adversarial to respectful. She acknowledges that FDA regulators are public servants with decades of experience and a legitimate mission to protect consumers. The onus is on the startup to communicate in the regulator's language: data, publications, and structured evidence [1].

## Key Frameworks

**The Trough of Sorrow in Consumer Products**: After a strong launch, 23andMe dropped to selling 15-25 kits per day. The fix was education: iterating on public talks (never giving the same one twice), identifying barriers to adoption (people did not understand genetics), and leading with specific value propositions (over 50% of customers get a medically meaningful result) [1].

**Hiring for Humility**: Between smart people who have humility and smart people who want to demonstrate how smart they are, always choose humility. People who are open to feedback and criticism create environments that attract more talent [1].

**Publishing as Credibility**: Science companies cannot sell credibility through claims. "No one believes that. What you need to do is you need to publish" [1]. Wojcicki never argues with scientists; she hands over publications.

## Distinct Perspective

Wojcicki is distinctive for having built 23andMe at the intersection of consumer technology, genetics, and regulated healthcare. Her Wall Street background gave her a systems-level view of healthcare incentives that most founders lack. Her strategy of crowdsourcing health data from millions of consumers to enable drug discovery and personalized medicine is unusual in the YC canon -- most health tech companies sell to providers or payers, not directly to consumers [1].
