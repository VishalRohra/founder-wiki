# Parker Conrad

Parker Conrad is the co-founder and CEO of [[Rippling: Building the Same Company Twice|Rippling]], a $13.5 billion compound software business for HR, finance, and IT. He previously co-founded SigFig (a consumer finance company that went through seven years of pivots) and Zenefits (which reached $65M ARR before Conrad was forced out). His career arc -- seven years of consumer failure, meteoric B2B success, public ouster, and a comeback building the same company better -- gives him an unusual perspective on persistence, product-market fit, and when to give up [1].

## Worldview

**PMF is binary and unmistakable.** Conrad draws the sharpest possible contrast between his SigFig and Zenefits experiences. At SigFig: four ideas out of five fail completely; one mostly fails with a glimmer of hope. At Zenefits: all five ideas work wildly well, plus the ideas you thought would not work also work. "That's how you know if something's working. And if you have that, then you want to keep going. If you don't have that, you probably want to start over somewhere else" [1]. See [[Product-Market Fit]].

**Give up if it is not working.** "There's a lot of advice out there to kind of stick with it, keep going, don't give up. And I think it's terrible advice. I think if it's not working, give up immediately." Conrad spent seven years on SigFig before reaching this conclusion [1].

**Don't fundraise; be unfundable to ignore.** "You need to make your business so good it sort of doesn't matter whether you fit into whatever mold or construct the VCs are looking for." Conrad's approach: wait until investors are sending signed term sheets with blank valuations before engaging in fundraising [1].

**Consumer is random; B2B is deliberate.** "A lot of consumer businesses end up being kind of random... It's one of the reasons I hate consumer businesses." Conrad believes many consumer-turned-B2B founders share this discovery [1].

## Distinct Perspective

Conrad is one of the few founders who built the same company twice. This gives him unusual certainty: "I had a lot of insights about the market and what was needed and very little self-doubt this time around" [1]. His framing of Rippling's founding -- "There's a hundred billion dollar opportunity just sitting right there on the floor, and nobody can see it except for us" -- reflects conviction born of operational experience rather than market analysis [1].

His compound startup thesis challenges the conventional YC advice to "do one thing and do it extremely well." Conrad argues that narrow point solutions hit a ceiling and that the deeper problems in organizations require interoperable application suites. He acknowledges this is "almost heretical" in American startup culture and may result in only three or four companies succeeding at it [1].

On AI, Conrad is a contrarian: the important thing about LLMs is not that they can write but that they can read. Their ability to ingest organizational data and surface anomalies -- where the rep's forecast disagrees with the AI's assessment, where a new hire's 90-day data predicts trouble -- is the transformative application for enterprise software [1].

## Key Frameworks

- **The Blank Valuation Test**: Do not fundraise until investors are sending signed term sheets with the valuation left blank [1].
- **PMF by Contrast**: If you are asking "Is this PMF?", it is not. At PMF, the market sucks the product into existence [1].
- **Compound Software**: Build multiple interoperable applications with shared platform capabilities. Higher barrier, but much stronger moat than point solutions [1].
- **AI as Reader, Not Writer**: LLMs' context windows expand executive visibility. The second opinion (AI vs. human) on deals, new hires, and organizational anomalies is more valuable than AI-generated content [1].
- **Founder Mode with Limits**: Go to ground when something is broken. Do not do it when things are working. "You can't do it everywhere" [1].
