# Ali Rowghani

Ali Rowghani is a member of YC's Continuity team. Before YC, he worked closely with four leaders he considers extraordinary: [[Ed Catmull]] (Pixar founder), [[Steve Jobs]] (Pixar CEO), John Lasseter (Pixar Chief Creative Officer), and Bob Iger (Disney CEO). He also served at Twitter during a period of rapid growth (50 hires per month across global offices for 18 months).

## Worldview

Rowghani's thinking centers on the CEO transition from builder to leader. He frames the startup CEO's role as evolving through three phases: building a product (Doer-in-Chief), building a company (Company-Builder-in-Chief), and investing in transformative new products. The Phase 2 transition is the critical inflection point where most founders either grow or fail.

His leadership philosophy is trust-centric. Despite observing radically different leadership styles at Pixar/Disney, he found that all great leaders share three foundational traits: clarity of thought and communication, judgment about people, and personal integrity and commitment. Trust, he argues, is both a science (making accurate predictions and good people choices) and an art (saying the right thing at the right time, standing for ideals bigger than yourself).

## Key Frameworks

- **[[Mission-to-Metrics]]**: Mission (permanent purpose), Strategy (revisited 2x/year), Metrics (drivers, not top-line results). The SpaceX manufacturing floor anecdote is his signature example.
- **[[Three Traits of Great Leaders]]**: Clarity, judgment about people, integrity. Includes detailed survey questions for measuring each trait.
- **Three phases of a startup CEO**: Phase 1 (product), Phase 2 (company, ~20-500 employees), Phase 3 (reinvestment in new products).

## Distinct Perspective

Rowghani is unusual among startup advisors in emphasizing that leadership can be measured systematically, not just through business outcomes. He provides concrete survey instruments and argues that gathering employee data should begin around 50 people. His advice on executive hiring is specific: expect 25% failure rate, most CEOs take too long to fire their first executive, and 50% of CEO time should go to hiring and managing the senior team.

He draws heavily on Pixar culture stories (Toy Story 2 rewrite, Ed Catmull's studio closure for recuperation) to illustrate how authentic values emerge from past behavior, not from CEO-authored mission statements.
