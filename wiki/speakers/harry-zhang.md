# Harry Zhang

Harry Zhang is the co-founder and CEO of [Lob](https://lob.com/) (YC S13), a company that provides APIs for enterprises to programmatically send physical mail. Before founding Lob, he worked at Microsoft where he built a direct mail program that became one of the most effective marketing channels for his team [1].

## Worldview

Zhang approaches startups with a sales-driven, customer-obsessed methodology. His philosophy: "We want customers to pay us for something before we actually go and do it" [1]. He treats recruiting, enterprise sales, and product development with the same framework -- understand the individual's motivations and tailor the message accordingly.

He is pragmatic about the unsexy nature of his business. "Mail is not exactly the most sexy thing in the world" [1]. But he sees this as an advantage: the market is enormous, the incumbents are slow, and an API layer can give small companies access to the same capabilities that previously required enterprise-scale operations [1].

## Key Frameworks

**The Platform Fee Model**: When enterprise customers compare your product apples-to-apples against commoditized alternatives, restructure pricing to separate the technology value from the commodity. Lob charges a $25-30K platform fee for API access plus competitive per-piece mail pricing. This shifts the buying conversation from "why is your mail more expensive?" to "is our technology worth $25K?" [1].

**The Lighthouse Customer**: Enterprise sales is a chicken-and-egg problem. You need one big customer to get others. Getting the first one combines luck, hard work, and domain understanding. Once you succeed with one, take "that exact same story to a similar customer" [1].

**Sell Ahead, But Align**: It is acceptable to sell capabilities you do not yet have, but you must be transparent about timelines, get commitments in writing, and treat the customer as a partner in building what they need. The mugs API taught Zhang the cost of building without written commitment; the Booking.com deal taught him the value of honest negotiation [1].

**Founder Role Evolution**: A founder's role changes constantly -- from product builder to sales leader to marketing driver. This is not a problem; it is a signal that the company has matured enough in one area to hire specialists. But the founder must be comfortable with every function: "If you as the founder can't sell the product, nobody else is going to be able to either" [1].

## Distinct Perspective

Zhang is distinctive for running a B2B infrastructure company that bridges the physical and digital worlds. While most API companies are purely software, Lob must coordinate with hundreds of physical print partners across multiple countries. His decision not to vertically integrate (not buying printers) reflects a deliberate strategic choice to keep the business asset-light and flexible [1].

He is also notable for implementing RSUs instead of stock options at a private company -- an unusual choice designed to remove financial risk from employees and ensure they do not stay for the wrong reasons [1].
